href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">\xd0\xa1\xd0\xbe\xd1\x8e\xd0\xb7</span>, meaning "union", <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GRAU_index" title="GRAU index">GRAU index</a> <b>11A511</b>) is a family of <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable launch systems</a> developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OKB-1" title="OKB-1">OKB-1</a>, and manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Progress_Rocket_Space_Centre" title="Progress Rocket Space Centre">Progress Rocket Space Centre</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samara,_Russia" title="Samara, Russia">Samara, Russia</a>. The Soyuz launch vehicle is the most frequently used launch vehicle in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> program ended in 2011, Soyuz rockets became the only launch vehicle able to transport astronauts to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>.</p>\n<p>The Soyuz vehicles are used as the launcher for the manned <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(spacecraft)" title="Soyuz (spacecraft)">Soyuz spacecraft</a> as part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_program" title="Soyuz program">Soyuz program</a>, as well as to launch unmanned <a href="/wiki/Progress_(spacecraft)" title="Progress (spacecraft)">Progress supply spacecraft</a> to the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> and for commercial launches marketed and operated by <a href="/wiki/Starsem" title="Starsem">Starsem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arianespace" title="Arianespace">Arianespace</a>. All Soyuz rockets use <a href="/wiki/RP-1" title="RP-1">RP-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> (LOX) propellant, with the exception of the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U2" title="Soyuz-U2">Soyuz-U2</a>, which used <a href="/wiki/Syntin" title="Syntin">Syntin</a>, a variant of RP-1, with LOX. In the United States, it has the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> designation <b>A-2</b>. The Soyuz family is a subset of the <a href="/wiki/R-7_(rocket_family)" title="R-7 (rocket family)">R-7 family</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1926" data-file-width="3000" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Soyuz_rocket_engines.jpg/220px-Soyuz_rocket_engines.jpg" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_rocket_engines.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSoyuz rocket engines</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_space_workhorse">A space workhorse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: A space workhorse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket)" title="Soyuz (rocket)">Soyuz (rocket)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U" title="Soyuz-U">Soyuz-U</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-FG" title="Soyuz-FG">Soyuz-FG</a></div>\n<p>The Soyuz launcher was introduced in 1966, deriving from the <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(rocket_family)" title="Vostok (rocket family)">Vostok</a> launcher, which in turn was based on the 8K74 or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R-7a_rocket" title="R-7a rocket">R-7a</a> <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">intercontinental ballistic missile</a>. It was initially a three-stage rocket with a Block I upper stage. Later a <a href="/wiki/Molniya_(rocket)" title="Molniya (rocket)">Molniya variant</a> was produced by adding a fourth stage, allowing it to reach the highly elliptical <a href="/wiki/Molniya_orbit" title="Molniya orbit">molniya orbit</a>. A later variant was the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U" title="Soyuz-U">Soyuz-U</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The production of Soyuz launchers reached a peak of 60 per year in the early 1980s. It has become the world\'s most used space launcher, flying over 1700 times, far more than any other rocket. Despite its age and perhaps thanks to its simplicity, this rocket family has been notable for its low cost and very high reliability, both of which appeal to commercial clients.</p>\n<h3><span id="Soyuz_/_Fregat"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Soyuz_.2F_Fregat">Soyuz / Fregat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Soyuz / Fregat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fregat" title="Fregat">Fregat</a></div>\n<p>In the early 1990s plans were made for a redesigned Soyuz with a <a href="/wiki/Fregat" title="Fregat">Fregat</a> upper stage. The Fregat engine was developed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NPO_Lavochkin" title="NPO Lavochkin">NPO Lavochkin</a> from the propulsion module of its <a href="/wiki/Phobos_program" title="Phobos program">Phobos interplanetary probes</a>. Although endorsed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Space_Agency" title="Russian Space Agency">Russian Space Agency</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Ministry_of_Defence" title="Russian Ministry of Defence">Russian Ministry of Defence</a> in 1993 and designated "Rus" as a Russification and modernisation of Soyuz, and later renamed Soyuz-2, a funding shortage prevented implementation of the plan. The creation of <a href="/wiki/Starsem" title="Starsem">Starsem</a> in July 1996 provided new funding for the creation of a less ambitious variant, the Soyuz-Fregat or Soyuz U/Fregat. This consisted of a slightly modified Soyuz U combined with the Fregat upper stage, with a capacity of up to 1,350\xc2\xa0kg to <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_transfer_orbit" title="Geostationary transfer orbit">geostationary transfer orbit</a>. In April 1997, Starsem obtained a contract from the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> to launch two pairs of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_mission" title="Cluster mission">Cluster 2 plasma science satellites</a> using the Soyuz-Fregat. Before the introduction of this new model, Starsem launched 24 satellites of the <a href="/wiki/Globalstar" title="Globalstar">Globalstar</a> constellation in 6 launches with a restartable Ikar upper stage, between September 22, 1999 and November 22, 1999. After successful test flights of Soyuz-Fregat on February 9, 2000 and March 20, 2000, the Cluster 2 satellites were launched on July 16, 2000 and August 9, 2000. Another Soyuz-Fregat launched the ESA\'s <a href="/wiki/Mars_Express" title="Mars Express">Mars Express</a> probe from Baikonur in June 2003. Now the Soyuz-Fregat launcher is used by Starsem for commercial payloads.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_TMA-13_erected_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3783" data-file-width="2832" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Soyuz_TMA-13_erected_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg/170px-Soyuz_TMA-13_erected_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Soyuz_TMA-13_erected_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg/255px-Soyuz_TMA-13_erected_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Soyuz_TMA-13_erected_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg/340px-Soyuz_TMA-13_erected_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_TMA-13_erected_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Soyuz-FG" title="Soyuz-FG">Soyuz-FG</a> erected at the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> launch pad 1/5 <i><a href="/wiki/Gagarin%27s_Start" title="Gagarin\'s Start">Gagarin\'s Start</a></i> (October 2008)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="ISS_crew_transport">ISS crew transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: ISS crew transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Between February 1, 2003 and July 26, 2005 with the grounding of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> fleet, Soyuz was the only means of transportation to and from the International Space Station. This included the transfer of supplies, via Progress spacecraft, and crew changeovers. Since the retirement of the Space Shuttle fleet in 2011, the U.S. space program is without any means to take astronauts into orbit, and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> is dependent on the Soyuz to send crew into space for the immediate future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfn-20140527_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfn-20140527-3">[3]</a></sup> NASA is scheduled to resume manned flights from the United States in late 2018 or 2019 through the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_Crew_Development" title="Commercial Crew Development">Commercial Crew Development</a> program.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_incidents">Recent incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recent incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A long string of successful Soyuz launches was broken on October 15, 2002 when the unmanned Soyuz U launch of the Photon-M satellite from Plesetsk fell back near the launch pad and exploded 29 seconds after lift-off. One person from the ground crew was killed and eight injured. Another failure occurred on June 21, 2005, during a <a href="/wiki/Molniya_(satellite)" title="Molniya (satellite)">Molniya</a> <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">communications satellite</a> launch from the Plesetsk launch site, which used a four-stage version of the rocket called <a href="/wiki/Molniya-M" title="Molniya-M">Molniya-M</a>. The flight ended six minutes after the launch because of a failure of the third stage engine or an unfulfilled order to separate the second and third stages. The rocket\'s second and third stages, which are identical to the Soyuz, and its payload (a Molniya-3K satellite) crashed in the Uvatski region of <a href="/wiki/Tyumen" title="Tyumen">Tyumen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> On August 24, 2011, an unmanned Soyuz-U carrying cargo to the International Space Station crashed, failing to reach orbit. On December 23, 2011 a Soyuz-2.1b launching a Meridian-5 military communications satellite failed in the 7th minute of launch because of an anomaly in the third stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soyuz-2_and_Guiana_spaceport">Soyuz-2 and Guiana spaceport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Soyuz-2 and Guiana spaceport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_2_metop.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2272" data-file-width="1704" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Soyuz_2_metop.jpg/220px-Soyuz_2_metop.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Soyuz_2_metop.jpg/330px-Soyuz_2_metop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Soyuz_2_metop.jpg/440px-Soyuz_2_metop.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_2_metop.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2_(rocket)" title="Soyuz-2 (rocket)">Soyuz 2</a> ready to launch (2007)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-2" title="Soyuz-2">Soyuz-2</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_at_the_Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Soyuz at the Guiana Space Centre">Soyuz at the Guiana Space Centre</a></div>\n<p>The venerable Soyuz launcher is gradually being replaced by a new version, now named Soyuz/ST (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2_(rocket)" title="Soyuz-2 (rocket)">Soyuz-2</a>), which has a new digital guidance system and a highly modified third stage with a new engine. The first development version of Soyuz 2 called Soyuz-2-1a, which is already equipped with the digital guidance system, but is still propelled by an old third stage engine, started on November 4, 2004 from Plesetsk on a suborbital test flight, followed by an orbital flight on October 23, 2006 from Baikonur. The fully modified launcher (version Soyuz-2-1b) flew first on December 27, 2006 with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CoRoT" title="CoRoT">CoRoT</a> satellite from the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a>.</p>\n<p>On 19 January 2005, the <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federal_Space_Agency" title="Russian Federal Space Agency">Russian Federal Space Agency</a> agreed to launch Soyuz/ST rockets from the <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Guiana Space Centre">Guiana Space Centre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The equatorial launch site allows the Soyuz to deliver 2.7 to 4.9 tonnes into <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">sun-synchronous orbit</a>, depending on the third-stage engine used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Construction of a new pad started in 2005 and was completed in April 2011. The pad used vertical loading common at Guiana, unlike the horizontal loading used at the <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A simulated launch was conducted in early May 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The first operational launch happened on 21 October 2011, bearing the first two satellites in <a href="/wiki/Galileo_(satellite_navigation)" title="Galileo (satellite navigation)">Galileo</a> global positioning system.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U" title="Soyuz-U">Soyuz-U</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-FG" title="Soyuz-FG">Soyuz-FG</a> rockets, which are still in service, are gradually being replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2_(rocket)" title="Soyuz-2 (rocket)">Soyuz-2</a> from 2014 onwards and should be retired in 2016.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" title="Molniya-M">Molniya-M</a> (1964-2010) was also derived from the Soyuz family.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Assembling_the_rocket">Assembling the rocket</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Assembling the rocket">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_rocket_assembly.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1954" data-file-width="3000" height="130" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Soyuz_rocket_assembly.jpg/300px-Soyuz_rocket_assembly.jpg 1.5x, class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_rocket_assembly.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSoyuz rocket assembly: the first and second stages are in the background, already joined together; the third The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_spacecraft" title="Soyuz spacecraft">Soyuz spacecraft</a>, covered by its launch shroud, is in the lower right corner.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The rocket is assembled horizontally in the Assembly and Testing Building. The assembled rocket is transported to the launch site in its horizontal state and then raised. This is different from the vertical assembly of, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a>, and is one of the features that makes Soyuz cheaper<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to prepare for launch.</p>\n<p>Assembling a horizontally positioned rocket is relatively simple as all modules are easily accessible. Assembling the rocket in vertical position would require a windproof high-rise hangar, which was not considered financially feasible at the time the rocket was designed, due to the failing economy of the Soviet Union.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_pad">Launch pad</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Launch pad">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_TMA-13_arrives_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2832" data-file-width="4256" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Soyuz_TMA-13_arrives_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg/250px-Soyuz_TMA-13_arrives_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Soyuz_TMA-13_arrives_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg/375px-Soyuz_TMA-13_arrives_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Soyuz_TMA-13_arrives_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg/500px-Soyuz_TMA-13_arrives_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_TMA-13_arrives_at_Baikonur_Cosmodrome_launch_pad.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-13" title="Soyuz TMA-13">Soyuz TMA-13</a> being erected at the <a href="/wiki/Gagarin%27s_Start" title="Gagarin\'s Start">Gagarin\'s Start</a> launch pad, October 10, 2008.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The entire rocket is suspended in the launch system by the load-bearing mechanisms on the strap-on boosters where they are attached to the central core. The latter rests on the nose sections of the strap-on boosters. This scheme resembles flight conditions when the strap-on boosters push the central core forward. The concept of suspending the rocket was one of the novelties introduced with the R-7/Soyuz.</p>\n<p>Since the launch pad has been eliminated, the bottom portion of the missile is lowered. The launch system trusses bear the wind loads. Resistance to high wind is an important feature of the launch system, as the Kazakhstan steppes, where the Baikonur launch site is located, are known for windstorms.</p>\n<div style="clear:left;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_rocket_ASTP.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2723" data-file-width="1856" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Soyuz_rocket_ASTP.jpg/170px-Soyuz_rocket_ASTP.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Soyuz_rocket_ASTP.jpg/255px-Soyuz_rocket_ASTP.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Soyuz_rocket_ASTP.jpg/340px-Soyuz_rocket_ASTP.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Soyuz_rocket_ASTP.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA <a href="/wiki/Soyuz-U" title="Soyuz-U">Soyuz-U</a> on the launch pad, ahead of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo-Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo-Soyuz Test Project"><span class="nowrap">Soyuz 19</span> (ASTP)</a> launch on 15 July 1975</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>During launch, the support booms track the movement of the rocket. After the support boom heads emerge from the special support recess in the nose sections of the strapons, the support booms and trusses disconnect from the rocket airframe, swiveling on the support axes and freeing the way for the rocket to lift off. During launch, the rocket and the launch facility form a single dynamic system.</p>\n<p>When the strapon booster engines stop, the boosters fall away, providing nonimpact separation. If the skies are clear, ground observers can see a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korolev_cross" title="Korolev cross">Korolev cross</a> formed by the falling boosters.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fairings_used_for_unmanned_missions">Fairings used for unmanned missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soyuz_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fairings used for unmanned missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Soyuz <a href="/wiki/Launch_vehicle" title="Launch vehicle">launch vehicle</a> is used for various Russian unmanned missions and is also marketed by Starsem for commercial satellite launches.
