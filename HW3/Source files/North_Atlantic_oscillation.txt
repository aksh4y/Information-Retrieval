title="Atlantic multidecadal oscillation">Atlantic multidecadal oscillation</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:174px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nao_indices_comparison.jpg"><img alt="Nao indices comparison.jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="437" data-file-width="172" height="437" href="/wiki/File:Nao_indices_comparison.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <b>North Atlantic Oscillation</b> (<b>NAO</b>) is a weather phenomenon in the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> of fluctuations in the difference of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure#Mean_sea_level_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure at sea level (SLP)</a> between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icelandic_low" title="Icelandic low">Icelandic low</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azores_high" title="Azores high">Azores high</a>. Through fluctuations in the strength of the Icelandic low and the Azores high, it controls the strength and direction of westerly <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> and location of <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> tracks across the North Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a>, and varies over time with no particular periodicity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>The NAO was discovered through several studies in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Unlike the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation</a> phenomenon in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, the NAO is a largely atmospheric mode. It is one of the most important manifestations of climate fluctuations in the North Atlantic and surrounding humid climates.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>The North Atlantic Oscillation is closely related to the Arctic oscillation (AO) or Northern Annular Mode (NAM), but should not be confused with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation" title="Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</a> (AMO).</p>\n<p></p>\n<div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The NAO has multiple possible definitions. The easiest to understand are those based on measuring the seasonal average air pressure difference between stations, such as:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stykkisholmur" title="Stykkisholmur">Stykkisholmur</a>/<a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjav\xc3\xadk">Reykjav\xc3\xadk</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ponta_Delgada" title="Ponta Delgada">Ponta Delgada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> and Stykkisholmur/Reykjav\xc3\xadk</li>\n<li>Azores (1865\xe2\x80\x932002), <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> (1821\xe2\x80\x932007), and Reykjav\xc3\xadk<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurrell_CGD_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hurrell_CGD-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<p>These definitions all have in common the same northern point (because this is the only station in the region with a long record) in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>; and various southern points. All are attempting to capture the same pattern of variation, by choosing stations in the "eye" of the two stable pressure areas, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azores_high" title="Azores high">Azores high</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icelandic_low" title="Icelandic low">Icelandic low</a> (shown in the graphic).</p>\n<p>A more complex definition, only possible with more complete modern records generated by <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a>, is based on the principal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empirical_orthogonal_function" title="Empirical orthogonal function">empirical orthogonal function</a> (EOF) of surface pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hurrell_CGD_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hurrell_CGD-3">[3]</a></sup> This definition has a high degree of correlation with the station-based definition. This then leads onto a debate as to whether the NAO is distinct from the AO/NAM, and if not, which of the two is to be considered the most physically based expression of atmospheric structure (as opposed to the one that most clearly falls out of mathematical expression).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Winter-NAO-Index.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="351" data-file-width="566" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Winter-NAO-Index.svg/300px-Winter-NAO-Index.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Winter-NAO-Index.svg/450px-Winter-NAO-Index.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Winter-NAO-Index.svg/600px-Winter-NAO-Index.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Winter-NAO-Index.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nWinter index of the NAO based on the difference of normalized <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_level_pressure" title="Sea level pressure">sea level pressure</a> (SLP) between <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stykkisholmur" title="Stykkisholmur">Stykkisholmur</a>/<a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjav\xc3\xadk">Reykjav\xc3\xadk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> since 1864, with a <a href="/wiki/Local_regression" title="Local regression">loess smoothing</a> (black)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Westerly winds blowing across the Atlantic bring moist air into <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. In years when <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> are strong, summers are cool, winters are mild and <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> is frequent. If westerlies are suppressed, the temperature is more extreme in summer and winter leading to <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a>, deep freezes and reduced rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPC-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A permanent low-pressure system over <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Low" title="Icelandic Low">Icelandic Low</a>) and a permanent high-pressure system over the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Azores_High" title="Azores High">Azores High</a>) control the direction and strength of westerly winds into Europe. The relative strengths and positions of these systems vary from year to year and this variation is known as the NAO. A large difference in the pressure at the two stations (a high index year, denoted NAO+) leads to increased westerlies and, consequently, cool summers and mild and wet winters in Central Europe and its Atlantic facade. In contrast, if the index is low (NAO-), westerlies are suppressed, northern European areas suffer cold dry winters and storms track southwards toward the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. This brings increased storm activity and rainfall to southern Europe and <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>.</p>\n<p>Especially during the months of November to April, the NAO is responsible for much of the variability of weather in the North Atlantic region, affecting wind speed and wind direction changes, changes in temperature and moisture distribution and the intensity, number and track of storms. Research now suggests that the NAO may be more predictable than previously assumed and skilful winter forecasts may be possible for the NAO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Although having a less direct influence than for Western Europe, the NAO is also believed to affect the weather over much of eastern North America. During the winter, when the index is high (NAO+), the Icelandic low draws a stronger south-westerly circulation over the eastern half of the North American continent which prevents Arctic air from plunging southward. In combination with the El Ni\xc3\xb1o, this effect can produce significantly warmer winters over the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">northeastern United States</a> and southeastern <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Conversely, when the NAO index is low (NAO-), the eastern seaboard and southeastern United States can incur winter cold outbreaks more than the norm with associated heavy snowstorms in Appalachia/mid-Atlantic region and sub-freezing conditions into Florida. Another condition that occurs as result of NAO- is increased polar front activity plunging farther south than usual, beyond the Gulf of Mexico/Caribbean Sea basins resulting in a break in tropical weather for those regions, as instance of this is when <a href="/wiki/Tehuantepecer" title="Tehuantepecer">Tehuantepecer</a> winds blow outward into the Pacific Ocean away from the coastline of Southern Mexico. In summer, a strong NAO- is thought to contribute to a weakened jet stream that normally pulls zonal systems into the Atlantic Basin contributing significantly to excessively long lasting heat waves over Europe.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_North_Atlantic_sea_level">Effects on North Atlantic sea level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects on North Atlantic sea level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Under a positive NAO index (NAO+), regional reduction in atmospheric pressure results in a regional rise in sea level due to the \'inverse barometer effect\'. This effect is important to both the interpretation of historic sea level records and predictions of future sea level trends, as mean pressure fluctuations of the order of millibars can lead to sea level fluctuations of the order of centimeters.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Atlantic_hurricanes">North Atlantic hurricanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: North Atlantic hurricanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-1600_Atlantic_hurricane_seasons" title="Pre-1600 Atlantic hurricane seasons">Pre-1600 Atlantic hurricane seasons</a></div>\n<p>By controlling the position of the Azores high, the NAO also influences the direction of general storm paths for major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">North Atlantic tropical cyclones</a>: a position of the Azores high farther to the south tends to force storms into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, whereas a northern position allows them to track up the North American Atlantic Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As <a href="/wiki/Paleotempestology" title="Paleotempestology">paleotempestological</a> research has shown, few major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> struck the Gulf coast during 3000\xe2\x80\x931400 BC and again during the most recent millennium. These quiescent intervals were separated by a hyperactive period during 1400 BC and 1000 AD, when the Gulf coast was struck frequently by catastrophic hurricanes and their landfall probabilities increased by 3\xe2\x80\x935 times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LiuFearn2000_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-LiuFearn2000-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_effects">Ecological effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecological effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Until recently, the NAO had been in an overall more positive regime since the late 1970s, bringing colder conditions to the North-West Atlantic, which has been linked with the thriving populations of <a href="/wiki/Labrador_Sea" title="Labrador Sea">Labrador Sea</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snow_crab" title="Snow crab">snow crabs</a>, which have a low temperature optimum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearson_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The NAO+ warming of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> reduces survival of <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a> larvae which are at the upper limits of their temperature tolerance, as does the cooling in the Labrador Sea, where the cod larvae are at their lower temperature limits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearson_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-13">[13]</a></sup> Though not the critical factor, the NAO+ peak in the early 1990s may have contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Cod_fishing_in_Newfoundland" title="Cod fishing in Newfoundland">collapse of the Newfoundland cod fishery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearson_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> an NAO+ causes warmer temperatures and increased rainfall, and thus warmer, less <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">saline</a> surface water. This prevents nutrient-rich <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> which has reduced productivity. <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bank" title="Georges Bank">Georges Bank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Maine" title="Gulf of Maine">Gulf of Maine</a> are affected by this reduced cod catch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pearson_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-pearson-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The strength of the NAO is also a determinant in the population fluctuations of the intensively studied <a href="/wiki/Soay_sheep" title="Soay sheep">Soay sheep</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coulson_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-coulson-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Strangely enough, Jonas and Joern (2007) found a strong signal between NAO and grasshopper species composition in the tall grass prairies of the midwestern United States. They found that, even though NAO does not significantly affect the weather in the midwest, there was a significant increase in abundance of common grasshopper species (i.e. <i>Hypochlora alba, Hesperotettix</i> spp., <i>Phoetaliotes nebrascensis, M. scudderi, M. keeleri, and Pseudopomala brachyptera</i>) following winters during the positive phase of NAO and a significant increase in the abundance of less common species (i.e. <i>Campylacantha olivacea, Melanoplus sanguinipes, Mermiria picta, Melanoplus packardii, and Boopedon gracile</i>) following winters during a negative phase of the NAO. This is thought to be the first study showing a link between NAO and terrestrial insects in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jonas_and_Joern_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jonas_and_Joern-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span id="Winter_of_2009\xe2\x80\x9310_in_Europe"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Winter_of_2009.E2.80.9310_in_Europe">Winter of 2009\xe2\x80\x9310 in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Winter of 2009\xe2\x80\x9310 in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Winter_of_2009%E2%80%9310_in_Europe" title="Winter of 2009\xe2\x80\x9310 in Europe">winter of 2009\xe2\x80\x9310 in Europe</a> was unusually cold. It is hypothesized that this may be due to a combination of low solar activity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> a warm phase of the El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation and a strong easterly phase of the Quasi-Biennial Oscillation all occurring simultaneously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> reported that the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, for example, had experienced its coldest winter for 30 years. This coincided with an exceptionally negative phase of the NAO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Analysis published in mid-2010 confirmed that the concurrent \'<a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</a>\' event and the rare occurrence of an extremely negative NAO were involved, this has become known as a "Hybrid El Ni\xc3\xb1o".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>However, during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_of_2010%E2%80%932011_in_Europe" title="Winter of 2010\xe2\x80\x932011 in Europe">winter of 2010-11 in Northern and Western Europe</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Icelandic_low" title="Icelandic low">Icelandic low</a>, typically positioned west of Iceland and east of Greenland, appeared regularly to the east of Iceland and so allowed exceptionally cold air into Europe from the Arctic. A strong area of high pressure was initially situated over <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, reversing the normal wind pattern in the northwestern Atlantic, creating a <a href="/wiki/Block_(meteorology)" title="Block (meteorology)">blocking</a> pattern driving warm air into northeastern Canada and cold air into Western Europe, as was the case during the previous winter. This occurred during a La Ni\xc3\xb1a season, and is connected to the rare <a href="/wiki/Arctic_dipole_anomaly" title="Arctic dipole anomaly">Arctic dipole anomaly</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the north western part of the Atlantic, both of these winters were mild, especially 2009-2010, which was the warmest recorded in Canada. The winter of 2010-2011 was particularly above normal in the northern Arctic regions of that country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The probability of cold winters with much snow in Central Europe rises when the Arctic is covered by less sea ice in summer. Scientists of the Research Unit Potsdam of the Alfred Wegener Institute for Polar and Marine Research in the Helmholtz Association have decrypted a mechanism in which a shrinking summertime sea ice cover changes the air pressure zones in the Arctic atmosphere and effects on European winter weather.</p>\n<p>If there is a particularly large-scale melt of Arctic sea ice in summer, as observed in recent years, two important effects are intensified. Firstly, the retreat of the light ice surface reveals the darker ocean, causing it to warm up more in summer from the solar radiation (<a href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a> mechanism). Secondly, the diminished ice cover can no longer prevent the heat stored in the ocean being released into the atmosphere (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lid_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lid effect (page does not exist)">lid effect</a>). As a result of the decreased sea ice cover the air is warmed more greatly than it used to be particularly in autumn and winter because during this period the ocean is warmer than the atmosphere.</p>\n<p>The warming of the air near to the ground leads to rising movements and the atmosphere becomes less stable. One of these patterns is the air pressure difference between the Arctic and mid-latitudes: the so-called <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a> with the Azores highs and Iceland lows known from the weather reports. If this difference is high, a strong westerly wind will result which in winter carries warm and humid Atlantic air masses right down to Europe. In the negative phase when pressure differences are low, cold Arctic air can then easily penetrate southward through Europe without being interrupted by the usual westerlies, as has been the case frequently over the last three winters. Model calculations show that the air pressure difference with decreased sea ice cover in the Arctic summer is weakened in the following winter, enabling Arctic cold to push down to mid-latitudes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span id="Winter_of_2015\xe2\x80\x9316_in_Europe"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Winter_of_2015.E2.80.9316_in_Europe">Winter of 2015\xe2\x80\x9316 in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Atlantic_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Winter of 2015\xe2\x80\x9316 in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Despite one of the strongest El Nino ever recorded in the Pacific Ocean, a largely positive North Atlantic Oscillation prevailed over Europe during the Winter of 2015-2016. For example, Cumbria in England <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> registered one of the wettest months on record. Meanwhile, the Maltese Islands in the Mediterranean Sea registered one of the driest years ever recorded up to beginning of March as the Island\'s national average was only 235mm to date with some areas registering even less than 200mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
