href="/wiki/File:Cape_hatteras_1989.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCape Hatteras from space, October 1989</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Cape Hatteras</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="\'h\' in \'hi\'">h</span><span title="/\xc3\xa6/: \'a\' in \'bad\'">\xc3\xa6</span><span title="\'t\' in \'tie\'">t</span><span title="/\xc9\x99/: \'a\' in \'about\'">\xc9\x99</span><span title="\'r\' in \'rye\'">r</span><span title="/\xc9\x99/: \'a\' in \'about\'">\xc9\x99</span><span title="\'s\' in \'sigh\'">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a thin, broken strand of islands that arch out into the Atlantic Ocean away from the US mainland, then back toward the mainland, creating a series of sheltered islands between the Outer Banks and the mainland. For thousands of years these barrier islands have survived onslaughts of wind and sea. Long stretches of beach, sand dunes, marshes, and maritime forests create a unique environment where wind and waves shape the topography. A large area of the Outer Banks is part of a National Park, called the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras_National_Seashore" title="Cape Hatteras National Seashore">Cape Hatteras National Seashore</a>. It is also the nearest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landmass" title="Landmass">landmass</a> on the US mainland to <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, which is about 563 nautical miles (648\xc2\xa0mi; 1,043\xc2\xa0km) to the east-southeast</p>\n<p>The treacherous waters off the coast of the Outer Banks is known as the Graveyard of the Atlantic, Over 600 ships wrecked here as victims of shallow shoals, storms, and war. Diamond Shoals, a bank of shifting sand ridges hidden beneath the turbulent sea off Cape Hatteras, has never promised safe passage for ships. In the past 400 years the graveyard has claimed many lives, but island villagers saved many. As early as the 1870s, villagers served in the US Life-Saving Service. Others staffed lighthouses built to guide mariners. Few ships wreck today, but storms still uncover the ruins of the old wrecks that lie along the beaches of the Outer Banks.</p>\n<p>Cape Hatteras National Seashore protects parts of three barrier islands: Bodie Island, Hatteras Island, and Ocracoke Island. Beach and sound access ramps, campgrounds, nature trails, and lighthouses can be found and explored on all three islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The community of <a href="/wiki/Buxton,_North_Carolina" title="Buxton, North Carolina">Buxton</a> lies on the inland side of the Cape itself, at the widest part of <a href="/wiki/Hatteras_Island" title="Hatteras Island">Hatteras Island</a>. It is the largest community on the island, and is home to the governmental offices and schools for the Island.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Geography"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Geography</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-2"><a href="#Climate"><span class="tocnumber">1.1</span> <span class="toctext">Climate</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#History"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">History</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Awards_and_recognition"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Awards and recognition</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#Notes"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Notes</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-7"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Hatteras&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cape Hatteras is a bend in <a href="/wiki/Hatteras_Island" title="Hatteras Island">Hatteras Island</a>, one of the long thin <a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">barrier islands</a> that make up the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a>. It is the site where the two great basins of the East Coast meet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-point_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-point-2">[2]</a></sup> The cape\'s <a href="/wiki/Shoal" title="Shoal">shoals</a> are known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diamond_Shoals" title="Diamond Shoals">Diamond Shoals</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cape_Hatteras&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3054" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg/220px-Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg/330px-Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg/440px-Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAn aerial view of where Hurricane Isabel cut inlets into Hatteras Island</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Cape Hatteras has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification">K\xc3\xb6ppen</a> <i>Cfa</i>), with long hot summers, and short cool winters. Most of the area falls into <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_zone" title="Hardiness zone">USDA Plant Hardiness Zone</a> 9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Cape Hatteras is surrounded by water, with Pamlico Sound to the west and the Atlantic Ocean to the east. The proximity to water moderates conditions throughout the year, producing cooler summers and warmer winters than inland areas of North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAALCD_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAALCD-4">[4]</a></sup> The cape is the northern limit of tropical fauna.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During the summer, average daily highs are in the 85\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (29\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C) range, and occasional intense (but brief) thundershowers occur. As a result of its proximity to water, temperatures above 90\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C) are rare, with an average of only 2.3 days annually above 90\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C);<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDC_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NCDC-6">[6]</a></sup> one to three years out of each decade will not see any 90\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F readings. The coolest month, January, has a daily high of 52\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (11.1\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C), with lows normally well above freezing (32 F). The average window for freezing temperatures is from December 12 to March 11 (allowing a growing season of 275 days), between which there is an average of 21 nights with lows at or below the freezing mark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDC_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NCDC-6">[6]</a></sup> Extremes in temperature range from 6\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (\xe2\x88\x9214\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C) on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/January_1985_Arctic_outbreak" title="January 1985 Arctic outbreak">January 21, 1985</a> up to 97\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (36\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C) on June 27, 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCDC_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-NCDC-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Snowfall is observed only occasionally, and usually very light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAALCD_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAALCD-4">[4]</a></sup> Precipitation, mostly in the form of rain, is over 58 inches (1,500\xc2\xa0mm) per year, making it the wettest coastal location in North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAALCD_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAALCD-4">[4]</a></sup> Precipitation is fairly evenly distributed throughout the year. However, April and May represent a slightly drier season, while August to October are the wettest months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAALCD_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-NOAALCD-4">[4]</a></sup> On average, August is the wettest month, owing to high frequencies of both summer thunderstorms and tropical systems (hurricanes, tropical storms and tropical depressions) that affect the area, mostly from August to early October.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Due to its exposed position, Cape Hatteras is virtually the highest-risk area for hurricanes and tropical storms along the entire U.S. Eastern seaboard. Cape Hatteras can experience significant wind and/or water damage from tropical systems moving (usually northward or northeastward) near or over North Carolina\'s Outer Banks, while other areas (i.e. Wilmington, NC or Myrtle Beach and Charleston, SC to the south and Norfolk, VA and Maryland\'s Eastern Shore to the north) experience much less, minimal or no damage. The Cape Hatteras area is infamous for being frequently struck by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> that move up the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a>. The strike of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> in 2003 was particularly devastating for the area. Isabel devastated the entire Outer Banks and also split Hatteras Island between the two small towns of <a href="/wiki/Frisco,_North_Carolina" title="Frisco, North Carolina">Frisco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Hatteras, North Carolina">Hatteras</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Carolina_State_Highway_12" title="North Carolina State Highway 12">NC 12</a>, which provides a direct route from <a href="/wiki/Nags_Head,_North_Carolina" title="Nags Head, North Carolina">Nags Head</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hatteras_Island" title="Hatteras Island">Hatteras Island</a>, was washed out when the hurricane created a new <a href="/wiki/Inlet" title="Inlet">inlet</a>. Students had to use a <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> to get to school. The inlet was filled in with sand by the Army Corps of Engineers which took nearly two months to complete. The road, electrical and water lines were quickly rebuilt when the inlet was filled.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<table class="wikitable collapsible" style="width:90%; text-align:center; font-size:90%; line-height: 1.2em; margin:auto;">\n<tr>\n<th Isabel in 2003</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The name Hatteras is the sixth oldest surviving English place-name in the U.S. An inlet north of the cape was named "Hatrask" in 1585 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Grenville" title="Sir Richard Grenville">Sir Richard Grenville</a>, the admiral leading the <a href="/wiki/Roanoke_Colony" title="Roanoke Colony">Roanoke Colony</a> expedition sent by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Walter_Raleigh" title="Sir Walter Raleigh">Sir Walter Raleigh</a>. It was later applied to the island and cape as well, and modified to "Hatteras."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> Hatteras is the name of the <a href="/wiki/Hatteras_Indians" title="Hatteras Indians">Hatteras Indians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Because mariners utilize ocean currents to speed their journey, many ships venture close to Cape Hatteras when traveling along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">eastern seaboard</a>, risking the perils of sailing close to the shoals amid turbulent water and the frequent storms occurring in the area. So many ships have been lost off Cape Hatteras that the area is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Graveyard_of_the_Atlantic" title="Graveyard of the Atlantic">Graveyard of the Atlantic</a>." Cape Hatteras is also well known for surfing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouse</a> at the cape was built in 1803; it was replaced by the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras_Lighthouse" title="Cape Hatteras Lighthouse">Cape Hatteras Lighthouse</a> in 1870, which at 198.48 feet (60.50\xc2\xa0m) from the ground to the tip of its lightning rod is the tallest lighthouse in the United States and one of the tallest brick lighthouses in the world. In 1999, as the receding shoreline had come dangerously close to Cape Hatteras Lighthouse, the 4830-ton lighthouse was lifted and moved inland a distance of 2,900 feet (880\xc2\xa0m). Its distance from the seashore is now 1,500 feet (460\xc2\xa0m), about the same as when it was originally built.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/SS_E.M._Clark" title="SS E.M. Clark">E.M. Clark</a> (shipwreck and remains), <a href="/wiki/List_of_Empire_ships_(G)#Empire_Gem" title="List of Empire ships (G)">Empire Gem (shipwreck and remains)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/USS_Monitor" title="USS Monitor">USS Monitor</a> are listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-nris-15">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nps_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-nps-16">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span
