class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> is an English singer-songwriter and record producer, who has written songs for <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richard" title="Cliff Richard">Cliff Richard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olivia_Newton-John" title="Olivia Newton-John">Olivia Newton-John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Status_Quo_(band)" title="Status Quo (band)">Status Quo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> amongst many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-amg-3">[3]</a></sup> Britten (along with co-writer <a href="/wiki/Graham_Lyle" title="Graham Lyle">Graham Lyle</a>) won the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Song_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Song of the Year">Grammy Award for Song of the Year</a> in 1985 for "<a href="/wiki/What%27s_Love_Got_to_Do_with_It_(song)" title="What\'s Love Got to Do with It (song)">What\'s Love Got to Do with It</a>".</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terry_Britten&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Britten is a native of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, but began writing for the <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>, Australia band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Twilights_(band)" title="The Twilights (band)">The Twilights</a>, a popular 1960s band for which he played lead guitar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> At times he co-wrote with <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Shorrock" title="Glenn Shorrock">Glenn Shorrock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Brideoake" title="Peter Brideoake">Peter Brideoake</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atkin_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Atkin-5">[5]</a></sup> He also recorded a single under his own name, "2000 Weeks" / "Bargain Day" (1969).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Britten was a band member of Quartet with <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Peek" title="Kevin Peek">Kevin Peek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Tarney" title="Alan Tarney">Alan Tarney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Spencer" title="Trevor Spencer">Trevor Spencer</a> who recorded one album with <a href="/wiki/Decca_Records" title="Decca Records">Decca Records</a> in the UK, which remains unreleased.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> One single was issued in 1969 on <a href="/wiki/Decca_Records" title="Decca Records">Decca</a> in the UK and Australia and <a href="/wiki/London_Records" title="London Records">London</a> in the US: "Now" / "Will My Lady Come" (Decca UK-F12974,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Aust Y-8977, US London LON 1031) and a second single in the UK only in 1970 "Joseph" / "Mama Where Did You Fail" (Decca F13072).</p>\n<p>After the Twilights broke up, he returned to England and moved to London, where he did session work. Britten\'s multi layered guitars featured on <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Stardust" title="Alvin Stardust">Alvin Stardust</a>\'s 1973 hit "<a href="/wiki/My_Coo_Ca_Choo" title="My Coo Ca Choo">My Coo Ca Choo</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1973 he was part of <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richard" title="Cliff Richard">Cliff Richard</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_1973" title="Eurovision Song Contest 1973">Eurovision Song Contest 1973</a> entry and, along with <a href="/wiki/John_Farrar" title="John Farrar">John Farrar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Tarney" title="Alan Tarney">Alan Tarney</a> and Trevor Spencer, submitted six songs; of which "<a href="/wiki/Power_to_All_Our_Friends" title="Power to All Our Friends">Power to All Our Friends</a>" was chosen and came third. After a lean charting period for Cliff Richard, Britten gave him "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devil_Woman_(Cliff_Richard_song)" title="Devil Woman (Cliff Richard song)">Devil Woman</a>" and, in 1976, it became Richard\'s first top 10 in the UK for three years (and his first top 10 hit in the US).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_2-10">[10]</a></sup> He was a guitarist in Richard\'s band for many years and was the co-producer and main songwriter for Richard\'s 1979 album <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_%27n%27_Roll_Juvenile" title="Rock \'n\' Roll Juvenile">Rock \'n\' Roll Juvenile</a></i>, which reached No. 3 in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Album_Chart" title="UK Album Chart">UK Album Chart</a>. He wrote and co-wrote with <a href="/wiki/B._A._Robertson" title="B. A. Robertson">B. A. Robertson</a> 10 of the 12 songs, of which "Carrie" reached No. 4 in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the early 1980s, Britten\'s <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a> song, "9.50", originally a hit for The Twilights, was revived by Australia\'s <a href="/wiki/Divinyls" title="Divinyls">Divinyls</a> as a <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">b-side</a> to their 1984 single, "Good Die Young".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>With <a href="/wiki/Graham_Lyle" title="Graham Lyle">Graham Lyle</a>, Britten also wrote "<a href="/wiki/What%27s_Love_Got_to_Do_with_It_(song)" title="What\'s Love Got to Do with It (song)">What\'s Love Got to Do with It</a>" and this became Tina Turner\'s million selling hit. "What\'s Love Got to Do with It" (1984), reached No. 3 in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums-13">[13]</a></sup> and No. 1 in the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>, and won Britten and Lyle the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammy_Awards_of_1985" title="Grammy Awards of 1985">Grammy Award</a> for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Song_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Song of the Year">Song of the Year</a> in 1985. It also won the <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Record_of_the_Year" title="Grammy Award for Record of the Year">Grammy Award for Record of the Year</a> which went to <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Later that year, they co-wrote "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/We_Don%27t_Need_Another_Hero" title="We Don\'t Need Another Hero">We Don\'t Need Another Hero</a>" for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_Max_Beyond_Thunderdome" title="Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome">Mad Max Beyond Thunderdome</a></i>. Also sung by Tina Turner, the song reached No. 2 in the US and No. 3 in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-British_Hit_Singles_.26_Albums-13">[13]</a></sup> Britten and Lyle received a <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award" title="Golden Globe Award">Golden Globe Award</a> nomination for <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globe_Award_for_Best_Original_Song" title="Golden Globe Award for Best Original Song">Best Original Song</a> in 1986. It also earned Turner a 1986 Grammy nomination for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Female_Pop_Vocal_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Female Pop Vocal Performance">best female pop vocal performance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> He also acted as a record producer for Turner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Britten co-wrote "<a href="/wiki/Just_Good_Friends_(song)" title="Just Good Friends (song)">Just Good Friends</a>" for <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_(album)" title="Bad (album)">Bad</a></i> album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Britten has also penned songs for Olivia Newton-John, including "Love Make Me Strong" (1981)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and "Toughen Up" with Graham Lyle (1985).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> He has also written for <a href="/wiki/Meat_Loaf" title="Meat Loaf">Meat Loaf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melissa_Manchester" title="Melissa Manchester">Melissa Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Raitt" title="Bonnie Raitt">Bonnie Raitt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hank_Marvin" title="Hank Marvin">Hank Marvin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Britten continues to compose from his home base in rural England, but has returned to Australia on occasion, including the Twilights\' reunion for the <i><a href="/wiki/Long_Way_to_the_Top#Concert" title="Long Way to the Top">Long Way to the Top</a></i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concert_tour" title="Concert tour">concert tour</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Atkin_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Atkin-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In 2002, the song "Rain, Tax (It\'s Inevitable)", co-written by Britten and <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Dore" title="Charlie Dore">Charlie Dore</a>, appeared on <a href="/wiki/Celine_Dion" title="Celine Dion">Celine Dion</a>\'s album <i><a href="/wiki/A_New_Day_Has_Come" title="A New Day Has Come">A New Day Has Come</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Britten presently has a home in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_London" title="Richmond, London">Richmond, London</a>, and a home recording studio called "State of the Ark".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Songwriting_credits">Songwriting credits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
