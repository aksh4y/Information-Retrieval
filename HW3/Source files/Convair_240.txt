href="#cite_note-Wegg-1">[1]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The <b>Convair CV-240</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Airliner" title="Airliner">airliner</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/Convair" title="Convair">Convair</a> from 1947 to 1954, initially as a possible replacement of the ubiquitous <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3</a>. Featuring a more modern design with <a href="/wiki/Cabin_pressurization" title="Cabin pressurization">cabin pressurization</a>, the 240 series was able to make some inroads as a commercial airliner and also had a long development cycle which resulted in various civil and military variants. Although reduced in numbers through attrition, the "Convairliners" in various forms continue to fly into the 21st century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convair_CV-240_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The design began its life in a requirement by <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">American Airlines</a> for an airliner to replace its <a href="/wiki/Douglas_DC-3" title="Douglas DC-3">Douglas DC-3s</a>. Convair\'s original design, the unpressurised Model 110, was a twin-engine, low-wing <a href="/wiki/Monoplane" title="Monoplane">monoplane</a> of all-metal construction, with 30 seats. It was powered by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_R-2800" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800">Pratt &amp; Whitney R-2800 Double Wasp</a> <a href="/wiki/Radial_engine" title="Radial engine">radial engines</a> and had a <a href="/wiki/Tricycle_landing_gear" title="Tricycle landing gear">tricycle landing gear</a> and a ventral <a href="/wiki/Airstair" title="Airstair">airstair</a> for passenger boarding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_p183_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg_p183-2">[2]</a></sup> The prototype Model 110, <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_registration" title="Aircraft registration">registration</a> NX90653, first flew on July 8, 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_p183_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg_p183-2">[2]</a></sup> By this time, American had changed its requirements to require <a href="/wiki/Cabin_pressurization" title="Cabin pressurization">pressurization</a> and deemed the design too small. The first prototype was used by Convair for development work for the 240 series before being broken up in 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gradidge_p10_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gradidge_p10-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Convair_240_HB-IRV_Swiss_Air_Lines_Ringway_25.03.50.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1056" data-file-width="1776" height="131" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Convair_240_HB-IRV_Swiss_Air_Lines_Ringway_25.03.50.jpg/220px-Convair_240_HB-IRV_Swiss_Air_Lines_Ringway_25.03.50.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Convair_240_HB-IRV_Swiss_Air_Lines_Ringway_25.03.50.jpg/330px-Convair_240_HB-IRV_Swiss_Air_Lines_Ringway_25.03.50.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Convair_240_HB-IRV_Swiss_Air_Lines_Ringway_25.03.50.jpg/440px-Convair_240_HB-IRV_Swiss_Air_Lines_Ringway_25.03.50.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Convair_240_HB-IRV_Swiss_Air_Lines_Ringway_25.03.50.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA 1949-built Convair 240 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swiss_Air_Lines" title="Swiss Air Lines">Swiss Air Lines</a> at <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, England, in March 1950</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>To meet the requirements of airlines for a pressurized airliner, Convair produced a revised design\xe2\x80\x94the Model 240. This had a longer but thinner fuselage than the Model 110, accommodating 40 passengers in the first pressurized, twin-engined airliner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_p187-8_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg_p187-8-4">[4]</a></sup> The 240 first flew on March 16, 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_p188_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg_p188-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Model 240 was followed by the Model 340 that had a longer fuselage, longer-span wings and more powerful engines. The 340 first flew on October 5, 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_p188-9_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg_p188-9-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1954, in an attempt to compete with <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprop</a>-powered airliners like the <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Viscount" title="Vickers Viscount">Vickers Viscount</a>, Convair produced the Model 440 Metropolitan, with more streamlined cowlings, new engine exhausts, and better soundproofing for the cabin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_p192_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg_p192-7">[7]</a></sup> As the "Super 240" evolved into the CV-340 and CV-440, the limit of piston-engine performance was reached, and future development centered on conversion to turboprop power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convair_CV-240_family&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first delivery of a production Convairliner was to American on February 29, 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_p188_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg_p188-5">[5]</a></sup> A total of 75 were delivered to American, with another 50 going to <a href="/wiki/Western_Airlines" title="Western Airlines">Western Airlines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Continental_Airlines" title="Continental Airlines">Continental Airlines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_American_Airways" title="Pan American Airways">Pan American Airways</a>, <a href="/wiki/KLM" title="KLM">KLM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swissair" title="Swissair">Swissair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sabena" title="Sabena">Sabena</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Trans_Australia_Airlines" title="Trans Australia Airlines">Trans Australia Airlines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gradidge_p10-1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gradidge_p10-1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Convair_580_N5818_Aspen_Aws_DEN_Stapleton_20.07.86_edited-3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="673" data-file-width="1024" height="145" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Convair_580_N5818_Aspen_Aws_DEN_Stapleton_20.07.86_edited-3.jpg" In 1960, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> used a CV-240 named <i>Caroline</i> (after his daughter) during his campaign. This aircraft is now preserved in the <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a>.</p>\n<p>After aborted negotiations with TWA and Eastern for "Super 240" orders, the production of the 240 series was temporarily halted. In response to a United inquiry, Convair redesigned the Super 240, calling it the CV-340. United ordered 55, and more US orders came from Braniff, Continental, Delta, Northeast and National. Other orders came from abroad, and the CV-340 proved popular in South America. The CV-340 earned an enviable reputation for reliability and profitability, and was developed into the CV-440 Metropolitan, the final piston-engined variant of the "Convairliners."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wegg_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Wegg-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelowna_Flightcraft_Air_Charter" title="Kelowna Flightcraft Air Charter">Kelowna Flightcraft Air Charter</a>, the major remaining operator of this model, currently holds the <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">type certificate</a> for this aircraft.</p>\n<p>Used price for a Convair 240 in 1960 was around \xc2\xa340,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flightglobal1960_9-0"><a
