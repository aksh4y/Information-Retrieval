tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GID_Landspout.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="308" data-file-width="272" height="249" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/GID_Landspout.jpg/220px-GID_Landspout.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/GID_Landspout.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GID_Landspout.jpg" title="\xe6\x94\xbe\xe5\xa4\xa7"></a></div>\nA landspout near <a href="/wiki/North_Platte,_Nebraska" title="North Platte, Nebraska">North Platte, Nebraska</a> on May 22, 2004. Note the characteristic smooth, tubular shape, similar to that of a <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspout</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>landspout</b> is a term coined by meteorologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Howard_B._Bluestein" title="Howard B. Bluestein">Howard B. Bluestein</a> in 1985 for a kind of <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> not associated with a <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landspout_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-landspout-1">[1]</a></sup> The <i>Glossary of Meteorology</i> defines a landspout as</p>\n<dl>\n<dd>"Colloquial expression describing tornadoes occurring with a parent <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> in its growth stage and with its vorticity originating in the boundary layer.</dd>\n<dd>The parent cloud does not contain a preexisting mid-level mesocyclone. The landspout was so named because it looks like "a weak <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a> <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspout</a> over land."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glossary_of_Meteorology_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glossary_of_Meteorology-2">[2]</a></sup></dd>\n</dl>\n<p>Landspouts form during the growth stage of a <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus_cloud" title="Cumulus congestus cloud">cumulus congestus cloud</a> by stretching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_boundary_layer" title="Atmospheric boundary layer">boundary layer</a> <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> upward and into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus" title="Cumulus congestus">cumulus congestus</a>\'s updraft. They generally are smaller and weaker than <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a> tornadoes and do not form from a <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a> or pre-existing rotation in the cloud. Because of this, landspouts are rarely detected by <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Landspouts share a strong resemblance and development process to that of <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspouts</a>, usually taking the form of a translucent and highly <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar</a> helical tube. Landspouts are considered tornadoes since a rotating column of air is in contact with both the surface and a cumuliform cloud. Not all landspouts are visible, and many are first sighted as debris swirling at the surface before eventually filling in with condensation and dust.</p>\n<p>A few landspouts can persist in excess of 15 minutes and have produced <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">F3</a> damage;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> however, they rarely produce damage greater than EF-2.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Landspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Dust_devil" title="Dust devil">Dust devil</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">Waterspout</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Vortex_engine" title="Vortex engine">Vortex engine</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Landspout&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-landspout-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-landspout_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web">Bluestein, Howard B.
