Precipitation Measurement</b> (<b>GPM</b>) is a joint mission between <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">JAXA</a> and <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> as well as other international space agencies to make frequent (every 2\xe2\x80\x933 hours) observations of Earth\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a>. It is part of NASA\'s Earth Systematic Missions program and works with a <a href="/wiki/Satellite_constellation" title="Satellite constellation">satellite constellation</a> to provide full global coverage. The project provides global precipitation maps to assist researchers in improving the forecasting of extreme events, studying global climate, and adding to current capabilities for using such satellite data to benefit society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> GPM builds on the notable successes of the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Rainfall_Measuring_Mission" title="Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission">Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission</a> (TRMM), which was also a joint NASA-JAXA activity.</p>\n<p>The project is managed by NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a>, and consists of a GPM Core Observatory satellite assisted by a constellation of spacecraft from other agencies and missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constellation_Partners_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Constellation_Partners-2">[2]</a></sup> The Core Observatory satellite measures the two and three dimensional structure of Earth\xe2\x80\x99s precipitation patterns and provides a new calibration standard for the rest of the satellite constellation. The GPM Core Observatory was assembled and tested at Goddard Space Flight Center, and launched from <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima_Space_Center" title="Tanegashima Space Center">Tanegashima Space Center</a>, Japan, on a <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Heavy_Industries" title="Mitsubishi Heavy Industries">Mitsubishi Heavy Industries</a> <a href="/wiki/H-IIA" title="H-IIA">H-IIA</a> rocket. The launch occurred on February 28, 2014 at 3:37am JST on the first attempt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Agencies in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (together with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eumetsat" title="Eumetsat">Eumetsat</a>) operate the remaining satellites in the constellation for agency-specific goals, but also cooperatively provide data for GPM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constellation_Partners_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Constellation_Partners-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div The silver disc and drum (center) is the GPM Microwave Imager, and the large block on the base is the Dual-frequency Precipitation Radar.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h3><span id="Dual-Frequency_Precipitation_Radar_(DPR)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dual-Frequency_Precipitation_Radar_.28DPR.29">Dual-Frequency Precipitation Radar (DPR)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_Precipitation_Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dual-Frequency Precipitation Radar (DPR)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The DPR is a spaceborne radar, providing three-dimensional maps of storm structure across its swath, including the intensity of rainfall and snowfall at the surface. The DPR has two frequencies, allowing researchers to estimate the sizes of precipitation particles and detect a wider range of precipitation rates. The Ku-band radar, similar to the PR on TRMM, covers a 245\xc2\xa0km (152 mile) swath. Nested inside that, the Ka-band radar covers a 120\xc2\xa0km (74.5 mile) swath.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="GPM_Microwave_Imager_(GMI)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="GPM_Microwave_Imager_.28GMI.29">GPM Microwave Imager (GMI)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_Precipitation_Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: GPM Microwave Imager (GMI)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The GMI is a passive sensor that observes the microwave energy emitted by the Earth and atmosphere at 13 different frequency/polarization channels. These data allow quantitative maps of precipitation across a swath that is 885\xc2\xa0km (550 miles) wide. This instrument continues the legacy of TRMM microwave observations, while adding four additional channels, better resolution, and more reliable calibration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation_data_sets">Precipitation data sets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_Precipitation_Measurement&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Precipitation data sets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>GPM produces and distributes a wide variety of precipitation data products. Processing takes place at the Precipitation Processing System (PPS) at NASA <a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a>, as well as at the JAXA facility in Japan. Data is provided at multiple "levels" of processing, from raw satellite measurements to best-estimate global precipitation maps using combinations of all the constellation observations and other meteorological data. All data from the mission is made freely available to the public on NASA websites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Precipitation data is made available in a variety of formats, spatial and temporal resolutions, and processing levels which are accessible on the Precipitation Measurement Missions "Data Access" webpage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Several data visualization and analysis tools have been made available to provide easy access for the science and applications communities, which include the in-browser Earth science data analysis tool Giovanni,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> a web API,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and a 3D near-realtime global precipitation viewer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div
