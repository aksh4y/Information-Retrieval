border-top:#aaa 1px solid;">Part of the <b><a href="/wiki/1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1954 Atlantic hurricane season">1954 Atlantic hurricane season</a></b></td>\n</tr>\n<tr style="display:none">\n<td colspan="2"></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Hurricane Hazel</b> was the deadliest and costliest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a> of the <a href="/wiki/1954_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1954 Atlantic hurricane season">1954 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. The storm killed at least 400\xc2\xa0people in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> before striking the United States near the border between <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale#Category_4" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4 hurricane</a>. After causing 95\xc2\xa0fatalities in the US, Hazel struck Canada as an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> storm, raising the death toll by 81\xc2\xa0people, mostly in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>. As a result of the high death toll and the damage caused by Hazel, its name was <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming#Retirement" title="Tropical cyclone naming">retired</a> from use for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">North Atlantic hurricanes</a>.</p>\n<p>In Haiti, Hazel destroyed 40%\xc2\xa0of the coffee trees and 50%\xc2\xa0of the cacao crop, affecting the economy for several years to come. The hurricane made landfall near Calabash, North Carolina, and destroyed most waterfront dwellings near its point of impact. From North Carolina, it traveled north along the Atlantic coast. Hazel affected <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, Washington, D.C., <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>; it brought gusts near 160\xc2\xa0km/h (100\xc2\xa0mph) and caused $281\xc2\xa0million (1954 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>) in damage. When it was over Pennsylvania, Hazel consolidated with a cold front, and turned northwest towards Canada. When it hit Ontario as an extratropical storm, rivers and streams in and around Toronto overflowed their banks, which caused severe flooding. As a result, many residential areas located in the local floodplains, such as the <a href="/wiki/Raymore_Drive" title="Raymore Drive">Raymore Drive</a> area, were subsequently converted to parkland. In Canada alone, over <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">C$</a>135\xc2\xa0million (2016: $1.2\xc2\xa0billion) of damage was incurred.</p>\n<p>The effects of Hazel were particularly unprecedented in Toronto, due to a combination of heavy rainfall during the preceding weeks, a lack of experience in dealing with tropical storms and the storm\'s unexpected retention of power. Hazel had traveled 1,100\xc2\xa0km (680\xc2\xa0mi) over land, but while approaching Canada, it had merged with an existing powerful cold front. The storm stalled over the Greater Toronto Area, and although it was now extratropical, it remained as powerful as a category\xc2\xa01 hurricane. To help with the cleanup, 800\xc2\xa0members of the military were summoned, and a Hurricane Relief Fund was established that distributed $5.1\xc2\xa0million (2009: $41.7\xc2\xa0million) in aid.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Meteorological_history"><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hazel_1954_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2700" data-file-width="2700" height="220" href="/wiki/File:Hazel_1954_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMap plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>On October\xc2\xa05, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> with tropical-storm force winds was approaching the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>. Due to the potential for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical storm formation</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> plane flew from <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> to investigate the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> When the plane reached the system, they observed a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> about 30\xc2\xa0mi (50\xc2\xa0km) east of the island of <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> with winds estimated at 100\xc2\xa0mph (160\xc2\xa0km/h). The <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">United States Weather Bureau</a> promptly classified the system as Hurricane Hazel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis">Atlantic hurricane reanalysis</a> later assessed that Hazel developed at 06:00\xc2\xa0UTC on October\xc2\xa05 about 220\xc2\xa0mi (350\xc2\xa0km) east of Grenada. Although the Hurricane Hunters observed hurricane-force winds, the storm had a small <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> 5\xc2\xa0mi (8\xc2\xa0km) in diameter and a central <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 1,002\xc2\xa0mbar (29.6\xc2\xa0inHg). The 100\xc2\xa0mph (160\xc2\xa0km/h) winds were therefore revised downward to 65\xc2\xa0mph (100\xc2\xa0km/h) late on October\xc2\xa05, though it was estimated that the storm attained hurricane status at 00:00\xc2\xa0UTC on October\xc2\xa06. At the same time, Hazel made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on Grenada with winds of 75\xc2\xa0mph (120\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After entering the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, Hazel continued to present a small eye and wind diameter. The winds gradually increased as the storm moved westward, parallel to the northern Venezuela coast. On October\xc2\xa08, the tiny eye increased to a diameter of 29\xc2\xa0mi (46\xc2\xa0km), while the winds reached 85\xc2\xa0mph (135\xc2\xa0km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup> although winds were estimated as high as 125\xc2\xa0mph (201\xc2\xa0km/h). That day, the Hurricane Hunters encountered severe turbulence, which hospitalized one crew member and injured another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup> For the next five days, there were no further flights into the core of the hurricane, although the planes continued to monitor the storm, with radar imagery indicating that the eye became open and ragged. Late on October\xc2\xa09, it is estimated Hazel intensified into a major hurricane with winds of 115\xc2\xa0mph (185\xc2\xa0km/h). Originally, it was estimated that Hazel attained these winds the day prior. The intensification was confirmed on October\xc2\xa010, when aircraft noted a well-defined eye on radar imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On October\xc2\xa010, Hazel slowed in the central Caribbean and turned sharply to the north-northeast toward <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup> steered by an <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">upper-level low</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncnws_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncnws-5">[5]</a></sup> At 09:00\xc2\xa0UTC on October\xc2\xa012, the hurricane made landfall near <a href="/wiki/Chardonni%C3%A8res" title="Chardonni\xc3\xa8res">Chardonni\xc3\xa8res</a> along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tiburon_Peninsula_(Haiti)" title="Tiburon Peninsula (Haiti)">Tiburon Peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> as a Category 3 hurricane on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>. There were no strong wind readings at landfall, but based on previous radar and aircraft observations the sustained winds were officially estimated at 120\xc2\xa0mph (195\xc2\xa0km/h). After moving through the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Gon%C3%A2ve" title="Gulf of Gon\xc3\xa2ve">Gulf of Gon\xc3\xa2ve</a>, Hazel struck northwestern Haiti near <a href="/wiki/Baie-de-Henne" title="Baie-de-Henne">Baie-de-Henne</a> at 00:00\xc2\xa0UTC on October\xc2\xa013, as a Category 2 hurricane with winds estimated at 100\xc2\xa0mph (160\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup> Around that time, the hurricane curved more to the north and northwest due to an approaching <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siec_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-siec-6">[6]</a></sup> and the Hurricane Hunters resumed entering the center of Hazel, reporting an eye and hurricane force winds. At 14:00\xc2\xa0UTC on October\xc2\xa013, Hazel struck <a href="/wiki/Inagua" title="Inagua">Inagua</a> in the Bahamas with winds of 100\xc2\xa0mph (160\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Hazel_--_Weston_Golf_Club.jpg"><img alt="A generally flat area is completely submerged by water; trees are scattered throughout." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="898" data-file-width="930" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Hurricane_Hazel_--_Weston_Golf_Club.jpg/250px-Hurricane_Hazel_--_Weston_Golf_Club.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Hurricane_Hazel_--_Weston_Golf_Club.jpg/375px-Hurricane_Hazel_--_Weston_Golf_Club.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Hurricane_Hazel_--_Weston_Golf_Club.jpg/500px-Hurricane_Hazel_--_Weston_Golf_Club.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Hazel_--_Weston_Golf_Club.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe Weston Golf Club was left submerged after the <a href="/wiki/Humber_River_(Ontario)" title="Humber River (Ontario)">Humber River</a> overflowed its banks during Hurricane Hazel in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>After affecting the Bahamas, Hazel accelerated northwest toward the southeastern United States,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup> steered between a trough and a ridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siec_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-siec-6">[6]</a></sup> Hurricanes are generally expected to lose power after going north of Florida due to lower water temperatures, however, Hazel restrengthened as it tracked northward over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natgeo2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo2-7">[7]</a></sup> The Hurricane Hunters were unable to observe the core of the storm until it neared land, reporting an eye 18\xc2\xa0mi (33\xc2\xa0km) in diameter on October\xc2\xa015. Later that day, a ship just offshore South Carolina reported a central pressure of 938\xc2\xa0mbar (27.7\xc2\xa0inHg), which was the lowest in association with the hurricane. At 15:30\xc2\xa0UTC on October\xc2\xa015, Hazel made landfall just west of the North Carolina/South Carolina border with a 40\xc2\xa0mi (64\xc2\xa0km) eye, slightly northeast of <a href="/wiki/Myrtle_Beach,_South_Carolina" title="Myrtle Beach, South Carolina">Myrtle Beach</a> in the latter state. Based on the pressure, a larger than average size, and the fast forward movement, the landfall intensity was estimated at 130\xc2\xa0mph (215\xc2\xa0km/h), or a Category\xc2\xa04 on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir-Simpson scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The eye of Hazel was tracked across several towns in North Carolina as the system continued northward. At 18:00\xc2\xa0UTC on October\xc2\xa015, the hurricane became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> near <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh</a>, and within six hours had passed near <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> The extratropical remnants of Hazel accelerated to the north through Pennsylvania and New York along an extended <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>. Despite widespread observations across the eastern United States, there was uncertainty in the track of Hazel as an extratropical storm. Although it was possible the original circulation dissipated over western Pennsylvania and a new one formed over western New York, the Atlantic hurricane reanalysis assessed the circulation as becoming elongated but continuous. On October\xc2\xa016, the storm crossed into <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and over the <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, the former Hurricane Hazel dropped to gale-force winds after moving almost 1,000\xc2\xa0km (620\xc2\xa0mi) over land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siec_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-siec-6">[6]</a></sup> The storm slowed and turned northward, passing over <a href="/wiki/James_Bay" title="James Bay">James Bay</a> early on October\xc2\xa017. After turning to the northeast, the remnants of Hazel were absorbed by a larger extratropical storm over Canada on October\xc2\xa018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siec_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-siec-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On October 6, small craft warnings were issued for the Dutch islands of <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Cura\xc3\xa7ao">Cura\xc3\xa7ao</a>, despite predictions that Hazel would pass to the north;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami6_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami6-8">[8]</a></sup> these warnings were canceled a day later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami7_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami7-9">[9]</a></sup> After the storm had turned northward, tropical cyclone warnings were posted for Haiti, the Dominican Republic, and the waters of the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Passage" title="Mona Passage">Mona Passage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Small craft near Jamaica were advised to head for port ahead of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The U.S. Navy completed extensive preparations at its <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">Guantanamo Bay base</a>, ordering about 1,000 civilians into hardened, fortified <a href="/wiki/Quonset_hut" title="Quonset hut">Quonset huts</a>, flying aircraft out of the storm\'s path, and positioning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_ship" title="Surface ship">surface ships</a> out at sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cuba_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cuba-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Then, on October 14, the National Weather Bureau issued a warning for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carolinas" title="Carolinas">Carolinas</a>, with the caveat that the worst of the hurricane was expected to stay offshore. Instead, the storm took a sharp northwest turn and headed toward land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-st169_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-st169-13">[13]</a></sup> By evening of the same day, the storm was forecast to make landfall near the Carolinas border.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-st169_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-st169-13">[13]</a></sup> Further forecasts expected Hazel to lose its power and dissipate over the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Mountains" title="Allegheny Mountains">Allegheny Mountains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siec_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-siec-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In her book <i>Hurricane Hazel</i>, Canadian journalist <a href="/wiki/Betty_Kennedy" title="Betty Kennedy">Betty Kennedy</a> argued that in Canada, the impressions that Hazel was "the best-kept secret in town" and that it was a "fully documented meteorological event that should have taken nobody by surprise" both "paradoxically [...] contain a great deal of truth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Meteorologists predicted that if Hazel merged with the cold front, the storm would not lose intensity, but would instead potentially strengthen. Two Special Weather Bulletins were issued by the Dominion Weather Office, but since it was expected that the storm would pass east of <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, few other warnings were given and there were no evacuations, which increased the eventual property damage and loss of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siec_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-siec-6">[6]</a></sup> The forecast called for high winds between 65\xc2\xa0km/h (40\xc2\xa0mph) and 80\xc2\xa0km/h (50\xc2\xa0mph), with only occasional showers. On lakes <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Erie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Ontario</a>, ships received warnings of strong winds, and the predicted wind speeds ranged from 65\xc2\xa0km/h (40\xc2\xa0mph) to 120\xc2\xa0km/h (75\xc2\xa0mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siec_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-siec-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Hydro" title="Toronto Hydro">Toronto Hydro</a> had called in standby crews as heavy winds were forecast, although they were almost sent home at one point due to a lull in the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tlec_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-tlec-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>There had been significant rainfall in the Toronto area in the two weeks prior to Hazel, so the ground was already saturated. Few people in Canada had any experience with hurricanes, since it was unheard of for them to travel as far north and inland as Toronto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fi192_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-fi192-16">[16]</a></sup> Kennedy also notes that if "Toronto had been about to face a blizzard, or was threatened by a 14-inch [36-cm] snowfall, <i>that</i> [sic] would have been something understandable. [...] This was different. This was the unknown, the unfamiliar, the totally unexpected crisis. Hurricanes belonged in the tropics."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="wikitable" style="float:right;margin:0 0 0.5em 1em">\n<caption>Deaths and damage by region</caption>\n<tr>\n<th>Region</th>\n<th>Deaths</th>\n<th>Damage</th>\n<th>Sources</th>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC islands</a></td>\n<td>0</td>\n<td><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005350000000000000\xe2\x99\xa0</span>$350\xc2\xa0thousand</td>\n<td></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Grenada</td>\n<td>0</td>\n<td><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7004800000000000000\xe2\x99\xa0</span>$80\xc2\xa0thousand</td>\n<td><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Haiti</td>\n<td>400\xe2\x80\x931,000</td>\n<td><span class="sortkey" style="display:none">7005500000000000000\xe2\x99\xa0</span>$500\xc2\xa0thousand</td>\n<td><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bob_Burtt_B.1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bob_Burtt_B.1-19">[19]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Puerto Rico</td>\n<td>9</td>\n<td class="table-na" data-sort-value="" style="background: #ececec; one was severely enough injured to require hospitalisation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA2-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">ABC Islands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">Bonaire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Cura\xc3\xa7ao">Cura\xc3\xa7ao</a>, located north of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, received rough winds and rains of up to 9.8 inches (250\xc2\xa0mm) when the intensifying cyclone passed to the north. Flash flooding in Aruba and Cura\xc3\xa7ao destroyed a bridge and several water dams and resulted in losses of $350,000 (1954 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> suffered its worst flooding since 1899 as a result of the hurricane. Due to timely warnings, only nine people were killed: eight by drowning and one by a landslide; however, infrastructure, buildings, and agricultural areas suffered serious damage, and over 11,000 people were evacuated from flooded areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In Haiti, Hazel brought flash floods which destroyed numerous villages, and high winds which caused considerable damage to major cities. The death toll was estimated to be as high as 1,000\xc2\xa0people; most of the casualties drowned when the water flowed in a flood down the mountains, some of which were as high as 2,400\xc2\xa0m (8,000\xc2\xa0ft). The situation was exacerbated by deforestation, which lessened the ability of the soil to hold water. Haiti\'s South Peninsula took the brunt of the storm: the largest town, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aux_Cayes" title="Aux Cayes">Aux Cayes</a>, reported at least 200 casualties, while the second-largest town of <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%A9mie" title="J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xa9mie">J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xa9mie</a> was reported to have been washed in the sea, with at least 200 more casualties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami13_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami13-25">[25]</a></sup> Damage in Aux Cayes was estimated to be $500,000 (1954 USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beaver13_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-beaver13-26">[26]</a></sup> Estimates of people left homeless in the wake of Hazel are as high as 100,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bob_Burtt_B.1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bob_Burtt_B.1-19">[19]</a></sup> Hazel destroyed about 40% of the coffee trees and 50% of the cacao crop, affecting the country\'s economy for several years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Objects from Haiti, such as bowls, were reported to have been transported by the hurricane to the Carolinian coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cent22_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-cent22-29">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<p>West of Haiti, the hurricane brought hurricane-force winds to Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-meta-3">[3]</a></sup> A fisherman on the eastern tip of Cuba was killed by rough seas while trying to secure his boats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cuba_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-cuba-12">[12]</a></sup> Since the hurricane passed largely east of the Bahamas, only minor damage was reported there. Hazel passed directly over <a href="/wiki/Inagua" title="Inagua">Inagua</a>, where it claimed six lives when a sailboat capsized while taking shelter from the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA2_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In South Carolina, the storm produced 10\xe2\x80\x9311\xc2\xa0ft (3.0\xe2\x80\x933.4\xc2\xa0m) tides along the coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwssc_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-nwssc-30">[30]</a></sup> Hundreds of buildings were destroyed, including about 80% of waterfront dwellings in <a href="/wiki/Myrtle_Beach,_South_Carolina" title="Myrtle Beach, South Carolina">Myrtle Beach</a>. As a result of the high storm surge, the low-lying sandy barrier islands were completely flooded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Two piers in Myrtle Beach were damaged. In <a href="/wiki/North_Myrtle_Beach,_South_Carolina" title="North Myrtle Beach, South Carolina">North Myrtle Beach</a>, a 3-story hotel and a 800\xc2\xa0ft (240\xc2\xa0m) pier were washed out to sea. At <a href="/wiki/Cherry_Grove_Beach,_South_Carolina" title="Cherry Grove Beach, South Carolina">Cherry Grove Beach</a>, 75\xc2\xa0residences were demolished, while at <a href="/wiki/Pawleys_Island,_South_Carolina" title="Pawleys Island, South Carolina">Pawleys Island</a>, 40\xc2\xa0homes were destroyed, while numerous others were damaged. One death occurred in South Carolina. Damage in the state totaled about $27\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwssc_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-nwssc-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At landfall, the hurricane brought a <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> of over 5.5\xc2\xa0m (18\xc2\xa0ft) to a large area of coastline, producing severe coastal damage; the damage was greater since the hurricane coincided with the highest lunar tide of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaanc_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-noaanc-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_County,_North_Carolina" title="Brunswick County, North Carolina">Brunswick County, North Carolina</a>, suffered the heaviest damage, where most coastal dwellings were either destroyed or severely damaged. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Long_Beach,_North_Carolina" title="Long Beach, North Carolina">Long Beach, North Carolina</a>, only five of the 357 buildings were left standing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cent22_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-cent22-29">[29]</a></sup> The official report from the Weather Bureau in Raleigh, North Carolina stated that as a result of Hazel, "all traces of civilization on the immediate waterfront between the state line and Cape Fear were practically annihilated."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nc135_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-nc135-33">[33]</a></sup> According to NOAA, "every pier in a distance of 170 miles [270 kilometres] of coastline was demolished".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA2_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA2-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At the Raleigh-Durham Airport in North Carolina, gusts of 90\xc2\xa0mph (140\xc2\xa0km/h) were recorded; in surrounding cities, including Kinston, Goldsboro, and Faison, wind gusts were estimated to have reached 120\xc2\xa0mph (190\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> With such high winds state-wide, heavy damage was caused to forests, and to property as a result of falling trees. However, since the Carolinas, like the rest of the Southeastern United States, were suffering from a severe drought, the heavy rainfall brought by Hazel was welcome. In North Carolina, the most rain was received in the interior of the state: <a href="/wiki/Robbins,_North_Carolina" title="Robbins, North Carolina">Robbins</a> received 286\xc2\xa0mm (11.3\xc2\xa0in) of rain, and <a href="/wiki/Carthage,_North_Carolina" title="Carthage, North Carolina">Carthage</a> received 247\xc2\xa0mm (9.7\xc2\xa0in).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nc135_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-nc135-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Nineteen people were killed in North Carolina, with several hundred more injured; 15,000 homes were destroyed and another 39,000 were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaanc_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-noaanc-32">[32]</a></sup> The number of people left homeless by the storm was "uncounted thousands".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Damages in the Carolinas amounted to $163\xc2\xa0million, with $61\xc2\xa0million incurred by beachfront property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ERHcase_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-ERHcase-36">[36]</a></sup> Total damage in the United States ranged from $281\xc2\xa0million to $308\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1983_Deadly_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-1983_Deadly-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ERHcase_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-ERHcase-36">[36]</a></sup></p>\n<p>While Hazel caused the most damage in the Carolinas, the storm did not lose all of its intensity. Going north, Hazel turned extratropical by midday when it merged with a cold front; however, it retained hurricane-strength winds and it was continuing to drop heavy rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA2_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA2-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami16_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami16-37">[37]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, wind gusts of over 100\xc2\xa0mph (160\xc2\xa0km/h) were observed at the Weather Bureau office in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a>. Roofs were blown off, trees and power lines were toppled, windows were shattered, and awnings were torn. The <a href="/wiki/Naval_Station_Norfolk" title="Naval Station Norfolk">naval station</a> suffered mainly minor damage to its temporary installations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryan_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ryan-38">[38]</a></sup> The tugboat <i>Indian</i>, carrying five barges, sank in the <a href="/wiki/James_River" title="James River">James River</a>; four of the eight crew members drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwsva_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-nwsva-39">[39]</a></sup> One building was demolished and several others were damaged in <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth,_Virginia" title="Portsmouth, Virginia">Portsmouth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryan_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-ryan-38">[38]</a></sup> The ferry <i>Princess Anne</i> was beached into the docks at <a href="/wiki/Kiptopeke,_Virginia" title="Kiptopeke, Virginia">Kiptopeke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwsva_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-nwsva-39">[39]</a></sup> An unfinished vessel at the <a href="/wiki/Newport_News_Shipbuilding" title="Newport News Shipbuilding">Newport News Shipbuilding</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newport_News,_Virginia" title="Newport News, Virginia">Newport News</a> broke from its moorings and was swept into the shore of the James River. The roof of the Custom house was detached and landed on the street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryan_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-ryan-38">[38]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a>, 200\xc2\xa0store fronts were damaged, while part of the steeple at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Methodist_Church_(Richmond,_Virginia)" title="Trinity Methodist Church (Richmond, Virginia)">Trinity Methodist Church</a> was toppled. Additionally, a 150\xc2\xa0ft (46\xc2\xa0m) <a href="/wiki/Microwave_transmission" title="Microwave transmission">microwave</a> tower in <a href="/wiki/Warsaw,_Virginia" title="Warsaw, Virginia">Warsaw</a> was knocked over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwsva_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-nwsva-39">[39]</a></sup> Approximately 50% of residents in Virginia lost telephone and electrical service for six days. The storm damaged approximately 18,000\xc2\xa0homes and businesses. Overall, there were 12\xc2\xa0deaths and about $15\xc2\xa0million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nwsva_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-nwsva-39">[39]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After North Carolina, the storm went through Virginia, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. In general, power was knocked out and trees were downed. Wind gusts throughout the area reached 160\xc2\xa0km/h (99\xc2\xa0mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA2_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA2-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami16_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-miami16-37">[37]</a></sup> Though not near the center, a gust of 182\xc2\xa0km/h (113\xc2\xa0mph) was recorded in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a>, the highest wind speed ever recorded within the municipal boundaries of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</p>\n<p>Rain amounts were heavier on the western side of the storm. In West Virginia, the average amount of rain received was 230\xc2\xa0mm (9\xc2\xa0in) with localized amounts of 300\xc2\xa0mm (12\xc2\xa0in) reported in the Appalachians. To the north in Pittsburgh, only 90\xc2\xa0mm (3.5\xc2\xa0in) of rain was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>\n<p>To the east, the Washington, D.C. area was particularly affected, and considerable flooding was reported in the Virginias and Maryland. Much of the grounds of the Naval Academy in Annapolis, MD were flooded and the Midshipmen were put to work the next day cleaning up debris. New Jersey escaped major flooding as the high tide was low enough, but to the south in <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>, the majority of crab pots were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Hazel lost a considerable amount of moisture when crossing the Allegheny Mountains, which raised rivers and streams in the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> area significantly above the flood mark. In Pennsylvania, the winds were still high enough to unroof several homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami16_37-2"><a href="#cite_note-miami16-37">[37]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In upstate New York, the storm blocked highways and railroads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami16_37-3"><a href="#cite_note-miami16-37">[37]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Hazel_--_Lawrence_Avenue.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Avenue" title="Lawrence Avenue">Lawrence Avenue</a> bridge was washed out by the Humber River; part of it remained attached to the shore, while the rest was swept away by the river.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>After weeks of unusually high rainfall saturated the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Toronto_Area" title="Greater Toronto Area">Greater Toronto Area</a>, Hazel dropped additional rainfall, peaking at 8.4\xc2\xa0in (214\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Snelgrove,_Ontario" title="Snelgrove, Ontario">Snelgrove, Ontario</a>. In three hours, the city received 3.5\xc2\xa0in (90\xc2\xa0mm) of precipitation. Most of these rains ran off into rivers and creeks of Toronto, which raised water levels by as much as 20\xe2\x80\x9326\xc2\xa0ft (6\xe2\x80\x938\xc2\xa0m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siec_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-siec-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fi192_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-fi192-16">[16]</a></sup> Not built to withstand heavy flooding, Toronto\'s infrastructure took a heavy hit: over 50\xc2\xa0bridges, many parts of important highways, as well as numerous roads and railways were destroyed when the high water washed them out or carried debris and smashed them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trans_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-trans-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-impec_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-impec-43">[43]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Floodwaters slowly rose in <a href="/wiki/Holland_Marsh" title="Holland Marsh">Holland Marsh</a> \xe2\x80\x93 a bowl-shaped valley near <a href="/wiki/Bradford,_Ontario" title="Bradford, Ontario">Bradford</a> \xe2\x80\x93 allowing people to escape to the town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Highway_400" title="Ontario Highway 400">Highway 400</a>, which passes through the marsh, was under as much as 10\xc2\xa0ft (3\xc2\xa0m) of water in some places when as much 6\xc2\xa0m (20\xc2\xa0ft) of water backed up. Much of the crops in the area were either swept away or ruined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> After the water pumps failed due to debris, better equipment allowed the Holland Marsh to be drained by November 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Humber_River_(Ontario)" title="Humber River (Ontario)">Humber River</a> in the west end of Toronto caused the most destruction as a result of an intense flash flood, after most minor rivers and creeks drained into it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrec_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-hrec-47">[47]</a></sup> A team of five volunteer firefighters were killed when their fire truck was swept away as they were responding to help a stranded motorist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fi193_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-fi193-48">[48]</a></sup> Communities in the Humber floodplain were devastated. At Woodbridge, the river swelled from its usual width of 20\xc2\xa0m (66\xc2\xa0ft) at its narrowest point to 107\xc2\xa0m (351\xc2\xa0ft), and left hundreds homeless and nine dead. The Humber swept away 366\xc2\xa0m (1,201\xc2\xa0ft) of <a href="/wiki/Raymore_Drive" title="Raymore Drive">Raymore Drive</a> and 14\xc2\xa0nearby homes, killing 35\xc2\xa0people out of the 81\xc2\xa0Canadian fatalities. The rise of the river was unprecedented and the residents did not evacuate, which led to the high death toll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrec_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-hrec-47">[47]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Etobicoke_Creek" title="Etobicoke Creek">Etobicoke Creek</a> also overflowed its banks at the village of Long Branch, killing seven people after many dwellings were swept into Lake Ontario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbec_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-lbec-49">[49]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The death toll of 81 people has not since been equaled by a natural disaster in Canada. In addition to the casualties, over 4,000 families were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gif13_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-gif13-50">[50]</a></sup> The Department of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness Canada estimates the total cost of Hurricane Hazel for Canada, taking into account long-term effects such as economic disruption, the cost of lost property, and recovery costs, to be <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">C$</a>137,552,400.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evec_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-evec-51">[51]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Haiti">Haiti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Haiti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the aftermath of Hazel, a three-day period of national mourning was declared in Haiti for hurricane victims. With existing infrastructure already poor, the recovery was very slow since many of the few existing roads were blocked, and communications equipment was either out, damaged, or destroyed. The Haitian Red Cross appealed for assistance to the International Red Cross, while the American Red Cross made a donation of $25,000 (1954 USD). <a href="/wiki/Pan_American_World_Airways" title="Pan American World Airways">Pan American World Airways</a> offered the use of its planes to assist with the delivery of aid,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami14_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami14-52">[52]</a></sup> and the US aircraft carrier <a href="/wiki/USS_Saipan_(CVL-48)" title="USS Saipan (CVL-48)">USS <i>Saipan</i></a> deployed 18 helicopters to help deliver supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miami15_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-miami15-53">[53]</a></sup> Despite the relief effort, there was an outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Typhoid_fever" title="Typhoid fever">typhoid fever</a> following Hazel due to a lack of clean water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the Carolinas, the National Guard was mobilised by the evening of October 15 to prevent looting along affected areas of the coastline. On October 17, President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> declared a "major disaster" in the Carolinas, and offered "immediate and unlimited federal assistance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-time_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-time-55">[55]</a></sup> Recovery was quick, and by October 24, all but two units were demobilised. Another concern was the rebuilding of the sand dunes along waterfronts. An artificial sand dune barrier, 39\xc2\xa0km (24\xc2\xa0mi) long, was completed by October 30, which in the long run led to a more rapid natural build-up of larger dunes. With Myrtle Beach a popular tourist destination, the Chamber of Commerce began an information campaign to inform the public, which might have erroneously concluded from the massive media coverage that the city had been destroyed, that the city would be ready for the coming summer. The rebuilding after the partial destruction would transform Myrtle Beach from a "quaint summer colony to a high-rise resort city".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-st178-9_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-st178-9-56">[56]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada_2">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Hazel_in_Canada" title="Effects of Hurricane Hazel in Canada">Effects of Hurricane Hazel in Canada</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Humber_Weir.JPG"><img alt="Water flows over a low dam over a river; one tree-lined bank of the river is visible." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/63/Humber_Weir.JPG/220px-Humber_Weir.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/63/Humber_Weir.JPG/330px-Humber_Weir.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/63/Humber_Weir.JPG/440px-Humber_Weir.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Humber_Weir.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weir</a> was built on the Humber River near Raymore Drive to lessen the risk of a similar catastrophic flood.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The army sent about 800\xc2\xa0soldiers to Toronto to assist with the cleanup, providing blankets and mattresses. Toronto residents helped out with the relief effort: the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a> received so many donations of clothes, footwear, blankets, food, and money that its storage facilities were overfilled, forcing it to advise against further donations until they were needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raec_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-raec-57">[57]</a></sup> A Hurricane Relief Fund was established to coordinate financial donations, ultimately raising about $5.3\xc2\xa0million (CAD). The fund received donations from organisations, companies, and individuals including <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company_of_Canada" title="Ford Motor Company of Canada">Ford Motor Company of Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Church_of_Canada" title="United Church of Canada">United Church of Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laura_Secord_Chocolates" title="Laura Secord Chocolates">Laura Secord Candy Shops</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British-American_Oil" title="British-American Oil">British-American Oil Company</a>. A portion of the fund was set aside as a contingency reserve in the event of unresolved claims and also toward administrative expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raec_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-raec-57">[57]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Toronto_and_Region_Conservation_Authority" title="Toronto and Region Conservation Authority">Toronto and Region Conservation Authority</a> was created after the storm to manage the area\'s floodplains and rivers, such as building dams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mitec_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-mitec-58">[58]</a></sup> The heavily flooded areas were expropriated and barred from rebuilding homes, and most of the land was later converted into an extensive park system along Toronto\'s rivers. A footbridge dedicated to the victims crosses the Humber river in Raymore Park, which was the former Raymore Drive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gif99_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-gif99-59">[59]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Hazel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As a result of the catastrophic damage and severe death tolls in the Caribbean, United States and Canada, the name Hazel was retired, and will never again be used for an Atlantic hurricane. However, since it was retired before the inception of naming lists with the modern six-year cycle, it was not directly replaced with any particular name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a
