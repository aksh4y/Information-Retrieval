Atlantic hurricane season</a></b></td>\n</tr>\n<tr style="display:none">\n<td colspan="2"></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The <b>Okeechobee hurricane</b>, also known as the <b>San Felipe Segundo hurricane</b>, was one of the deadliest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricanes" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a> in the recorded history of the North Atlantic basin. The fourth <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>, third hurricane, and only major hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/1928_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1928 Atlantic hurricane season">1928 season</a>, this system developed just offshore the west coast of Africa on September\xc2\xa06. Initially a tropical depression, it strengthened into a tropical storm later that day, shortly before passing south of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde Islands</a>. Further intensification was slow and halted by late on September\xc2\xa07. About 48\xc2\xa0hours later, the storm resumed strengthening and became a Category\xc2\xa01 hurricane on the modern-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>. Still moving westward, the system reached Category\xc2\xa04 intensity before striking <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> on September\xc2\xa012. There, the storm brought "great destruction" and 1,200\xc2\xa0deaths. The islands of <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nevis" title="Nevis">Nevis</a> also reported damage and fatalities, but not nearly as severe as in Guadeloupe.</p>\n<p>Around midday on September\xc2\xa013, the storm strengthened into a Category\xc2\xa05 hurricane and peaked with sustained winds of 160\xc2\xa0mph (260\xc2\xa0km/h). About six hours later, the system made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>; it remains the only recorded Category\xc2\xa05 to strike the island as of 2017. Very strong winds resulted in severe damage in Puerto Rico. Throughout the island, an estimated 24,728\xc2\xa0homes were destroyed and 192,444\xc2\xa0were damaged, leaving over 500,000\xc2\xa0people homeless. Heavy rainfall also led to extreme damage to vegetation and agriculture. On Puerto Rico alone, there were 312\xc2\xa0deaths and about $50 million USD ($697 million today) in damage. While crossing the island and emerging into the Atlantic, the storm weakened slightly, falling to Category\xc2\xa04 intensity. The storm began crossing through the Bahamas on September\xc2\xa016, where it resulted in 18 fatalities.</p>\n<p>Early on September\xc2\xa017, the storm made landfall near <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="West Palm Beach, Florida">West Palm Beach, Florida</a>, with winds of 145\xc2\xa0mph (233\xc2\xa0km/h). In the city, more than 1,711\xc2\xa0homes were destroyed. Elsewhere in the county, impact was most severe around <a href="/wiki/Lake_Okeechobee" title="Lake Okeechobee">Lake Okeechobee</a>. The storm surge caused water to pour out of the southern edge of the lake, flooding hundreds of square miles as high as 20 feet (6.1\xc2\xa0m) above ground. Numerous houses and buildings were swept away in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Belle_Glade,_Florida" title="Belle Glade, Florida">Belle Glade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canal_Point,_Florida" title="Canal Point, Florida">Canal Point</a>, Chosen, <a href="/wiki/Pahokee,_Florida" title="Pahokee, Florida">Pahokee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Bay,_Florida" title="South Bay, Florida">South Bay</a>. At least 2,500\xc2\xa0people drowned, while damage was estimated at $25\xc2\xa0million. While crossing Florida, the system weakened significantly, falling to Category\xc2\xa01 intensity late on September\xc2\xa017. It curved north-northeastward and briefly re-emerged into the Atlantic on September\xc2\xa018, but soon made another landfall near <a href="/wiki/Edisto_Island,_South_Carolina" title="Edisto Island, South Carolina">Edisto Island, South Carolina</a>, with winds of 85\xc2\xa0mph (140\xc2\xa0km/h). Early on the following day, the system weakened to a tropical storm and became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> over <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> hours later. Overall, the system caused $100\xc2\xa0million in damage and at least 4,079\xc2\xa0deaths.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li depression developing just off the west coast of Africa near <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>. The next day, a ship reported winds of 60\xc2\xa0mph (95\xc2\xa0km/h), or tropical storm status; on this basis, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis_project" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis project">Atlantic hurricane reanalysis project</a> estimated that the system attained tropical storm status late on September\xc2\xa06. However, lack of observations for several days prevented the system from being classified in real time as it moved generally westward across the Atlantic Ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> On September\xc2\xa010, the <i>S.S. Commack</i> first observed the storm about 900\xc2\xa0miles (1450\xc2\xa0km) to the east of <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, which at the time was the most easterly report of a tropical cyclone ever received through ship\'s radio. Later that day, two other ships confirmed the intensity of the storm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> and the <a On September\xc2\xa013, the 15\xc2\xa0mi (25\xc2\xa0km) eye crossed Puerto Rico in eight hours from the southeast to the northwest, moving ashore near <a href="/wiki/Guayama,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guayama, Puerto Rico">Guayama</a> and exiting between <a href="/wiki/Aguadilla,_Puerto_Rico" title="Aguadilla, Puerto Rico">Aguadilla</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabela,_Puerto_Rico" title="Isabela, Puerto Rico">Isabela</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aoml_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-aoml-5">[5]</a></sup> A ship near the southern coast reported a pressure of 931\xc2\xa0mbar (27.5\xc2\xa0inHg), and the cup <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a> at <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a> reported sustained winds of 160\xc2\xa0mph (257\xc2\xa0km/h) before failing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> As the wind station was 30\xc2\xa0miles (50\xc2\xa0km) north of the storm\'s center, winds near the landfall point were unofficially estimated as high as 200\xc2\xa0mph (320\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> On this basis, the hurricane is believed to have made landfall in Puerto Rico as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> hurricane on the Saffir-Simpson scale, although there was uncertainty in the peak intensity, due to the large size and slow movement of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After emerging from Puerto Rico, the hurricane had weakened to winds of about 140\xc2\xa0mph (240\xc2\xa0km/h), based on a pressure reading of 941\xc2\xa0mbar (27.8\xc2\xa0inHg) at Isabela. The storm brushed the northern coast of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> while moving west-northwestward, gradually restrengthening. On September\xc2\xa015, it passed within 35\xc2\xa0mi (55\xc2\xa0km) of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Turk_Island" title="Grand Turk Island">Grand Turk</a>, by which time the winds increased to 155\xc2\xa0mph (250\xc2\xa0km/h). The storm continued through the Bahamas as a strong Category 4 hurricane, passing near <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a> at 10:00\xc2\xa0UTC on September\xc2\xa016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> Initially, Richard Gray of the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">U.S. Weather Bureau</a> was optimistic that the storm would spare <a href="/wiki/South_Florida" title="South Florida">South Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> However, at 00:00\xc2\xa0UTC on September\xc2\xa017, the large hurricane made landfall in southeastern Florida near <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="West Palm Beach, Florida">West Palm Beach</a>, with estimated winds of 145\xc2\xa0mph (230\xc2\xa0km/h). This was based on a pressure reading of 929\xc2\xa0mbar (27.4\xc2\xa0inHg) in the city,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> which at the time was the lowest pressure reading in the mainland United States; this broke the previous record of 935\xc2\xa0mbar (27.6\xc2\xa0inHg) set during the <a href="/wiki/1926_Miami_hurricane" title="1926 Miami hurricane">1926 Miami hurricane</a>. Peak gusts were estimated near 160\xc2\xa0mph (260\xc2\xa0km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Canal_Point,_Florida" title="Canal Point, Florida">Canal Point</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The hurricane quickly weakened as it progressed inland and moved over <a href="/wiki/Lake_Okeechobee" title="Lake Okeechobee">Lake Okeechobee</a>, although the large size allowed it to maintain hurricane status for several more days. Late on September\xc2\xa017, the hurricane recurved to the northeast and passed near <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville,_Florida" title="Jacksonville, Florida">Jacksonville</a> early the next day with winds of 75\xc2\xa0mph (120\xc2\xa0km/h). At 08:00\xc2\xa0UTC on September\xc2\xa018, the storm once again reached open waters. Later that day, the hurricane restrengthened slightly over open waters, making a second United States landfall near <a href="/wiki/Edisto_Island,_South_Carolina" title="Edisto Island, South Carolina">Edisto Island, South Carolina</a>, at 19:00\xc2\xa0UTC with winds of 85\xc2\xa0mph (140\xc2\xa0km/h). Accelerating northeastward, the system quickly weakened into a tropical storm over North Carolina. On September\xc2\xa019, the storm evolved into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>, although it restrengthened slightly to hurricane status. The cyclone turned to the north-northwest, moving quickly through the eastern United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> On September\xc2\xa021, the former hurricane dissipated over <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> having merged with another disturbance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" Leeward Islands in the Caribbean Sea, strengthening as it did so. On the island of <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a> winds were clocked at 24\xc2\xa0mph (39\xc2\xa0km/h); there were no reports of damage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> though one fatality occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-7"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, even further south of the storm\'s path, there were three fatalities. Guadeloupe received a direct hit from the storm, apparently with little warning; the death toll there was 1,200,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pastdeadly_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-pastdeadly-10">[10]</a></sup> and damage reports relayed through Paris indicated "great destruction" on the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> About three-fourths of the island\'s residents were left homeless. In the community of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Fran%C3%A7ois,_Guadeloupe" title="Saint-Fran\xc3\xa7ois, Guadeloupe">Saint-Fran\xc3\xa7ois</a>, the only structure to remain standing was the police station, which was built with reinforced concrete. To the east of the town, the merchant ship <i>Albatros</i>, carrying 80\xc2\xa0casks of rum, sank. The crew and the five men attempting to save the ship drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-8"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup> Approximately 85%\xe2\x80\x9395% of banana crops were destroyed, 70%\xe2\x80\x9380% of tree crops suffered severe damage, and 40% of the sugar cane crop was ruined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a>, just north of the storm\'s center, was warned in advance of the storm but still suffered \xc2\xa3150,000 (1928 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GBP" title="GBP">UKP</a>) in damages and 42 deaths; <a href="/wiki/Plymouth,_Montserrat" title="Plymouth, Montserrat">Plymouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salem,_Montserrat" title="Salem, Montserrat">Salem</a> were devastated and crop losses caused near-starvation conditions before relief could arrive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montserrat_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Montserrat-8">[8]</a></sup> All commercial and government buildings on the island were destroyed, as were more than 600\xc2\xa0homes. <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis" title="Saint Kitts and Nevis">Saint Kitts and Nevis</a> also suffered heavily. On the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Kitts" title="St. Kitts">St. Kitts</a>, a number of homes built on wooden foundations were demolished. Nine deaths were reported, six of which occurred as a result of a schoolhouse collapse. Thirteen people were killed on the island of <a href="/wiki/Nevis" title="Nevis">Nevis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-9"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The storm destroyed hundreds of home on <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>, including a doctor\'s home and a "poor house". Government offices, hospitals, and school were also damaged. On <a href="/wiki/Saint_Croix,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Croix, U.S. Virgin Islands">Saint Croix</a>, nearly all of the island\'s 11,000\xc2\xa0residents suffered some degree of loss. A total of 143\xc2\xa0buildings were destroyed, including a sugar mill. The storm left nine deaths on the island. Throughout the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a>, as many as 700,000\xc2\xa0people were rendered homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-10"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico">Puerto Rico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Puerto Rico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_winds_drive_a_10-foot_2X4_through_a_palm_tree.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1804" data-file-width="1208" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Hurricane_winds_drive_a_10-foot_2X4_through_a_palm_tree.jpg/170px-Hurricane_winds_drive_a_10-foot_2X4_through_a_palm_tree.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Hurricane_winds_drive_a_10-foot_2X4_through_a_palm_tree.jpg/255px-Hurricane_winds_drive_a_10-foot_2X4_through_a_palm_tree.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Hurricane_winds_drive_a_10-foot_2X4_through_a_palm_tree.jpg/340px-Hurricane_winds_drive_a_10-foot_2X4_through_a_palm_tree.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_winds_drive_a_10-foot_2X4_through_a_palm_tree.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHurricane-force winds drove this 10-foot (3\xc2\xa0m) piece of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dimensional_lumber" title="Dimensional lumber">2x4 lumber</a> through a palm tree in Puerto Rico.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>While the storm was passing near Dominica, the San Juan, Puerto Rico Weather Bureau warned about the threat of the hurricane which would strike the island within a day or two. The advisory was sent via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> to 75\xc2\xa0police districts and was broadcast from the naval radio station every two hours;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-pr_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-pr-12">[12]</a></sup> this was the first hurricane warning broadcast by radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aoml_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-aoml-5">[5]</a></sup> Warnings were also posted for 12\xc2\xa0ports along the southern coast, causing ships to avoid the island or remain at port. Effective preparation is credited for the relatively low death toll of 312, and not a single ship was lost at sea in the vicinity of Puerto Rico. By comparison, the weaker <a href="/wiki/1899_San_Ciriaco_hurricane" title="1899 San Ciriaco hurricane">1899 San Ciriaco hurricane</a> killed approximately 3,000\xc2\xa0people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-pr_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-pr-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>According to the San Juan National Weather Service office, the storm was "up to this time the greatest and more  [<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] intense and destructive hurricane of record in Puerto Rico."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aoml_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-aoml-5">[5]</a></sup> Along the storm path, the eye passed over Guayama, Cayey, and Aibonito, resulting in a period of calm lasting 20\xc2\xa0minutes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-pr_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-pr-12">[12]</a></sup> The island of Puerto Rico received the worst of the storm\'s winds when the hurricane moved directly across the island at Category 5 strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-pr_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-pr-12">[12]</a></sup> The hurricane was extremely large as it crossed Puerto Rico. Hurricane-force winds were measured in <a href="/wiki/Guayama,_Puerto_Rico" title="Guayama, Puerto Rico">Guayama</a> for 18\xc2\xa0hours; since the storm is estimated to have been moving at 13\xc2\xa0mph (21\xc2\xa0km/h), the diameter of the storm\'s hurricane winds was estimated very roughly to be 234\xc2\xa0miles (376\xc2\xa0km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-pr_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-pr-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The storm was named the <i>San Felipe II Hurricane</i> because the eye of the cyclone made landfall on the Christian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feast_day" title="Feast day">feast day</a> of Saint Philip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mujica_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-13">[13]</a></sup> It was named "Segundo", Spanish for "the Second", because of another destructive "<a href="/wiki/1876_San_Felipe_hurricane" title="1876 San Felipe hurricane">San Felipe hurricane</a>" which struck Puerto Rico on that same day in 1876. Since European arrival in the Americas in 1492 all storms and hurricanes were named after the name of the saint of the day the storm hit Puerto Rico. In 1953 the United States started naming hurricanes by female names until 1978 when both gender names began to be used. Yet it was only in 1960 that hurricanes stopped being officially named after saints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mujica_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The rainfall recorded on September 13\xe2\x80\x9314, 1928, remains the record for the maximum rainfall associated with a hurricane in Puerto Rico within a period of forty-eight hours. In those regions where precipitation is more common place, as in Adjuntas in the <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Central_(Puerto_Rico)" title="Cordillera Central (Puerto Rico)">Cordillera Central</a> and in the Sierra de Luquillo, the rain was over 25 inches (640\xc2\xa0mm), with 29.60 inches (752\xc2\xa0mm) recorded in Adjuntas. The <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Puerta_de_Tierra,_Puerto_Rico" title="Puerta de Tierra, Puerto Rico">Puerta de Tierra</a> lost one of its cups at 11:44\xc2\xa0am on September 13, just when it had registered a maximum speed of 150 miles (240\xc2\xa0km) per hour\xe2\x80\x94a speed that was sustained for five consecutive minutes. Previously the same instrument had measured 160 miles (260\xc2\xa0km) per hour for one minute. Because these measurements were taken 30 miles (48\xc2\xa0km) from San Felipe\'s eye, at the time, it seemed possible that some estimates of 200 miles (320\xc2\xa0km) per hour near the center of the storm were not overdrawn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mujica_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>There was general destruction through the island, with the towns where the eye passed being swept away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mujica_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-13">[13]</a></sup> Property damage on the island from winds and rain was catastrophic. The northeast portion of the island received winds in excess of Category\xc2\xa03 strength, with hurricane-force winds lasting as long as 18\xc2\xa0hours. Official reports stated "several hundred thousand" people were left homeless, and property damages were estimated at $50\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-pr_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-pr-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On the island there was no building that was not affected. Some sugar mills ("Centrales") that had cost millions of dollars to build were reduced to rubble. Reports say that 24,728 homes were destroyed and 192,444 were partially destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mujica_13-4"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-13">[13]</a></sup> Most of the <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> fields were flooded, ruining the year\'s crops. Half of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coffee_(plant)" title="Coffee (plant)">coffee plants</a> and half of the <a href="/wiki/Shade_tree" title="Shade tree">shade trees</a> that covered these were destroyed; almost all of the coffee harvest was lost. The coffee industry would take years to recover since coffee needs shade trees to grow. The tobacco farms also had great losses. After this hurricane, Puerto Rico never regained its position as a major coffee exporter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Communications were impacted by fallen trees, landslides, and damaged bridges. Of the school buildings 770\xc2\xa0were destroyed or damaged. According to some estimates of the day, excluding personal losses, the damages reached $85.312\xc2\xa0million and over 500,000\xc2\xa0people were left homeless. San Felipe II is officially classified as Puerto Rico\'s biggest, worst, and most devastating hurricane to ever have impacted the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mujica_13-5"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greater_Antilles_and_Bahamas">Greater Antilles and Bahamas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Rico, the hurricane passed just north of the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, producing very little damage. This was due to the small core and weaker winds to the south of the center. Advance warning reduced the number of ships traversing the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>While the hurricane was passing nearby, Grand Turk reported winds of 120\xc2\xa0mph (193\xc2\xa0km/h). According to a ship report in the region, "The force of the wind\xc2\xa0... could only be judged by the noise made by the storm, which reminded me of the New York subway going full speed passing switches." Winds approached 120\xc2\xa0mph (193\xc2\xa0km/h) at Nassau before the anemometer failed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition to the winds, the storm dropped heavy rainfall in the region, totaling 9\xc2\xa0in (228\xc2\xa0mm) in Nassau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> As in Puerto Rico, authorities in the Bahamas had ample warning of the hurricane\'s approach, and preparations minimized the loss of life in the islands. Two boats were wrecked as they washed ashore in Grand Turk, although the crews were saved. A <a href="/wiki/Sloop" title="Sloop">sloop</a> traversing from <a href="/wiki/Ambergris_Caye" title="Ambergris Caye">Ambergris Caye</a> to Grand Turk was lost, killing all 18\xc2\xa0people on board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> The storm caused heavy damage throughout the Bahamas, mostly to property and crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a>, some buildings which were recently repaired after the <a href="/wiki/1926_Nassau_hurricane" title="1926 Nassau hurricane">1926 Nassau hurricane</a> were destroyed during this storm. A 10-year-old girl drowned after falling into an open trench filled with water. At the Fort Montague Hotel, the windows, doors, and furniture were badly damaged. Similar impact was reported at the Royal Victoria Hotel, while the British Colonial Hotel was largely spared. However, the gardens of the three hotels were "damaged almost beyond recognition".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-11"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On <a href="/wiki/Bimini" title="Bimini">Bimini</a>, sustained winds of 140\xc2\xa0mph (230\xc2\xa0km/h) were observed, causing major damage to buildings. Ninety-five houses and some other buildings, including a few churches and government buildings, were damaged or destroyed on <a href="/wiki/Eleuthera" title="Eleuthera">Eleuthera</a>. Minor damage was reported on <a href="/wiki/Rum_Cay" title="Rum Cay">Rum Cay</a>. Most of the food crops were destroyed. On <a href="/wiki/San_Salvador_Island" title="San Salvador Island">San Salvador Island</a>, four buildings were demolished, including two churches, while several other structures suffered minor damage. Food crops were nearly wiped out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-12"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="576" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath.jpg/220px-1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath.jpg/330px-1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath.jpg/440px-1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nDamage from the hurricane</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_the_1928_Okeechobee_hurricane_in_Florida" title="Effects of the 1928 Okeechobee hurricane in Florida">Effects of the 1928 Okeechobee hurricane in Florida</a></div>\n<p>While the hurricane was moving through the Bahamas, the Weather Bureau issued storm warnings from Miami to <a href="/wiki/Titusville,_Florida" title="Titusville, Florida">Titusville</a>, later upgrading to a hurricane warning from Miami to <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach,_Florida" title="Daytona Beach, Florida">Daytona Beach</a>. The agency advised residents to take precautions for the hurricane, citing the potential for strong winds and waves. Hurricane warnings were also posted for the west coast from <a href="/wiki/Punta_Rassa,_Florida" title="Punta Rassa, Florida">Punta Rassa</a> to <a href="/wiki/Apalachicola,_Florida" title="Apalachicola, Florida">Apalachicola</a>, and after the storm recurved, hurricane warnings were extended along the east coast to Jacksonville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> Because of well-issued hurricane warnings, residents were prepared for the storm, and the number of lives lost in the coastal Palm Beach area was only 26.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Strong winds struck southern Florida as the hurricane moved ashore, with three unofficial reports of 100\xc2\xa0mph (160\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> In Miami to the south of the center, winds reached 78\xc2\xa0mph (126\xc2\xa0km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> and farther south, <a href="/wiki/Key_West,_Florida" title="Key West, Florida">Key West</a> reported winds of only 39\xc2\xa0mph (63\xc2\xa0km/h). The eye at landfall was 25\xc2\xa0mi (40\xc2\xa0km) wide, and after moving inland crossed Lake Okeechobee, where a calm was reported for 30\xc2\xa0minutes. Winds at <a href="/wiki/Canal_Point,_Florida" title="Canal Point, Florida">Canal Point</a>, adjacent to the lake, were estimated as high as 160\xc2\xa0mph (255\xc2\xa0km/h); the anemometer blew away after reporting sustained winds of 75\xc2\xa0mph (120\xc2\xa0km/h). The pressure at Canal Point dropped to 942\xc2\xa0mbar (27.82\xc2\xa0inHg). The lowest pressure north of Lake Okeechobee was 966\xc2\xa0mbar (28.54\xc2\xa0inHg) in <a href="/wiki/Bartow,_Florida" title="Bartow, Florida">Bartow</a>, and along the west coast, winds reached 31\xc2\xa0mph (50\xc2\xa0km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The hurricane left thousands of people homeless in Florida; property damage was estimated at $25\xc2\xa0million ($349\xc2\xa0million). It is estimated if a similar storm were to strike as of the year 2003, it would cause $18.7\xc2\xa0billion in damages. The cyclone remains one of three Atlantic hurricanes to strike the southern mainland of Florida with a central pressure below 940\xc2\xa0mbar (27.76\xc2\xa0inHg), the others being the <a href="/wiki/1926_Miami_hurricane" title="1926 Miami hurricane">1926 Miami hurricane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a> of <a href="/wiki/1992_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1992 Atlantic hurricane season">1992</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USHurricanes_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-USHurricanes-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In addition to the human fatalities, 1,278\xc2\xa0livestock and 47,389 poultry were killed, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1928remember_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-1928remember-17">[17]</a></sup> Agriculture was impacted significantly, with the storm destroying what may have been the largest "citrus crop in the history of the industry". Approximately 6% of oranges and 18% of grapefruit were ruined, respectively. Harvesting the remaining crops was delayed until mid-October due to inundated groves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-13"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup> Communications also suffered severely. Throughout the state, 32,000\xc2\xa0households were left without telephone service and 400\xc2\xa0poles were broken and about 2,500\xc2\xa0others leaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-14"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup> Then-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Florida" title="Governor of Florida">Governor of Florida</a> <a href="/wiki/John_W._Martin" title="John W. Martin">John W. Martin</a> estimated that 15,000\xc2\xa0families were left homeless in Palm Beach County alone. Additionally, about 11,500\xc2\xa0families would need to be "re-established".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uf_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-uf-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_South_Florida">Coastal South Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coastal South Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1928_Okeechobee_Flood.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="819" data-file-width="855" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/1928_Okeechobee_Flood.png/220px-1928_Okeechobee_Flood.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/1928_Okeechobee_Flood.png/330px-1928_Okeechobee_Flood.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/1928_Okeechobee_Flood.png/440px-1928_Okeechobee_Flood.png 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1928_Okeechobee_Flood.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nApproximate area of the flood. Note: The Palm Beach County label is misplaced. North of Canal Point has been in Martin County since 1925.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In Miami, damage was minimal, limited to broken windows and awnings. In <a href="/wiki/Hollywood,_Florida" title="Hollywood, Florida">Hollywood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale</a>, windows and roofs were damaged, although damage was still fairly minor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> Numerous power lines and telephone wires were downed in the latter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fldn_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-fldn-19">[19]</a></sup> Northward, from <a href="/wiki/Pompano_Beach,_Florida" title="Pompano Beach, Florida">Pompano Beach</a> to <a href="/wiki/Jupiter,_Florida" title="Jupiter, Florida">Jupiter</a>, buildings suffered serious damage from the heavy winds and 10\xc2\xa0ft (3\xc2\xa0m) storm surge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> Nearly all small frame houses were destroyed in <a href="/wiki/Deerfield_Beach,_Florida" title="Deerfield Beach, Florida">Deerfield Beach</a>, while several citizens estimated that at least 50% of homes were demolished. The town\'s post office, depot, and an entire business block were also destroyed. An eight-year-old boy drowned in a ditch near where his family sought refuge. In <a href="/wiki/Boca_Raton,_Florida" title="Boca Raton, Florida">Boca Raton</a>, two garages and several houses were destroyed. At the Cloister Inn, windows were shattered and the roof was damaged; across the street, 32\xc2\xa0freight cars belonging to a train along the <a href="/wiki/Florida_East_Coast_Railway" title="Florida East Coast Railway">Florida East Coast Railway</a> were tossed by the wind into a nearby ditch. A short distance to the north, a warehouse was flattened. A building occupied by a restaurant and a store was flattened. In <a href="/wiki/Delray_Beach,_Florida" title="Delray Beach, Florida">Delray Beach</a>, four churches suffered severe damage and the Alta Repp and Seacrest hotels both lost a portion of their roof. The police reported three deaths within the city. In Delray Beach and <a href="/wiki/Lantana,_Florida" title="Lantana, Florida">Lantana</a>, all houses and the railroad station were badly damaged. In <a href="/wiki/Boynton_Beach,_Florida" title="Boynton Beach, Florida">Boynton Beach</a>, about 75% of businesses suffered complete destruction. Fifteen people were injured by a roof collapse while taking refuge in the auditorium of a high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36_lives_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-36_lives-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath_17.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="457" data-file-width="690" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath_17.jpg/220px-1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath_17.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath_17.jpg/330px-1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath_17.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath_17.jpg/440px-1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath_17.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1928_Okeechobee_Aftermath_17.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAftermath of the hurricane in southern Florida</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Lake_Worth,_Florida" title="Lake Worth, Florida">Lake Worth</a>, approximately 50% of homes were damaged or destroyed, while 75% of buildings in the business district suffered damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36_lives_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-36_lives-20">[20]</a></sup> Impact along the coast was most severe in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="Palm Beach, Florida">Palm Beach</a>. Total coastal damages were estimated as "several million" dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/West_Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="West Palm Beach, Florida">West Palm Beach</a>, the storm destroyed 1,711\xc2\xa0homes and damaged 6,369\xc2\xa0others, and demolished 268\xc2\xa0businesses and impacted 490\xc2\xa0other businesses; the city suffered the worst damage, totaling just under $13.8\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uf_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-uf-18">[18]</a></sup> Likewise, there was also severe wind damage in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="Palm Beach, Florida">Palm Beach</a>. A few buildings constructed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Flagler" title="Henry Flagler">Henry Flagler</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Breakers" title="The Breakers">The Breakers</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Poinciana_Hotel" title="Royal Poinciana Hotel">Royal Poinciana Hotel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a>, were damaged. <a href="/wiki/Mar-a-Lago" title="Mar-a-Lago">Mar-a-Lago</a> suffered little impact other than uprooted trees and the destruction of a large Roman-style window, according to <a href="/wiki/Marjorie_Merriweather_Post" title="Marjorie Merriweather Post">Marjorie Merriweather Post</a>. <a href="/wiki/Rodman_Wanamaker" title="Rodman Wanamaker">Rodman Wanamaker</a>\'s house, known as "<a href="/wiki/Addison_Mizner#Selected_buildings" title="Addison Mizner">La Guerida</a>" and later the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_White_House" title="Winter White House">Winter White House</a>" during the <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_presidency_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Timeline of the presidency of John F. Kennedy">presidency of</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, suffered heavy damage during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uf_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-uf-18">[18]</a></sup> The Alba, Billows, New Palm Beach, and Royal Daneli hotels all suffered water damage, while the Alba Hotel was also deroofed. Nearby, the Rainbow Pier only received structural damage to its railings, though the pier office was blown away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fewbuildings_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-fewbuildings-21">[21]</a></sup> Approximately 600\xc2\xa0structures, including 10 hotels, were damaged in Palm Beach. Damage totaled over $2\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uf_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-uf-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The strongest winds in the eyewall affected northern Palm Beach County, particularly the vicinity of Jupiter, as the eye made landfall farther south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NWSMiamipage-2_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-NWSMiamipage-2-22">[22]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_Inlet_Light" title="Jupiter Inlet Light">Jupiter Inlet Lighthouse</a>, the mortar was reportedly "squeezed\xc2\xa0... like toothpaste" between the bricks during the storm, swaying the tower 17\xc2\xa0in (430\xc2\xa0mm) off the base.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnes_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-barnes-23">[23]</a></sup> The lighthouse keeper, Captain Seabrook, and his son, Franklin, worked to keep the light on during the storm after the electricity went out. After the generator failed to work, they hand-cranked the light\'s mantle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The building formerly used as a <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service_Miami,_Florida" title="National Weather Service Miami, Florida">Weather Bureau Office</a> was destroyed. Nearby, six people died after a house was demolished. Six other fatalities occurred west of Jupiter after a school where people sought shelter collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-15"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lake_Okeechobee_and_Everglades">Lake Okeechobee and Everglades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lake Okeechobee and Everglades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Inland, the hurricane wreaked much more widespread destruction along the more heavily populated coast of Lake Okeechobee. Residents had been warned to evacuate the low ground earlier in the day, but after the hurricane did not arrive on schedule, many thought it had missed and returned to their homes. In the weeks prior to storm, heavy rainfall caused the lake to rise 3\xc2\xa0ft (0.91\xc2\xa0m) between August\xc2\xa010 and September\xc2\xa010 and filled nearby canals and ditches. Precipitation from the hurricane itself caused Lake Okeechobee to rise further.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnes_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-barnes-23">[23]</a></sup> When the worst of the storm crossed the lake, the south-blowing wind caused a <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> to overflow the small dike that had been built at the south end of the lake. The resulting flood covered an area of hundreds of square miles with water that in some places was over 20\xc2\xa0ft (6\xc2\xa0m) deep. Houses were floated off of their foundations and dashed to pieces against any obstacle they encountered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sptimes_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-sptimes-25">[25]</a></sup> Most survivors and bodies were washed out into the <a href="/wiki/Everglades" title="Everglades">Everglades</a> where many of the bodies were never found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brochu_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brochu-26">[26]</a></sup> Agricultural losses in the area surrounding Lake Okeechobee were also significant, with virtually all crops destroyed and over 150\xc2\xa0tractors suffering damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-16"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pcpn1928091519isohyet.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="623" data-file-width="434" height="316" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Pcpn1928091519isohyet.png/220px-Pcpn1928091519isohyet.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Pcpn1928091519isohyet.png/330px-Pcpn1928091519isohyet.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/70/Pcpn1928091519isohyet.png 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pcpn1928091519isohyet.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nStorm total precipitation for September\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9319, 1928</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>As the rear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall" title="Eyewall">eyewall</a> passed over the area, the flood reversed itself, breaking the dikes along the northern coast of the lake and causing similar but smaller flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brochu_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brochu-26">[26]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_98_in_Florida" title="U.S. Route 98 in Florida">Route 98</a>, then known as Conner\'s Highway, was closed until January, when the bridge across the Onosohatchee River at <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Creek,_Florida" title="Taylor Creek, Florida">Taylor Creek</a> was replaced after the original bridge was carried about 150\xc2\xa0ft (46\xc2\xa0m) upstream during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Okeechobee_County,_Florida" title="Okeechobee County, Florida">Okeechobee County</a>, homes along the lake were destroyed by the storm surge, while dwellings within the city of <a href="/wiki/Okeechobee,_Florida" title="Okeechobee, Florida">Okeechobee</a> were severely impacted or demolished by winds of at least 90\xc2\xa0mph (140\xc2\xa0km/h). However, brick and concrete-structured dwellings received little damage. A number of three-story business buildings collapsed during the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigwave_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-bigwave-28">[28]</a></sup> Almost all roads were left impassable, while communications were nearly wiped out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exaggerated_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-exaggerated-29">[29]</a></sup> Overall, 27\xc2\xa0deaths occurred in <a href="/wiki/Okeechobee_County,_Florida" title="Okeechobee County, Florida">Okeechobee County</a>. Along the southwestern shore of Lake Okeechobee, the towns of <a href="/wiki/Clewiston,_Florida" title="Clewiston, Florida">Clewiston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moore_Haven,_Florida" title="Moore Haven, Florida">Moore Haven</a> were both flooded, but much of the damage to houses was due to strong winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bigwave_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-bigwave-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On Kreamer Island, many residents received information about the storm when it was too late to evacuate. In some homes, 20\xe2\x80\x9330\xc2\xa0people sought shelter inside and later stood on tables and chairs to remain above the water. Most of the homes were swept away into rows of pine trees and others more than half a mile (0.8\xc2\xa0km) away. Despite this, only one person drowned on the island. Residents of Torry Island also did not have ample time to prepare for the storm. They attempted to evacuate, but the causeway was already inundated, forcing twenty-three people to seek refuge in a packinghouse. Floodwaters entered the building, forcing the occupants into the rafters. However, the building was eventually pushed into a nearby canal. Ten people drowned, but thirteen others survived by clinging to a barge or tree tops, while one woman tied herself to a telegraph pole. Others that survived were swept far away from where the building once stood or the barge. A teenage boy was carried from the packinghouse to the Everglades Experiment Station in Belle Glade \xe2\x80\x93 a distance of about 8\xc2\xa0mi (13\xc2\xa0km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnes_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-barnes-23">[23]</a></sup> On Ritta Island, a number who successfully climbed to the roof of their houses were struck by trees or received fatal bites from <a href="/wiki/Agkistrodon_piscivorus" title="Agkistrodon piscivorus">water moccasin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/South_Bay,_Florida" title="South Bay, Florida">South Bay</a>, nearly all houses were destroyed and several buildings were unroofed. At least 160\xc2\xa0fatalities occurred in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uf_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-uf-18">[18]</a></sup> The future first mayor of South Bay, Aubrey (a.k.a. "Orb" or "A.O.") Walker, along with his brother, Haughty D. Walker, (a.k.a. "Haught"), survived the great hurricane of 1928 by gathering family members and joining a number of other South Bay citizens on a barge in the canal which allowed them to survive the flood waters that swept over South Bay and ultimately engulfed Okeelanta <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>. Throughout the 1920s, <a href="/wiki/Okeelanta,_Florida" title="Okeelanta, Florida">Okeelanta</a> suffered several floods and muck fires. Finally, the town was flooded severely during the storm and was subsequently abandoned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Bean City was also destroyed during the hurricane, but it was eventually rebuilt by Arthur Wells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Sebring Farms was reduced to piles of rubber, with only four tall royal palm trees left standing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-17"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup> Only the hotel at Miami Locks survived the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-18"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup> Ninety-nine people died in that town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-neely_7-19"><a href="#cite_note-neely-7">[7]</a></sup> In Chosen, only two people managed to escape a house that sheltered nineteen people. Twenty other residents took refuge in a building which lost its roof during the storm, forcing the occupants to move into the restroom. A house that was full of people floated about half a mile (0.8\xc2\xa0km) from its original location. The refugees were unaware that the house was moving until it collided with a railroad embankment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-barnes_23-3"><a href="#cite_note-barnes-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burial_of_victims_of_the_1928_hurricane-_West_Palm_Beach,_Florida_(3674596695).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="474" data-file-width="600" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Burial_of_victims_of_the_1928_hurricane-_West_Palm_Beach%2C_Florida_%283674596695%29.jpg/220px-Burial_of_victims_of_the_1928_hurricane-_West_Palm_Beach%2C_Florida_%283674596695%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Burial_of_victims_of_the_1928_hurricane-_West_Palm_Beach%2C_Florida_%283674596695%29.jpg/330px-Burial_of_victims_of_the_1928_hurricane-_West_Palm_Beach%2C_Florida_%283674596695%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Burial_of_victims_of_the_1928_hurricane-_West_Palm_Beach%2C_Florida_%283674596695%29.jpg/440px-Burial_of_victims_of_the_1928_hurricane-_West_Palm_Beach%2C_Florida_%283674596695%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burial_of_victims_of_the_1928_hurricane-_West_Palm_Beach,_Florida_(3674596695).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nBurial of victims in West Palm Beach</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Floodwaters persisted for several weeks, greatly impeding attempts to clean up the devastation. Burial services were quickly overwhelmed, and many of the bodies were placed into mass graves. Around 75% of the fatalities were migrant farm workers, making identification of both dead and missing bodies very difficult; as a result of this, the count of the dead is not very accurate. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Cross" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> estimated the number of fatalities as 1,836, which was taken as the official count by the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> for many years (and exactly equal to the official count for <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>). Older sources usually list 3,411 as the hurricane\'s total count of fatalities, including the Caribbean. However, in 2003 the U.S. death count was revised to "at least" 2,500, making the Okeechobee hurricane the second-deadliest natural disaster in United States history behind the <a href="/wiki/1900_Galveston_hurricane" title="1900 Galveston hurricane">1900 Galveston hurricane</a>. A mass grave at the Port Mayaca Cemetery east of <a href="/wiki/Port_Mayaca,_Florida" title="Port Mayaca, Florida">Port Mayaca</a> contains the bodies of 1,600 victims of the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brochu_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-Brochu-26">[26]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_and_North_Florida">Central and North Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Central and North Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Fort_Myers,_Florida" title="Fort Myers, Florida">Fort Myers</a>, property damage was slight, limited mostly to scores of small boats and fishing shacks along the waterfront.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dadswell_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-dadswell-34">[34]</a></sup> Nearly all cigar factories in <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_Florida" title="Tampa, Florida">Tampa</a> were closed after wind and rain drove too much moisture into the buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Offshore, the fishing smack <i>Wallace A. McDonnell</i> was beached near Piney Point, though all of the crew survived. Additionally, the Cuban schooner <i>Isabel Alvado</i> sank offshore <a href="/wiki/Boca_Grande,_Florida" title="Boca Grande, Florida">Boca Grande</a>. The crew, who were immigrants, were rescued by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">Coast Guard</a> and later deported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Martin_County,_Florida" title="Martin County, Florida">Martin County</a>, a bridge connecting <a href="/wiki/Stuart,_Florida" title="Stuart, Florida">Stuart</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palm_City,_Florida" title="Palm City, Florida">Palm City</a> was severely damaged and closed to traffic as a result. A temporary ferry service across the <a href="/wiki/St._Lucie_River" title="St. Lucie River">St. Lucie River</a> was established and operated until repairs to the bridge were complete in the summer of 1929.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Fort_Pierce,_Florida" title="Fort Pierce, Florida">Fort Pierce</a>, most of the impact was confined to the waterfront areas. A warehouse, fish houses, docks, and a bridge across the Indian River were destroyed, while several other buildings were unroofed. Damage in the city totaled about $150,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uf_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-uf-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the interior areas of Central and North Florida, impact was mainly confined to agricultural losses, particularly citrus, though wind damage occurred to structures. Between <a href="/wiki/Sebring,_Florida" title="Sebring, Florida">Sebring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Wales,_Florida" title="Lake Wales, Florida">Lake Wales</a>, 200\xc2\xa0telephone poles were toppled. In <a href="/wiki/Bartow,_Florida" title="Bartow, Florida">Bartow</a>, business building windows were shattered and signs were knocked down, while several roofs and chimneys also suffered damage. Winds gusting up to 70\xc2\xa0mph (110\xc2\xa0km/h) lashed <a href="/wiki/Lakeland,_Florida" title="Lakeland, Florida">Lakeland</a>. Many trees were uprooted and several buildings were impacted, including the hospital and a number of businesses. At <a href="/wiki/Florida_Southern_College" title="Florida Southern College">Florida Southern College</a> (FSC), the north side of the gymnasium collapsed while other buildings on campus were damaged to a lesser degree. The trees in the citrus grove surrounding FSC lost much of their fruit. Overall, Lakeland suffered about $50,000 in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando</a>, impact to properties was described as slight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dadswell_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-dadswell-34">[34]</a></sup> Winds up to 50\xc2\xa0mph (80\xc2\xa0km/h) impacted the <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_metropolitan_area" title="Jacksonville metropolitan area">Jacksonville area</a>, resulting in minor damage at <a href="/wiki/Jacksonville_Beach,_Florida" title="Jacksonville Beach, Florida">Jacksonville Beach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere">Elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Outside of Florida, damage in the United States was minor from the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr-westindian_2-18"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-westindian-2">[2]</a></sup> In Georgia, low-lying streets were flooded or washed out in the <a href="/wiki/Savannah_metropolitan_area" title="Savannah metropolitan area">Savannah area</a>. Additionally, winds downed trees and power lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup> Heavy rainfall occurred from eastern Florida through coastal Georgia, the Carolinas, and southeast Virginia. The highest rainfall total was 12.53 inches (318\xc2\xa0mm) at <a href="/wiki/Darlington,_South_Carolina" title="Darlington, South Carolina">Darlington, South Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The storm caused flooding in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and brought near-hurricane-force winds and a 7\xc2\xa0foot (2.1\xc2\xa0m) storm surge to the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> After the hurricane became extratropical, its wind field became very large. <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City,_New_Jersey" title="Atlantic City, New Jersey">Atlantic City, New Jersey</a>, recorded winds of 76\xc2\xa0mph (122\xc2\xa0km/h) despite being far from the center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-meta-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a arrived from nearby areas such as <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>. Early on September\xc2\xa018, a train leaving Miami carried 20\xc2\xa0doctors and 20\xc2\xa0nurses to West Palm Beach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The Miami <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">Red Cross</a> Citizens Relief Committee, which was established to provide aid for victims of the storm, transported "hundreds of loaves of bread, gallons of milk, pounds of coffee and sugar, blankets, cots, and medical supplies." The first relief train was ridden by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Taylor_Robinson" title="Joseph Taylor Robinson">Joseph T. Robinson</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> vice presidential nominee during <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_1928" title="United States presidential election, 1928">the election that year</a>. At least 100\xc2\xa0people were brought to Miami for medical treatment. In Lake Worth, 25\xc2\xa0people were treated for various injuries at the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream_Hotel" title="Gulf Stream Hotel">Gulf Stream Hotel</a> and the local fire station. Dr. W. A. Claxton, chief of the Miami Department of Public Welfare, requested <a href="/wiki/Antitoxin" title="Antitoxin">antitoxin</a>, typhoid serum, and at least 200\xc2\xa0tetanus serums. There was also a request for 1,000\xc2\xa0more cots in West Palm Beach and Kelsey City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Racial_issues">Racial issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Racial issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_of_1928_African_American_Mass_Burial_Site" title="Hurricane of 1928 African American Mass Burial Site">Hurricane of 1928 African American Mass Burial Site</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mass_Grave_020.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1944" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Mass_Grave_020.JPG/220px-Mass_Grave_020.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Mass_Grave_020.JPG/330px-Mass_Grave_020.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Mass_Grave_020.JPG/440px-Mass_Grave_020.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mass_Grave_020.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHistorical marker at the <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_of_1928_African_American_Mass_Burial_Site" title="Hurricane of 1928 African American Mass Burial Site">mass grave</a> in West Palm Beach</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In Florida, although the hurricane\'s destruction affected everything in its path, the death toll was by far highest and the aftermath the worst in the economically poor areas in the low-lying ground right around Lake Okeechobee, such as Belle Glade, Chosen, Pahokee, South Bay, and Bean City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hspbc_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-hspbc-45">[45]</a></sup> Around 75% of the fatalities were among <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Migrant_farm_workers" title="Migrant farm workers">migrant farm workers</a>, most of whom were black.</p>\n<p>The black workers did most of the post-hurricane cleanup work, and the few caskets available for burials were mostly used for the bodies of whites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sharp_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-sharp-46">[46]</a></sup> Additionally, the white victims received a proper burial at Woodlawn Cemetery in downtown West Palm Beach,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hspbc_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-hspbc-45">[45]</a></sup> which was the only mass gravesite to receive a timely memorial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sharp_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-sharp-46">[46]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In contrast, the bodies of black victims were burned in funeral pyres or thrown into mass burial sites such as the ones in <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_of_1928_African_American_Mass_Burial_Site" title="Hurricane of 1928 African American Mass Burial Site">West Palm Beach</a> and Port Mayaca.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:West_PB_FL_1928_Mass_Burial_Site_marker01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2448" data-file-width="3264" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/West_PB_FL_1928_Mass_Burial_Site_marker01.jpg/220px-West_PB_FL_1928_Mass_Burial_Site_marker01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/West_PB_FL_1928_Mass_Burial_Site_marker01.jpg/330px-West_PB_FL_1928_Mass_Burial_Site_marker01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/West_PB_FL_1928_Mass_Burial_Site_marker01.jpg/440px-West_PB_FL_1928_Mass_Burial_Site_marker01.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:West_PB_FL_1928_Mass_Burial_Site_marker01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe historical marker added to the memorial site in 2003, the seventy-fifth anniversary of the storm</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Robert Hazard, a resident of West Palm Beach, established the Storm of \'28 Memorial Park Coalition Inc. to fight for recognition of the black victims of the storm. In 2000, the West Palm Beach burial site was reacquired by the city of West Palm Beach and plans for construction of a memorial began. The <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_of_1928_African_American_Mass_Burial_Site" title="Hurricane of 1928 African American Mass Burial Site">site</a> was listed on the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 2002 and a state historical marker was added in 2003 during the 75th anniversary of the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hspbc_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-hspbc-45">[45]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The effects of the hurricane on black migrant workers were dramatized in African-American writer <a href="/wiki/Zora_Neale_Hurston" title="Zora Neale Hurston">Zora Neale Hurston</a>\'s 1937 seminal novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Their_Eyes_Were_Watching_God" title="Their Eyes Were Watching God">Their Eyes Were Watching God</a></i>, her best known work and a selection on <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">TIME</a></i> magazine\'s 2005 list of the 100 best English-language novels published since 1923.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Improved_building_codes">Improved building codes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Improved building codes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the aftermath of the hurricane in coastal Florida, it became apparent that well-constructed buildings with shutters had suffered practically no damage from winds that caused serious structural problems to lesser buildings. Buildings with well-constructed frames, and those made of steel, concrete, brick, or stone were largely immune to winds, and the use of shutters prevented damage to windows and the interior of the buildings. Coming on the heels of the 1926 Miami hurricane where a similar pattern had been noticed, one lasting result of the 1928 storm was improved building codes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flood_control">Flood control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1928_Okeechobee_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Flood control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Army_CoE_sign_Hoover_Dike.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="233" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Army_CoE_sign_Hoover_Dike.jpg/220px-Army_CoE_sign_Hoover_Dike.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/56/Army_CoE_sign_Hoover_Dike.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Army_CoE_sign_Hoover_Dike.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA sign advertising the completion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoover_Dike" title="Hoover Dike">Hoover Dike</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>To prevent a recurrence of disasters like this one and the Great Miami Hurricane of 1926, the Florida State Legislature created the Okeechobee Flood Control District, which was authorized to cooperate with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="U.S. Army Corps of Engineers">U.S. Army Corps of Engineers</a> in flood control undertakings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USArmyCorps_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-USArmyCorps-50">[50]</a></sup> After a personal inspection of the area by President <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a>, the Corps drafted a new plan which provided for the construction of floodway channels, control gates, and major levees along Lake Okeechobee\'s shores. A long term system was designed for the purpose of flood control, water conservation, prevention of saltwater intrusion, and preservation of fish and wildlife populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USArmyCorps_50-1"><a href="#cite_note-USArmyCorps-50">[50]</a></sup> One of the solutions was the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover_Dike" title="Herbert Hoover Dike">Herbert Hoover Dike</a>. Today, concerns related to the dike\'s stability have grown in response to studies indicating long term problems with "piping" and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>. Leaks have been reported after several heavy rain events. Proposed solutions to the dike\'s problems have included the construction of a <a href="/wiki/Soil_mechanics" title="Soil mechanics">seepage</a> <a href="/wiki/Berm" title="Berm">berm</a> on the landward side of the dike, with the first stage costing approximately $67\xc2\xa0million (US$).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USArmyCorps2_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-USArmyCorps2-51">[51]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
