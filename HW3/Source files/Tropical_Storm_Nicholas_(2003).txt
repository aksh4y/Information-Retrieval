colspan="2"></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Tropical Storm Nicholas</b> was a long-lived <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical storm</a> in October and November of the <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. Forming from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> on October 13 in the central tropical <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, Nicholas slowly developed due to moderate levels of wind shear throughout its lifetime. Deep convection slowly organized, and Nicholas attained a peak intensity of 70\xc2\xa0mph (110\xc2\xa0km/h) on October 17. After moving west-northwestward for much of its lifetime, it turned northward and weakened due to increasing shear. The storm again turned to the west and briefly restrengthened, but after turning again to the north Nicholas transitioned to an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical" title="Extratropical">extratropical</a> cyclone on October 24. As an extratropical storm, Nicholas executed a large loop to the west, and after moving erratically for a week and organizing into a tropical low, it was absorbed by a non-tropical low. The low continued westward, crossed <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, and ultimately dissipated over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast of the United States</a> on November 5.</p>\n<p>Nicholas had no impact as a tropical cyclone, and impact from the low that absorbed the storm was limited to rainfall, gusty winds, and rough surf. Nicholas extended the above-average levels of tropical cyclone activity throughout the 2003 season, and was one of fourteen seasons to reach fourteen storms. The low that absorbed the storm nearly developed into a tropical cyclone, which would have been called Odette. However, moderate <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> prevented further development.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nicholas_2003_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="751" data-file-width="1024" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Nicholas_2003_track.png/220px-Nicholas_2003_track.png" href="/wiki/File:Nicholas_2003_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMap plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The origin of Tropical Storm Nicholas can be traced to a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> that moved off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> on October 9. It moved westward, and developed a broad <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">area of low pressure</a> along the wave on October 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Convection organized around the low early on October 12 while located 800\xc2\xa0miles (1300\xc2\xa0km) southwest of the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct12two_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct12two-2">[2]</a></sup> As upper level winds became more favorable,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct12two2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct12two2-3">[3]</a></sup> the system became better organized, though the circulation center initially remained located to the west of the area of heavy showers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct13two_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct13two-4">[4]</a></sup> The surface circulation of the large circulation envelope moved towards a position near the western edge of the deepest convection, and it is estimated the system developed into Tropical Depression Nineteen late on October 13 while located 1,030\xc2\xa0miles (1,660\xc2\xa0km) west-southwest of the Cape Verde islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-td19disc1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-td19disc1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Located within an area of moderate southwesterly <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, the depression was slow to organize;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> early on October 14, a clear center was not depicted on <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">Infrared</a> satellite imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-td19disc2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-td19disc2-6">[6]</a></sup> The depression moved to the west-northwest and gradually strengthened into Tropical Storm Nicholas late on October 14 while located 1,200\xc2\xa0miles (1,930\xc2\xa0km) east of the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> On October 15, deep convection became much better organized, though the center of Nicholas remained displaced from the deepest of the shower activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicholas7_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nicholas7-7">[7]</a></sup> Despite the shear, the storm continued to strengthen as it moved to the west-northwest, a motion caused by a mid-level <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to its north. On October 16, Nicholas transitioned from a sheared, unorganized system to a more organized system, as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbands" title="Rainbands">banding features</a> and improvement in its convective pattern<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicholas11_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nicholas11-8">[8]</a></sup> due to lesser amounts of vertical shear and a healthier <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> over the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicholas13_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nicholas13-9">[9]</a></sup> Nicholas continued to strengthen, and attained a peak intensity of 70\xc2\xa0mph (110\xc2\xa0km/h) on October 17 while located 875\xc2\xa0miles (1,410\xc2\xa0km) east of the Leeward Islands. <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak estimates</a> suggested Nicholas potentially attained hurricane status, though microwave images of the storm showed a sheared system without any evidence of an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye feature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nicholas_Radar.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="447" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Nicholas_Radar.JPG/330px-Nicholas_Radar.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Nicholas_Radar.JPG/440px-Nicholas_Radar.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nicholas_Radar.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nRadar image of the remnants of Nicholas hitting <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> on November 3</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Shortly after peaking in strength, Nicholas weakened due to increasing southwesterly wind shear, and the center of circulation briefly became exposed from the deep convection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicholas17_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nicholas17-10">[10]</a></sup> In response to a break in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>, the storm turned to the north on October 18. Nicholas turned more to the west on October 20, and later on the winds in the storm decreased to minimal storm strength.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Convection diminished greatly, though Nicholas unexpectedly re-strengthened as deep convection refired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nicholas30_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nicholas30-11">[11]</a></sup> After reaching winds of 50\xc2\xa0mph (85\xc2\xa0km/h), the storm again weakened due to increasing shear, and after turning to the northwest Nicholas degenerated into a tropical depression on October 23. Nicholas tracked northward in response to a break in the subtropical ridge, and on October 24, after degenerating to a remnant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" title="Low pressure area">low pressure area</a>, Nicholas lost what tropical characteristics it retained and transitioned into an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical" title="Extratropical">extratropical</a> cyclone. The remnant storm executed a large anticyclonic loop, resulting in a westward motion and a brief re-intensification into an extratropical storm on October 29. The storm continued to the west along a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> associated with a non-tropical low to its southwest, and executed a second anticyclonic loop to the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> On October 30, convection increased over the low-level circulation of what was left to Nicholas, and the National Hurricane Center indicated a potential for tropical or <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical</a> development as it moved towards warmer waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oct30two_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-oct30two-12">[12]</a></sup> The system executed a small cyclonic loop, and on October 31 the system organized into a tropical low. It turned westward towards a non-tropical low, and on November 1 the remnants of Nicholas was absorbed by the low.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On November 1, convection increased in the complex system that absorbed the remnants of Nicholas, and the National Hurricane Center indicated the system could develop into a tropical or subtropical cyclone at any time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nov1two_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-nov1two-13">[13]</a></sup> The low continued quickly westward, generating only sporadic convection, and crossed <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> on November 3. After entering the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, it turned to the west-northwest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> and without organizing it made landfall in southeast <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a> on November 4.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nov4two_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-nov4two-14">[14]</a></sup> It continued inland, and quickly weakened over Louisiana and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> on November 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nov5two_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-nov5two-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span id="Impact,_records,_and_naming"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact.2C_records.2C_and_naming">Impact, records, and naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_Storm_Nicholas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact, records, and naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The non-tropical low that absorbed Nicholas dropped locally heavy rainfall across the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nov1two_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-nov1two-13">[13]</a></sup> as well as in portions of the northern <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nov4two_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-nov4two-14">[14]</a></sup> Gusty winds from the low produced heavy surf and rip currents along the east and west coast of Florida,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fdem_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-fdem-16">[16]</a></sup> and a wind gust of 35\xc2\xa0mph (55\xc2\xa0km/h) was recorded in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainband</a> from the system moved ashore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-srh_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-srh-17">[17]</a></sup> The low also resulted in two to four foot (0.6 to 1.2\xc2\xa0m) swells along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surfline_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-surfline-18">[18]</a></sup> Overall, no damage or deaths were associated with Nicholas or the low that absorbed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Operationally, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> determined if the non-tropical low that absorbed Nicholas developed into a tropical or subtropical storm, it would be named Odette. In post-season analysis, the center considered classifying the system as an unnumbered tropical depression, though lack of sufficient characteristics of a tropical depression prevented the classification.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nov2003summary_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-nov2003summary-19">[19]</a></sup> If Nicholas had attained hurricane status, it would have been one of eight tropical cyclones to reach hurricane status in October in the deep tropical Atlantic since 1900.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-td19disc5_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-td19disc5-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
