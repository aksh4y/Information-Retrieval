mitigation</b> encompasses the actions and planning taken before a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> strikes to mitigate damage and injury from the storm. Knowledge of tropical cyclone impacts on an area help plan for future possibilities. Preparedness may involve preparations made by individuals as well as centralized efforts by governments or other organizations. Tracking storms during the tropical cyclone season helps individuals know current threats. Regional Specialized Meteorological Centers and Tropical Cyclone Warning Centers provide current information and forecasts to help individuals make the best decision possible.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_preparedness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Potential impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="166" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gulfport,_Mississippi" title="Gulfport, Mississippi">Gulfport, Mississippi</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_tropical_cyclones" title="Effects of tropical cyclones">Effects of tropical cyclones</a></div>\n<p>Tropical cyclones out at sea cause large waves, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heavy_rain_(meteorology)" title="Heavy rain (meteorology)">heavy rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> and high winds, disrupting international shipping and, at times, causing shipwrecks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18cva_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-18cva-1">[1]</a></sup> On land, strong <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> can damage or destroy vehicles, buildings, bridges, and other outside objects, turning loose debris into deadly flying projectiles. The <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, or the increase in sea level due to the cyclone, is typically the worst effect from landfalling tropical cyclones, historically resulting in 90% of tropical cyclone deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shultz_Epid_Reviews_2005_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shultz_Epid_Reviews_2005-2">[2]</a></sup> The broad rotation of a landfalling tropical cyclone, and vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> at its periphery, spawns <a href="/wiki/History_of_tropical_cyclone-spawned_tornadoes" title="History of tropical cyclone-spawned tornadoes">tornadoes</a>. Tornadoes can also be spawned as a result of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eyewall_mesovortices" title="Eyewall mesovortices">eyewall mesovortices</a>, which persist until landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_L6_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_L6-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Over the past two centuries, tropical cyclones have been responsible for the deaths of about 1.9\xc2\xa0million people worldwide. Large areas of standing water caused by flooding lead to infection, as well as contributing to mosquito-borne illnesses. Crowded evacuees in <a href="/wiki/Emergency_shelter" title="Emergency shelter">shelters</a> increase the risk of disease propagation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shultz_Epid_Reviews_2005_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shultz_Epid_Reviews_2005-2">[2]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones significantly interrupt infrastructure, leading to power outages, bridge destruction, and the hampering of reconstruction efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shultz_Epid_Reviews_2005_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Shultz_Epid_Reviews_2005-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Power_failures_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Power_failures-4">[4]</a></sup> On average, the Gulf and east coasts of the United States suffer approximately US $5 billion (1995 US $) in cyclone damage every year. The majority (83%) of tropical cyclone damage is caused by severe hurricanes, category 3 or greater. However, category 3 or greater hurricanes only account for about one-fifth of cyclones that make landfall every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Although cyclones take an enormous toll in lives and personal property, they may be important factors in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> regimes of places they impact, as they may bring much-needed precipitation to otherwise dry regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005_EPac_outlook_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-2005_EPac_outlook-6">[6]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones also help maintain the global heat balance by moving warm, moist tropical air to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_latitudes" title="Middle latitudes">middle latitudes</a> and polar regions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JetStream_introduction_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-JetStream_introduction-7">[7]</a></sup> and by regulating the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> through <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The storm surge and winds of hurricanes may be destructive to human-made structures, but they also stir up the waters of coastal <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuaries</a>, which are typically important fish breeding locales. Tropical cyclone destruction spurs redevelopment, greatly increasing local property values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christopherson_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christopherson-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>When hurricanes surge upon shore from the ocean, salt is introduced to many freshwater areas and raises the <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> levels too high for some habitats to withstand. Some are able to cope with the salt and recycle it back into the ocean, but others can not release the extra surface water quickly enough or do not have a large enough freshwater source to replace it. Because of this, some species of plants and vegetation die due to the excess salt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In addition, hurricanes can carry <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toxins" title="Toxins">toxins</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acids" title="Acids">acids</a> onto shore when they make landfall. The flood water can pick up the toxins from different spills and contaminate the land that it passes over. The toxins are very harmful to the people and animals in the area, as well as the environment around them. The flooding water can also spark many dangerous oil spills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Recent windstorm activity, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in particular<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, has focused interest in measures that can be used to lower the likelihood of damage to structures. Billions of dollars of damages have resulted from such strong <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> and the manner in which structures have been built in the affected areas in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Some building codes address mitigation measures. For example, the Florida Building Code,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> specifies the type of nail used to secure roof sheathing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The specification was determined by scientific research conducted by <a href="/wiki/Florida_International_University" title="Florida International University">Florida International University</a>\'s International Hurricane Research Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span title="Tropical cyclone tracking chart">Tropical cyclone tracking chart</a></div>\n<p>There have been many lessons learned about individual preparedness since Hurricane Katrina. The biggest responsibility was helping the children of New Orleans. Supplies were sufficient until there was more damage than the hospitals were prepared for. People were afraid that their safety was in danger due to lack of security and support at hospitals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>An important decision in individual preparedness is determining if and when to evacuate an area that will be affected by a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_tracking_chart" title="Tropical cyclone tracking chart">Tropical cyclone tracking charts</a> allow people to track ongoing systems to form their own opinions regarding where the storms are going and whether or not they need to prepare for the system being tracked, including possible evacuation. This continues to be encouraged by the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> and National Hurricane Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTH_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTH-17">[17]</a></sup> Some agencies provide track storms in their immediate vicinity,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> while others cover entire ocean basins. One can choose to track one storm per map, use the map until the table is filled, or use one map per season. Some tracking charts have important contact information in case of an emergency or to locate nearby hurricane shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BTR_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-BTR-19">[19]</a></sup> Tracking charts allow tropical cyclones to be better understood by the end user.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> If evacuation is not possible or necessary, other preparedness actions include storing supplies, securing a home against extreme winds and rain, and making plans with others prior to the storm\'s landfall.</p>\n<p>Hurricane preparedness kits usually include drinkable water, sealed pre-prepared meals <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRE" title="MRE">MRE</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First-aid" title="First-aid">first-aid</a> kits, prescription medications in sealed containers, waterproof battery-powered or hand-crank-powered flashlights and radios, a whistle or other sound-signaling device, a <a href="/wiki/Multi-tool" title="Multi-tool">multi-tool</a> with a knife, identification and medical cards, any necessary medical records, waterproof bags or portable waterproof containers, and other supplies helpful to a survival situation. If your pets will be with you make sure you include canned or dry food for them as well(any dry food included should be rotated every 2 months).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> You should also include veterinarian records and proof of vaccinations for all of your pets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Preparedness also may include having discussed evacuation plans and routes, and informing others of those plans before a disaster occurs.</p>\n<p>Evacuation to hurricane shelters is an option of last resort<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Shelter space is first-come, first-served and only intended preserve human life. Buildings designated as shelters in Florida are required to only have been constructed to meet minimum code requirements applicable at the time of design<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Some shelters are expected to protect occupants from wind and water but are not expected to provide food, water, sanitation, or bedding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Based on preparedness of the people in a region, the preparedness level was greatly affected by the salary, race, age, etc. Many people are not prepared for the worst-case scenario, but they should be. Being prepared for a huge disaster is what will ultimately save your life if worse comes to worst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Hospitals are needed to be prepared in advance for huge natural disasters. Nurses are in high demand when people are in trouble. They need to be prepared for ready for any situation that enters the hospital. After hurricanes such as Katrina and Rita, nurses are needed to be fully capable of any possible scenario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_retrofitting">Home retrofitting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_preparedness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Home retrofitting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An important decision a homeowner should make is to locate the building outside of range from the coast that is exposed to <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>. Regardless of protection from the effects of wind, a building can be flooded or destroyed by storm surge waters.</p>\n<p>To mitigate the effects of high winds and associated debris impact, the home can be examined out by an experienced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_mitigation" title="Hurricane mitigation">hurricane mitigation</a> professional. Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> protection companies offer free consultations as part of their marketing strategy. The State of Florida has taken steps to help its homeowners in the area of hurricane preparedness through its My Safe Florida Home grant program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Qualified homeowners receive a free in-home assessment of their home\'s hurricane readiness. The state matches funds spent by the homeowner, up to a $5,000 limit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>When making these types of home improvements, there are two major areas of focus: the roof and the openings.</p>\n<p>Homes may be retrofitted to withstand the extreme conditions of a tropical cyclone. Common modifications include reinforcing <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gabled_roofs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gabled roofs (page does not exist)">gabled roofs</a>, applying additional adhesives to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roof_shingles" title="Roof shingles">roof shingles</a>, installing <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_straps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hurricane straps (page does not exist)">hurricane straps</a> and clips to ensure the roof stays in place despite high winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Hurricane resistant <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_shutter" title="Hurricane shutter">shutters</a>, as well as impact resistant glass may help keep windows closed from driving rain, despite flying debris.</p>\n<p>External patio and pool screen enclosures are especially vulnerable during a hurricane. A homeowner who anticipates sustained winds greater than 100 mph may use a razor knife taped to a telescopic pole to cut down the screens from the enclosure\'s skeleton structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> This will prevent the screens from acting as a sail and pulling down the entire enclosure structure.</p>\n<p>Reinforcing garage doors and entry doors is also common practice for hurricane preparing a home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Garage doors may be protected by fabric screens, made of a strong woven fabric, to keep out projectiles and to reinforce the door.</p>\n<p>The goal of these mitigation measures and products is to decrease the likelihood of severe damage a home. There is no guarantee that these measures will safeguard any home against any kind of <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectiles</a> that may come with a <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a>, but a well-protected home is far more likely to come through a hurricane in better shape than a home that has little or no protection.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organized_preparation">Organized preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span In addition to facilities themselves being at risk, the internal contents of the structures can be damaged as result of exposure to water if the building envelope is breached, usually as a result of the strong <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_storms" title="Tropical storms">tropical storms</a>. Although the negative pressure caused by high velocity wind flowing over a building roof can cause the roof to fail with the building envelope intact,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> broken windows allow the air pressure to rise inside a building, creating an even greater pressure difference, and increasing the likelihood of roof failure. This pressure increase results after an opening, that is, a window or door, is breached after being struck and broken by wind blown debris.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_certification">Product certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_preparedness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Product certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Few codes make the use of hurricane mitigation products mandatory<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. As the field is rather new to <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a>, few standards exist to address uniform testing or <a href="/wiki/Product_certification" title="Product certification">product certification</a> of product installations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_facilities">Test facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_preparedness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Test facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There is no single test standard or facility capable of qualifying the protection of an entire <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">building</a>. The Cyclone Testing Station<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> in Australia can test building systems (e.g., roofs, exterior cladding), and indirectly test entire small buildings with structural loading to simulate wind pressures. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the test facilities at the <a href="/wiki/FM_Global" title="FM Global">FM Global</a> Research Campus includes both direct wind testing of roof components to speeds up to 160\xc2\xa0mph (260\xc2\xa0km/h),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> and roof uplift simulation using suction tables per industry standards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_simulator">Hurricane simulator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_preparedness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hurricane simulator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">University of Florida</a>\'s team led by Forrest Masters developed "the world\'s most powerful portable hurricane simulator, a giant machine capable of reproducing winds in excess of 120\xc2\xa0mph (190\xc2\xa0km/h) and recreating rain."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It consists of eight large fans driven by four diesel engines, with a 5,000-gallon (19,000 litre) water tank to keep the engines cooled. The university is currently designing water-resistant windows, wind-proof tiles and altogether stronger structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>\n<p>This machine was used in the <i>MythBusters</i> <a href="/wiki/MythBusters_(2009_season)#Hurricane_Windows" title="MythBusters (2009 season)">2009 season</a> to test the myth that it\'s better to leave the windows open during a hurricane than closed (the myth was declared Busted).</p>\n<h2><span
