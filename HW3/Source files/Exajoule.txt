after <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a>. As with every International System of Units (SI) unit named for a person, the first letter of its <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> is <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">upper case</a> (J). However, when an SI unit is spelled out in English, it should always begin with a <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">lower case</a> letter (<i>joule</i>)\xe2\x80\x94except in a situation where <i>any</i> word in that position would be capitalized, such as at the beginning of a sentence or in material using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Title_case" title="Title case">title case</a>. Note that "degree <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a>" conforms to this rule because the "d" is lowercase.\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0Based on <i><a class="external text" href="http://www.bipm.org/en/publications/si-brochure/section5-2.html" rel="nofollow">The International System of Units</a></i>, section 5.2.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Confusion_with_newton_metre">Confusion with newton metre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joule&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Confusion with newton metre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Newton_metre" title="Newton metre">newton metre</a></div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanics</a>, the concept of <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">force</a> (in some direction) has a close analog in the concept of <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> (about some angle):</p>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tr>\n<th>Linear</th>\n<th>Angular</th>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>force</td>\n<td>torque</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a></td>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">moment of inertia</a></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>distance</td>\n<td>angle</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>A result of this similarity is that the SI unit for torque is the <a href="/wiki/Newton_metre" title="Newton metre">newton metre</a>, which works out <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebraically</a> to have the same <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensions</a> as the Joule. But they are not interchangeable. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CGPM" title="CGPM">CGPM</a> has given the unit of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> the name <i>Joule</i>, but has not given the unit of torque any special name, hence it is simply the newton metre (N\xe2\x8b\x85m) \xe2\x80\x93 a compound name derived from its constituent parts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2-5">[5]</a></sup> The use of newton metres for torque and joules for energy is helpful to avoid misunderstandings and miscommunications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIPM2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BIPM2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The distinction may be seen also in the fact that energy is a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(physics)" title="Scalar (physics)">scalar</a> \xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Dot_product" title="Dot product">dot product</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">vector</a> force and a vector displacement. By contrast, torque is a vector \xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Cross_product" title="Cross product">cross product</a> of a distance vector and a force vector.
