showing the large, circular, and symmetric eye characteristic of annular tropical cyclones</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>An <b>annular tropical cyclone</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that features a normal to large, symmetric <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> surrounded by a thick and uniform ring of intense <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a>, often having a relative lack of discrete <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a>. As a result, the appearance of an annular tropical cyclone can be referred to as akin to a truck tire or doughnut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnnularHurricanes_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnnularHurricanes-1">[1]</a></sup> Annular characteristics can be attained as tropical cyclones intensify; however, outside the processes that drive the transition from asymmetric systems to annular systems and the abnormal resistance to negative environmental factors found in storms with annular features, annular tropical cyclones behave similarly to asymmetric storms. Most research related to annular tropical cyclones is limited to <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aircraft_reconnaissance" title="Aircraft reconnaissance">aircraft reconnaissance</a> as the conditions thought to give rise to annular characteristics normally occur over water well removed from landmasses where surface observations are possible.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_and_identification">Characteristics and identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Annular_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics and identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The annular hurricane was first defined as a subset of tropical cyclones by John Knaff of <a href="/wiki/Colorado_State_University" title="Colorado State University">Colorado State University</a> and James Kossin of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin\xe2\x80\x93Madison">University of Wisconsin\xe2\x80\x93Madison</a> in 2002 by use of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> satellite imagery, which serves as the visual means of ascertaining annular characteristics within tropical cyclone. Knaff and Kossin defined an annular tropical cyclone as a tropical cyclone that maintains both average or larger-than-average <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> surrounded by deep <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a> containing the storm\'s inner core and a lack of convection occurring outside the <a href="/wiki/Central_dense_overcast" title="Central dense overcast">central dense overcast</a> for at least three hours. As a result, annular storms lack the <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> characteristic of typical tropical cyclone. These features lend the storm an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axisymmetric" title="Axisymmetric">axisymmetric</a> appearance common to annular tropical cyclones. However, this definition is only applicable while a storm maintains these characteristics\xe2\x80\x94when and while a storm is not feature annular characteristics, the tropical cyclone is considered <i>asymmetric</i>. In addition to the primary defining characteristics, the <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnal</a> pulsation the <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus</a> cloud canopy associated with <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> is subdued once storms become annular.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnnularHurricanes_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-AnnularHurricanes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Although tropical cyclones can achieve annular characteristics across a wide spectrum of intensities, annular storms are typically strong tropical cyclones, with average <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained windspeeds</a> of 108\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kn</a> (200\xc2\xa0km, 124\xc2\xa0mph). In addition, storms attaining annular characteristics are less prone to weakening as a result of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Dissipation" title="Tropical cyclone">negative environmental factors</a>. Annular cyclones can maintain their respective peak intensities for extended periods of time unlike their asymmetric counterparts. Following peak intensity, such systems will tend to gradually taper off. This unusual intensity persistence makes their future intensities difficult to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone forecasting">forecast</a> and often results in large forecast errors. In an analysis of hurricanes in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane" title="Pacific hurricane">East Pacific</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic</a> between 1995 and 1999, Knaff and Kossin observed that the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> underestimated the intensity of annular hurricanes 72\xc2\xa0hours out by 18.9\xc2\xa0kn (35.0\xc2\xa0km, 21.7\xc2\xa0mph).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnnularHurricanes_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-AnnularHurricanes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>An algorithm for identification of annular tropical cyclones in real-time by objective criteria has been developed, and shows some power, but is not yet operational.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-algorithm_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-algorithm-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transition_from_asymmetric_cyclones_and_necessary_conditions">Transition from asymmetric cyclones and necessary conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Annular_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transition from asymmetric cyclones and necessary conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Noru_2017-07-31_0415Z.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6000" data-file-width="4500" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Noru_2017-07-31_0415Z.jpg/220px-Noru_2017-07-31_0415Z.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Noru_2017-07-31_0415Z.jpg/330px-Noru_2017-07-31_0415Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Noru_2017-07-31_0415Z.jpg/440px-Noru_2017-07-31_0415Z.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Noru_2017-07-31_0415Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Noru_(2017)" title="Typhoon Noru (2017)">Typhoon Noru</a> of 2017, displaying all annular characteristics</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Tropical cyclones can become annular as a result of <a href="/wiki/Mesovortices#Eyewall_mesovortices" title="Mesovortices">eyewall mesovortices</a> <a href="/wiki/Entrainment_(meteorology)" title="Entrainment (meteorology)">mixing</a> the strong winds found in the eyewalls of storms with the weak winds of the eye, which helps to expand the eye. In addition, this process helps to make the <a href="/wiki/Equivalent_potential_temperature" title="Equivalent potential temperature">equivalent potential temperature</a> (often referred to as theta-e or <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<msub>\n<mi>\xce\xb8<!-- \xce\xb8 --></mi>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mi>e</mi>\n</mrow>\n</msub>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\theta _{e}}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\theta _{e}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/930299aaf5570dad3cfeb4dbca8d9623e9d739cd" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:2.107ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>) within the eye relatively uniform. This transition takes roughly 24\xc2\xa0hours to complete and can be considered a type of <a href="/wiki/Eyewall_replacement_cycle" title="Eyewall replacement cycle">eyewall replacement cycle</a>. Winds have also been found to decrease in a stairstep like fashion within the <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">radius of maximum wind</a>, which may indicate that more wind is mixed between the eye and eyewall as cyclones strengthen, which helps to explain why annular characteristics are generally exclusive to storms of higher intensities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnnularHurricanes_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-AnnularHurricanes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The intensity of annular systems is typically greater than 83.5%\xc2\xa0of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_potential_intensity" title="Maximum potential intensity">maximum potential intensity</a>, suggesting that the conditions in which storms gain annular characteristics are generally conducive for tropical cyclone persistence. Annular tropical cyclones also require low <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, and of the storms in the East Pacific and North Atlantic studied by Knaff and Kossin, all exhibited easterly winds and cold air in the upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, suggesting that the conditions that give rise to annular tropical cyclones are most optimal towards the equatorward side of a <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> and within the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>. However, warmer <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> (SSTs) are not a required for annular tropical cyclones, with annular characteristics developing only within a narrow range of modest SSTs ranging from 25.4\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C\xe2\x80\x9328.5\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (77.7\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F\xe2\x80\x9383.3\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F).</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
