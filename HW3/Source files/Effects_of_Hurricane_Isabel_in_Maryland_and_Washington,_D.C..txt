among the most damaging from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore-Washington_Metropolitan_Area" title="Baltimore-Washington Metropolitan Area">Baltimore-Washington Metropolitan Area</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> formed from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> on September 6, 2003, in the tropical <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. It moved northwestward, and within an environment of light wind shear and warm waters, it steadily strengthened to reach peak winds of 165\xc2\xa0mph (265\xc2\xa0km/h) on September 11. After fluctuating in intensity for four days, Isabel gradually weakened and made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> with winds of 105\xc2\xa0mph (165\xc2\xa0km/h) on September 18. It quickly weakened over land and became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> over western <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> the next day.</p>\n<p>On September 19, Tropical Storm Isabel passed through extreme western <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, though its large circulation produced tropical storm force winds throughout the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> About 1.24\xc2\xa0million people lost power throughout the state. The worst of its effects came from its <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, which inundated areas along the coast and resulted in severe beach erosion. On the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland" title="Eastern Shore of Maryland">Eastern Shore</a>, hundreds of buildings were damaged or destroyed, primarily in <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne%27s_County,_Maryland" title="Queen Anne\'s County, Maryland">Queen Anne\'s County</a> from tidal flooding. Thousands of houses were affected in Central Maryland, with severe storm surge flooding reported in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annapolis,_Maryland" title="Annapolis, Maryland">Annapolis</a>. Washington, D.C., sustained moderate damage, primarily from the winds. Throughout Maryland and Washington, damage totaled about $945\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($1.23\xc2\xa0billion 2017\xc2\xa0USD),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> with only one fatality due to flooding.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TRC4isabel261_G12.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="948" data-file-width="1280" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/TRC4isabel261_G12.jpg/220px-TRC4isabel261_G12.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/TRC4isabel261_G12.jpg/330px-TRC4isabel261_G12.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/TRC4isabel261_G12.jpg/440px-TRC4isabel261_G12.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TRC4isabel261_G12.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHurricane Isabel shortly after landfall</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>While Isabel was still over the western Atlantic Ocean as a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5 hurricane</a>, forecasters predicted the storm would move northwestward and within five days be at a position 130\xc2\xa0miles (210\xc2\xa0km) east-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_City,_Maryland" title="Ocean City, Maryland">Ocean City, Maryland</a> as a 115\xc2\xa0mph (185\xc2\xa0km/h) major hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawrence32_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-lawrence32-2">[2]</a></sup> By four days before Isabel made landfall, most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" title="Global climate model">computer models</a> predicted Isabel to make landfall between North Carolina and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-avila33_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-avila33-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-archive_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-archive-4">[4]</a></sup> On September 16, about two days before the hurricane struck land, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane watch</a> for the southern region of the Maryland coastline on the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>. Later that day, a tropical storm watch was issued for the eastern Maryland coastline, which was upgraded to a tropical storm warning by about 24\xc2\xa0hours before landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Officials recommended the citizens on <a href="/wiki/Smith_Island,_Maryland" title="Smith Island, Maryland">Smith Island</a> to evacuate on the day prior to the hurricane making landfall, the only evacuations for the Eastern shore of Maryland. Mandatory evacuations were ordered for low-lying and other specific areas in <a href="/wiki/Calvert_County,_Maryland" title="Calvert County, Maryland">Calvert</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._Mary%27s_County,_Maryland" title="St. Mary\'s County, Maryland">St. Mary\'s</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_County,_Maryland" title="Charles County, Maryland">Charles counties</a>, and the residents in six homes were asked to leave in <a href="/wiki/Howard_County,_Maryland" title="Howard County, Maryland">Howard County</a>. Officials recommended residents in flood areas of <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_County,_Maryland" title="Baltimore County, Maryland">Baltimore County</a> to evacuate, and some in the city of Baltimore left spontaneously due to flooding. Evacuation rates were low throughout the Maryland coastline, with the highest rate being in the southernmost counties on the western shore. The primary reason for people staying was due to the track of the hurricane. Between 20% and 33% of those who participated in an evacuation survey said they had concerns about being caught on roadways while trying to evacuate as the storm arrived, with similar numbers expressing concern about being able to return to their homes if they evacuated. Of those who left, most went to the house of a friend or family member, with two-thirds to three-fourths going to destinations in their own neighborhood or county. Most did not evacuate until the day before or the day of landfall. In most areas, the public response to the evacuation orders was normal to fast, with traffic during the evacuation described as light to normal. The most significant problems experienced during the evacuation were downed trees, inadequate signage, flooded roads, or stalled cars. In all, 3,080\xc2\xa0people evacuated their homes in preparation for Hurricane Isabel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assess_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-assess-5">[5]</a></sup> Boats, trucks, and school buses helped more than 1,000\xc2\xa0people evacuate the <a href="/wiki/Inner_Harbor" title="Inner Harbor">Inner Harbor</a> of Baltimore, as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-news-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Washington,_D.C." title="Mayor of Washington, D.C.">mayor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_A._Williams" title="Anthony A. Williams">Anthony Williams</a>, declared a state of emergency for the district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ens_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ens-7">[7]</a></sup> In Washington, preparations began several days prior to the arrival of the hurricane, including placing sandbags in flood-prone areas. Many people, including non-emergency government personnel, were told to stay home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcncdc_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-dcncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Most people in the district took shelter by the time the hurricane passed through the area, and the streets across the city were largely empty. <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_II_of_Jordan" title="Abdullah II of Jordan">Abdullah II of Jordan</a> were evacuated from the city to the presidential retreat at <a href="/wiki/Camp_David" title="Camp David">Camp David</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-919bbc_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-919bbc-9">[9]</a></sup> Most <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> representatives and members of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> also left the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman-10">[10]</a></sup> Many buildings, including some federal, were shut down. All three airports in the Baltimore-Washington Metropolitan Area were closed, with flights into the capital cancelled or diverted. Many train routes were canceled or delayed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-919bbc_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-919bbc-9">[9]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Metro" title="Washington Metro">Washington Metro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metrobus_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="Metrobus (Washington, D.C.)">Metrobus</a> systems were closed entirely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WMATA_shutdown_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-WMATA_shutdown-11">[11]</a></sup> Workers at the <a href="/wiki/National_Zoological_Park_(United_States)" title="National Zoological Park (United States)">National Zoo</a> prepared by moving animals inside and lowering water levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scotsman_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Scotsman-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Shore">Eastern Shore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eastern Shore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="878" data-file-width="992" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/220px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/330px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/440px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nRainfall from Hurricane Isabel</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>On the Eastern Shore of Maryland, Hurricane Isabel produced a storm surge peaking at 8\xc2\xa0feet (2.4\xc2\xa0m) on the Chesapeake Bay in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_islands_in_Maryland" title="List of islands in Maryland">Hoopers Island</a> and 6.5\xc2\xa0feet (2\xc2\xa0m) on the Atlantic coast in Ocean City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wakefield_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-wakefield-12">[12]</a></sup> The track of the hurricane to the west funneled into the bay and was so strong it negated the normal tide cycle in the bay. <a href="/wiki/Kent_County,_Maryland" title="Kent County, Maryland">Tolchester Beach</a> recorded a record-breaking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Storm_tide" title="Storm tide">high tide</a> of 7.91\xc2\xa0feet (2.4\xc2\xa0m) above mean low water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esncdc_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-esncdc-13">[13]</a></sup> Rainfall in the Maryland portion of the <a href="/wiki/Delmarva_Peninsula" title="Delmarva Peninsula">Delmarva Peninsula</a> reached 3.2\xc2\xa0inches (81\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Vienna,_Maryland" title="Vienna, Maryland">Steeles Neck</a>. The large circulation of the hurricane produced strong winds across the area, including maximum sustained winds of 52\xc2\xa0mph (84\xc2\xa0km/h) and a gust of 66\xc2\xa0mph (106\xc2\xa0km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Maryland" title="Cambridge, Maryland">Cambridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wakefield_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-wakefield-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Tidal flooding from the storm surge forced residents to evacuate near the bay. Tidal flooding was reported in and around <a href="/wiki/Rock_Hall,_Maryland" title="Rock Hall, Maryland">Rock Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chestertown,_Maryland" title="Chestertown, Maryland">Chestertown</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Skinners_Neck,_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Skinners Neck, Maryland (page does not exist)">Skinners Neck</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Piney_Neck,_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Piney Neck, Maryland (page does not exist)">Piney Neck</a>, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cliffs_City,_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cliffs City, Maryland (page does not exist)">Cliffs City</a>, and was carried along the <a href="/wiki/Chester_River" title="Chester River">Chester River</a> slightly inland. The flooding destroyed or damaged over 100 homes, vehicles, and boats, and also destroyed the <a href="/wiki/Romancoke,_Maryland" title="Romancoke, Maryland">Romancoke</a> pier, one of the pre-<a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay_Bridge" title="Chesapeake Bay Bridge">Bay Bridge</a> <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> landings which was still in use as a fishing pier at the time. The flooding also severely damaged eleven marinas, three restaurants, four hotels, one boat-building business, and one marine railway. In Queen Anne\'s County, the hurricane destroyed 37\xc2\xa0homes, greatly damaged 151, and moderately damaged 192, with damage totaling $37\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($48.2\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD). The damage was mainly due to the tidal flooding, though four homes were destroyed due to falling trees. Moderate wind gusts knocked down numerous trees, tree limbs, and power lines, leaving about 65,750\xc2\xa0homes and businesses without power, half of which occurred in <a href="/wiki/Cecil_County,_Maryland" title="Cecil County, Maryland">Cecil County</a>. The resulting power outage was described as one of the worst in the history of <a href="/wiki/Pepco_Holdings" title="Pepco Holdings">Conectiv Energy</a>. Damage in the Eastern Shore of Maryland totaled about $50\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($65.1\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esncdc_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-esncdc-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Maryland">Central Maryland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Central Maryland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Wind gusts in central Maryland peaked at 83\xc2\xa0mph (134\xc2\xa0km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Silver_Spring,_Maryland" title="Silver Spring, Maryland">Silver Spring</a>, with a location along the <a href="/wiki/Patuxent_River" title="Patuxent River">Patuxent River</a> recording sustained winds of 55\xc2\xa0mph (88\xc2\xa0km/h). Unofficially, rainfall reached 7\xc2\xa0inches (178\xc2\xa0mm) in portions of Maryland, though the highest precipitation amount totaled 3.21\xc2\xa0inches (82\xc2\xa0mm) at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore-Washington_International_Thurgood_Marshall_Airport" title="Baltimore-Washington International Thurgood Marshall Airport">Baltimore-Washington International Airport</a>. Swells from the hurricane produced high storm surges along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Shore" title="Western Shore">Western Shore</a> which peaked at 8.2\xc2\xa0feet (2.5\xc2\xa0m) in Baltimore. Annapolis also reported a surge of 7.2\xc2\xa0feet (2.2\xc2\xa0m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> In many locations, the storm surge was higher than the previous record set by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1933_Chesapeake_Potomac_Hurricane" title="1933 Chesapeake Potomac Hurricane">1933 Chesapeake Potomac Hurricane</a>. Throughout central Maryland, 472\xc2\xa0buildings and homes were destroyed, with 3,260 greatly damaged and another 3,600 affected in general. Strong storm surge from the hurricane washed 43,000\xc2\xa0tons (39,000 t) of salt, millions of pounds of nitrogen and phosphorus, and millions of gallons of raw sewage into the Chesapeake Bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup> One person died as a result of a traffic death in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scot_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-scot-15">[15]</a></sup> Property damage totaled $530.5\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($691\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD), with an additional $190,000 (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($247\xc2\xa0thousand 2017\xc2\xa0USD) in crop damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup> The crop damage was primarily limited to minor damage to livestock buildings and sheds, though the winds destroyed a hay barn and a dairy barn at one farm in <a href="/wiki/Frederick_County,_Maryland" title="Frederick County, Maryland">Frederick County</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> estimated the winds of the hurricane destroyed 15\xe2\x80\x9330% of the field corn and 10\xe2\x80\x9315% of the soybean crop. Excess amounts of water led to the closure of many crab processing plants and crab house restaurants, and officials restricted harvest on shellfish for eleven days due to unsafe impurities in the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agri_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-agri-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Most coastal areas under 10\xc2\xa0feet (3\xc2\xa0m) above sea level experienced severe wave damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup> The Maryland Geological Survey estimates the waves removed 20 acres (8.1\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">ha</a>) of sand along the coastline, based on an estimate of losses in Baltimore County. The 20 acres (81,000\xc2\xa0m<sup>2</sup>) of lost in single day represent about 15% of the total beach loss in an average year. The average location lost about 5\xc2\xa0feet (1.5\xc2\xa0m) of beach, and throughout the Chesapeake Bay the erosion deposited about 81,000\xc2\xa0tons (73,500\xc2\xa0t) of sediment into the bay. The erosion was irregular, however; some locations were unaffected, while others experienced greater losses, despite that the two locations were similar. The erosion and storm surge damaged many bulkheads, and after the storm surge retreated into the bay it washed away freestanding structures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erosion_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-erosion-17">[17]</a></sup> The passage of Isabel resulted in an unusual increase in <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> in the middle portion of the Chesapeake Bay, followed by an abrupt return to normal conditions by early October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-plankton_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-plankton-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_by_county">Effects by county</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effects by county">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Isab_damage_bridge.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="476" data-file-width="735" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Isab_damage_bridge.JPG/220px-Isab_damage_bridge.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Isab_damage_bridge.JPG/330px-Isab_damage_bridge.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Isab_damage_bridge.JPG/440px-Isab_damage_bridge.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Isab_damage_bridge.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nDamage to the ferry bridge in Hoopersvile, Maryland</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Six foot (2\xc2\xa0m) waves along the coastline of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Mary%27s_County,_Maryland" title="Saint Mary\'s County, Maryland">Saint Mary\'s County</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Lookout,_Maryland" title="Point Lookout, Maryland">Point Lookout State Park</a> suffering $3\xc2\xa0million in damages (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($4.3\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD). In the county, 2,500\xc2\xa0wharves and piers were destroyed, with another 1,000\xc2\xa0moderately damaged, resulting in $10.25\xc2\xa0million in monetary damage (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($13.3\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD). The storm surge destroyed the bridge to <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Saint_George_Island_(Maryland)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Saint George Island (Maryland) (page does not exist)">Saint George Island</a> by a few hours after the hurricane made landfall, and the flooding covered much of the island for a week. There, 20\xc2\xa0homes were destroyed. Shore <a href="/wiki/Revetment" title="Revetment">revetments</a> experienced $53.4\xc2\xa0million in damage (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($81.4\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD). Throughout Saint Mary\'s County, residential damage amounted to about $17\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($242\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD) with about $1.5\xc2\xa0million to commercial buildings (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($1.95\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD). The damage was caused primarily by the storm surge, though the winds produced minor to moderate damage, the worst effects in the state from winds. In the county, the winds blew off the roof of a home and downed 70\xc2\xa0trees, leaving 27,092 without power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In Charles County, the hurricane resulted in about $2\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($2.6\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD) in damage to roads. Two homes were destroyed with several others damaged on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cobb_Island,_Maryland" title="Cobb Island, Maryland">Cobb Island</a>. Elsewhere in the county, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chigger_City,_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chigger City, Maryland (page does not exist)">Chigger City</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Tobacco,_Maryland" title="Port Tobacco, Maryland">Port Tobacco</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Woodland_Point,_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Woodland Point, Maryland (page does not exist)">Woodland Point</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swan_Point,_Maryland" title="Swan Point, Maryland">Swan Point</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Morgantown,_Maryland" title="Morgantown, Maryland">Morgantown</a> also reported damage from Hurricane Isabel, with 52\xc2\xa0houses damaged<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-4"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup> and eight homes destroyed or severely damaged. Seven businesses were also destroyed or greatly damaged. Many piers along the shore were damaged or lost, and two private community wells were contaminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-char_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-char-19">[19]</a></sup> The winds downed several trees and power lines, leaving about 32,000\xc2\xa0people without power. Residential damage totaled $1.1\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, $1.2\xc2\xa0million 2006\xc2\xa0USD), with another $600,000 (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($781\xc2\xa0thousand 2017\xc2\xa0USD) to commercial buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-5"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Four to five\xc2\xa0foot (1.2 to 1.5\xc2\xa0m) waves hit the eastern portion of Calvert County, causing a home to be knocked off its foundation in <a href="/wiki/North_Beach,_Maryland" title="North Beach, Maryland">North Beach</a>. The storm surge destroyed the pier and greatly damaged buildings along the shoreline at <a href="/wiki/Solomons,_Maryland" title="Solomons, Maryland">Solomons Island</a>. Power was lost to 22,400\xc2\xa0residents as a result of the passage of the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-6"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The storm surge produced higher than usual tides in along the Patuxent, <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anacostia_River" title="Anacostia River">Anacostia River</a> coastlines. Much of the shoreline of <a href="/wiki/Prince_George%27s_County,_Maryland" title="Prince George\'s County, Maryland">Prince George\'s County</a> possessed adequate rise of terrain to prevent significant flooding, though the high waters closed three roads where the shoreline was not high enough. The passage of the hurricane created about 5,000\xc2\xa0tons (4,500\xc2\xa0t) of debris to be removed. Fifteen buildings sustained major damage, with another 53\xc2\xa0buildings affected to some degree. Nearly 200,000 residents in the county lost power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-7"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup> When two of the largest sewage treatment plants in the state lost power, 96\xc2\xa0million US gallons (360,000\xc2\xa0m<sup>3</sup>) of hazardous sewage overflowed to mix with storm water into two waterways in the county. The wastewater combined with rainwater, making it less threatening. No humans or animals were known to have become sick due to the overflows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pgc_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-pgc-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In Annapolis in <a href="/wiki/Anne_Arundel_County,_Maryland" title="Anne Arundel County, Maryland">Anne Arundel County</a>, the storm surge surrounded many of the buildings at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">United States Naval Academy</a>, resulting in about $116\xc2\xa0million in damage (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($151\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-8"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup> The Annapolis Maritime Museum suffered severe damage as well, though volunteers moved artifacts to a safer location. There, the storm surge flooded the building in 6\xc2\xa0feet (1.8\xc2\xa0m) of water, causing great damage to the floor, drywall, and electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ama_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-ama-21">[21]</a></sup> About 93% of the 211,000 power customers in the county were left without electricity after the storm. <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">FEMA</a> estimated a damage total of up to $500\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($651\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD) in the county.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-9"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Moderate wave action produced severe beach erosion along the shore of Baltimore County at a loss of $3\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($3.91\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD)). Residential areas of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Millers_Island,_Maryland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Millers Island, Maryland (page does not exist)">Millers Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edgemere,_Maryland" title="Edgemere, Maryland">Edgemere</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Point,_Maryland" title="North Point, Maryland">North Point</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bowleys_Quarters,_Maryland" title="Bowleys Quarters, Maryland">Bowleys Quarters</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turners_Station,_Maryland" title="Turners Station, Maryland">Turners Station</a> suffered severe damage from the hurricane, with over 400\xc2\xa0people needing to be rescued. Over 300\xc2\xa0buildings were destroyed. The storm surge flooding killed a man in <a href="/wiki/Dundalk,_Maryland" title="Dundalk, Maryland">Dundalk</a>, and either destroyed or greatly damaged most marinas. Strong winds downed 118\xc2\xa0trees on school grounds and hundreds of others elsewhere, some of which fell on power lines which resulted in leaving 220,000\xc2\xa0customers without power. The passage of Hurricane Isabel left 3,189\xc2\xa0tons (2,893\xc2\xa0t) of debris across the county.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-10"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The storm surge flooded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fells_Point,_Baltimore" title="Fells Point, Baltimore">Fells Point</a> and the Inner Harbor of Baltimore, causing millions of dollars of damage to waterfront properties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-11"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup> As the Inner Harbor began to flood, boat owners helped evacuate 35\xc2\xa0people in flooded homes. Winds from Isabel destroyed at least three buildings in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-news-6">[6]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Museum_of_Industry" title="Baltimore Museum of Industry">Baltimore Museum of Industry</a> alone suffered $1.5\xc2\xa0million in damage (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($1.95\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD). 70,000 were left without power in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-12"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Harford_County,_Maryland" title="Harford County, Maryland">Harford County</a>, high waves and the storm surge caused moderate flooding in areas, forcing 55 to evacuate along the <a href="/wiki/Bush_River_(Maryland)" title="Bush River (Maryland)">Bush River</a> and 12 to evacuate along the waterfront to <a href="/wiki/Havre_de_Grace,_Maryland" title="Havre de Grace, Maryland">Havre de Grace</a>. The promenade boardwalk in Havre de Grace was destroyed. Moderate winds left 51,600\xc2\xa0residents in the county without power. Damage in the county totaled at least $12.5\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($16.3\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-13"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Maryland">Western Maryland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Western Maryland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The center of Tropical Storm Isabel passed over <a href="/wiki/Garrett_County,_Maryland" title="Garrett County, Maryland">Garrett County</a> on September 19, with its unusually large and powerful wind field producing 50 to 60\xc2\xa0mph (80 to 95\xc2\xa0km/h) wind gusts across western Maryland. Some <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> produced up to 70\xc2\xa0mph (110\xc2\xa0km/h) gusts (113\xc2\xa0km/h), and in some areas isolated streaks of damage were described as being caused by tornadoes. Rainfall varied between 2 and 4\xc2\xa0inches (50 to 100\xc2\xa0mm) across the area, resulting in the Potomac River overflowing its banks. At <a href="/wiki/Point_of_Rocks,_Maryland" title="Point of Rocks, Maryland">Point of Rocks</a>, the river crested at 23.12\xc2\xa0feet (7\xc2\xa0m), 7.12\xc2\xa0feet (2.2\xc2\xa0m) above <a href="/wiki/Flood_stage" title="Flood stage">flood stage</a>. Moderate flooding occurred there, and minor flooding occurred in <a href="/wiki/Paw_Paw,_West_Virginia" title="Paw Paw, West Virginia">Paw Paw, West Virginia</a> where the river crested at 2.91\xc2\xa0feet (0.9\xc2\xa0m). The flooding closed a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_and_Ohio_Canal" title="Chesapeake and Ohio Canal">Chesapeake and Ohio Canal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmdncdc_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-wmdncdc-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The gusty winds from the passage of the hurricane resulted in widespread and extensive tree damage. Due to previous rainfall, moist grounds easily allowed for trees to be uprooted. Wind damage to structures was minor, however, with one house in <a href="/wiki/Carroll_County,_Maryland" title="Carroll County, Maryland">Carroll County</a> being damaged when a tree fell on it. Trees fell onto cars, roads, homes, and power lines, leaving 358,000 without power after the storm. 600\xc2\xa0traffic signals were without power in <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_County,_Maryland" title="Montgomery County, Maryland">Montgomery County</a>, and about 70\xc2\xa0roads were closed due to fallen trees. Isabel destroyed 94\xc2\xa0buildings and damaged 87, including 33\xc2\xa0homes, six of which were severely impacted with one losing a portion of its roof. Fallen trees injured two in Frederick County. In western Maryland, damage totaled $2.07\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($2.69\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmdncdc_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-wmdncdc-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="Washington,_D.C."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Washington.2C_D.C.">Washington, D.C.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Washington, D.C.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Washington_D.C._Damage_(Isabel).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1488" data-file-width="2240" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Washington_D.C._Damage_%28Isabel%29.jpg/220px-Washington_D.C._Damage_%28Isabel%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Washington_D.C._Damage_%28Isabel%29.jpg/330px-Washington_D.C._Damage_%28Isabel%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Washington_D.C._Damage_%28Isabel%29.jpg/440px-Washington_D.C._Damage_%28Isabel%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Washington_D.C._Damage_(Isabel).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTree and power line damage in Washington, D.C.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In Washington, D.C., sustained winds remained below tropical storm force, though gusts reached 71\xc2\xa0mph (114\xc2\xa0km/h) as recorded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Science</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcr_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Hurricane-force winds existed several hundred feet in the air, with wind sensors on the top of tall buildings recording winds of 70 to 85\xc2\xa0mph (115 to 135\xc2\xa0km/h). Rainfall was minimal, amounting to around two inches across the district. A gauge on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Avenue_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="Wisconsin Avenue (Washington, D.C.)">Wisconsin Avenue</a> recorded a storm surge of 11.3\xc2\xa0feet (3.4\xc2\xa0m), and a gauge on a pier in the southwest portion of the district recorded a storm surge of 10.25\xc2\xa0feet (3.1\xc2\xa0m). Both observations were records, surpassing the previous observation set by the 1933 Chesapeake Potomac Hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcncdc_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-dcncdc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Moderate winds blew down about 1,600\xc2\xa0trees in the district,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-femtre_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-femtre-23">[23]</a></sup> 23 of which fell onto houses. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Arboretum" title="United States National Arboretum">United States National Arboretum</a> lost many mature trees and azaleas on the bank of the Anacostia River. The winds also downed many power lines, leaving 129,000\xc2\xa0customers without power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcncdc_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-dcncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Many streets were blocked by the fallen trees and power lines. At the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>, the winds downed one tree in the front garden, though there was no damage to the building itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-919bbc_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-919bbc-9">[9]</a></sup> The storm surge resulted in flooding at the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Navy_Yard" title="Washington Navy Yard">Washington Navy Yard</a>. There, the marina was damaged, and some buildings and cars in low area garages were flooded. Five people were rescued from cars in flood waters during the storm, and several roads were closed due to the flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcncdc_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-dcncdc-8">[8]</a></sup> Throughout the district, the passage of Isabel created approximately 30,000\xc2\xa0pounds (13,600\xc2\xa0kg) of debris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-femtre_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-femtre-23">[23]</a></sup> Damage in Washington, D.C., totaled $125\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($163\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dcncdc_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-dcncdc-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Power crews restored 75% of the power outages in the Eastern Shore of Maryland by two days after the hurricane struck, and by one week after the hurricane the power was restored completely to the Eastern Shore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esncdc_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-esncdc-13">[13]</a></sup> Buildings in Washington, D.C., remained closed for two days after the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-919bbc_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-919bbc-9">[9]</a></sup> After the hurricane, several people in central and western Maryland were injured with three killed from <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> due to improperly running generators in their houses. Additionally, others were injured due to chain saws and cleaning up debris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-14"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmdncdc_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-wmdncdc-22">[22]</a></sup> In the days after the hurricane, the Calvert County Hospital reported an extra 130 patients per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midncdc_14-15"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The strong winds and storm surge of the hurricane dislodged and damaged a large number of home heating oil tanks, releasing thousands of gallons of oil onto yards and into the environment. The Maryland Department of the Environment and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> worked together to recover more than 50,000 US gallons (190,000\xc2\xa0L) of oil from the tanks. Five hundred properties were contaminated by the oil, causing the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">Federal Emergency Management Agency</a> to provide 75% of the funding for cleaning the properties, with the government of Maryland providing for the remainder of the cost.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mde1_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-mde1-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On September 19, 2003, President George W. Bush declared the entire state of Maryland as a disaster area, which allowed residents affected by the hurricane to apply for federal aid. FEMA officials urged residents and business owners with significant damage to call as soon as possible, while those with minimal damage were asked to wait for a few days. All residents in the state who received damage from the storm had sixty days to apply for assistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema1-25">[25]</a></sup> Federal and state officials opened disaster recovery centers in Annapolis, Baltimore, <a href="/wiki/Essex,_Maryland" title="Essex, Maryland">Essex</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Frederick,_Maryland" title="Prince Frederick, Maryland">Prince Frederick</a> five days after the hurricane passed through the area, with the centers designed to provide more information to those who applied for federal assistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema2_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema2-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema3_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema3-27">[27]</a></sup> About six days after Isabel made landfall, additional disaster recovery centers opened in Rock Hall in Kent County,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema4_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema4-28">[28]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/California,_Maryland" title="California, Maryland">California</a> in Saint Mary\'s County,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema5_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema5-29">[29]</a></sup> Silver Spring in Montgomery County,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema6_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema6-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Chester,_Maryland" title="Chester, Maryland">Chester</a> in <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne%27s_County,_Maryland" title="Queen Anne\'s County, Maryland">Queen Anne\'s County</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema7_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema7-31">[31]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Crisfield,_Maryland" title="Crisfield, Maryland">Crisfield</a> in <a href="/wiki/Somerset_County,_Maryland" title="Somerset County, Maryland">Somerset County</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema8_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema8-32">[32]</a></sup> Cambridge in <a href="/wiki/Dorchester_County,_Maryland" title="Dorchester County, Maryland">Dorchester County</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema9_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema9-33">[33]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Easton,_Maryland" title="Easton, Maryland">Easton</a> in <a href="/wiki/Talbot_County,_Maryland" title="Talbot County, Maryland">Talbot County</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fema10_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-fema10-34">[34]</a></sup> Around a month after the hurricane, officials placed some residents unable to live in their damaged homes into travel trailers because they had no options for alternate housing, such as staying with a relative or friend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-femtem_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-femtem-35">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On September 26, the affected residents began receiving housing assistance grants, totaling $4.3\xc2\xa0million in aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fem_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-fem-36">[36]</a></sup> By a month after the hurricane passed through the area, disaster aid amounted to just over $30\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($39.1\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fem2_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-fem2-37">[37]</a></sup> Ultimately, aid in the state totalled just under $100\xc2\xa0million ($130\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD). Business owners applied for 14,595\xc2\xa0loans and received $53.4\xc2\xa0million ($69.5\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD) in low interest loans from the <a href="/wiki/Small_Business_Administration" title="Small Business Administration">Small Business Administration</a>. The state also received more than $16.9\xc2\xa0million in funds (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($22\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD) to rebuild infrastructure damaged by Isabel. 17,855\xc2\xa0residents applied to receive more than $20.8\xc2\xa0million ($27.1\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD) in housing assistance, and $8\xc2\xa0million (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($10.4\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD) in assistance for other needs such as personal property, transportation and medical expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-femtat_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-femtat-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<p>President Bush also declared Washington, D.C., as a disaster area two days after Isabel passed through the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-femdc_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-femdc-39">[39]</a></sup> The United States Department of Agriculture approved a request for an emergency <a href="/wiki/Supplemental_Nutrition_Assistance_Program" title="Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program">food stamp</a> program for two weeks for the city to ensure those who lost food as a result of the hurricane would not go hungry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-food_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-food-40">[40]</a></sup> By eight days after the hurricane, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">Department of Employment Services</a> created disaster unemployment benefits for those who lost their work as a direct result of Isabel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-job_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-job-41">[41]</a></sup> By 11\xc2\xa0days after Isabel, the government approved $5.21\xc2\xa0million in disaster assistance (2003\xc2\xa0USD, $6.78\xc2\xa0million 2017\xc2\xa0USD). The U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">Army Corps of Engineers</a> delivered nearly 800,000\xc2\xa0pounds (363,000\xc2\xa0kg) of dry ice to four distribution sites for use with cold food storage to areas in the District suffering from power outages. Various groups joined to provide more than 15,000\xc2\xa0meals for residents who lost stored food due to spoilage resulting from the power outages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fud_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-fud-42">[42]</a></sup> Volunteers assisted in distributing 515\xc2\xa0tons (467\xc2\xa0t) of ice, along with 21,000 sandbags throughout the district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-volun_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-volun-43">[43]</a></sup> Officials opened two disaster recovery centers which moved through the neighborhoods of the district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drcdc_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-drcdc-44">[44]</a></sup> Ultimately, nearly 1,500\xc2\xa0residents in the district applied for federal assistance, with FEMA providing the residents more than $600,000 in aid (2003\xc2\xa0USD, ($781\xc2\xa0thousand 2017\xc2\xa0USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dc_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-dc-45">[45]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
