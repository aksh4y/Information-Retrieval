As density is related to temperature, on a surface map, isobars and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line)" title="Isotherm (contour line)">isotherms</a> cross too</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>, the <b>baroclinity</b> (often called <b>baroclinicity</b>) of a stratified fluid is a measure of how misaligned the gradient of pressure is from the gradient of density in a fluid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> a baroclinic atmosphere is one for which the density depends on both the temperature and the pressure; contrast this with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barotropic" title="Barotropic">barotropic</a> atmosphere, for which the density depends only on the pressure. In atmospheric terms, the barotropic zones of the Earth are generally found in the central latitudes, or <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, whereas the baroclinic areas are generally found in the mid-latitude/polar regions.</p>\n<p>Baroclinity is proportional to</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi mathvariant="normal">\xe2\x88\x87<!-- \xe2\x88\x87 --></mi>\n<mi>p</mi>\n<mo>\xc3\x97<!-- \xc3\x97 --></mo>\n<mi mathvariant="normal">\xe2\x88\x87<!-- \xe2\x88\x87 --></mi>\n<mi>\xcf\x81<!-- \xcf\x81 --></mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle \\nabla p\\times \\nabla \\rho }</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="\\nabla p\\times \\nabla \\rho " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1588caabcbe3b6ae8f64ea326297598ccea3e67e" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:9.136ex; height:2.676ex;"/></span></dd>\n</dl>\n<p>which is proportional to the sine of the angle between surfaces of constant <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> and surfaces of constant <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>. Thus, in a <i>barotropic</i> fluid (which is defined by zero baroclinity), these surfaces are parallel.</p>\n<p>Areas of high atmospheric baroclinity are characterized by the frequent formation of <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baroclinic_instability">Baroclinic instability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baroclinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Baroclinic instability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Baroclinic instability is a fluid dynamical instability of fundamental importance in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceans" title="Oceans">oceans</a>. In the atmosphere it is the dominant mechanism shaping the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclones</a> that dominate <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> in mid-latitudes. In the ocean it generates a field of <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> (100\xc2\xa0km or smaller) <a href="/wiki/Eddy_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Eddy (fluid dynamics)">eddies</a> that play various roles in oceanic dynamics and the transport of <a href="/wiki/Flow_tracer" title="Flow tracer">tracers</a>. Baroclinic instability is a concept relevant to <i>rapidly rotating</i>, <i>strongly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_stratification" title="Atmospheric stratification">stratified</a></i> fluids.</p>\n<p>Whether a fluid counts as <i>rapidly rotating</i> is determined in this context by the <a href="/wiki/Rossby_number" title="Rossby number">Rossby number</a>, which is a measure of how close the flow is to solid body rotation. More precisely, a flow in solid body rotation has <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> that is proportional to its <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular velocity</a>. The Rossby number is a measure of the departure of the vorticity from that of solid body rotation. The Rossby number must be small for the concept of baroclinic instability to be relevant. When the Rossby number is large, other kinds of instabilities, often referred to as inertial, become more relevant.</p>\n<p>The simplest example of a stably stratified flow is an incompressible flow with density decreasing with height. In a compressible gas such as the atmosphere, the relevant measure is the vertical gradient of the <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>, which must increase with height for the flow to be stably stratified. One measures the strength of the stratification by asking how large the vertical shear of the horizontal winds has to be in order to destabilize the flow and produce the classic <a href="/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_instability" title="Kelvin\xe2\x80\x93Helmholtz instability">Kelvin\xe2\x80\x93Helmholtz instability</a>. This measure is the <a href="/wiki/Richardson_number" title="Richardson number">Richardson number</a>. When the Richardson number is large, the stratification is strong enough to prevent this shear instability.</p>\n<p>Before the classic work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jule_Charney" title="Jule Charney">Jule Charney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eric_Eady" title="Eric Eady">Eric Eady</a> on baroclinic instability in the late 1940s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> most theories trying to explain the structure of mid-latitude eddies took as their starting points the high Rossby number or small Richardson number instabilities familiar to fluid dynamicists at that time. The most important feature of baroclinic instability is that it exists even in the situation of rapid rotation (small Rossby number) and strong stable stratification (large Richardson\'s number) typically observed in the atmosphere.</p>\n<p>The energy source for baroclinic instability is the <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a> in the environmental flow. As the instability grows, the <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a> of the fluid is lowered. In growing waves in the atmosphere, cold air moving downwards and equatorwards displaces the warmer air moving polewards and upwards.</p>\n<p>Baroclinic instability can be investigated in the laboratory using a rotating, fluid filled <a href="/wiki/Annulus_(mathematics)" title="Annulus (mathematics)">annulus</a>. The annulus is heated at the outer wall and cooled at the inner wall, and the resulting fluid flows give rise to baroclinically unstable waves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The term "baroclinic" refers to the mechanism by which <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> is generated. Vorticity is the curl of the velocity field. In general, the evolution of vorticity can be broken into contributions from advection (as vortex tubes move with the flow), <a href="/wiki/Vortex_stretching" title="Vortex stretching">stretching</a> and twisting (as vortex tubes are pulled or twisted by the flow) and baroclinic vorticity generation, which occurs whenever there is a density gradient along surfaces of constant pressure. Baroclinic flows can be contrasted with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barotropic" title="Barotropic">barotropic</a> flows in which density and pressure surfaces coincide and there is no baroclinic generation of vorticity.</p>\n<p>The study of the evolution of these baroclinic instabilities as they grow and then decay is a crucial part of developing theories for the fundamental characteristics of midlatitude weather.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baroclinic_vector">Baroclinic vector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baroclinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Baroclinic vector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Beginning with the equation of motion for a frictionless fluid (the <a href="/wiki/Euler_equations_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Euler equations (fluid dynamics)">Euler equations</a>) and taking the curl, one arrives at the <a href="/wiki/Vorticity_equation" title="Vorticity equation">equation of motion for the curl of the fluid velocity</a>, that is to say, the <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a>.</p>\n<p>In a fluid that is not all of the same density, a source term appears in the <a href="/wiki/Vorticity_equation" title="Vorticity equation">vorticity equation</a> whenever surfaces of constant density (<a href="/wiki/Isopycnic" title="Isopycnic">isopycnic</a> surfaces) and surfaces of constant pressure (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/isobaric" title="wikt:isobaric">isobaric</a> surfaces) are not aligned. Internal <a href="/wiki/Gravity_wave" title="Gravity wave">gravity waves</a> as well as unstable Rayleigh\xe2\x80\x93Taylor modes can be analyzed from the perspective of the baroclinic vector. It is also of interest in the creation of vorticity by the passage of shocks through inhomogeneous media, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Richtmyer%E2%80%93Meshkov_instability" title="Richtmyer\xe2\x80\x93Meshkov instability">Richtmyer\xe2\x80\x93Meshkov instability</a>.</p>\n<p>Divers may be familiar with the very slow waves that can be excited at a thermocline or a halocline; these are internal waves. Similar waves can be generated between a layer of water and a layer of oil. When the interface between these two surfaces is not horizontal and the system is close to hydrostatic equilibrium, the gradient of the pressure is vertical but the gradient of the density is not. Therefore the baroclinic vector is nonzero, and the sense of the baroclinic vector is to create vorticity to make the interface level out. In the process, the interface overshoots, and the result is an oscillation which is an internal gravity wave. Unlike surface gravity waves, internal gravity waves do not require a sharp interface. For example, in bodies of water, a gradual gradient in temperature or salinity is sufficient to support internal gravity waves driven by the baroclinic vector.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baroclinity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Marshall, J., and R.A.
