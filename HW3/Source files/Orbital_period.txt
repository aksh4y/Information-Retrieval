The name <i>sidereal</i> is added as it implies that the object returns to the same position relative to the <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">fixed stars</a> <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">projected in the sky</a>. When describing orbits of binary stars, the orbital period is usually referred to as just the <b>period</b>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> has a sidereal period of 11.86 years while the main binary star <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Centauri" title="Alpha Centauri">Alpha Centauri</a> AB has a period of about 79.91 years.</p>\n<p>Another important orbital period definition can refer to the repeated cycles for celestial bodies as observed from the Earth\'s surface. An example is the so-called <b>synodic period</b>, applying to the elapsed time where planets return to the same kind of phenomena or location. For example, when any planet returns between its consecutive observed <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy)" title="Conjunction (astronomy)">conjunctions</a> with or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposition_(astronomy)" title="Opposition (astronomy)">oppositions</a> to the Sun. For example, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> has a synodic period of 398.8 days from Earth; thus, Jupiter\'s opposition occurs once roughly every 13 months.</p>\n<p>Periods in astronomy are conveniently expressed in various units of time, often in hours, days, or years. They can be also defined under different specific astronomical definitions that are mostly caused by small complex eternal gravitational influences by other celestial objects. Such variations also include the true placement of the centre of gravity between two astronomical bodies (<a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">barycenter</a>), <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">perturbations</a> by other planets or bodies, <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">orbital resonance</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">general relativity</a>, etc. Most are investigated by detailed complex astronomical theories using <a href="/wiki/Celestial_mechanics" title="Celestial mechanics">celestial mechanics</a> using precise positional observations of celestial objects via <a href="/wiki/Astrometry" title="Astrometry">astrometry</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="Other_periods_related_to_the_orbital_period">Other periods related to the orbital period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbital_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Other periods related to the orbital period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are many <a href="/wiki/Periodic_function" title="Periodic function">periods</a> related to the orbits of objects, each of which are often used in the various fields of <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a>. Examples of some of the common ones include\xc2\xa0:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>The <b>sidereal period</b> is the amount of time that it takes an object to make a full orbit, relative to the <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">stars</a>. This is the orbital period in an inertial (non-rotating) <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">frame of reference</a>.</li>\n<li>The <b>synodic period</b> is the amount of time that it takes for an object to reappear at the same point in relation to two or more other objects (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>\'s phase and its position relative to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> repeats every 29.5 day synodic period, longer than its 27.3 day orbit around the Earth, due to the motion of the Earth about the Sun). The time between two successive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposition_(astronomy)" title="Opposition (astronomy)">oppositions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy)" title="Conjunction (astronomy)">conjunctions</a> is also an example of the synodic period. For the planets in the solar system, the synodic period (with respect to Earth) differs from the sidereal period due to the Earth\'s orbiting around the Sun.</li>\n<li>The <b>draconitic period</b>, or <b>draconic period</b>, is the time that elapses between two passages of the object through its <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">ascending node</a>, the point of its orbit where it crosses the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a> from the southern to the northern hemisphere. This period differs from the sidereal period because both the orbital plane of the object and the plane of the ecliptic precess with respect to the fixed stars, so their intersection, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_of_nodes" title="Line of nodes">line of nodes</a>, also precesses with respect to the fixed stars. Although the plane of the ecliptic is often held fixed at the position it occupied at a specific <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy)" title="Epoch (astronomy)">epoch</a>, the orbital plane of the object still precesses causing the draconitic period to differ from the sidereal period.</li>\n<li>The <b>anomalistic period</b> is the time that elapses between two passages of an object at its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">periapsis</a> (in the case of the planets in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">solar system</a>, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a>), the point of its closest approach to the attracting body. It differs from the sidereal period because the object\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> typically advances slowly.</li>\n<li>Also, the Earth\'s <b>tropical period</b> (or simply its "year") is the time that elapses between two alignments of its axis of rotation with the Sun, also viewed as two passages of the object at <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">right ascension</a> zero. One Earth year has a slightly shorter interval than the solar orbit (sidereal period) because the inclined axis and equatorial plane slowly <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precess</a> (rotate with respect to the stars), realigning with the Sun before the orbit completes. The Earth\'s precession cycle completes in about 25,770 years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
