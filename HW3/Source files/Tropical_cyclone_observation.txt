class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Tropicalcyclone" title="Template:Tropicalcyclone"><abbr title="View this template">v</abbr></a></li>\n<li class="nv-talk"><a observation</b> has been carried out over the past couple of centuries in various ways. The passage of typhoons, hurricanes, as well as other tropical cyclones have been detected by word of mouth from sailors recently coming to port or by radio transmissions from ships at sea, from sediment deposits in near shore estuaries, to the wiping out of cities near the coastline. Since <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, advances in technology have included using planes to survey the ocean basins, satellites to monitor the world\'s oceans from <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">outer space</a> using a variety of methods, radars to monitor their progress near the coastline, and recently the introduction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicles" title="Unmanned aerial vehicles">unmanned aerial vehicles</a> to penetrate storms. Recent studies have concentrated on studying hurricane impacts lying within rocks or near shore lake sediments, which are branches of a new field known as <a href="/wiki/Paleotempestology" title="Paleotempestology">paleotempestology</a>. This article details the various methods employed in the creation of the hurricane database, as well as reconstructions necessary for reanalysis of past storms used in projects such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis">Atlantic hurricane reanalysis</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geological markers of past activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Paleotempestology" title="Paleotempestology">Paleotempestology</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stalagmites_in_caves">Stalagmites in caves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Stalagmites in caves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Recent studies of the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-18" title="Oxygen-18"><sup>18</sup>O</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon-13" title="Carbon-13"><sup>13</sup>C</a> isotopes found in <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a> show that tropical cyclone events can leave markers that can be separated out on a week-by-week basis. The error rate of this type of microanalysis was 1 error in 1,200 sampling points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frappier2007_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frappier2007-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Markers_in_coral">Markers in coral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Markers in coral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Rocks contain certain isotopes of elements, known as natural tracers, which describe the conditions under which they formed. By studying the calcium carbonate in coral rock, past sea surface temperature and hurricane information can be revealed. Lighter oxygen isotopes (<sup>16</sup>O) are left behind in coral during periods of very heavy rainfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Since hurricanes are the main source of extreme rainfall in the tropical oceans, past hurricane events can be dated to the days of their impact on the coral by looking at the increased <sup>18</sup>O concentration within the coral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sediment_deposition_in_coastal_lakes">Sediment deposition in coastal lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sediment deposition in coastal lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kam Biu-Liu, a professor at <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_University" title="Louisiana State University">Louisiana State University</a>, has been studying sediment lying at the bottom of coastal lakes and marshes in order to study the frequency and intensity of hurricanes over the past 5,000\xc2\xa0years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Since storm surges sweep coastal sands with them as they progress inland, a layer of sand is left behind in coastal lakes and marshes. <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">Radiocarbon dating</a> is then used to date the layers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Newspapers">Newspapers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Newspapers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Before the invention of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> in the early to mid-19th century, news was as fast as the quickest horse, stage, or ship. Normally, there was no advance warning of a tropical cyclone impact. However, the situation changed in the 19th century as seafaring people and land-based researchers, such as Father Vi\xc3\xb1es in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, came up with systematic methods of reading the sky\'s appearance or the sea state, which could foretell a tropical cyclone\'s approach up to a couple days in advance.</p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, the abundance of historical documentary records in the form of <i><span lang="zh-Latn" xml:lang="zh-Latn">Fang Zhi</span></i> (semiofficial local gazettes) offers an extraordinary opportunity for providing a high-resolution historical dataset for the frequency of typhoon strikes. Kam-biu Liu <i>et al.</i> (2001) reconstructed a 1,000-year time series of typhoon landfalls in the Guangdong Province of southern China since AD 975 and found that on a decadal timescale, the twenty-year interval from AD 1660 to 1680 is the most active period on record, with twenty-eight to thirty-seven typhoon landfalls per decade. The variability in typhoon landfalls in Guangdong mimics that observed in other paleoclimatic proxies (e.g., tree rings, ice cores) from China and the northern hemisphere. Remarkably, the two periods of most frequent typhoon strikes in Guangdong (AD 1660-1680, 1850\xe2\x80\x931880) coincide with two of the coldest and driest periods in northern and central China during the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liu2001_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Liu2001-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_observations">Surface observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surface observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tmulti tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:508px;max-width:508px">\n<div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:252px;max-width:252px">\n<div class="thumbimage"><a Note that no hurricane was detected on Mid-Atlantic in 1933.</small></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ship_reports">Ship reports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ship reports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>For centuries, people have sailed the world\'s oceans and seas, and for just as long, they have encountered storms. The worst of the cyclones over the open seas likely took those that observed them into the depths of the oceans. However, some did survive to report harrowing tales. Before the invention of the wireless <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a> in 1905, reports about storms at sea either coincided with their arrival at the coast as ships scrambled into port, or came weeks and months afterwards from remote ports of call. Ship and buoy reports, available since the 1970s, are used in real-time not only for their temperature, pressure, and wind measurements, but also for their sea surface temperature and wave height measurements.</p>\n<p>Wind reports from ships at sea have become increasingly based on anemometers, and less so on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beaufort_Scale" title="Beaufort Scale">Beaufort Scale</a>. This is important to note as the Beaufort Scale underestimates winds at higher wind speeds, indicating ship wind observations taken for older storms are likely to underrepresent their true value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TM22_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-TM22-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Landsea" title="Christopher Landsea">Christopher Landsea</a> <i>et al.</i> point out, many tropical cyclones that formed on the open sea and did not affect any coast usually went undetected prior to satellite observation since the 1970s. They estimated an undercount bias of zero to six tropical cyclones per year between 1851 and 1885 and zero to four per year between 1886 and 1910. These undercounts roughly take into account the typical size of tropical cyclones, the density of shipping tracks over the Atlantic basin, and the amount of populated coastline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land-based_observations">Land-based observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Land-based observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the early 20th century, forecasting the track of cyclones was still confined to areas of the greatest surface pressure falls, based upon <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_observation" title="Surface weather observation">surface weather observations</a>, and climatology. These methods proved to be the cutting edge of tropical cyclone forecasting through the mid 20th century. Land-based surface observations remain invaluable as a source of real-time information at locations near the coastline and inland. Combined with ship observations and newspapers, they formed the total information network for hurricane detection until radiosondes were introduced in 1941 and reconnaissance aircraft began in 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TM22_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-TM22-7">[7]</a></sup> Land-based observations of pressure and wind can show how quickly a tropical cyclone is decaying as it moves inland. Their rainfall reports show where significant rainfall is occurring, and can be an alert for possible flooding. With the establishment of the ASOS network in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> during the 1990s, more locations are reporting around the clock than ever before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mobile_platforms">Mobile platforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mobile platforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Since the 1990s, academic researchers have begun to deploy mobile weather stations fortified to withstand hurricane-force winds. The two largest programs are the Florida Coastal Monitoring Program<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FCMP_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FCMP-10">[10]</a></sup> and the Wind Engineering Mobile Instrumented Tower Experiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WEMITE_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-WEMITE-11">[11]</a></sup> During landfall, the NOAA Hurricane Research Division compares and verifies data from reconnaissance aircraft, including wind speed data taken at flight level and from GPS dropwindsondes and stepped-frequency microwave radiometers, to wind speed data transmitted in real time from weather stations erected near or at the coast. The National Hurricane Center uses the data to evaluate conditions at landfall and to verify forecasts.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_air_observations">Upper air observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Upper air observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconnaissance_aircraft">Reconnaissance aircraft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reconnaissance aircraft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone forecasting">Tropical cyclone forecasting</a></div>\n<p>The idea of aircraft reconnaissance of tropical cyclones first was put forth by Captain W. L. Farnsworth of the Galveston Commercial Association in the early 1930s. Supported by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a>, it passed both the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> in 1936.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Since 1944, aircraft have been flying out to sea to find tropical cyclones. Before regular satellite coverage, this was a hit-or-miss affair. Thereafter, aircraft flights into tropical systems became more targeted and precise. Nowadays, a C-130 is used as a hurricane hunter by the Air Force, while the P-3 Orion is used by the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> for research projects used to better understand <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> and improve hurricane forecasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-9">[9]</a></sup> The implementation of synoptic observation missions by a Gulfstream jet, where dropwindsondes are used to investigate a tropical cyclone\'s environment, has led to a 15-20 percent reduction in track forecast errors where such missions were present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEE_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-GEE-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Historical aircraft used for weather and hurricane tracking include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" id="Unmanned_aerial_vehicles">Unmanned aerial vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Unmanned aerial vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">Unmanned aerial vehicle</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insitu_Aerosonde" title="Insitu Aerosonde">Insitu Aerosonde</a></div>\n<p>The era of the aerosonde began in 1998, when the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Meteorology" title="Bureau of Meteorology">Bureau of Meteorology</a> flew an aerosonde into Tropical Cyclone Tiffany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEE_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-GEE-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ophelia_(2005)" title="Hurricane Ophelia (2005)">Hurricane Ophelia</a> became the first Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> where an unmanned aerial vehicle, known as an aerosonde, mission was used for a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>. The first typhoon was penetrated by an aerosonde in 2005 as well. Unlike normal reconnaissance flights, the aerosonde stayed near the surface after a 10-hour flight within the tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Remote_sensing">Remote sensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a It soon became apparent that large areas became obscured when significant weather was in the area. In 1957, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> established the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>\' first radar network to cover the coastline and act as first warning of an impending tropical cyclone. Upgraded in the 1990s to use doppler technology, radar can provide rainfall estimates, wind estimates, possible locations of tornadoes within a system\'s spiral bands, as well as the center location of a tropical cyclone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CPHC_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-9">[9]</a></sup> The United States operates with a network of 158 Doppler Radars across the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite">Satellite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Satellite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Beginning with the launching of TIROS-I in April 1960, satellites have been used to look for tropical cyclones. The Dvorak technique was developed from early satellite images of tropical cyclones to determine real-time a tropical cyclone\'s strength from characteristics seen on satellite imagery. In most tropical cyclone basins, use of the satellite-based <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a> is the primary method used to determine a tropical cyclone\'s maximum sustained winds. The extent of banding and difference in temperature between the eye and eyewall is used within the technique to assign a maximum sustained wind and pressure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Since the mid-1990s, microwave imagery has been able to determine the center of rotation when that center is obscured by mid to high level cloudiness.
