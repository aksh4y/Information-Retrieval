class="internal" href="/wiki/File:98C_dec_19_2010_0000Z.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Omeka" title="Tropical Storm Omeka">subtropical storm</a> on December 19, 2010, originally a Kona storm</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Kona storms</b> (also called <b>Kona lows</b>) are a type of seasonal <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>, usually formed in the winter from winds coming from the westerly "kona" (normally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leeward" title="Leeward">leeward</a>) direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrdc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-wrdc-1">[1]</a></sup> They are mainly cold core cyclones, which places them in the <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> rather than the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> category. Hawaii typically experiences two to three annually, which can affect the state for a week or more. Among their hazards are heavy <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hailstorms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash floods</a> and their associated <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>, high elevation <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, high winds which result in large surf and <a href="/wiki/Swell_(ocean)" title="Swell (ocean)">swells</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">waterspouts</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_term">Origin of term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kona_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin of term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Kona is a <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">Hawaiian language</a> term (related to similar words in other <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_languages" title="Polynesian languages">Polynesian</a> languages) for the western (to southwestern) side of an island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Kona_District,_Hawaii" title="Kona District, Hawaii">Kona District</a> for example on the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Big Island of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i</a> still uses this name. Although normally dry and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leeward" title="Leeward">leeward</a>, the traditional easterly (to northeasterly) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">tradewinds</a> slacken and reverse during one of these cyclones.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Character_of_the_cyclones">Character of the cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kona_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Character of the cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Once termed as <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclones</a>, a change in the definition of the term during the early 1970s makes categorization of the systems not straightforward. Kona lows are typically cold core, making them <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, they generally share the subtropical cyclone characteristic of losing their associated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" title="Weather fronts">weather fronts</a> with time, since the cyclones tend to retrograde slowly.</p>\n<p>Otkin and Martin identified three types of kona storms: cold-frontal cyclogenesis cyclones (CFCs), trade wind easterlies, and cold-frontal cyclogenesis/trade wind easterlies cyclones. Of the three, CFC cyclones are the most common.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Rainfall in a Kona storm is more pronounced than a <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">cold front storm</a>, and most intense from south to east of the storm and in front of the storm\'s center. Steady rains may last from several hours to days, with longer lasting storms typically interrupted by intervals of lighter rain and partial clearing. Intense showers may appear on more moderate but steady rain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrdc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-wrdc-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency_and_effects">Frequency and effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kona_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Frequency and effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Some winters occur without a single Kona storm, with a high of four or five. Hawaii typically experiences two to three annually<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> between October and April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BIRCH_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-BIRCH-6">[6]</a></sup> The cyclone events for Hawaii can be long-lived, affecting the state for a week or more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Konaposter_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Konaposter-7">[7]</a></sup> Kona lows produce a wide range of weather hazards for Hawaii. Among them are heavy rain, hailstorms, flash floods and their associated landslides, high winds which result in large surf and swells, waterspouts, and heavy snows on the higher mountains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kona_winds">Kona winds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kona_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Kona winds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kona winds are associated with cyclones with central pressures below 1,000.0 hectopascals (29.53\xc2\xa0inHg) which pass within 500\xc2\xa0miles/820\xc2\xa0km northwest of the Hawaiian Islands. Strong Kona winds usually last for a day. Considerable damage is caused to boats lying southwest of the islands. On land, trees are uprooted and roofs can be blown off homes. When passing through mountain gaps and over mountains, downsloped winds gust over 100\xc2\xa0mph/165\xc2\xa0km/h, which causes much of the destruction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kona_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KonaLowNovember041995IR.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="287" data-file-width="302" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/KonaLowNovember041995IR.jpg/220px-KonaLowNovember041995IR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c9/KonaLowNovember041995IR.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KonaLowNovember041995IR.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA kona storm on November 4, 1995</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The most powerful Kona storm in the last fifty years struck the Hawaiian islands between 8 January and 11 January 1980. The low pressure primarily responsible for the severe conditions had a barometric pressure of 975mb on January 8, while passing north of the state, one of the lowest pressure readings ever recorded in Hawaiian waters as a result of an extratropical storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unusual_Winter_Storm.2C_Hawaii_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Unusual_Winter_Storm.2C_Hawaii-10">[10]</a></sup> The storm caused severe weather across the entire state, forcing the closure of all airports. Rainfall from the storm was extremely heavy, surpassing twenty inches in many locations, particularly on the Big Island and Maui. High surf from the storm caused extensive damage, particularly along the Kona coast of the Big Island, where the towns of Kailua-Kona and Puako received extensive damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unusual_Winter_Storm.2C_Hawaii_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Unusual_Winter_Storm.2C_Hawaii-10">[10]</a></sup> Winds from the storm averaged around 40-50\xc2\xa0mph on the open ocean, but varied tremendously on land due to unique geographic features. Some areas experienced wind gusts well over hurricane force, including a low altitude gust of 106\xc2\xa0mph (171\xc2\xa0km/h) on Lahaina Roads, Maui. Haiku, on the windward coast of Maui, experienced a wind gust of 75\xc2\xa0mph (121\xc2\xa0km/h). On the high mountain summits of the Big Island and Maui, winds of over 110\xc2\xa0mph (180\xc2\xa0km/h) were recorded. Damage from the storm was estimated at between $25 and 35 million (in 1980 dollars).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unusual_Winter_Storm.2C_Hawaii_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Unusual_Winter_Storm.2C_Hawaii-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In December 2008, lightning strikes from a Kona storm caused the entire power grid of the island of Oahu to black out, while then President-elect, <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> was visiting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In December\xc2\xa02010, a Kona storm became fully subtropical and eventually became <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Omeka" title="Tropical Storm Omeka">Tropical Storm Omeka</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kona_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The fourth episode of the sixth season of <a href="/wiki/Magnum,_P.I." title="Magnum, P.I.">Magnum, P.I.</a> is entitled "The Kona Winds."</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kona_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
