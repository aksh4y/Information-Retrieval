Built on a <a href="/wiki/Beat_(music)" title="Beat (music)">beat</a> and multi-tracked harmonies, the song\'s instrumentation includes slow-bouncing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Keyboard_(instrument)" title="Keyboard (instrument)">keyboard</a> tones, and <a href="/wiki/Drum" title="Drum">drums</a>. The song was written by Mendler and American songwriters <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Kiriakou" title="Emanuel Kiriakou">Emanuel Kiriakou</a>, <a href="/wiki/E._Kidd_Bogart" title="E. Kidd Bogart">Evan Kidd Bogart</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Goldstein" title="Andrew Goldstein">Andrew Goldstein</a>, and produced by Kiriakou and Goldstein. Lyrically, the song gives ode to a disaster love, and reconciliation and perseverance in love. Mendler\'s vocals span from the low note of F<sub>3</sub> to the high note of F<sub>5</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In interview for <i>Coup de Main Magazine</i>, Mendler commented about recording a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rap" title="Rap">rap</a> song for the first time. She said:</p>\n<blockquote class="templatequote">\n<p>I loved recording... you know the rap in \'Hurricane\'? That was pretty fun. It was very experimental for me, I haven\xe2\x80\x99t done much rapping before.
