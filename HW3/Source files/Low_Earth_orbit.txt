article.)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>low Earth orbit</b> (<b>LEO</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> around <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> with an altitude of 2,000 kilometres (1,200\xc2\xa0mi) or less, and an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">orbital period</a> of between about 84 and 127 minutes. Objects below approximately 160 kilometres (99\xc2\xa0mi) will experience very rapid <a href="/wiki/Orbital_decay" title="Orbital decay">orbital decay</a> and altitude loss due to atmospheric drag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>With the exception of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Apollo_astronauts#Apollo_astronauts_who_flew_to_the_Moon_without_landing" title="List of Apollo astronauts">24 astronauts</a> who flew lunar flights in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> during the four-year period spanning 1968 through 1972, all <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflights</a> have taken place in LEO or below. The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> conducts operations in LEO. The altitude record for a human spaceflight in LEO was <a href="/wiki/Gemini_11" title="Gemini 11">Gemini 11</a> with an <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">apogee</a> of 1,374.1 kilometres (853.8\xc2\xa0mi). All crewed <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space stations</a> to date, as well as the majority of <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>, have been in LEO.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Orbital_characteristics"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbital characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Objects in LEO encounter atmospheric drag from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gases" title="Gases">gases</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">thermosphere</a> (approximately 80\xe2\x80\x93500\xc2\xa0km up) or <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">exosphere</a> (approximately 500\xc2\xa0km and up), depending on orbit height. Due to atmospheric drag, satellites do not usually orbit below 300\xc2\xa0km. Objects in LEO orbit Earth between the denser part of the atmosphere and below the inner <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">Van Allen radiation belt</a>.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Gravity_Field_and_Steady-State_Ocean_Circulation_Explorer" title="Gravity Field and Steady-State Ocean Circulation Explorer">GOCE</a> orbited at about 255 km and had an aerodynamic shape and ion thrusters to reduce and compensate for atmospheric drag.</p>\n<p>The mean orbital velocity needed to maintain a stable low Earth orbit is about 7.8\xc2\xa0km/s, but reduces with increased orbital altitude. Calculated for circular orbit of 200\xc2\xa0km it is 7.79\xc2\xa0km/s and for 1500\xc2\xa0km it is 7.12\xc2\xa0km/s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[3]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> needed to achieve low Earth orbit starts around 9.4\xc2\xa0km/s. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drag" title="Atmospheric drag">Atmospheric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gravity_drag" title="Gravity drag">gravity drag</a> associated with launch typically adds 1.3\xe2\x80\x931.8\xc2\xa0km/s to the launch vehicle <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> required to reach normal LEO orbital velocity of around 7.8\xc2\xa0km/s (28,080\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="center">\n<div class="floatnone"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orbitalaltitudes.jpg"><img alt="Orbitalaltitudes.jpg" data-file-height="1656" data-file-width="4500" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Orbitalaltitudes.jpg/700px-Orbitalaltitudes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Orbitalaltitudes.jpg/1050px-Orbitalaltitudes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Orbitalaltitudes.jpg/1400px-Orbitalaltitudes.jpg 2x" width="700"/></a></div>\n</div>\n<p>Equatorial low Earth orbits (ELEO) are a subset of LEO. These orbits, with low inclination to the Equator, allow rapid revisit times and have the lowest <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">delta-v</a> requirement (i.e., fuel spend) of any orbit. Orbits with a high inclination angle to the equator are usually called <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">polar orbits</a>.</p>\n<p>Higher orbits include <a href="/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit" title="Medium Earth orbit">medium Earth orbit</a> (MEO), sometimes called intermediate circular orbit (ICO), and further above, <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbit</a> (GEO). Orbits higher than low orbit can lead to early failure of electronic components due to intense <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> and charge accumulation.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_LEO">Use of LEO</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use of LEO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n<div class="PopUpMediaTransform" id="mwe_player_0" style="width:220px;" videopayload=\'&lt;div class="mediaContainer" style="width:854px"&gt;&lt;video id="mwe_player_1" poster="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv/854px--Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv.jpg" controls="" preload="none" autoplay="" style="width:854px;height:480px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="76" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"&gt;&lt;source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/4/4c/Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv/Sunrise_To_Sunset_Aboard_The_ISS.ogv.480p.webm" type="video/webm; It provides high bandwidth and low communication time lag (<a href="/wiki/Latency_(engineering)" title="Latency (engineering)">latency</a>), but satellites in LEO will not be visible from any given point on the Earth at all times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellites</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spy_satellite" title="Spy satellite">spy satellites</a> use LEO as they are able to see the surface of the Earth more clearly as they are not so far away. They are also able to traverse the surface of the Earth. A majority of artificial <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> are placed in LEO,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[6]</a></sup> making one complete revolution around the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> in about 90 minutes.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> is in a LEO about 400\xc2\xa0km (250\xc2\xa0mi) above Earth\'s surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[7]</a></sup> and needs reboosting a few times a year due to orbital decay.</li>\n<li>Since it requires less energy to place a satellite into a LEO and the LEO satellite needs less powerful amplifiers for successful transmission, LEO is used for many communication applications. Because these LEO orbits are not geostationary, a network (or "<a href="/wiki/Satellite_constellation" title="Satellite constellation">constellation</a>") of satellites is required to provide continuous coverage. (Many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communication_satellite" title="Communication satellite">communication satellites</a> require <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary orbits</a>, and move at the same angular velocity as the Earth. Some communications satellites including the <a href="/wiki/Iridium_Communications" title="Iridium Communications">Iridium phone system</a> use LEO.)</li>\n<li>Lower orbits also aid <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">remote sensing</a> satellites because of the added detail that can be gained. Remote sensing satellites can also take advantage of <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">sun-synchronous LEO orbits</a> at an altitude of about 800\xc2\xa0km (500\xc2\xa0mi) and near polar inclination. <a href="/wiki/Envisat" title="Envisat">Envisat</a> is one example of an Earth observation satellite that makes use of this particular type of LEO.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gravity_Field_and_Steady-State_Ocean_Circulation_Explorer" title="Gravity Field and Steady-State Ocean Circulation Explorer">GOCE</a> orbited at about 255\xc2\xa0km (158\xc2\xa0mi) to measure Earth\'s gravity field.</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> orbits at about 540\xc2\xa0km (340\xc2\xa0mi) above Earth.</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_debris">Space debris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_Earth_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Space debris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The LEO environment is becoming congested with <a href="/wiki/Space_debris" title="Space debris">space debris</a> due to the frequency of object launches. This has caused growing concern in recent years, since collisions at orbital velocities can easily be dangerous, and even deadly. Collisions can produce even more space debris in the process, creating a <a href="/wiki/Domino_effect" title="Domino effect">domino effect</a>, something known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kessler_Syndrome" title="Kessler Syndrome">Kessler Syndrome</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Joint_Space_Operations_Center" title="Joint Space Operations Center">Joint Space Operations Center</a>, part of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Strategic_Command" title="United States Strategic Command">United States Strategic Command</a> (formerly the United States Space Command), currently tracks more than 8,500 objects larger than 10\xc2\xa0cm in LEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[8]</a></sup> However, a limited <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">Arecibo Observatory</a> study suggested there could be approximately one million objects larger than 2 millimeters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[9]</a></sup> which are too small to be visible from Earth-based observatories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
