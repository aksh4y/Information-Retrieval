title="Blowing snow">blowing snow</a> and strong, gusty surface <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is often referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Whiteout_(weather)" title="Whiteout (weather)">whiteout</a> and is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">blizzard</a> but is localized in <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a> or in <a href="/wiki/Location_(geography)" title="Location (geography)">location</a> and snow accumulations may or may not be significant.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div href="/w/index.php?title=Snowsquall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are two primary types of snowsqualls, lake effect and frontal.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lake-effect_snow">Lake-effect snow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowsquall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lake-effect snow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">Lake-effect snow</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" Over 200cm of snow from this squall, fell north of <a href="/wiki/London,_Ontario" title="London, Ontario">London, Ontario</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_effect_snowsquall_york_region_ontario_jan08_03.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1204" data-file-width="3024" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Lake_effect_snowsquall_york_region_ontario_jan08_03.jpg/220px-Lake_effect_snowsquall_york_region_ontario_jan08_03.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Lake_effect_snowsquall_york_region_ontario_jan08_03.jpg/330px-Lake_effect_snowsquall_york_region_ontario_jan08_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Lake_effect_snowsquall_york_region_ontario_jan08_03.jpg/440px-Lake_effect_snowsquall_york_region_ontario_jan08_03.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_effect_snowsquall_york_region_ontario_jan08_03.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA linear single banded snow squall over Southern Ontario</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>When arctic air moves over large expanses of warmer open waters in winter, <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> clouds develop which cause heavy snow showers due to the large amount of moisture available. This occurs southwest of extratropical cyclones, with the curved cyclonic wind flow bringing cold air across the relatively warm <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> which then leads to narrow lake-effect snow bands that can produce significant localized snowfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Whiteout_(weather)" title="Whiteout (weather)">Whiteout</a> conditions will affect narrow corridors from shores to inland areas aligned along the <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_winds" title="Prevailing winds">prevailing wind</a> direction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC-2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC-2-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This will be enhanced when the moving air mass is <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">uplifted</a> by higher elevations. The name originates from the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> area of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, however any body of water can produce them. Regions in <a href="/wiki/Windward_and_leeward" title="Windward and leeward">lee</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>, such as the Canadian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritimes" title="Maritimes">Maritimes</a> could experience such snowsqualls.</p>\n<p>The areas affected by lake-effect snow are called <a href="/wiki/Snowbelt" title="Snowbelt">snowbelts</a> and deposition rate of many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches" title="Inches">inches</a> (<a href="/wiki/Centimetre" title="Centimetre">centimetres</a>) of snow per hour are common in these situations. In order for lake-effect snow to form, the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> difference between the water and 850 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> should be at least 23\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (13\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C), surface temperature be around the freezing mark, the lake unfrozen, the path over the lake at least 100\xc2\xa0km, and the directional <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> with height should be less than 30\xc2\xb0 from the surface to 850 millibars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haby_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haby-5">[5]</a></sup> Extremely cold air over still warm water in early winter can even produce <a href="/wiki/Thundersnow" title="Thundersnow">thundersnow</a>, snow showers accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a>.</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frontal_snowsquall">Frontal snowsquall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snowsquall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Frontal snowsquall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Snowsquall_line-Bourrasque_neige_frontal_NOAA.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="821" data-file-width="1041" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Snowsquall_line-Bourrasque_neige_frontal_NOAA.png/220px-Snowsquall_line-Bourrasque_neige_frontal_NOAA.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Snowsquall_line-Bourrasque_neige_frontal_NOAA.png/330px-Snowsquall_line-Bourrasque_neige_frontal_NOAA.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Snowsquall_line-Bourrasque_neige_frontal_NOAA.png/440px-Snowsquall_line-Bourrasque_neige_frontal_NOAA.png 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Snowsquall_line-Bourrasque_neige_frontal_NOAA.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nFrontal snowsquall moving toward <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>, United States</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A frontal snowsquall is an intense frontal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convective" title="Convective">convective</a> line (similar to a <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainband</a>), when <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> is near freezing at the surface. The strong convection that develops has enough moisture to produce whiteout conditions at places which line passes over as the wind causes intense blowing snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC-2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EC-2-3">[3]</a></sup> This type of snowsquall generally lasts less than 30 minutes at any point along its path but the motion of the line can cover large distances. Frontal squalls may form a short distance ahead of the surface cold front or behind the cold front in situations where there are other contributing factors such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamic_lifting&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dynamic lifting (page does not exist)">dynamic lifting</a> from a deepening low pressure system or a series of <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> lines which act similar to a traditional cold frontal passage. In situations where squalls develop post-frontally it is not unusual to have two or three linear squall bands pass in rapid succession only separated by 25 miles (40 kilometers) with each passing the same point in roughly 30 minutes apart.</p>\n<p>This is similar to a line of thunderstorms in the summer but the tops of the clouds are only 5,000 to 10,000 feet (1,500 to 3,000 m), often difficult to see on radar. Forecasting these types of events is equivalent to summer severe weather forecast for squall lines: presence of a sharp frontal trough with wind shift and low level jet of more than 30 knots (55.58\xc2\xa0km/h). However, the cold dome behind the trough is at 850 mbar instead of a higher level and must be at least -13\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F (-25\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C). The presence of surface moisture from bodies of water or preexisting liquid precipitation is also a significant contributing factor helping to raise the dew point temperature and saturate the boundary layer. This saturate can significantly increase the amount of convective available potential energy leading to deeper vertical growth and higher precipitable water levels increasing the volume of snow which can be produced by the squall. In cases where there is a large amount of vertical growth and mixing the squall may develop embedded cumulonimbus clouds resulting in lightning and thunder which is dubbed <a href="/wiki/Thundersnow" title="Thundersnow">thundersnow</a>.</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers">Dangers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nRapidly deteriorating weather conditions during snowsqualls often lead to traffic accidents.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Both types of snowsqualls are very dangerous for <a href="/wiki/Driving" title="Driving">motorists</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airplanes" title="Airplanes">airplanes</a> or generally any <a href="/wiki/Travel" title="Travel">traveler</a> unfortunate enough to get stuck in one. The change in conditions is very sudden, and slippery conditions and abrupt loss of visibility due to <a href="/wiki/Whiteout_(weather)" title="Whiteout (weather)">whiteouts</a> often cause <a href="/wiki/Multiple-vehicle_collision" title="Multiple-vehicle collision">multiple-vehicle collisions</a>. In the case of lake effect snow, heavy amounts of snow can accumulate in short periods of time, possibly causing road closures and paralyzing cities. For instance, on January 9, 2015, a localized, heavy snow squall caused a 193-vehicle pile-up on I-94 highway near Galesburg, Michigan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
