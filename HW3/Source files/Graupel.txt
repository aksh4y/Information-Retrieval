Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[\xcb\x88\xc9\xa1\xca\x81a\xca\x8ap\xc9\x99l]</a></span>; <span class="nowrap"><small>English:</small> <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="/\xc9\xa1/: \'g\' in \'guy\'">\xc9\xa1</span><span title="\'r\' in \'rye\'">r</span><span title="/a\xca\x8a/: \'ou\' in \'mouth\'">a\xca\x8a</span><span title="\'p\' in \'pie\'">p</span><span href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> that forms when <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" The term <i>graupel</i> comes from the German language.</p>\n<p>Graupel is distinct from <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, small hail and <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">ice pellets</a>: the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> defines small hail as snow pellets encapsulated by ice, a precipitation halfway between graupel and hail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a> code for graupel is href="/wiki/File:Graupel_Falling.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nFalling graupel</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Under some <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> conditions, <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> crystals may encounter <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercooled</a> water <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Droplets" title="Droplets">droplets</a>. These droplets, which have a diameter of about 10\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">\xc2\xb5m</a> (0.00039\xc2\xa0in), can exist in the liquid <a href="/wiki/State_of_matter" title="State of matter">state</a> at temperatures as low as \xe2\x88\x9240\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9240\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F), far below the normal <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freezing</a> point. Contact between a snow crystal and the supercooled droplets results in freezing of the liquid droplets onto the surface of the crystal. This process of crystal growth is known as <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(atmosphere)" title="Accretion (atmosphere)">accretion</a>. Crystals that exhibit frozen droplets on their surfaces are often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Hard_rime" title="Hard rime">rimed</a>. When this process continues so that the shape of the original snow crystal is no longer identifiable, the resulting crystal is referred to as graupel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emu_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-emu-3">[3]</a></sup> Graupel was formerly referred to by meteorologists as soft hail. However, graupel is easily distinguishable from hail in both the shape and strength of the pellet and the circumstances in which it falls. Ice from <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> is formed in hard, relatively uniform layers and usually falls only during <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a>. Graupel forms fragile, oblong shapes and falls in place of typical <a href="/wiki/Snowflake" title="Snowflake">snowflakes</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wintry_mix" title="Wintry mix">wintry mix</a> situations, often in concert with <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">ice pellets</a>. Graupel is also fragile enough that it will typically fall apart when touched.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microscopic_structure">Microscopic structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graupel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Microscopic structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graupel_encasing_a_snow_crystal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1768" data-file-width="2500" height="156" class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graupel_encasing_a_snow_crystal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nGraupel encasing and hiding a snow crystal from view</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Snowflake_300um_LTSEM,_13368.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1788" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Snowflake_300um_LTSEM%2C_13368.jpg/330px-Snowflake_300um_LTSEM%2C_13368.jpg 1.5x, class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Snowflake_300um_LTSEM,_13368.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nRime on both ends of a columnar snow crystal</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The frozen droplets on the surface of rimed crystals are difficult to see, and the topography of a graupel particle is not easy to record with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_microscope" title="Light microscope">light microscope</a> because of the limited <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resolution_(microscopy)" title="Resolution (microscopy)">resolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">depth of field</a> in the instrument.</p>\n<p>However, observations of snow crystals with a low-temperature <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope" title="Scanning electron microscope">scanning electron microscope</a> (LT-SEM) clearly show cloud droplets measuring up to 50\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb5m (0.002\xc2\xa0in) on the surface of the crystals. The rime has been observed on all four basic forms of snow crystals, including plates, dendrites, columns, and needles. As the riming process continues, the mass of frozen, accumulated cloud droplets obscures the form of the original snow crystal, thereby giving rise to a graupel particle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-emu_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-emu-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Graupel_and_avalanches">Graupel and avalanches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graupel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Graupel and avalanches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Graupel commonly forms in high-altitude climates and is both denser and more <a href="/wiki/Granularity" title="Granularity">granular</a> than ordinary <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, due to its rimed exterior. Macroscopically, graupel resembles small beads of <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a>. The combination of density and low viscosity makes fresh layers of graupel unstable on slopes, and layers of 20\xe2\x80\x9330\xc2\xa0cm (7.9\xe2\x80\x9311.8\xc2\xa0in) present a high risk of dangerous slab <a href="/wiki/Avalanche" title="Avalanche">avalanches</a>.
