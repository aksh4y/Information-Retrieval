In summer it is replaced with an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_Low" title="Arctic Low">Arctic Low</a>, or if it moves to continental land, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_Low" title="North American Low">North American Low</a>.</p>\n<p>North American Highs move eastwards across the continent, often in the company of one or more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low-pressure_cell" title="Low-pressure cell">low-pressure cells</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclones" title="Cyclones">cyclones</a>. Its cold, dense air does not extend usually above 3\xc2\xa0km (1.9\xc2\xa0mi), lower than the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Rockies" title="Canadian Rockies">Canadian Rockies</a>. Sometimes, in winter it breaks free and passes over the Rockies and brings a cold front into Southwestern United States and Mexico, freezing crops and bringing snow into Mexico\'s mountains as far south as <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">Jalisco</a>. The high\xe2\x80\x99s usual location east of the Rockies shelters it from the relatively warm <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and helps it maintain its strength. The average January sea level pressure at its centre is about 1,020 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibars" title="Millibars">millibars</a> (30.12 inches of mercury). The Canadian high often moves southeastward until it eventually reaches the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, where it merges with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azores_high" title="Azores high">Azores high</a>. In the summer the Canadian high circulates cool, dry air to the United States east of the Rockies and parts of southern Canada.</p>\n<p>The North American High is akin to the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_High" title="Siberian High">Siberian High</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">Eurasia</a>, but it is much smaller, and it has much less influence, merely affecting the weather of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea-level_pressure" title="Sea-level pressure">sea-level pressure</a> (<a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>) rarely, if ever, exceeds 1055.0 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibars" title="Millibars">millibars</a> (1055.0 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hectopascal" title="Hectopascal">hectopascals</a>)(hPa)(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI" title="SI">SI</a>).</p>\n<p>Often, in the winter months, cool or cold dry air settles over the land in the vicinity of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a> where it builds into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure_cell" title="High-pressure cell">high-pressure cell</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> that moves across the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> on its leading edge. After reaching the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, the moist environment brings on changes of the qualities of the air and the dissipation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-pressure_cell" title="High-pressure cell">high-pressure cell</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> as the cold air warms and becomes humid. In Europe, a portion of the North American/Canadian high usually over <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> called the Greenland high which settles over Greenland affects northern European weather and may merge with the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavian_High&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Scandinavian High (page does not exist)">Scandinavian High</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
