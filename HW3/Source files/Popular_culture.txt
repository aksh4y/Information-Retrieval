For the Madeon song, see <a href="/wiki/Pop_Culture_(song)" title="Pop Culture (song)">Pop Culture (song)</a>.</div>\n<p><b>Popular culture</b> or <b>pop culture</b> is the entirety of <a href="/wiki/Attitude_(psychology)" title="Attitude (psychology)">attitudes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Idea" title="Idea">ideas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Image" title="Image">images</a>, <a href="/wiki/Point_of_view_(philosophy)" title="Point of view (philosophy)">perspectives</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomena</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">mainstream</a> of a given <a href="/wiki/Cultural_anthropology" title="Cultural anthropology">culture</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a> of the early to mid-20th century and the emerging <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">global</a> mainstream of the late 20th and early 21st century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>, this collection of ideas permeates the <a href="/wiki/Everyday_life" title="Everyday life">everyday lives</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>. The most common pop culture categories are: entertainment (such as movies, music, television, and video games), sports, news (as in people/places in news), politics, fashion/clothes, technology, and slang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Popular culture has a way of influencing an individual\'s attitudes towards certain topics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Popular culture is sometimes viewed as being trivial and "<a href="/wiki/Dumbing_down" title="Dumbing down">dumbed down</a>" in order to find consensual acceptance throughout the mainstream. As a result, it comes under heavy criticism from various <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">non-mainstream</a> sources (most notably <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> groups and <a href="/wiki/Counterculture" title="Counterculture">countercultural</a> groups) which deem it superficial, <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensationalist</a>, or corrupt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 and definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cultural_history" title="Cultural history">Cultural history</a></div>\n<p>The term "popular culture" was coined in the 19th century or earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Traditionally, popular culture was associated with poor <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">education</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_classes" title="Lower classes">lower classes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> as opposed to the "official culture" and higher education of the upper classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The stress in the distinction from "official culture" became more pronounced towards the end of the 19th century,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (August 2012)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup> a usage that became established by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interbellum" title="Interbellum">interbellum</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (August 2012)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>From the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, following major cultural and social changes brought by <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a> innovations, the meaning of popular culture began to overlap with those of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_culture" title="Mass culture">mass culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Media_culture" title="Media culture">media culture</a>, image culture, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumer_culture" title="Consumer culture">consumer culture</a>, and culture for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_consumption" title="Mass consumption">mass consumption</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Social and cultural changes in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> were a pioneer in this with respect to other western countries.</p>\n<p>The abbreviated form "pop" for popular, as in <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop music</a>, dates from the late 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewGrovev15p85_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewGrovev15p85-15">[15]</a></sup> Although terms "pop" and "popular" are in some cases used interchangeably, and their meaning partially overlap, the term "pop" is narrower. Pop is specific of something containing qualities of mass appeal, while "popular" refers to what has gained popularity, regardless of its style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steinem1965p73_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Steinem1965p73-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-top40.about.com_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-top40.about.com-17">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<p>According to author John Storey, there are six definitions of popular culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantitative_property" title="Quantitative property">quantitative</a> definition of culture has the problem that much "<a href="/wiki/High_culture" title="High culture">high culture</a>" (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> dramatizations of <a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">Jane Austen</a>) is also "popular." "Pop culture" is also defined as the culture that is "left over" when we have decided what high culture is. However, many works straddle the boundaries, e.g., <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>.</p>\n<p>A third definition equates pop culture with "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_culture" title="Mass culture">mass culture</a>" and ideas. This is seen as a commercial culture, <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass-produced</a> for mass consumption by <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> From a Western European perspective, this may be compared to American culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Alternatively, "pop culture" can be defined as an "authentic" culture of the people, but this can be problematic as there are many ways of defining the "people."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2012)">page\xc2\xa0needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Storey argued that there is a political dimension to popular culture; <a href="/wiki/Neo-Gramscianism#Hegemony" title="Neo-Gramscianism">neo-Gramscian hegemony</a> theory "... sees popular culture as a site of struggle between the \'resistance\' of subordinate groups in society and the forces of \'incorporation\' operating in the interests of dominant groups in society." A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postmodern" title="Postmodern">postmodernist</a> approach to popular culture would "no longer recognize the distinction between high and popular culture."</p>\n<p>Storey claims that popular culture emerged from the <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. Studies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> (by Weimann, Barber, or Bristol, for example) locate much of the characteristic vitality of his drama in its participation in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> popular culture, while contemporary practitioners like <a href="/wiki/Dario_Fo" title="Dario Fo">Dario Fo</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_McGrath_(playwright)" title="John McGrath (playwright)">John McGrath</a> use popular culture in its <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gramsci" title="Antonio Gramsci">Gramscian</a> sense that includes ancient folk traditions (the <a href="/wiki/Commedia_dell%27arte" title="Commedia dell\'arte">commedia dell\'arte</a> for example).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (August 2012)">need quotation to verify</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Popular culture is constantly evolving and occurs uniquely in place and <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>. It forms currents and eddies, and represents a complex of mutually interdependent perspectives and values that influence society and its institutions in various ways. For example, certain currents of pop culture may originate from, (or diverge into) a <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">subculture</a>, representing perspectives with which the <a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">mainstream</a> popular culture has only limited familiarity. Items of popular culture most typically appeal to a broad spectrum of the public. Important contemporary contributions for understanding what popular culture means have been given by the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> researcher <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Daus" title="Ronald Daus">Ronald Daus</a>, who studies the impact of extra-European cultures in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, and especially in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore">Folklore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Folklore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Adaptations based on traditional <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> provide a source of popular culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> This early layer of cultural mainstream still persists today, in a form separate from mass-produced popular culture, propagating by word of mouth rather than via mass media, e.g. in the form of <a href="/wiki/Joke" title="Joke">jokes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">urban legend</a>. With the widespread use of the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> from the 1990s, the distinction between mass media and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word-of-mouth" title="Word-of-mouth">word-of-mouth</a> has become blurred.</p>\n<p>Although the folkloric element of popular culture engages heavily with the <a href="/wiki/Commerce" title="Commerce">commercial</a> element, the public has its own tastes and it may not always embrace every cultural or subculture item sold. Moreover, beliefs and opinions about the products of commercial culture spread by <a href="/wiki/Word_of_mouth" title="Word of mouth">word-of-mouth</a>, and become modified in the process in the same manner that folklore evolves.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entertainment">Entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_programs">Television programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Television programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">Television program</a></div>\n<p>A television program is a segment of content intended for broadcast on over-the-air, cable television, or Internet television, other than a commercial, trailer, or any other segment of content not serving as attraction for viewership.</p>\n<p>Television programs may be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fictional" title="Fictional">fictional</a> (as in <a href="/wiki/Television_comedy" title="Television comedy">comedies</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dramatic_programming" title="Dramatic programming">dramas</a>), or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-fictional" title="Non-fictional">non-fictional</a> (as in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Documentary_television" title="Documentary television">documentary</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Television_news" title="Television news">news</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reality_television" title="Reality television">reality television</a>). It may be topical (as in the case of a <a href="/wiki/Local_programming" title="Local programming">local</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newscast" title="Newscast">newscast</a> and some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Made-for-television" title="Made-for-television">made-for-television</a> movies), or historical (as in the case of many documentaries and fictional series). They could be primarily <a href="/wiki/Instructional_television" title="Instructional television">instructional</a> or <a href="/wiki/Educational_television" title="Educational television">educational</a>, or entertaining as is the case in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Situation_comedy" title="Situation comedy">situation comedy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Game_shows" title="Game shows">game shows</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">Popular music</a></div>\n<p><b>Popular music</b> is music with wide appeal<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMusic1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopMusic1-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> that is typically distributed to large audiences through the <a href="/wiki/Music_industry" title="Music industry">music industry</a>. These forms and styles can be enjoyed and performed by people with little or no <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musical_training" title="Musical training">musical training</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMusic1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-PopMusic1-23">[23]</a></sup> It stands in contrast to both <a href="/wiki/Art_music" title="Art music">art music</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMusic2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopMusic2-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMusic4_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopMusic4-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">traditional or "folk" music</a>. Art music was historically disseminated through the performances of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Written_music" title="Written music">written music</a>, although since the beginning of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recording_industry" title="Recording industry">recording industry</a>, it is also disseminated through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sound_recording" title="Sound recording">recordings</a>. Traditional music forms such as early <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> songs or <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymns</a> were passed along orally, or to smaller, local audiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopMusic2_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-PopMusic2-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">Sport</a></div>\n<p>Sport includes all forms of <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">competitive</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_activity" title="Physical activity">physical activity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">games</a> which,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportaccord_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-sportaccord-27">[27]</a></sup> through casual or organised participation, aim to use, maintain or improve physical ability and skills while providing enjoyment to participants, and in some cases, <a href="/wiki/Entertainment" title="Entertainment">entertainment</a> for spectators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-council_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-council-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_branding">Corporate branding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Corporate branding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Corporate_branding" title="Corporate branding">Corporate branding</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Corporate_branding" title="Corporate branding">Corporate branding</a> refers to the practice of promoting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brand_name" title="Brand name">brand name</a> of a corporate entity, as opposed to specific products or services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhilosophyNow_29-0"><a
