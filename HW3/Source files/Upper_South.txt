id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upland_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_Secession_map_1861.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="483" data-file-width="787" height="135" text-align:center;">\xc2\xa0</span>\xc2\xa0 Territories</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>There is a slight difference in usage between the two terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> "Upland South" is usually defined based on landforms, generally referring to the southern <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian Mountains</a> or <a href="/wiki/Appalachia" title="Appalachia">Appalachia</a> (although not the full region defined by the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Regional_Commission" title="Appalachian Regional Commission">Appalachian Regional Commission</a>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ozarks" title="The Ozarks">Ozarks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ouachita_Mountains" title="Ouachita Mountains">Ouachita Mountains</a>, and the plateaus, hills, and basins between the Appalachians and Ozarks, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cumberland_Plateau" title="Cumberland Plateau">Cumberland Plateau</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Plateau" title="Allegheny Plateau">Allegheny Plateau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nashville_Basin" title="Nashville Basin">Nashville Basin</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_region" title="Bluegrass region">Bluegrass Basin</a>, among others. The southern <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_(United_States)" title="Piedmont (United States)">Piedmont</a> region is often considered part of the Upland South, while the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coastal_Plain" title="Atlantic Coastal Plain">Atlantic Coastal Plain</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake</a> region and Carolina\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Carolina_Low_Country" title="South Carolina Low Country">Lowcountry</a>) is generally not.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USA_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1553" data-file-width="1824" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/USA_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification.svg/300px-USA_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/USA_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification.svg/450px-USA_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/USA_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification.svg/600px-USA_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USA_map_of_K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe United States map of K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In contrast, the term "Upper South" tends to be defined politically by <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a>. The term dates to the early 19th century and the rise of the Lower South, which became noted for its differences from the more northerly parts of the American South. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antebellum_era" title="Antebellum era">antebellum</a> times, the term Upper South generally referred to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_state" title="Slave state">Slave states</a> north of the Lower or Deep South.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> era, the term Upper South was often used to refer specifically to the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederate states</a> that did not secede until after the attack on <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fort_Sumter" title="Battle of Fort Sumter">Fort Sumter</a> \xe2\x80\x94 <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>. This can also include the <a href="/wiki/Border_states_(American_Civil_War)" title="Border states (American Civil War)">border states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> in the Upper South.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Today, although many definitions are still based on Civil War-era politics, the term Upper South is often used for all of the American South north of the <a href="/wiki/Deep_South" title="Deep South">Deep South</a>.</p>\n<p>The <i>Encyclop\xc3\xa6dia Britannica</i> defines the Upper South as the states of North Carolina, Tennessee, Virginia, Kentucky, and West Virginia. The Upland South is defined by landforms rather than states but encompasses the same general region. The Upper/Upland South is also described in the <i>Encyclop\xc3\xa6dia Britannica</i> as the "Yeoman South", in contrast to the "Plantation South".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>These two definitions cover the same general area. The Upland South, not being defined by state lines, includes parts of Lower South states, such as northwestern <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Upstate_South_Carolina" title="Upstate South Carolina">Upstate</a>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Georgia" title="North Georgia">North Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Alabama" title="North Alabama">North Alabama</a> (and, in some definitions, <a href="/wiki/Central_Alabama" title="Central Alabama">Central Alabama</a>), and eastern <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>. It also includes parts of some Northern states, such as <a href="/wiki/Southern_Illinois" title="Southern Illinois">Southern Illinois</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_Hills" title="Shawnee Hills">Shawnee Hills</a>), <a href="/wiki/Southern_Indiana" title="Southern Indiana">Southern Indiana</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Ohio" title="Southern Ohio">Southern Ohio</a>. Sometimes northeastern <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> are included as well. In the same way, the Upland South usually does not include parts of some Upper South states, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_embayment" title="Mississippi embayment">Mississippi embayment</a> (which includes eastern Arkansas, the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Bootheel" title="Missouri Bootheel">Missouri Bootheel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Purchase" title="Jackson Purchase">Purchase area</a> of Kentucky, and part of <a href="/wiki/West_Tennessee" title="West Tennessee">West Tennessee</a>), and the coastal lowlands of North Carolina and Virginia.</p>\n<p>Despite these differences, the two terms, Upland South and Upper South, refer to the same general region \xe2\x80\x94 the northern part of the American South \xe2\x80\x94 and are frequently used synonymously. The corresponding terms, Lower South and Deep South, similarly refer to the same general region to the south of, and lower in elevation, than the Upland or Upper South. Likewise, the terms Lower South and Deep South are often used interchangeably.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_culture">History and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upland_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Upland South differs from the Deep South in several significant ways; terrain, histories, economics, demographics, and patterns of settlement.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upland_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Upland South emerged as a distinct region in the late 18th century and early 19th century. Migration and settlement patterns from colonial coastal regions into the interior had been established for many decades, but the scale grew dramatically toward the end of the 18th century. The general pattern was a westward migration from the lowcountry and Piedmont regions of Virginia, North Carolina, and Maryland, as well as a southwestern migration from <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>. Large numbers of European immigrants arrived in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> and followed the <a href="/wiki/Great_Wagon_Road" title="Great Wagon Road">Great Wagon Road</a> west and south into the Appalachian Highlands, via the <a href="/wiki/Great_Appalachian_Valley" title="Great Appalachian Valley">Great Appalachian Valley</a>. These migration streams from Virginia and Pennsylvania resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_Valley" title="Shenandoah Valley">Shenandoah Valley</a> becoming well-settled as early as 1750. The early settlers of the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio Valley</a> were mainly Upland Southerners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Much of the culture of the Upland South originated in southeastern Pennsylvania and spread down the Shenandoah Valley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sisson-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>These migration streams eventually spread through Appalachia and westward through the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Plateau" title="Appalachian Plateau">Appalachian Plateau</a> region into the Ozarks and Ouachitas, and ultimately contributed to the settlement of the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Hill_Country" title="Texas Hill Country">Texas Hill Country</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The main ethnicities of these early settlers included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_American" title="English American">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotch-Irish_Americans" title="Scotch-Irish Americans">Scots-Irish</a>, Scottish, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_American" title="German American">German</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The early culture of the Upland South was influenced by other European ethnicities. For example, the Swedes and Finns of <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">New Sweden</a> \xe2\x80\x94 relatively few in number but pioneering Pennsylvania before the Germans and Irish arrived \xe2\x80\x94 contributed techniques of forest pioneering such as the <a href="/wiki/Log_cabin" title="Log cabin">log cabin</a>, the "zig-zag" <a href="/wiki/Split-rail_fence" title="Split-rail fence">split-rail fence</a>, and frontier methods of <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">shifting cultivation</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Girdling" title="Girdling">girdling</a> trees and using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> to convert forest into temporary crop and pasture land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The pattern of settlement that had begun in the Appalachian foothills was continued and extended through the mountains and highlands to the west and across the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> into the Ozark highland region. Where there was the danger of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indian</a> attacks, people settled at first in clustered "stations", but as danger lessened settlement tended to be in a rural, dispersed, kin-structured pattern, with relatively few towns and cities. These early settlers of the Upland South tended to practice small-scale farming, stock raising, and hunting. This settlement pattern of the Upland South was markedly different from the Deep South and the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a>.</p>\n<p>A significant portion of the 19th century settlers of the Midwest were from the Upland South. The southern Midwest was most heavily settled by Upland Southerners, especially in Missouri, southern Indiana and southern Illinois.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisson_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sisson-7">[7]</a></sup> This early migration to the southern Midwest included many African Americans. They were mainly freed slaves, but slavery was permitted in some places such as <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>, under the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Compromise" title="Missouri Compromise">Missouri Compromise</a> of 1820. In the mid 19th century there were concentrations of African Americans in east-central Indiana, southwest Michigan, and elsewhere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisson_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Sisson-7">[7]</a></sup> Due to their early settlement of the Midwest, Upland Southerners initially controlled territorial and state governments, and played a major role in establishing the political and social culture, such as the <a href="/wiki/Black_Codes_(United_States)" title="Black Codes (United States)">Black Laws</a> of Ohio, Indiana, and Illinois.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisson_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Sisson-7">[7]</a></sup> Over the 19th century the percentage of Upland Southerners fell, especially as large numbers of native born Midwesterners joined the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sisson_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-Sisson-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinct_from_neighboring_regions">Distinct from neighboring regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upland_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distinct from neighboring regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Deep South is generally associated historically with <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>. By 1850 the term "Cotton States" was in common use and the differences between the Deep South (lower) and Upland South (upper) recognized. A key difference was the Deep South\'s <a href="/wiki/Plantations_in_the_American_South" title="Plantations in the American South">plantation</a>-style <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crop</a> agriculture (mainly cotton, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>), using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African American</a> <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slaves</a> working large farms while plantation owners tended to live in towns and cities. This system of plantation farming was originally developed in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">West Indies</a> and introduced to the United States in South Carolina and <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, from where it spread throughout the Deep South, although there were local exceptions wherever conditions did not support the system. The sharp division between town and country, the intensive use of a few cash crops, and the high proportion of slaves, all contrasted with the Upland South. Virginia and its surrounding region stands out as different from both the Upland South and the Deep South. Its history predates the West Indian plantation model, and while <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> was a cash crop from the start, and African slaves became widely used, Virginia did not share many of the Deep South\'s characteristics, such as the early proliferation of towns and cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As a result of the difference in the use of slaves, the boundary between the Upland South and Deep South can still be seen today on maps showing the population percentage of African-Americans. The term <a href="/wiki/Black_Belt_(U.S._region)" title="Black Belt (U.S. region)">Black Belt</a> originally referred to a region of black soil in Alabama that was especially good for cotton farming (the <a href="/wiki/Black_Belt_(region_of_Alabama)" title="Black Belt (region of Alabama)">Black Belt of Alabama</a>), but has become more commonly used today to refer to the region of the South with a high percentage of African-Americans. In contrast, the Upland South was less involved with slavery from the start.</p>\n<p>In addition, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotton_Belt_(region)" title="Cotton Belt (region)">Cotton Belt</a> of the Deep South was controlled by Indians (mainly the <a href="/wiki/Five_Civilized_Tribes" title="Five Civilized Tribes">Five Civilized Tribes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">Cherokee</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creek_people" title="Creek people">Creek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chickasaw" title="Chickasaw">Chickasaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Choctaw" title="Choctaw">Choctaw</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Seminole" title="Seminole">Seminole</a>) powerful enough to keep pioneering settlers from moving into the region. The Deep South\'s cotton boom did not occur until after the Indians were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Removal" title="Indian Removal">forced west</a> in the early 19th century. In contrast, the Upland South, Kentucky and Tennessee especially, were the scene of Indian resistance and pioneering settlement in the late 18th century. Thus the Upland South was already colonized and had established its particular settlement patterns before most of the Deep South was opened to general colonization.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mountain_Homeplace.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2432" data-file-width="3648" between the Upland South and lowlands of the South\'s Atlantic Seaboard and cotton belt often resulted in regional tension and conflict within states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-turner-12">[12]</a></sup> For example, during the late 18th century, the upland "backcountry" of North Carolina grew in population until the Upland Southerners there outnumbered the older, well-established, wealthier coastal populations. In some cases the conflict between the two resulted in warfare, such as <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Regulation" title="War of the Regulation">War of the Regulation</a> in North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turner_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-turner-12">[12]</a></sup> Later, similar processes resulted in divergent populations in states to the west. Northern Alabama, for example, was settled from Tennessee by Upland Southerners, while southern Alabama was one of the core regions of the Deep South cotton boom. During the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> some areas of the Upland South were noted for their resistance to the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America" title="Confederate States of America">Confederacy</a>. The uplands of western Virginia became the state of West Virginia as a result, though half the counties of the new state were Secessionist, and partisan warfare continued throughout the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Kentucky and Missouri remained in the <a href="/wiki/Union_(American_Civil_War)" title="Union (American Civil War)">Union</a> but were torn by internal strife. The southern Appalachian region of <a href="/wiki/East_Tennessee" title="East Tennessee">East Tennessee</a>, parts of western <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and some parts of northern Alabama and northern <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> were widely noted for their pro-Union sentiments.</p>\n<p>The two regions also differ physically. The upland south is dominated by deciduous hardwood forest, in contrast to the Deep South\'s predominantly evergreen pine forests. The upland south is often much hillier than the deep south, due to the Deep South being part of the coastal plain.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upland_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Upland South contains its own sub-regions. The fertile lowlands of the <a href="/wiki/Nashville_Basin" title="Nashville Basin">Nashville Basin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_region" title="Bluegrass region">Bluegrass Basin</a> gave rise to the truly urban cities of <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">Nashville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville</a>, which grew into banking and mercantile centers in the 19th century, home to an elite class of Upland Southerners, including bankers, lawyers, educators, and politicians. The abundance of iron ore, coal, and limestone in the <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_District" title="Birmingham District">Birmingham District</a> transformed a gritty boom town into the iron and steel city of <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a> which anchored the southern urban industrial powerhouse and gave rise to a class of blue collar workers who were the life blood of the city as well a cosmopolitan class of bankers, lawyers, and politicians. Most of the Upland South, however, remained rural in character<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>\n<p>Although historically very rural, the Upland South was one of the nation\'s early industrial regions and continues to be today. <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">Mining</a> of <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, and other minerals has been part of the region\'s economy since the 18th century. As early as 1750 lead and zinc were mined in <a href="/wiki/Wythe_County,_Virginia" title="Wythe County, Virginia">Wythe County, Virginia</a>, and copper was mined and <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelted</a> in <a href="/wiki/Polk_County,_Tennessee" title="Polk County, Tennessee">Polk County, Tennessee</a>. Two major Appalachian <a href="/wiki/Gold_mining" title="Gold mining">goldfields</a> were developed, the first in western North Carolina beginning in 1799. By 1825 <a href="/wiki/Rutherford_County,_North_Carolina" title="Rutherford County, North Carolina">Rutherford County</a> was the center of the nation\'s most extensive <a href="/wiki/Gold_mining" title="Gold mining">gold mining</a>. In 1828 a much richer Gold Strike was made in north Georgia, mostly within what was then the territory of the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_Nation" title="Cherokee Nation">Cherokee Nation</a>. The mining camp of <a href="/wiki/Dahlonega,_Georgia" title="Dahlonega, Georgia">Dahlonega</a> boomed during the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Gold_Rush" title="Georgia Gold Rush">Georgia Gold Rush</a>. Iron <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundries</a> in Virginia and early coal mining operations in central Appalachia date to before 1850.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Furnaces and forges were built in the Appalachians of North <a href="/wiki/Central_Alabama" title="Central Alabama">Central Alabama</a> as early as 1818 and by 1871 would give rise to <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a> later known as the Pittsburgh of the South as it became the leading industrial and transportation hub of the south. Some of these early furnaces and forges were fueled with nearby deposits of <a href="/wiki/Bituminous_coal" title="Bituminous coal">bituminous coal</a>. Similar examples of early urban-industrial areas include Embree\'s Iron Works in East Tennessee (1808), the Red River iron region of <a href="/wiki/Estill_County,_Kentucky" title="Estill County, Kentucky">Estill County, Kentucky</a> (1806-8), and the Jackson Iron Works near <a href="/wiki/Morgantown,_West_Virginia" title="Morgantown, West Virginia">Morgantown, West Virginia</a> (1830). <a href="/wiki/Wheeling,_West_Virginia" title="Wheeling, West Virginia">Wheeling, West Virginia</a> was known as "Nail City" in the 1840s and 1850s. By 1860 Tennessee was the third largest iron producing state in the nation, after Pennsylvania and New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The scale of mining, especially coal mining, increased dramatically after 1870.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The importance of mining and metallurgy can be seen in the many towns with names such as <a href="/wiki/Pigeon_Forge,_Tennessee" title="Pigeon Forge, Tennessee">Pigeon Forge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bloomery,_Hampshire_County,_West_Virginia" title="Bloomery, Hampshire County, West Virginia">Bloomery</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">bloomery</a> being a type of smelting furnace), scattered across the Upland South.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">Logging</a> has also been an important part of the Upland South\'s economy<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The region became the United States\' primary source of timber after railroads allowed large scale industrial logging in the late 19th century and early 20th century. Today, the historic importance of the Upland South\'s forests can be seen in its many national forests, such as <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_National_Forest" title="Cherokee National Forest">Cherokee National Forest</a> in Tennessee, <a href="/wiki/Nantahala_National_Forest" title="Nantahala National Forest">Nantahala National Forest</a> in North Carolina, and <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Boone_National_Forest" title="Daniel Boone National Forest">Daniel Boone National Forest</a> in Kentucky, among many others<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The Upland South\'s terrain and forests, as well as history and culture, occur in parts of states usually associated with the Midwest and Deep South. These areas are often associated with national forests, for example <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain_National_Forest" title="Mark Twain National Forest">Mark Twain National Forest</a> in southern Missouri, <a href="/wiki/Shawnee_National_Forest" title="Shawnee National Forest">Shawnee National Forest</a> in southern Illinois, <a href="/wiki/Hoosier_National_Forest" title="Hoosier National Forest">Hoosier National Forest</a> in southern Indiana, <a href="/wiki/Wayne_National_Forest" title="Wayne National Forest">Wayne National Forest</a> in southeast Ohio, <a href="/wiki/William_B._Bankhead_National_Forest" title="William B. Bankhead National Forest">William B. Bankhead National Forest</a> in northern Alabama, <a href="/wiki/Chattahoochee-Oconee_National_Forest" title="Chattahoochee-Oconee National Forest">Chattahoochee-Oconee National Forest</a> in northern Georgia, <a href="/wiki/Sumter_National_Forest" title="Sumter National Forest">Sumter National Forest</a> in South Carolina, and <a href="/wiki/Ouachita_National_Forest" title="Ouachita National Forest">Ouachita National Forest</a> in Arkansas and Oklahoma<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">Textile</a> mills and industry have been an important factor in the Upland South\'s economy since the time of the Deep South\'s cotton boom<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>\n<p>Today the Upland South contains a diversity of people and economics. Some parts, like the <a href="/wiki/Shenandoah_Valley" title="Shenandoah Valley">Shenandoah Valley</a>, are famous for their rural qualities, while other parts, like the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley" title="Tennessee Valley">Tennessee Valley</a>, are heavily industrialized. <a href="/wiki/Knoxville,_Tennessee" title="Knoxville, Tennessee">Knoxville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville</a> are both centers of industry and scientific research<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_cultural_region">As a cultural region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Upland_South&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: As a cultural region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tobacco_Production_US.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="618" data-file-width="1000" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Tobacco_Production_US.png/250px-Tobacco_Production_US.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Tobacco_Production_US.png/375px-Tobacco_Production_US.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Tobacco_Production_US.png/500px-Tobacco_Production_US.png 2x" width="250"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tobacco_Production_US.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMap of primary areas of Tobacco production in the U.S., with the areas of greater production in dark green and those of lesser production in light green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The Upper South today remains a culture region, with distinct ancestry,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> dialect,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> cuisine, religion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and other characteristics. The heavily rhotacized Upland Southern dialect still predominates in much of West Virginia, Kentucky, Tennessee, and the western portions of North Carolina, Maryland, and Virginia. Noticeable influence can even be found in parts of Deep South states such as northern Georgia and northern Alabama and parts of the southern portions of Missouri as far north as <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>, Indiana, Illinois, Pennsylvania, and Ohio. Like the Deep South, the region is heavily evangelical Protestant with <a href="/wiki/Baptists" title="Baptists">Baptists</a> making up a plurality in the vast majority of counties. The cuisine of the Upper South is generally closely related to the lowland south, excluding southern low-country areas in which the cuisine tends to involve seafood and rice, which are not common in the Upper South.
