(known informally as <b>UM</b> or <b>UMiami</b> or <b>The U</b>) are the <a href="/wiki/Varsity_team" title="Varsity team">varsity sports teams</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a>, located in the <a href="/wiki/Coral_Gables,_Florida" title="Coral Gables, Florida">Coral Gables</a> suburb of <a href="/wiki/Miami_metropolitan_area" title="Miami metropolitan area">Miami, Florida</a>. They compete in the Coastal Division of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Conference" title="Atlantic Coast Conference">Atlantic Coast Conference</a> (ACC). The university fields 15 <a href="/wiki/College_athletics" title="College athletics">athletic</a> teams for 17 varsity sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Men\'s teams compete in <a href="/wiki/College_baseball" title="College baseball">baseball</a>, <a href="/wiki/College_basketball" title="College basketball">basketball</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross_country_running" title="Cross country running">cross-country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diving" title="Diving">diving</a>, <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a>. Women\'s teams compete in basketball, cross-country, <a href="/wiki/Swimming_(sport)" title="Swimming (sport)">swimming</a> and diving, <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/College_rowing" title="College rowing">rowing</a>, <a href="/wiki/College_soccer" title="College soccer">soccer</a>, tennis, track and field, and <a href="/wiki/Volleyball" title="Volleyball">volleyball</a>. UM has approximately equal participation by male and female varsity athletes in these sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DOE_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-DOE-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Team colors are green, orange, and white. The school mascot is <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_the_Ibis" title="Sebastian the Ibis">Sebastian the Ibis</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ibis" title="Ibis">ibis</a> was selected as the school\'s mascot because, according to university legend, it is the last animal to flee an approaching <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> and the first to reappear after the storm, making it a symbol of leadership and courage. The school\'s athletics logo is a simple green and orange, color of an orange tree, letter "U." The school\'s <a href="/wiki/Marching_band" title="Marching band">marching band</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Band_of_the_Hour" title="Band of the Hour">Band of the Hour</a>.</p>\n<p>Aside from being an independent in baseball, the Hurricanes were a full member of the <a href="/wiki/Big_East_Conference_(1979%E2%80%932013)" title="Big East Conference (1979\xe2\x80\x932013)">Big East Conference</a> from 1991 to 2004. href="/wiki/Miami_Hurricanes_men%27s_basketball" title="Miami Hurricanes men\'s basketball">Miami Hurricanes men\'s basketball</a></div>\n<p>The Miami Hurricanes men\'s basketball team has produced three players who are currently on <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA</a> rosters. <a href="/wiki/Rick_Barry" title="Rick Barry">Rick Barry</a>, who played his collegiate basketball at UM, is a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basketball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Basketball Hall of Fame">Basketball Hall of Fame</a>. Barry is the Hurricanes\' only consensus All-American in basketball and led the nation in scoring his senior year with a 37.4 average during the 1964\xe2\x80\x9365 campaign. The team plays its home games at the <a href="/wiki/Watsco_Center" title="Watsco Center">Watsco Center</a> on the University of Miami\'s Coral Gables campus.</p>\n<p>The Board of Trustees attempted to shut the program down in the middle of the 1970 season, which forced <a href="/wiki/Will_Allen_(urban_farmer)" title="Will Allen (urban farmer)">Will Allen</a> to organize his teammates and strike because it was not sufficient notice for the players to transfer schools. They held a press conference and this caught the attention of the national press, and the university actually dropped the program after the 1971 season,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Biography_Today_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Biography_Today-6">[6]</a></sup> with the board citing inadequate facilities, sagging attendance, and serious financial losses as the reasons for the decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The program was revived before the 1985\xe2\x80\x9386 season, though UM would be minimally competitive over the next several years. The program\'s fortunes turned around in 1990 when Miami hired <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Hamilton" title="Leonard Hamilton">Leonard Hamilton</a> as head basketball coach and accepted an invitation to join the <a href="/wiki/Big_East_Conference_(1979%E2%80%932013)" title="Big East Conference (1979\xe2\x80\x932013)">Big East</a>. By the end of the decade, Hamilton had turned UM into one of the better basketball programs in the Big East and had guided UM to three straight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men\'s Division I Basketball Championship">NCAA tournament</a> appearances (1998\xe2\x80\x932000), including a #2 seed in the 1999 tournament and a Sweet 16 appearance in 2000. The 1998 tournament appearance was UM\'s first since 1960.</p>\n<p>Hamilton left at the end of the 2000 season to become head coach of the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">NBA\'s</a> <a href="/wiki/Washington_Wizards" title="Washington Wizards">Washington Wizards</a> and was replaced by Perry Clark. During Clark\'s second season (2001\xe2\x80\x9302) the team won 24 games and a #5 seed in the NCAA tournament. With the 2002\xe2\x80\x9303 season, the team moved into its newly completed on-campus arena, the <a href="/wiki/Watsco_Center" title="Watsco Center">Watsco Center</a>. Despite a win over powerhouse <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Tar_Heels" title="North Carolina Tar Heels">North Carolina</a> to christen the new arena, Clark\'s teams performed woefully over the next two seasons, leading<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to his dismissal following the 2003\xe2\x80\x9304 season (UM\'s last season in the Big East). Clark was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Haith" title="Frank Haith">Frank Haith</a>, whose teams were competitive<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in UM\'s first two seasons as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Conference" title="Atlantic Coast Conference">Atlantic Coast Conference</a>.</p>\n<p>In the 2007/2008 season, after being picked to finish last in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Conference" title="Atlantic Coast Conference">Atlantic Coast Conference</a> the Hurricanes finished the year 23\xe2\x80\x9311 (8\xe2\x80\x938 in the ACC) and reached the second round of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA_Men%27s_Division_I_Basketball_Championship" title="NCAA Men\'s Division I Basketball Championship">NCAA Tournament</a> before falling to the second seeded <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a>. This was the team\'s first NCAA tournament bid since the 2001\xe2\x80\x932002 season.</p>\n<p>For the 2009/2010 season, Miami had a winning record overall (20\xe2\x80\x9313), but finished in last place in the ACC with a record of 4\xe2\x80\x9312.</p>\n<p>On April 4, 2011, Miami Coach <a href="/wiki/Frank_Haith" title="Frank Haith">Frank Haith</a> accepted a Head Coaching position at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Missouri" title="University of Missouri">University of Missouri</a>.</p>\n<p>On April 22, 2011, <a href="/wiki/George_Mason_Patriots" title="George Mason Patriots">George Mason Patriots</a> Head Coach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jim_Larranaga" title="Jim Larranaga">Jim Larranaga</a> accepted the Head Coaching position after coaching the Patriots for 14 seasons.</p>\n<p>For the \'12\xe2\x80\x93\'13 season, Miami (FL) knocked down No. 1 Duke 90-63, won their first 13 ACC games, and attained the highest AP ranking in school history, attaining a #2 ranking.
