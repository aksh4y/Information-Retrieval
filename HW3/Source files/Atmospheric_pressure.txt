the pressure within the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere of Earth</a> (or that of another planet). In most circumstances atmospheric pressure is closely approximated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_pressure" title="Fluid pressure">hydrostatic pressure</a> caused by the <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">air</a> above the measurement point. As <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">elevation</a> increases, there is less overlying atmospheric mass, so that atmospheric pressure decreases with increasing elevation. Pressure measures force per unit area, with <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI units</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascals</a> (1 Pa = 1 N/m<sup>2</sup>). On average, a column of air one square centimetre [cm<sup>2</sup>] (0.16\xc2\xa0sq\xc2\xa0in) in cross-section, measured from <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a> to the top of the Earth\'s atmosphere, has a <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> of about 1.03 kilograms (2.3\xc2\xa0lb) and weight of about 10.1 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">newtons</a> (2.3\xc2\xa0<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pound-force" title="Pound-force">lb<sub>f</sub></a>). That weight (across one square centimeter) is a pressure of 10.1 N/cm<sup>2</sup> or 101\xc2\xa0kN/m<sup>2</sup> (kPa). A column 1 square inch (6.5\xc2\xa0cm<sup>2</sup>) in cross-section would have a weight of about 14.7\xc2\xa0lb (6.7\xc2\xa0kg) or about 65.4\xc2\xa0N.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. This is the atmospheric pressure normally given in weather reports on radio, television, and newspapers or on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. When <a href="/wiki/Barometer" title="Barometer">barometers</a> in the home are set to match the local weather reports, they measure pressure adjusted to sea level, not the actual local atmospheric pressure.</p>\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Altimeter_setting" title="Altimeter setting">altimeter setting</a></i> in aviation, is an atmospheric pressure adjustment.</p>\n<p>Average <i>sea-level pressure</i> is 1013.25\xc2\xa0mbar (101.325\xc2\xa0kPa; 29.921\xc2\xa0inHg; 760.00\xc2\xa0mmHg). In aviation weather reports (<a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a>), <a href="/wiki/QNH" title="QNH">QNH</a> is transmitted around the world in millibars or hectopascals (1 hectopascal = 1 millibar), except in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> where it is reported in inches (to two decimal places) of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>. The United States and Canada also report <i>sea level pressure</i> SLP, which is adjusted to sea level by a different method, in the remarks section, not in the internationally transmitted part of the code, in hectopascals or millibars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> However, in Canada\'s public weather reports, sea level pressure is instead reported in kilopascals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the US weather code remarks, three digits are all that are transmitted; decimal points and the one or two most significant digits are omitted: 1013.2\xc2\xa0mbar (101.32\xc2\xa0kPa) is transmitted as 132; 1000.0\xc2\xa0mbar (100.00\xc2\xa0kPa) is transmitted as 000; 998.7 mbar is transmitted as 987; etc. The highest <i>sea-level pressure</i> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> occurs in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Siberian_High" title="Siberian High">Siberian High</a> often attains a <i>sea-level pressure</i> above 1050\xc2\xa0mbar (105\xc2\xa0kPa; 31\xc2\xa0inHg), with record highs close to 1085\xc2\xa0mbar (108.5\xc2\xa0kPa; 32.0\xc2\xa0inHg). The lowest measurable <i>sea-level pressure</i> is found at the centers of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, with a record low of 870\xc2\xa0mbar (87\xc2\xa0kPa; 26\xc2\xa0inHg) (see <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure#Records" title="Atmospheric pressure">Atmospheric pressure id="Local_variation">Local variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Local variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wilma1315z-051019-1kg12.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1920" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wilma1315z-051019-1kg12.jpg/220px-Wilma1315z-051019-1kg12.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wilma1315z-051019-1kg12.jpg/330px-Wilma1315z-051019-1kg12.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Wilma1315z-051019-1kg12.jpg/440px-Wilma1315z-051019-1kg12.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wilma1315z-051019-1kg12.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> on 19 October 2005; 882\xc2\xa0hPa (12.79\xc2\xa0psi) in the storm\'s eye</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Atmospheric pressure varies widely on Earth, and these changes are important in studying <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>. See <a href="/wiki/Pressure_system" title="Pressure system">pressure system</a> for the effects of air pressure variations on weather.</p>\n<p>Atmospheric pressure shows a diurnal or semidiurnal (twice-daily) cycle caused by global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_tides" title="Atmospheric tides">atmospheric tides</a>. This effect is strongest in tropical zones, with an amplitude of a few millibars, and almost zero in polar areas. These variations have two superimposed cycles, a circadian (24\xc2\xa0h) cycle and semi-circadian (12\xc2\xa0h) cycle.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The highest adjusted-to-sea level barometric pressure ever recorded on Earth (above 750 meters) was 1085.7\xc2\xa0hPa (32.06\xc2\xa0inHg) measured in <a href="/wiki/Tosontsengel,_Zavkhan" title="Tosontsengel, Zavkhan">Tosontsengel, Mongolia</a> on 19 December 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo.asu.edu_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-wmo.asu.edu-5">[5]</a></sup> The highest adjusted-to-sea level barometric pressure ever recorded (below 750 meters) was at Agata in <a href="/wiki/Evenk_Autonomous_Okrug" title="Evenk Autonomous Okrug">Evenk Autonomous Okrug</a>, Russia (66\xc2\xb053\xe2\x80\x99N, 93\xc2\xb028\xe2\x80\x99E, elevation: 261\xc2\xa0m, 856\xc2\xa0ft) on 31 December 1968 of 1083.8\xc2\xa0hPa (32.005\xc2\xa0inHg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The discrimination is due to the problematic assumptions (assuming a standard lapse rate) associated with reduction of sea level from high elevations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmo.asu.edu_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-wmo.asu.edu-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>, the lowest place on Earth at 430 metres (1,410\xc2\xa0ft) below sea level, has a correspondingly high typical atmospheric pressure of 1065 hPa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The lowest non-tornadic atmospheric pressure ever measured was 870\xc2\xa0hPa (0.858\xc2\xa0atm; 25.69\xc2\xa0inHg), set on 12 October 1979, during <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">Typhoon Tip</a> in the western Pacific Ocean. The measurement was based on an instrumental observation made from a reconnaissance aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FAQ1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_based_on_depth_of_water">Measurement based on depth of water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Measurement based on depth of water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>One atmosphere (101\xc2\xa0kPa or 14.7\xc2\xa0psi) is also the pressure caused by the weight of a column of fresh water of approximately 10.3\xc2\xa0m (33.8\xc2\xa0ft). Thus, a diver 10.3\xc2\xa0m underwater experiences a pressure of about 2 atmospheres (1\xc2\xa0atm of air plus 1\xc2\xa0atm of water). Conversely, 10.3\xc2\xa0m is the maximum height to which water can be raised using <a href="/wiki/Suction" title="Suction">suction</a> under standard atmospheric conditions.</p>\n<p>Low pressures such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> lines are sometimes specified in <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_water" title="Inch of water">inches of water</a>, typically written as <i>w.c.</i> (water column) or <i>w.g.</i> (inches water gauge). A typical gas-using residential appliance in the US is rated for a maximum of 14\xc2\xa0w.c., which is approximately 35 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HPa" title="HPa">hPa</a>. Similar metric units with a wide variety of names and notation based on <a href="/wiki/Millimeters,_water_gauge" title="Millimeters, water gauge">millimetres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centimetre_of_water" title="Centimetre of water">centimetres</a> or metres are now less commonly used.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Boiling_point_of_water">Boiling point of water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Boiling point of water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kochendes_wasser02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="644" data-file-width="674" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kochendes_wasser02.jpg/220px-Kochendes_wasser02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kochendes_wasser02.jpg/330px-Kochendes_wasser02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Kochendes_wasser02.jpg/440px-Kochendes_wasser02.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kochendes_wasser02.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boiling_water" title="Boiling water">Boiling water</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Pure water <a href="/wiki/Boiling" title="Boiling">boils</a> at 100\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (212\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F) at earth\'s standard atmospheric pressure. The boiling point is the temperature at which the <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> is equal to the atmospheric pressure around the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Because of this, the boiling point of water is lower at lower pressure and higher at higher pressure. Cooking at high elevations, therefore, requires adjustments to recipes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A rough approximation of elevation can be obtained by measuring the temperature at which water boils; in the mid-19th century, this method was used by explorers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_and_maps">Measurement and maps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Atmospheric_pressure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Measurement and maps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>An important application of the knowledge that atmospheric pressure varies directly with altitude was in determining the height of hills and mountains thanks to the availability of reliable pressure measurement devices. While in 1774 Maskelyne was confirming Newton\'s theory of gravitation at and on Schiehallion in Scotland (using plumb bob deviation to show the effect of "gravity") and accurately measure elevation, William Roy using barometric pressure was able to confirm his height determinations, the agreement being to within one meter (3.28 feet). This was then a useful tool for survey work and map making and long has continued to be useful. It was part of the "application of science" which gave practical people the insight that applied science could easily and relatively cheaply be "useful".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
