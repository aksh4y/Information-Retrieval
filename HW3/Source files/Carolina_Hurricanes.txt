<b>Carolina Hurricanes</b> are a professional <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> team based in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a>. They are members of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Division" title="Metropolitan Division">Metropolitan Division</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Conference_(NHL)" title="Eastern Conference (NHL)">Eastern Conference</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> (NHL). The Hurricanes play their home games at the 18,680-seat <a href="/wiki/PNC_Arena" title="PNC Arena">PNC Arena</a>. The franchise was formed in 1971 as the New England Whalers of the <a href="/wiki/World_Hockey_Association" title="World Hockey Association">World Hockey Association</a> (WHA), and joined the NHL in 1979 as part of the <a href="/wiki/NHL%E2%80%93WHA_merger" title="NHL\xe2\x80\x93WHA merger">NHL\xe2\x80\x93WHA merger</a>, renaming themselves the <a href="/wiki/Hartford_Whalers" title="Hartford Whalers">Hartford Whalers</a>. The team relocated to North Carolina in 1997 and won its first <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cup" title="Stanley Cup">Stanley Cup</a> during the <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_NHL_season" title="2005\xe2\x80\x9306 NHL season">2005\xe2\x80\x9306 season</a>, beating the <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Oilers" title="Edmonton Oilers">Edmonton Oilers</a>, four games to three.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div With the increasing difficulty of scheduling games at Boston Garden (owned by the NHL rival <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">Boston Bruins</a>), the owners decided to move the team to <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford, Connecticut</a>, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/1974%E2%80%9375_WHA_season" title="1974\xe2\x80\x9375 WHA season">1974\xe2\x80\x9375 season</a>. While waiting for the completion of a new arena in Hartford, the Whalers played the first part of the season at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Big_E_Coliseum" title="The Big E Coliseum">The Big E Coliseum</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Springfield,_Massachusetts" title="West Springfield, Massachusetts">West Springfield, Massachusetts</a>. On January 11, 1975, the team played its first game in front of a sellout crowd at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartford_Civic_Center_Coliseum" title="Hartford Civic Center Coliseum">Hartford Civic Center Coliseum</a>, and would maintain its home there through 1997.</p>\n<p>As one of the most stable WHA teams, the Whalers, along with the <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Oilers" title="Edmonton Oilers">Edmonton Oilers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Nordiques" title="Quebec Nordiques">Quebec Nordiques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_Jets_(1972%E2%80%9396)" title="Winnipeg Jets (1972\xe2\x80\x9396)">Winnipeg Jets</a>, were admitted to the NHL when the rival leagues merged in <a href="/wiki/1978%E2%80%9379_NHL_season" title="1978\xe2\x80\x9379 NHL season">1979</a>. However, under pressure from the extant NHL team in the <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> area, the Boston Bruins, the Whalers were compelled to rename the team the <b><a href="/wiki/Hartford_Whalers" title="Hartford Whalers">Hartford Whalers</a></b>. The Whalers were never as successful in the NHL as they had been in the WHA, recording only three winning seasons. They peaked in the mid-to-late 1980s, winning their only playoff series in <a href="/wiki/1985%E2%80%9386_NHL_season" title="1985\xe2\x80\x9386 NHL season">1986</a> over the Nordiques before bowing out in the second round to the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a>, taking the Habs to overtime of Game 7 in the process. The <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_NHL_season" title="1986\xe2\x80\x9387 NHL season">next year</a>, the club secured the regular-season <a href="/wiki/Adams_Division" title="Adams Division">Adams Division</a> title, only to fall to the Nordiques in six games in the first round of the playoffs. In <a href="/wiki/1991%E2%80%9392_NHL_season" title="1991\xe2\x80\x9392 NHL season">1992</a>, the Whalers made the playoffs for the final time, but were bounced in the first round in seven games by the Canadiens.</p>\n<p>The organization retains many Whaler connections among its off-ice personnel; in addition to many members of executive management and the coaching staff, broadcasters <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Kaiton" title="Chuck Kaiton">Chuck Kaiton</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Forslund" title="John Forslund">John Forslund</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tripp_Tracy" title="Tripp Tracy">Tripp Tracy</a> (at the time a minor-league player), and equipment managers <a href="/wiki/Wally_Tatomir" title="Wally Tatomir">Wally Tatomir</a>, Skip Cunningham and Bob Gorman all made the move to North Carolina with the team. Finally, the old goal horn from the Hartford Civic Center remains in use at PNC Arena.</p>\n<h3><span id="Move_to_North_Carolina_(1997\xe2\x80\x932001)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_North_Carolina_.281997.E2.80.932001.29">Move to North Carolina (1997\xe2\x80\x932001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carolina_Hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Move to North Carolina (1997\xe2\x80\x932001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Whalers were plagued for most of their existence by limited marketability. Hartford was the smallest American market in the league, and was located on the traditional dividing line between the home territories for New York City and Boston teams. It did not help matters that the Hartford Civic Center was one of the smallest arenas in the league, seating just over 15,000 people for hockey. The Whalers\' off-ice problems were magnified when the start of the 1990s triggered a spike in player salaries.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Karmanos.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="228" data-file-width="175" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c9/Karmanos.jpeg" width="175"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Karmanos.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nIn May 1997, franchise owner <a href="/wiki/Peter_Karmanos_Jr." title="Peter Karmanos Jr.">Peter Karmanos Jr.</a> announced that the team would move to North Carolina.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Despite assurances made when he purchased the team in 1994 that the Whalers would remain in Hartford at least through 1998, in March 1997, owner <a href="/wiki/Peter_Karmanos_Jr." title="Peter Karmanos Jr.">Peter Karmanos</a> announced that the team would move elsewhere after the <a href="/wiki/1996%E2%80%9397_NHL_season" title="1996\xe2\x80\x9397 NHL season">1996\xe2\x80\x9397 season</a> because of the team\'s inability to negotiate a satisfactory construction and lease package for a new arena in Hartford. On May 6, 1997, Karmanos announced that the Whalers would move to the <a href="/wiki/Research_Triangle" title="Research Triangle">Research Triangle</a> area of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and the new <a href="/wiki/PNC_Arena" title="PNC Arena">Entertainment and Sports Arena</a> (ESA) in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh</a>. Due to the relatively short time frame for the move, Karmanos himself thought of and decided upon the new name for the club, the <b>Carolina Hurricanes</b>, rather than holding a contest as is sometimes done. Later that summer, the team dropped the Whalers\' colors of blue, green and silver for a new black-and-red scheme, matching the colors of the <a href="/wiki/NC_State_Wolfpack" title="NC State Wolfpack">North Carolina State University Wolfpack</a>, with whose men\'s basketball team they would share the arena in Raleigh. The Hurricanes inherited the Whalers\' place in the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Division_(NHL)" title="Northeast Division (NHL)">Northeast Division</a>.</p>\n<p>Unfortunately for the team, the ESA would not be complete for two more years. The only arena in the Triangle area with an ice plant was 45-year-old <a href="/wiki/Dorton_Arena" title="Dorton Arena">Dorton Arena</a>; at 5,100 seats, it was completely inadequate even for temporary use. The Hurricanes were thus forced to play home games in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greensboro" title="Greensboro">Greensboro</a>, 90 minutes west of Raleigh, for their first two seasons after the move. However, the team would be based in Raleigh and practice in nearby <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough,_North_Carolina" title="Hillsborough, North Carolina">Hillsborough</a>\xe2\x80\x94effectively saddling the Hurricanes with 82 road games for the next two years. This choice was disastrous for the franchise\'s attendance and reputation. With a capacity of over 21,000 people for hockey, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greensboro_Coliseum" title="Greensboro Coliseum">Greensboro Coliseum</a> was the highest-capacity arena in the NHL. However, Triangle-area fans balked at making the 80-mile drive down <a href="/wiki/Interstate_40" title="Interstate 40">I-40</a> to Greensboro. Likewise, fans from the <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_Triad" title="Piedmont Triad">Piedmont Triad</a> mostly refused to support a lame-duck team that had displaced the popular <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Monarchs" title="Carolina Monarchs">Greensboro/Carolina Monarchs</a> minor-league franchise. As a result, while the opening game drew a sellout (and is still the largest home crowd in franchise history), most games in Greensboro attracted crowds of 10,000 or fewer. The crowds looked even smaller than that in the cavernous environment. Furthermore, only 29 out of 82 games were televised (over-the-air and cable combined), and radio play-by-play coverage on <a href="/wiki/WPTF" title="WPTF">WPTF</a> was often pre-empted by Wolfpack basketball (for whose broadcasts WPTF was the flagship station), leaving these games totally unavailable to those who did not have a ticket. With by far the smallest season-ticket base in the NHL and attendance figures routinely well below the league average, <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a></i> ran a story titled "Natural Disaster,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/ESPN" title="ESPN">ESPN</a> anchors mocked the "Green Acres" of empty seats; in a 2006 interview, Karmanos admitted that "as it turns out, [Greensboro] was probably a mistake."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Under the circumstances, the Hurricanes managed to stay competitive, but still finished last in the Northeast Division with 74 points, nine points out of the playoffs.</p>\n<p>For <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_NHL_season" title="1998\xe2\x80\x9399 NHL season">1998\xe2\x80\x9399</a>, the Hurricanes curtained off most of the upper deck, lowering the Coliseum\'s listed capacity to about 12,000. Attendance continued to lag; most games attracted crowds of well under 10,000. On the ice, however, the \'Canes were now out of the doldrums; led by the return of longtime Whalers captain <a href="/wiki/Ron_Francis" title="Ron Francis">Ron Francis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keith_Primeau" title="Keith Primeau">Keith Primeau</a>\'s 30 goals and <a href="/wiki/Gary_Roberts_(ice_hockey)" title="Gary Roberts (ice hockey)">Gary Roberts</a>\' 178 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penalty_(hockey)" title="Penalty (hockey)">penalty</a> minutes, they tallied their first winning season and playoff appearance since 1992. They also won the new <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Division_(NHL)" title="Southeast Division (NHL)">Southeast Division</a> by eight points, only their second division title as an NHL team (following the 1987 Adams Division title as the Whalers). However, tragedy struck hours after the team\'s first-round loss to the Bruins, when <a href="/wiki/Defenceman" title="Defenceman">defenceman</a> <a href="/wiki/Steve_Chiasson" title="Steve Chiasson">Steve Chiasson</a> was thrown from his pickup truck and killed in a single-vehicle drunk-driving accident.</p>\n<p>Despite their move to the brand-new ESA, the Hurricanes played lackluster hockey in <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_NHL_season" title="1999\xe2\x80\x932000 NHL season">1999\xe2\x80\x932000</a>, failing to make the playoffs. In <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_NHL_season" title="2000\xe2\x80\x9301 NHL season">2000\xe2\x80\x9301</a>, however, they claimed the eighth seed, nosing out the Boston Bruins, and landed a first-round match-up with the defending champions, the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Devils" title="New Jersey Devils">New Jersey Devils</a>. Although the Devils eliminated the Hurricanes in six games, the series is seen as the real "arrival" of hockey in the Triangle. Down 3\xe2\x80\x930 in the series, the Hurricanes extended it to a sixth game, thereby becoming only the tenth team in NHL history to do so. Game 6 in Raleigh featured their best playoff crowd that year, as well as their loudest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Despite the 5\xe2\x80\x931 loss, Carolina was given a standing ovation by their home crowd as the game ended, erasing some of the doubts that the city would not warm up to the team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="Stanley_Cup_Finals_and_slow_starts_(2001\xe2\x80\x932006)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Stanley_Cup_Finals_and_slow_starts_.282001.E2.80.932006.29">Stanley Cup Finals and slow starts (2001\xe2\x80\x932006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carolina_Hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stanley Cup Finals and slow starts (2001\xe2\x80\x932006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Hurricanes made national waves for the first time in the <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_NHL_season" title="2001\xe2\x80\x9302 NHL season">2002</a> <a href="/wiki/2002_Stanley_Cup_playoffs" title="2002 Stanley Cup playoffs">playoffs</a>. They survived a late charge from the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Washington Capitals</a> to win the division, but expectations were low entering the first round against the defending <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Conference_(NHL)" title="Eastern Conference (NHL)">Eastern Conference</a> champion New Jersey Devils. However, <a href="/wiki/Art%C5%ABrs_Irbe" title="Art\xc5\xabrs Irbe">Art\xc5\xabrs Irbe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Weekes" title="Kevin Weekes">Kevin Weekes</a> were solid in goal, and the Hurricanes won two games in overtime as they defeated the Devils in six games. Their second-round matchup was against the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a>, who were riding a wave of emotion after their captain <a href="/wiki/Saku_Koivu" title="Saku Koivu">Saku Koivu</a>\'s return from cancer treatment. In the third period of Game 4 in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, down 2\xe2\x80\x931 in the series and 3\xe2\x80\x930 in the game, Carolina would tie the game and then win on <a href="/wiki/Niclas_Wallin" title="Niclas Wallin">Niclas Wallin</a>\'s overtime goal. The game became known to Hurricanes fans as the "Miracle at <a href="/wiki/Bell_Centre" title="Bell Centre">Molson</a>"; Carolina won the next two games by a combined 13\xe2\x80\x933 margin over a dejected Habs club to take the series.</p>\n<p>In the Eastern Conference Finals, Carolina met the heavily-favored <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Maple_Leafs" title="Toronto Maple Leafs">Toronto Maple Leafs</a>. In Game 6 in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, the Leafs\' <a href="/wiki/Mats_Sundin" title="Mats Sundin">Mats Sundin</a> tied the game with 22 seconds remaining to send it to overtime, where Carolina\'s <a href="/wiki/Martin_G%C3%A9linas" title="Martin G\xc3\xa9linas">Martin Gelinas</a> would score to send the franchise to their first <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Cup" title="Stanley Cup">Stanley Cup</a> Finals appearance. During this series, several Hurricanes fan traditions drew hockey-wide media attention for the first time: fans met the team at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raleigh-Durham_International_Airport" title="Raleigh-Durham International Airport">the airport</a> on the return from every road trip, and echoed <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a>-season habits honed for games <a href="/wiki/Carter%E2%80%93Finley_Stadium" title="Carter\xe2\x80\x93Finley Stadium">across the parking lot</a> by hosting massive <a href="/wiki/Tailgate_party" title="Tailgate party">tailgate parties</a> before each home game, a relative novelty in the cold-weather-centric NHL. Inside the building, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">CBC</a>\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don_Cherry_(ice_hockey)" title="Don Cherry (ice hockey)">Don Cherry</a> lauded the RBC Center as "the loudest building in the NHL", praise that would be echoed in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/2002_Stanley_Cup_Finals" title="2002 Stanley Cup Finals">Stanley Cup Finals</a>, Carolina would face the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Red_Wings" title="Detroit Red Wings">Detroit Red Wings</a>, thought to be the prohibitive favorite all year. Though the \'Canes stunned the Wings in Game 1, when <a href="/wiki/Ron_Francis" title="Ron Francis">Ron Francis</a> scored in the first minute of overtime, Detroit stormed back to win the next four games. Game 3 in Raleigh featured a triple-overtime thriller eventually won by Detroit\'s <a href="/wiki/Igor_Larionov" title="Igor Larionov">Igor Larionov</a>, the oldest player to score a last-round goal.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eric_Staal_2013-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="1441" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Eric_Staal_2013-2.jpg/220px-Eric_Staal_2013-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Eric_Staal_2013-2.jpg/330px-Eric_Staal_2013-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Eric_Staal_2013-2.jpg/440px-Eric_Staal_2013-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eric_Staal_2013-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Eric_Staal" title="Eric Staal">Eric Staal</a> was drafted by the Hurricanes in the <a href="/wiki/2003_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2003 NHL Entry Draft">2003 NHL Entry Draft</a>. He was later named team captain in 2010.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The Hurricanes looked poised to pick up where they left off in <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_NHL_season" title="2002\xe2\x80\x9303 NHL season">2002\xe2\x80\x9303</a>, but never recovered from a 10-loss January and finished dead last in the league with 61 points. After a similarly slow start to the <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_NHL_season" title="2003\xe2\x80\x9304 NHL season">2003\xe2\x80\x9304 season</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Maurice" title="Paul Maurice">Paul Maurice</a>, who had been the team\'s coach since midway through their next-to-last season in Hartford, was fired and replaced with former <a href="/wiki/New_York_Islanders" title="New York Islanders">New York Islanders</a> bench boss <a href="/wiki/Peter_Laviolette" title="Peter Laviolette">Peter Laviolette</a>. Under Laviolette, Weekes remained tough, but the offense was suspect; center <a href="/wiki/Josef_Va%C5%A1%C3%AD%C4%8Dek" title="Josef Va\xc5\xa1\xc3\xad\xc4\x8dek">Josef Vasicek</a> led the team with a mere 19 goals and 26 assists for 45 points. Many of the new fans attracted to the team (and to hockey itself) during the 2002 playoff run lost interest and attendance declined. One of the few positive results of these losing years, however, was the team\'s drafting of future star <a href="/wiki/Eric_Staal" title="Eric Staal">Eric Staal</a> in <a href="/wiki/2003_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2003 NHL Entry Draft">2003</a>.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stanley_Cup_champions">Stanley Cup champions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carolina_Hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Stanley Cup champions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The outcome of the <a href="/wiki/2004%E2%80%9305_NHL_lockout" title="2004\xe2\x80\x9305 NHL lockout">2004\xe2\x80\x9305 NHL lockout</a> led to the shrinking of the payroll to $26\xc2\xa0million. The Hurricanes, however, turned out to be one of the NHL\'s biggest surprises, turning in the best season in the franchise\'s 34-year history. They finished the regular season with a 52\xe2\x80\x9322\xe2\x80\x938 record and 112 points, shattering the previous franchise records of 94 points (in the WHA) set by the 1972\xe2\x80\x9373 Whalers and 93 points (in the NHL) set in <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_NHL_season" title="1986\xe2\x80\x9387 NHL season">1986\xe2\x80\x9387</a>. It was the first time ever that the franchise had passed the 50-win and 100-point plateaus. The 112-point figure was good for fourth overall in the league, easily their highest overall finish as an NHL team (tied with the third-overall <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Stars" title="Dallas Stars">Dallas Stars</a> in points, but with one fewer win than the Stars) and second in the East (one point behind the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Senators" title="Ottawa Senators">Ottawa Senators</a>). The Hurricanes also ran away with their third Southeast Division title, finishing 20 points ahead of the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Lightning" title="Tampa Bay Lightning">Tampa Bay Lightning</a>. Attendance increased from 2003\xe2\x80\x9304, averaging just under 15,600 per game, and the team made a profit for the first time since the move from Hartford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the playoffs, after losing the first two games of the conference quarterfinal series against the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Canadiens" title="Montreal Canadiens">Montreal Canadiens</a>, Laviolette lifted goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gerber" title="Martin Gerber">Martin Gerber</a> \xe2\x80\x93 who had been struggling to regain his form after playing through a bout of intestinal flu \xe2\x80\x93 in favor of rookie <a href="/wiki/Cam_Ward" title="Cam Ward">Cam Ward</a>. The Hurricanes went on to win both games in Montreal, tying up the playoff series and turning the momentum around, winning the series on a Game 6 overtime goal by <a href="/wiki/Cory_Stillman" title="Cory Stillman">Cory Stillman</a>. Carolina then faced the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Devils" title="New Jersey Devils">New Jersey Devils</a> in the conference semi-finals, which proved surprisingly one-sided, as the Hurricanes beat the Devils in five games. Stillman struck again, once again scoring the series-winning goal.</p>\n<p>In the Eastern Conference Finals, the Hurricanes faced the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Sabres" title="Buffalo Sabres">Buffalo Sabres</a>, who had finished just one spot behind the Hurricanes in the overall standings. The contentious series saw both coaches \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Lindy_Ruff" title="Lindy Ruff">Lindy Ruff</a> and Laviolette \xe2\x80\x93 taking public verbal shots at each other\'s team, but in the deciding Game 7, the Hurricanes rallied with three goals in the third to win by a score of 4\xe2\x80\x932. <a href="/wiki/Rod_Brind%27Amour" title="Rod Brind\'Amour">Rod Brind\'Amour</a> scored the game winner as the Hurricanes reached the Stanley Cup finals for the second time in team history.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RBC_Center_Stanley_Cup_Championship.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1712" data-file-width="2288" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/RBC_Center_Stanley_Cup_Championship.jpg/220px-RBC_Center_Stanley_Cup_Championship.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/RBC_Center_Stanley_Cup_Championship.jpg/330px-RBC_Center_Stanley_Cup_Championship.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/RBC_Center_Stanley_Cup_Championship.jpg/440px-RBC_Center_Stanley_Cup_Championship.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RBC_Center_Stanley_Cup_Championship.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe Hurricanes celebrate following their <a href="/wiki/Game_seven" title="Game seven">game seven</a> victory in the <a href="/wiki/2006_Stanley_Cup_Finals" title="2006 Stanley Cup Finals">2006 Stanley Cup Finals</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/2006_Stanley_Cup_Finals" title="2006 Stanley Cup Finals">Stanley Cup Finals</a> were against the <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Oilers" title="Edmonton Oilers">Edmonton Oilers</a>, the first time in NHL history that two former WHA franchises had played against one another in the finals. The Hurricanes rallied from a 3\xe2\x80\x930 deficit in Game 1 to win 5\xe2\x80\x934 after Rod Brind\'Amour scored with 30 seconds left. In Game 2, the Hurricanes shelled the Oilers 5\xe2\x80\x930 to take a two-game lead. The Oilers won Game 3 in <a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton</a>, 2\xe2\x80\x931, as <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Smyth" title="Ryan Smyth">Ryan Smyth</a> scored the game-winning goal with 2:47 left to play. Carolina rebounded in Game 4 with a 2\xe2\x80\x931 victory, and came home with a chance to win the Cup on home ice. However, Game 5 saw the Oilers come back with a stunning 4\xe2\x80\x933 overtime win on a shorthanded breakaway by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pisani" title="Fernando Pisani">Fernando Pisani</a>. In Game 6 in Edmonton, Carolina was soundly defeated 4\xe2\x80\x930; the only bright point for the Hurricanes was the return of forward <a href="/wiki/Erik_Cole" title="Erik Cole">Erik Cole</a> from a broken neck that had sidelined him since March. In Game 7, before the second-largest home crowd in franchise history (18,978), the Hurricanes won 3\xe2\x80\x931, sealing the Hurricanes\' first Stanley Cup championship in franchise history. Ward was honored with the <a href="/wiki/Conn_Smythe_Trophy" title="Conn Smythe Trophy">Conn Smythe Trophy</a> for the playoffs\' <a href="/wiki/Most_valuable_player" title="Most valuable player">most valuable player</a>, becoming just the fourth rookie to be honored with the award. Several Hurricanes raised the Cup for the first time in their long NHL careers; Rod Brind\'Amour and <a href="/wiki/Bret_Hedican" title="Bret Hedican">Bret Hedican</a> had both played over 15 years without winning the Cup, while <a href="/wiki/Glen_Wesley" title="Glen Wesley">Glen Wesley</a> \xe2\x80\x93 the last remaining Hartford Whaler on the Hurricanes\' roster \xe2\x80\x93 had waited 18 seasons.</p>\n<p>The Hurricanes Stanley Cup championship marked the first professional major league sports title for a team from North Carolina. As well, they were the first NHL team to win the Stanley Cup despite losing at least nine playoff games in that year; the 2011 <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">Boston Bruins</a>, the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Kings" title="Los Angeles Kings">Los Angeles Kings</a>, and the 2017 <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Pittsburgh Penguins</a> are the only other teams to have accomplished the feat.</p>\n<h3><span id="Post-championship_slump_(2006\xe2\x80\x93present)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-championship_slump_.282006.E2.80.93present.29">Post-championship slump (2006\xe2\x80\x93present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carolina_Hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-championship slump (2006\xe2\x80\x93present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Hurricanes were unable to follow up their success. Losing four players to free agency in the offseason and 222-man games to injury during <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_NHL_season" title="2006\xe2\x80\x9307 NHL season">2006\xe2\x80\x9307</a>, the team struggled throughout the regular season,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and once eliminated in the last game, the Hurricanes finished third in the Southeast and 11th overall in the Eastern Conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> This finish made them the first champions since the 1938\xe2\x80\x9339 <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Blackhawks" title="Chicago Blackhawks">Chicago Black Hawks</a> to have failed to qualify for the playoffs both the seasons before and after their championship season, and the third champion overall to not defend its title after both the Black Hawks and the 1995 New Jersey Devils. In <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_NHL_season" title="2007\xe2\x80\x9308 NHL season">2007\xe2\x80\x9308</a>, Carolina again missed out as <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Washington Capitals</a> stormed back to take the division title on the last day of the season, leaving the Hurricanes second in the division and ninth overall in the conference, and making the \'Canes only the second club in NHL history to miss the playoffs for two seasons running after a Cup triumph.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jokinen_2009-02-26_498v1_wiki.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="428" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Jokinen_2009-02-26_498v1_wiki.jpg/220px-Jokinen_2009-02-26_498v1_wiki.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Jokinen_2009-02-26_498v1_wiki.jpg/330px-Jokinen_2009-02-26_498v1_wiki.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c1/Jokinen_2009-02-26_498v1_wiki.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jokinen_2009-02-26_498v1_wiki.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nIn 2009 the Hurricanes acquired <a href="/wiki/Jussi_Jokinen" title="Jussi Jokinen">Jussi Jokinen</a> through a trade with the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Lightning" title="Tampa Bay Lightning">Tampa Bay Lightning</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>After a slow start to the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_NHL_season" title="2008\xe2\x80\x9309 NHL season">2008\xe2\x80\x9309 season</a>, Cup-winning coach <a href="/wiki/Peter_Laviolette" title="Peter Laviolette">Peter Laviolette</a> was fired in early December and replaced by his own predecessor, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Maurice" title="Paul Maurice">Paul Maurice</a>. Teetering on the edge of the playoff picture again, the club, on February 7, acquired utility forward <a href="/wiki/Jussi_Jokinen" title="Jussi Jokinen">Jussi Jokinen</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Lightning" title="Tampa Bay Lightning">Tampa Bay Lightning</a> in exchange for <a href="/wiki/Wade_Brookbank" title="Wade Brookbank">Wade Brookbank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Josef_Melichar" title="Josef Melichar">Josef Melichar</a> and Carolina\'s fourth round draft pick in <a href="/wiki/2009_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2009 NHL Entry Draft">2009</a>, then reacquired winger <a href="/wiki/Erik_Cole" title="Erik Cole">Erik Cole</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Edmonton_Oilers" title="Edmonton Oilers">Edmonton Oilers</a> at the March trade deadline and proceeded on a 12\xe2\x80\x933\xe2\x80\x932 run to close out the season. The stretch run included nine straight wins, matching a franchise record from the 2005\xe2\x80\x9306 season, and capped off a streak of 12 straight home wins, which set a new franchise mark. The team finished sixth in the Eastern Conference with 97 points, the second-most points in franchise history.</p>\n<p>The Canes\' <a href="/wiki/2009_Stanley_Cup_playoffs" title="2009 Stanley Cup playoffs">2009 playoff</a> run featured two tight series with dramatic finishes. Game 4 of the first round matchup with the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Devils" title="New Jersey Devils">New Jersey Devils</a> saw Stanley Cup playoff history when Jussi Jokinen scored with .2 seconds left in regulation to win the game, the latest regulation game-winning goal in NHL history. Then, in Game 7, the Devils took a 3\xe2\x80\x932 lead into the final two minutes of the game at the <a href="/wiki/Prudential_Center" title="Prudential Center">Prudential Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">Newark</a> before the \'Canes struck. With 1:20 to play, <a href="/wiki/Tim_Gleason" title="Tim Gleason">Tim Gleason</a> saved a puck on his knees at the right point, passed it to <a href="/wiki/Joni_Pitk%C3%A4nen" title="Joni Pitk\xc3\xa4nen">Joni Pitkanen</a> on the left boards, who then hit Game 4 hero Jussi Jokinen at the far post for the tying goal. Just 48 seconds later, <a href="/wiki/Chad_LaRose" title="Chad LaRose">Chad LaRose</a> sprang <a href="/wiki/Eric_Staal" title="Eric Staal">Eric Staal</a> for a solo down-ice rush to give the Canes 4\xe2\x80\x933 game and series wins; Staal\'s goal was the latest regulation Game 7 winning goal in playoff history. In the second round matchup with the top-seeded <a href="/wiki/Boston_Bruins" title="Boston Bruins">Boston Bruins</a>, the \'Canes ran out to a 3\xe2\x80\x931 lead before the Bruins battled back for two wins. In Game 7 in Boston, <a href="/wiki/Scott_Walker_(ice_hockey)" title="Scott Walker (ice hockey)">Scott Walker</a> scored the game and series winner 18:46 into overtime to send Carolina to the Eastern Conference finals against the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Penguins" title="Pittsburgh Penguins">Pittsburgh Penguins</a>. The Penguins, though, put a decisive end to the \'Canes\' string, sweeping the series 4\xe2\x80\x930 on the way to their own <a href="/wiki/2009_Stanley_Cup_Finals" title="2009 Stanley Cup Finals">Stanley Cup championship</a>.</p>\n<p>As a result of their surprise run, very few changes were made in the off-season. Veterans such as <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Ward_(ice_hockey)" title="Aaron Ward (ice hockey)">Aaron Ward</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Alberts" title="Andrew Alberts">Andrew Alberts</a> and <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Yelle" title="St\xc3\xa9phane Yelle">Stephane Yelle</a> were brought in to help drive the team further, but things did not go according to plan. The Hurricanes experienced a 14-game losing streak spanning October and November, and midway through the year, the \'Canes replaced their only post-lockout captain <a href="/wiki/Rod_Brind%27Amour" title="Rod Brind\'Amour">Rod Brind\'Amour</a> with Eric Staal. Despite improved play during the second half of the season, they could not overcome the deficit from early on in the season. The Hurricanes would end up with the seventh overall pick in the <a href="/wiki/2010_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2010 NHL Entry Draft">2010 NHL Entry Draft</a>, eventually selecting <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Skinner" title="Jeff Skinner">Jeff Skinner</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Kitchener_Rangers" title="Kitchener Rangers">Kitchener Rangers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Hockey_League" title="Ontario Hockey League">Ontario Hockey League</a> (OHL). Brind\'Amour retired over the 2010 off-season to take a coaching job with the club.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jeff_Skinner_canes2_2011-11-12.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2497" data-file-width="2347" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Jeff_Skinner_canes2_2011-11-12.JPG/220px-Jeff_Skinner_canes2_2011-11-12.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Jeff_Skinner_canes2_2011-11-12.JPG/330px-Jeff_Skinner_canes2_2011-11-12.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Jeff_Skinner_canes2_2011-11-12.JPG/440px-Jeff_Skinner_canes2_2011-11-12.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jeff_Skinner_canes2_2011-11-12.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Jeff_Skinner" title="Jeff Skinner">Jeff Skinner</a> was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calder_Trophy" title="Calder Trophy">Calder Trophy</a> for his rookie season performance in the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_NHL_season" title="2010\xe2\x80\x9311 NHL season">2010\xe2\x80\x9311</a> season.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_NHL_season" title="2010\xe2\x80\x9311 NHL season">2010\xe2\x80\x9311-year</a> was widely expected to be a transitional year from the veteran-heavy, high-salary club that opened 2009\xe2\x80\x9310 to a younger, cheaper base. The \'Canes, though, contended for a playoff slot for the entire season, aided by Skinner\'s emergence as an offensive phenomenon who, as the youngest player in the league, would lead all rookies in points. Raleigh hosted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_NHL_All-Star_Game" title="2011 NHL All-Star Game">2011 NHL All-Star Game</a> in January, and Eric Staal captained a team he selected (opposite a team selected by the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Red_Wings" title="Detroit Red Wings">Detroit Red Wings</a>\' <a href="/wiki/Nicklas_Lidstr%C3%B6m" title="Nicklas Lidstr\xc3\xb6m">Nicklas Lidstrom</a>) that featured Skinner (the youngest All-Star in NHL history), <a href="/wiki/Cam_Ward" title="Cam Ward">Cam Ward</a>, and (for the SuperSkills competition) defenseman <a href="/wiki/Jamie_McBain" title="Jamie McBain">Jamie McBain</a>. The Hurricanes went into the final day of the season able to determine their own fate, but lost 6\xe2\x80\x932 to the <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Lightning" title="Tampa Bay Lightning">Tampa Bay Lightning</a> to finish ninth in the East.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Skinner was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calder_Trophy" title="Calder Trophy">Calder Trophy</a> as rookie of the year, the first player in franchise history to receive that honor.</p>\n<p>In December 2011, the Carolina Hurricanes fired coach Paul Maurice and hired <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Muller" title="Kirk Muller">Kirk Muller</a>. On February 20, 2012, the Carolina Hurricanes signed Tim Gleason to a four-year, $16\xc2\xa0million extension and two days later, on February 22, they also signed <a href="/wiki/Tuomo_Ruutu" title="Tuomo Ruutu">Tuomo Ruutu</a> to a four-year, $19\xc2\xa0million extension. Two months later the Carolina Hurricanes announced that they had signed <a href="/wiki/Ji%C5%99%C3%AD_Tlust%C3%BD" title="Ji\xc5\x99\xc3\xad Tlust\xc3\xbd">Jiri Tlusty</a> to a two-year deal that would pay him $1.5\xc2\xa0million for 2012\xe2\x80\x9313 and $1.7\xc2\xa0million for 2013\xe2\x80\x9314 (Gleason and Tlusty would eventually be traded to the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitals" title="Washington Capitals">Washington Capitals</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_Jets" title="Winnipeg Jets">Winnipeg Jets</a> over the next few seasons). Despite the signings of Gleason, Ruutu, and Tlusty, the Hurricanes would finish fifth in the Southeast Division and twelfth in the Eastern Conference during the 2011\xe2\x80\x9312 season, which forced them to miss the playoffs for a third consecutive season.</p>\n<p>On May 9, 2012, the 2006 Stanley Cup Champions Carolina Hurricanes\' Game 7 victory was recognized as one of the NC Hall of Fame\'s "Great Moments" series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/2012_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2012 NHL Entry Draft">2012 NHL Entry Draft</a>, the Carolina Hurricanes traded <a href="/wiki/Brandon_Sutter" title="Brandon Sutter">Brandon Sutter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Dumoulin" title="Brian Dumoulin">Brian Dumoulin</a> and their <a href="/wiki/2012_NHL_Entry_Draft" title="2012 NHL Entry Draft">2012</a> first round draft pick (<a href="/wiki/Derrick_Pouliot" title="Derrick Pouliot">Derrick Pouliot</a>) to the Penguins in exchange for <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Staal" title="Jordan Staal">Jordan Staal</a>, uniting the player with his older brother, Eric Staal. On March 25, 2013, the Hurricanes signed <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Semin" title="Alexander Semin">Alexander Semin</a> to a five-year deal, worth $35\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, in the lockout-shortened 2012\xe2\x80\x9313 season, the team would finish third in the Southeast Division and thirteenth in the Eastern Conference, which would make the team miss the playoffs for the fourth consecutive season.</p>\n<p>Before the 2013\xe2\x80\x9314 season, the Hurricanes were realigned into the new <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Division" title="Metropolitan Division">Metropolitan Division</a>. They would finish seventh in the division during the 2013\xe2\x80\x9314 season (ahead of only the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Islanders" title="New York Islanders">New York Islanders</a>) and would miss the playoffs for the fifth consecutive season, which prompted management to fire head coach Kirk Muller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On June 19, 2014, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Peters_(ice_hockey)" title="Bill Peters (ice hockey)">Bill Peters</a> was named head coach of the team. During the 2014\xe2\x80\x9315 season, the team finished last in the Metropolitan Division and would miss the playoffs for the sixth consecutive season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> After team captain Eric Staal was traded to the New York Rangers at the trade deadline, the team finished sixth in the division during the 2015\xe2\x80\x9316 season. The Hurricanes finished seventh in the division in the 2016\xe2\x80\x9317 season, missing the playoffs for the eighth consecutive season. On July 13, 2017, it was reported that <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Greenberg_(businessman)" title="Chuck Greenberg (businessman)">Chuck Greenberg</a> had sent Karmanos a letter of intent to buy the team for $500\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span
