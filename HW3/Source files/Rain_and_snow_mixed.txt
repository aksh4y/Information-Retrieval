title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> and partially melted <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">ice pellets</a>, which are hard, and <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a>, which is fluid until striking an object, this precipitation is soft and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Translucent" title="Translucent">translucent</a>, but it contains some traces of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_crystal" title="Ice crystal">ice crystals</a>, from partially fused snowflakes. In any one location, it usually occurs briefly as a transition phase from rain to snow or vice versa. Its <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a> code is <b>RASN</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_and_snow_mixed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rain_snow_mix_soundings.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="181" data-file-width="560" which result in mixtures of rain and snow</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>This precipitation occurs when the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> in the lowest part of the atmosphere is slightly above the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freezing_point" title="Freezing point">freezing point</a> of water <span class="nowrap">(0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C or 32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F).</span> The depth of low-level warm air (below the freezing level) needed to melt snow falling from above to rain varies from about 230\xe2\x80\x93460\xc2\xa0m (750\xe2\x80\x931,500\xc2\xa0ft) and depends on the mass of the flakes and the lapse rate of the melting layer. Rain and snow typically mix when the melting layer depth falls between these values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span id=\'"Wintry_showers"_or_"wintry_mixes"\'></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Wintry_showers.22_or_.22wintry_mixes.22">"Wintry showers" or "wintry mixes"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rain_and_snow_mixed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title=\'Edit section: "Wintry showers" or "wintry mixes"\'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>Wintry showers</b> is a somewhat informal meteorological term, used primarily in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, to refer to various mixtures of rain, <a href="/wiki/Graupel" title="Graupel">graupel</a> and snow. Though no "official" criteria exist for the term, in the United Kingdom the term is not used when any significant accumulation of snow on the ground takes place. It is often used when the temperature of the ground surface is above 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F), preventing accumulation from occurring even if the air temperature is marginally below 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F); but even then, the falling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> must generally be something other than exclusively snow.</p>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <b>wintry mix</b> generally refers to a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_pellets" title="Ice pellets">ice pellets</a>, and snow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In contrast to the usage in the United Kingdom, in the United States it is usually used when air and ground temperatures are below 0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0C (32\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb0F). Additionally, it is generally used when some accumulation of ice and snow is expected to occur. During winter, a wide area can be affected by the multiple precipitation types typical of a wintry mix during a single winter storm, as counter-clockwise winds around a storm system bring warm air northwards ahead of the system, and then bring cold air back southwards behind it. Most often, it is the region ahead of the approaching storm system which sees the wintry mix, as warm air moves northward and above retreating cold air, causing snow to change to ice pellets, freezing rain and finally rain. The reverse transition can occur behind the departing low pressure system, though it is more common for precipitation to change directly from rain to snow, or for it to stop before a transition back.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
