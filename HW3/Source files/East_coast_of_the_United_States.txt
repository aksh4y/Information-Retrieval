class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coastline" title="Coastline">coastline</a> along which the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_United_States" title="Eastern United States">Eastern United States</a> meets the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a>. This area is also known as the <b>Eastern Seaboard</b>, the <b>Atlantic Coast</b>, and the <b>Atlantic Seaboard</b>. The <a href="/wiki/Coastal_states" title="Coastal states">coastal states</a> that have <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoreline" title="Shoreline">shoreline</a> on the Atlantic Ocean are, from north to south, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy and composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">place name</a> "East Coast" derives from the idea that the <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">contiguous 48 states</a> are defined by two major coastlines, one at the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">western edge</a> and one on the eastern edge. Other terms for referring to this area include the "Eastern <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/seaboard" title="wikt:seaboard">Seaboard</a>" ("seaboard" being American English for <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coast</a>), "Atlantic Coast", and "Atlantic Seaboard" (because the coastline lies along the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>).</p>\n<p>The fourteen states that have a shoreline on the Atlantic Ocean are, from north to south, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a> border tidal arms of the Atlantic (the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a>, respectively).</p>\n<p>Although <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> have no Atlantic coastline, they are sometimes grouped with the Eastern Seaboard states because of their locations in <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Old_South" title="Old South">Old South</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and their history as part of the land base of the original <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a> (viz. the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colony_of_New_Hampshire" title="Colony of New Hampshire">Colony of New Hampshire</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colony_of_New_York" title="Colony of New York">Colony of New York</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Colony of Virginia</a>).</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_history">Colonial history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Colonial history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thirteen_colonies" title="Thirteen colonies">thirteen colonies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a> in North America all lay along the East Coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[a]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Two additional <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. states</a> on the East Coast were not among the original thirteen colonies: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Maine" title="History of Maine">Maine</a> (became part of the English colony of Massachusetts in 1677)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Florida" title="History of Florida">Florida</a> (part of <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a> until 1821, though held by the British for 20 years after the <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">French and Indian War</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Middle_Colonies" title="Middle Colonies">Middle Colonies</a> (New Jersey, Pennsylvania, New York, and Delaware) had been owned by <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">the Dutch</a> as <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a>, until they were captured by <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">the English</a> in the mid-to-late 17th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_and_physical_geography">Climate and physical geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate and physical geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are three climate regions on the East Coast from north to south:</p>\n<p>The region from northern Maine south to about southern Connecticut has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_continental_climate" title="Humid continental climate">humid continental climate</a>, with warm summers and long, cold and snowy winters. The region from around New York City south through the Jersey Shore, the Chesapeake Bay region, Coastal Carolinas southwards to central Florida has a <a href="/wiki/Humid_subtropical_climate" title="Humid subtropical climate">humid subtropical climate</a>, with hot summers and milder winters, with winters noticeably warmer the further south you go. The far southern portion of the East Coast from south-central Florida southward (<a href="/wiki/Stuart,_Florida" title="Stuart, Florida">Stuart</a>, south through the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>) has a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a>, which is frost free and is warm to hot all year.</p>\n<p>Average monthly precipitation ranges from a slight late fall (November) maximum from Massachusetts northward (as at <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">Portland, Maine</a>), to a slight summer maximum from <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> south to Virginia (as at <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_Delaware" title="Wilmington, Delaware">Wilmington, Delaware</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>), to a more pronounced summer maximum from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a>, North Carolina, southward to <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>. Florida has a sharp wet-summer/dry-winter pattern, with 60 to 70 percent of precipitation falling between June and October in an average year.</p>\n<p>Although landfalls are rare, the Eastern seaboard is susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricanes</a> in the Atlantic hurricane season, officially running from June 1 to November 30, although hurricanes can occur before or after these dates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">Hazel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hugo" title="Hurricane Hugo">Hugo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bob" title="Hurricane Bob">Bob</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Isabel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene" title="Hurricane Irene">Irene</a>, and most recently <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Sandy</a> are some of the more significant storms to have affected the region.</p>\n<p>The East Coast is a low-relief, <a href="/wiki/Passive_margin" title="Passive margin">passive margin</a> coast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[6]</a></sup> It has been shaped by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pleistocene_glaciation" title="Pleistocene glaciation">Pleistocene glaciation</a> in the far northern areas from New York City northward, with offshore islands such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nantucket" title="Nantucket">Nantucket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Block_Island" title="Block Island">Block Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fishers_Island,_New_York" title="Fishers Island, New York">Fishers Island</a>, the nearly peninsular <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a> and New York City\'s <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a> the result of <a href="/wiki/Terminal_moraine" title="Terminal moraine">terminal moraines</a>, with Massachusetts\' unique peninsula of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a> showing the additional action of <a href="/wiki/Outwash_plain" title="Outwash plain">outwash plains</a>, besides terminal moraines. The coastal plain broadens southwards, separated from the <a href="/wiki/Piedmont_(United_States)" title="Piedmont (United States)">Piedmont</a> region by the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Seaboard_fall_line" title="Atlantic Seaboard fall line">Atlantic Seaboard fall line</a> of the East Coast rivers, often marking the head of navigation and prominent sites of cities. The coastal areas from Long Island south to Florida are often made up of <a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">barrier islands</a> that front the coastal areas. Many of the larger capes along the lower East Coast are in fact barrier islands, like the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a> of North Carolina and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral" title="Cape Canaveral">Cape Canaveral</a>, Florida. The Florida Keys are made up of limestone coral and provide the only <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a> on the US mainland.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 2010, the population of the states which have shoreline on the East Coast was estimated at 112,642,503 (36% of the country\'s total population). The East Coast is the most populated coastal area in the United States. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_Coast_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway" title="Interstate Highway">Interstate Highway</a> along the East Coast is <a href="/wiki/Interstate_95" title="Interstate 95">Interstate 95</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> completed in the late 1970s, which replaced the historic <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_1" title="U.S. Route 1">U.S. Route 1</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Highway_(US)" title="Atlantic Highway (US)">Atlantic Highway</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> the original federal highway that traversed all East Coast states (except Delaware).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> By water, the East Coast is connected from <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a>, by the <a href="/wiki/Intracoastal_Waterway" title="Intracoastal Waterway">Intracoastal Waterway</a>, also known as the East Coast Canal, which was completed in 1912.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Amtrak\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Downeaster_(train)" title="Downeaster (train)">Downeaster</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Northeast_Regional" title="Northeast Regional">Northeast Regional</a></i> offer the main <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passenger_rail" title="Passenger rail">passenger rail</a> service on the Seaboard. The <i><a href="/wiki/Acela_Express" title="Acela Express">Acela Express</a></i> offers the only <a href="/wiki/High-speed_rail" title="High-speed rail">high-speed rail</a> passenger service in the Americas. Between New York and Boston the <i>Acela Express</i> has up to a 54% share of the combined train and air passenger market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Some of the largest airports in the United States are located in states which lie in the East Coast of the United States, such as <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">John F. Kennedy International Airport</a> in New York, <a href="/wiki/Logan_International_Airport" title="Logan International Airport">Logan International Airport</a> in Boston, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newark_Liberty_Airport" title="Newark Liberty Airport">Newark Liberty Airport</a> in Newark, New Jersey, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_International_Airport" title="Philadelphia International Airport">Philadelphia International Airport</a> in Philadelphia, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore%E2%80%93Washington_International_Airport" title="Baltimore\xe2\x80\x93Washington International Airport">Baltimore\xe2\x80\x93Washington International Airport</a> near Baltimore, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington-Dulles_International_Airport" title="Washington-Dulles International Airport">Washington-Dulles International Airport</a> near Washington, D.C., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartsfield_International_Airport" title="Hartsfield International Airport">Hartsfield International Airport</a> in Atlanta, <a href="/wiki/Miami_International_Airport" title="Miami International Airport">Miami International Airport</a> in Miami, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Douglas_International_Airport" title="Charlotte Douglas International Airport">Charlotte Douglas International Airport</a> in Charlotte, North Carolina, <a Airport">Orlando International Airport</a> in Orlando, Florida.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
