song">protest song</a> by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a> co-written with <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Levy" title="Jacques Levy">Jacques Levy</a>, about the imprisonment of <a href="/wiki/Rubin_Carter" title="Rubin Carter">Rubin "Hurricane" Carter</a>. It compiles alleged acts of <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a> and profiling against Carter,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> which Dylan describes as leading to a false trial and conviction.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(Bob_Dylan_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Carter and a man named John Artis had been charged with a triple <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> at the Lafayette Grill in <a href="/wiki/Paterson,_New_Jersey" title="Paterson, New Jersey">Paterson, New Jersey</a>, in 1966. The following year Carter and Artis were found guilty of the murders, which were widely reported as racially motivated. In the years that followed, a substantial amount of controversy emerged over the case, ranging from allegations of faulty evidence and questionable eyewitness testimony to an unfair trial.</p>\n<p>In his autobiography, Carter maintained his innocence, and after reading it, Dylan visited him in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rahway_State_Prison" title="Rahway State Prison">Rahway State Prison</a> in <a href="/wiki/Woodbridge_Township,_New_Jersey" title="Woodbridge Township, New Jersey">Woodbridge Township</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RollingStone-LookBack_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-LookBack-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>"Dylan had written topical ballads such as "<a href="/wiki/The_Lonesome_Death_of_Hattie_Carroll" title="The Lonesome Death of Hattie Carroll">The Lonesome Death of Hattie Carroll</a>" and Bob wasn\'t sure that he could write a song [about Carter]... He was just filled with all these feelings about Hurricane. He couldn\'t make the first step. I think the first step was putting the song in a total storytelling mode. I don\'t remember whose idea it was to do that. But really, the beginning of the song is like stage directions, like what you would read in a script: \'Pistol shots ring out in a barroom night.... Here comes the story of the Hurricane.\' Boom! Titles. You know, Bob loves movies, and he can write these movies that take place in eight to ten minutes, yet seem as full or fuller than regular movies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After meeting with Carter in prison and later with a group of his supporters, Dylan began to write "Hurricane". The song was one of his few "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protest_songs" title="Protest songs">protest songs</a>" during the 1970s and proved to be his fourth most successful single of the decade, reaching #33 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy_and_re-recording">Controversy and re-recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(Bob_Dylan_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversy and re-recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Dylan first recorded the song in late July 1975; it featured <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_Rivera" title="Scarlet Rivera">Scarlet Rivera</a> on <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vinnie_Bell" title="Vinnie Bell">Vinnie Bell</a> on <a href="/wiki/Danelectro" title="Danelectro">Danelectro</a> Bellzouki <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/12-string_guitar" title="12-string guitar">12-string guitar</a>. Dylan was forced to re-record the song, with altered lyrics, in October 1975 after concerns were raised by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia</a>\'s lawyers that references to Alfred Bello and Arthur Dexter Bradley (the two star witnesses of the case) as having "robbed the bodies" could result in a lawsuit. Bello and Bradley had never been accused of such acts. Because there was too much leakage on the multitracks to make a vocal "punch in," Dylan decided to re-record the entire song. At this time, he was already rehearsing for his upcoming tour, and the musicians from the <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Thunder_Revue" title="Rolling Thunder Revue">Rolling Thunder Revue</a> were still at his disposal. Dylan took violinist Rivera, guitarist Steven Soles, bassist Rob Rothstein, drummer Howie Wyeth, and percussionist Luther Rix back into the studio, and a new, faster version of "Hurricane" was recorded with Don DeVito again producing, and <a href="/wiki/Ronee_Blakley" title="Ronee Blakley">Ronee Blakley</a> providing a harmony vocal. (There is a noticeable mistake in the 8-minute recording at 4:02 where the backing singer (Blakley) gets her line wrong. She sings: "Remember you saw (said) you saw the getaway car.") The final version of the song, which runs over eight minutes, was spliced together from two separate takes completed on October<span class="nowrap">\xc2\xa0</span>24, 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Even though some offending lyrics were removed, the song still drew legal action from eyewitness Patricia Graham Valentine, who believed that it portrayed her as part of a conspiracy to frame Carter. However, her lawsuit was dismissed by a <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_Florida" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida">federal district court</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Court_of_Appeals_for_the_Eleventh_Circuit" title="United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit">United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit</a> affirmed the dismissal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> More generally, even with the revised lyrics, "Hurricane" was accused of factual errors. The song included a description of Carter as the "number one contender"; according to the May 1966 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ring_(magazine)" title="The Ring (magazine)">The Ring</a></i>, he was ranked ninth around the time of his arrest and had never been ranked higher than third. Reporters for the <i><a href="/wiki/Herald_News" title="Herald News">Herald News</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> newspaper published not far from the scene of the crime, questioned Dylan\'s objectivity at the time of the song\'s release and accused him of excessive poetic license. Dylan biographer <a href="/wiki/Howard_Sounes" title="Howard Sounes">Howard Sounes</a> praised the song but noted "there was no reference to his antagonistic rhetoric, criminal history, or violent temper."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefit_concert_and_new_trial">Benefit concert and new trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_(Bob_Dylan_song)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Benefit concert and new trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The song was released on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Desire_(Bob_Dylan_album)" title="Desire (Bob Dylan album)">Desire</a></i> in January 1976, making the Carter case known to a broader public. "Hurricane" is credited with harnessing popular support to Carter\'s defense.</p>\n<p>During the fall tour preceding <i>Desire</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'</span>s release, Dylan and the Rolling Thunder Revue played a <a href="/wiki/Benefit_concert" title="Benefit concert">benefit concert</a> for Carter in New York City\'s <a href="/wiki/Madison_Square_Garden" title="Madison Square Garden">Madison Square Garden</a>, raising $100,000. The following year, they played another benefit at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston_Astrodome" title="Houston Astrodome">Houston Astrodome</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RollingStone-LookBack_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-LookBack-2">[2]</a></sup> Dylan met with managers Richard Flanzer and Roy Silver, who provided <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Wonder" title="Stevie Wonder">Stevie Wonder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ringo_Starr" title="Ringo Starr">Ringo Starr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dr._John" title="Dr. John">Dr. John</a> for the concert. After expenses were paid, however, the Houston event failed to raise any money.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Despite winning the right to a new trial, Carter and Artis were once again found guilty when the prosecution argued that the defendants committed triple murder at the Lafayette Grill in revenge for the killing of an African-American tavern owner earlier during the same night. In 1976, Carter was sentenced to two consecutive life terms. Dylan, and the other high-profile supporters, did not attend the trial. Artis was paroled five years later.</p>\n<p>In 1985 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Judge" title="Federal Judge">Federal Judge</a> <a href="/wiki/H._Lee_Sarokin" title="H. Lee Sarokin">H. Lee Sarokin</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_District_of_New_Jersey" title="United States District Court for the District of New Jersey">United States District Court for the District of New Jersey</a>, who declined to hear the song when it was offered to him by his family, ruled that Carter had not received a fair trial and overturned the conviction, resulting in Carter\'s release and the granting of a writ of <a href="/wiki/Habeas_corpus" title="Habeas corpus">habeas corpus</a> to Carter, commenting that the prosecution had been "based on racism rather than reason and concealment rather than disclosure". In 1988, after the prosecution said they would not seek a third trial and filed a motion to dismiss, following their failed appeal to the Supreme Court which declined to hear the case and upheld Sarokin\'s ruling, a Superior Court judge dropped all charges against Carter and Artis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RollingStone-LookBack_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-LookBack-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Certifications">Certifications</span><span
