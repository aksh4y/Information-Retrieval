title="1995 Atlantic hurricane season">1995 Atlantic hurricane season</a></b></td>\n</tr>\n<tr style="display:none">\n<td colspan="2"></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Hurricane Marilyn</b> was the fifteenth <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_depression" title="Tropical depression">tropical depression</a> and thirteenth named <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> of the unusually busy <a href="/wiki/1995_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1995 Atlantic hurricane season">1995 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, following closely on the heels of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Luis" title="Hurricane Luis">Hurricane Luis</a>. Hurricane Marilyn was the most powerful storm to hit the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a> since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hugo" title="Hurricane Hugo">Hurricane Hugo</a> of <a href="/wiki/1989_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1989 Atlantic hurricane season">1989</a>.</p>\n<p>Marilyn formed late in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> day on September 13, and reached hurricane strength soon thereafter. Marilyn struck the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> on September 14 at Category\xc2\xa01 strength, and intensified to nearly Category\xc2\xa03 strength by the time it reached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunter" title="Hurricane Hunter">Hurricane Hunter</a> <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> flight reported <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, which is unusual for tropical cyclones. After heading north past <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, Marilyn weakened and became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extratropical" title="Extratropical">extratropical</a> on September 22. The remnant circulation wandered the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> from September 23 \xe2\x80\x93 October 1, just south of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. Marilyn peaked at 115\xc2\xa0mph (185\xc2\xa0km/h), but was predicted to reach 125\xc2\xa0mph (205\xc2\xa0km/h) (maximum Category\xc2\xa03-status).</p>\n<p>Marilyn is directly responsible for thirteen deaths, most due to drowning on boats or offshore. Eleven thousand people were left <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homeless" title="Homeless">homeless</a> on the island of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Thomas, United States Virgin Islands">St. Thomas</a>, and estimated damages were set at $2\xc2\xa0billion (1995\xc2\xa0USD)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deaths1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-deaths1-1">[1]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">USVI</a>. The same area would be struck by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bertha_(1996)" title="Hurricane Bertha (1996)">Hurricane Bertha</a> the next year, while still repairing from Hurricanes Luis and Marilyn,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bertha_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bertha-2">[2]</a></sup> then successively hit by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hortense" title="Hurricane Hortense">Hortense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Erika_(1997)" title="Hurricane Erika (1997)">Erika</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Georges" title="Hurricane Georges">Georges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Jose_(1999)" title="Hurricane Jose (1999)">Jose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Lenny" title="Hurricane Lenny">Lenny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Debby_(2000)" title="Hurricane Debby (2000)">Debby</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Meteorological_history"><span href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Marilyn_1995_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="867" data-file-width="1024" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Marilyn_1995_track.png/220px-Marilyn_1995_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Marilyn_1995_track.png/330px-Marilyn_1995_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Marilyn_1995_track.png/440px-Marilyn_1995_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marilyn_1995_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMap plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Marilyn\'s origins were from a tropical wave that formed off the <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> coast on September 7. Large, low-level winds were included in the wave\'s circulation, but little convection existed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> The system continued west for a few days at about 19\xc2\xa0mph (31\xc2\xa0km/h) next to an anticyclonic aloft. On September 12, satellite pictures viewed the disorganized wave and declared it Tropical Depression Fifteen, after convection had increased. Tropical Depression Fifteen strengthened rapidly, becoming Tropical Storm Marilyn six hours later. One day after being named, Marilyn was upgraded to hurricane status, while turning on a more northwesterly track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion3-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>For the next few days, Marilyn\'s track took it towards the west-northwest and to the northwest, due to a weakness in the subtropical ridge. Marilyn was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 1</a> hurricane; it passed 52\xc2\xa0mi (84\xc2\xa0km) north of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> Marilyn passed over <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>, and just <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Eles_des_Saintes" title="\xc3\x8eles des Saintes">\xc3\x8eles des Saintes</a> and southwest of <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> on September 14, bringing heavy rains and strong winds with it. Marilyn continued on its northwesterly track, making landfall in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a> on September 15, packing winds of 110\xc2\xa0mph (180\xc2\xa0km/h), a strong Category\xc2\xa02 strength hurricane. The eyewall, east-southeast of the center of Marilyn, passed over <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Thomas, U.S. Virgin Islands">Saint Thomas</a> later that day. After passing over <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, Marilyn entered back into the Atlantic Ocean on September 16.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_marilyn_1995_goes_ir_2.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1080" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Hurricane_marilyn_1995_goes_ir_2.gif/185px-Hurricane_marilyn_1995_goes_ir_2.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Hurricane_marilyn_1995_goes_ir_2.gif/278px-Hurricane_marilyn_1995_goes_ir_2.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Hurricane_marilyn_1995_goes_ir_2.gif/370px-Hurricane_marilyn_1995_goes_ir_2.gif 2x" width="185"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_marilyn_1995_goes_ir_2.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHurricane Marilyn near <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A low had formed near Marilyn, which may have enhanced outflow from the system. An eye formed in the center of Marilyn, and the storm reached a peak intensity of 949 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibars" title="Millibars">millibars</a> and winds of only 115\xc2\xa0mph (185\xc2\xa0km/h), a lower-end Category\xc2\xa03 hurricane. At this time, Marilyn was predicted to peak in wind speeds of 120\xe2\x80\x93125\xc2\xa0mph (190\xe2\x80\x93195\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion18_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion18-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion19_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion19-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forecast18_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forecast18-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forecast19_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forecast19-8">[8]</a></sup> Reconnaissance data found a concentric pair of eyewall wind maxima. Marilyn started weakening rapidly, falling from peak-level winds of 141\xc2\xa0mph (227\xc2\xa0km/h) down to 102\xc2\xa0mph (164\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> The central pressure also rose up 20 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbars</a> in only 10\xc2\xa0hours. This rapid weakening was caused by westerly shear and dry air entrained in the system, a decaying eyewall, and especially cool waters upwelled from the previous powerful storm, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Luis" title="Hurricane Luis">Hurricane Luis</a>. As Marilyn weakened to Category 1 hurricane status and moved north-northwest, it passed about 174\xc2\xa0mi (280\xc2\xa0km) west of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> on September 19. As Marilyn encountered more westerly shear, it weakened to below hurricane status and made its extratropical transition on September 20, centered in the northeastern Atlantic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> The remnant circulation continued to erratically move through the central Atlantic Ocean for another 10\xc2\xa0days before being absorbed by a frontal low on September 30.</p>\n<p>A Hurricane Hunter reconnaissance flight reported hail, which is unusual for tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a Five hours later, a tropical storm watch was issued for <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a>. A tropical storm warning was issued for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lucia" title="St. Lucia">St. Lucia</a>, St. Vincent, <a href="/wiki/Grenadines" title="Grenadines">Grenadines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a> and Tobago at 900 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> September 13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion4_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion4-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Public4_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Public4-10">[10]</a></sup> At 2100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>, a Hurricane Warning was issued for <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, St. Vincent, <a href="/wiki/Grenadines" title="Grenadines">Grenadines</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lucia" title="St. Lucia">St. Lucia</a> and a hurricane watch for <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion5_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion5-11">[11]</a></sup> A hurricane watch as released for Martinique on September 14. The 2100 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> September 13 was extended for the Grenadines through St. Martin, except Guadeloupe, St. Barthelemy and French portion of St. Martin. At 1500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>, Puerto Rico was put under a Hurricane Watch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-9"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion9_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion9-12">[12]</a></sup> At 1700 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a>, Guadeloupe, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Barthelemy" title="St. Barthelemy">St. Barthelemy</a>, and French portion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Martin" title="St. Martin">St. Martin</a> were put under a hurricane watch. Four hours later, <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, U.S. and <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a>, and Guadeloupe were put under a hurricane warning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-10"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Throughout the night, several watches and warnings were discontinued. At 1500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> September 15, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cabrera,_Mar%C3%ADa_Trinidad_S%C3%A1nchez" title="Cabrera, Mar\xc3\xada Trinidad S\xc3\xa1nchez">Cabrera</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabo_Engano" title="Cabo Engano">Cabo Engano</a> were put under a hurricane watch as Marilyn approached. During the night of September 16, all currently active watches and warnings were discontinued. At around the same time, a new Hurricane watch was released for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos" title="Turks and Caicos">Turks and Caicos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mayaguana" title="Mayaguana">Mayaguana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acklins" title="Acklins">Acklins</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Crooked_Island,_Bahamas" title="Crooked Island, Bahamas">Crooked Islands</a> of the southeastern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahamas" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forecast19_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Forecast19-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forecast20_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forecast20-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Forecast21_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Forecast21-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion17_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion17-15">[15]</a></sup> This warning was discontinued within 48\xc2\xa0hours. At 1500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">UTC</a> September 18, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> came under a Tropical Storm Watch, which was upgraded to a warning in six hours. The warning was discontinued on September 19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-11"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion23_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion23-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion25_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion25-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion26_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion26-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discussion27_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Discussion27-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Public31A_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Public31A-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The 250,000 residents of <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> spent the night of September 13 in shelters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-News2_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-News2-21">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="wikitable" style="float:right; The exact figure for damages in the U.S. Virgin Islands and Puerto Rico is not available, although the American Insurance Services Group set the combined damage at $875\xc2\xa0million. Aside from high winds and seas, one possible explanation for the storm\'s heavy damage may have been complacency on the part of local island populations. Marilyn followed on the heels of Luis, a more powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 4</a> hurricane initially predicted to pass very close to St. Thomas. This prediction prompted locals to be particularly thorough in their pre-storm preparations; however, Luis passed further away from the island and caused relatively moderate damage. Virgin Islanders questioned the accuracy of the national hurricane center wind advisories, as hurricane Marilyn caused significant damage for a mere "category 2" storm. Damage on St. Thomas was remiscent of the damage seen on St. Croix in 1989, when Hurricane Hugo, a category 4 storm, caused catastrophic damage. Because St. Thomas is a very hilly island, it is possible that sustained winds on exposed hills (some of which have elevations that are thousands of feet above sea level) were significantly stronger than the sea-level wind speed estimates given by the hurricane center in its main advisory. Residents also reported tornado vortices in certain neighborhoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-15"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Martinique">Martinique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Martinique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Rainfalls in <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> peaked at 9.06\xc2\xa0in (230\xc2\xa0mm) in Morne Rouge, 8.94\xc2\xa0in (227\xc2\xa0mm) in Ajoupa Bouillon, 6.4\xc2\xa0in (160\xc2\xa0mm) in Saint Pierre, ranging as low as 6\xc2\xa0in (150\xc2\xa0mm) in Ducos. over the island of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Croix,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Croix, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Croix</a> was 100\xc2\xa0mph (160\xc2\xa0km/h). The highest rainfalls reported were 11.67\xc2\xa0in (296\xc2\xa0mm) in Annually, 9.96\xc2\xa0in (253\xc2\xa0mm) in Red Hook Bay and 5.25\xc2\xa0in (133\xc2\xa0mm) in Granard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-19"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> The highest storm surge reported was 6.6\xc2\xa0ft (2.0\xc2\xa0m) in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Thomas, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Thomas</a> and 6.0\xc2\xa0ft (1.8\xc2\xa0m) in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Croix,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Croix, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Croix</a>. According to NOAA, The storm surge in the U.S. Virgin Islands reached 6 to 7\xc2\xa0ft (2.1\xc2\xa0m), with an isolated storm tide of 11.7\xc2\xa0ft (3.6\xc2\xa0m) reported on St. Croix. In St. Croix and St. Thomas, rainfall totals reached about 10\xc2\xa0in.</p>\n<p>The highest reported gusts were 129\xc2\xa0mph (208\xc2\xa0km/h) in a Noncommissioned Automated Surface Observing System and 95\xc2\xa0mph (153\xc2\xa0km/h) gusts in Sailboat Puffin at Green Cay. The highest sustained winds were 105\xc2\xa0mph (169\xc2\xa0km/h) in a Noncommissioned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automated_Surface_Observing_System" title="Automated Surface Observing System">Automated Surface Observing System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-20"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> Marilyn was directly responsible for seven deaths, most due to drowning on boats or offshore. Ten thousand people were left homeless on St. Thomas, and most buildings sustained some damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-21"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The island of St. Thomas was the hardest hit by the storm, where about 80% of the homes and businesses were damaged or destroyed, including the hospital and the water desalination plant; five people were killed. Storm surge in the <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Amalie,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Charlotte Amalie, U.S. Virgin Islands">Charlotte Amalie</a> harbor beached the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard#Cutters" title="United States Coast Guard">USCGC</a> <i>Point Ledge</i> and damaged many smaller boats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coast_Guard_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coast_Guard-29">[29]</a></sup> Elsewhere in the territory, there was moderate to severe damage on <a href="/wiki/Saint_Croix,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Croix, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Croix</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saint_John,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint John, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. John</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">Federal Emergency Management Agency</a> (FEMA) estimated $1\xc2\xa0billion in losses, while an economic research group in the U.S. Virgin Islands set the damage toll at $3\xc2\xa0billion. Additionally, losses on St. Thomas were about $1.5\xc2\xa0billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-22"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Marilyn_1995_rainfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMarilyn Rainfall</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico">Puerto Rico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Puerto Rico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The highest rainfalls reported in Puerto Rico from Hurricane Marilyn were 5.6\xc2\xa0in (140\xc2\xa0mm) in Naguabo, 5.5\xc2\xa0in (140\xc2\xa0mm) in Luquillo Pico Del Este (Luquillo Pico Of the East), 2.52\xc2\xa0in (64\xc2\xa0mm) at Luis Munoz International Airport, and 2.45\xc2\xa0in (62\xc2\xa0mm) at <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_Roads_Naval_Station" title="Roosevelt Roads Naval Station">Roosevelt Roads Naval Station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-23"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> The highest gusts reported were 125\xc2\xa0mph (201\xc2\xa0km/h) in Culebra, 60\xc2\xa0mph (97\xc2\xa0km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_Roads_Naval_Station" title="Roosevelt Roads Naval Station">Roosevelt Roads Naval Station</a> and 50\xc2\xa0mph (80\xc2\xa0km/h) at a non-commissioned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automated_Surface_Observing_System" title="Automated Surface Observing System">Automated Surface Observing System</a>. The highest recorded sustained winds was 41\xc2\xa0mph (66\xc2\xa0km/h) at Roosevelt Roads Naval Station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-24"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> Marilyn passed over the islands of <a href="/wiki/Vieques,_Puerto_Rico" title="Vieques, Puerto Rico">Vieques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culebra,_Puerto_Rico" title="Culebra, Puerto Rico">Culebra</a>, where it caused much damage.</p>\n<p>There were reports of extensive flash flooding across northern and eastern <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and 12,000\xc2\xa0people were forced to ride out the storm in shelters. One person was killed in Culebra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-25"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> declared Puerto Rico a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_disaster_area" title="Federal disaster area">federal disaster area</a>, making it available to federal aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-News1_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-News1-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>100\xc2\xa0homes on Culebra Island, 20 miles (32\xc2\xa0km) east of Puerto Rico, were destroyed by Marilyn. Another two hundred homes on the island were damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-News3_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-News3-31">[31]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antigua">Antigua</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Antigua">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The highest reported gust in <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a> was 50\xc2\xa0mph (80\xc2\xa0km/h). The Antigua Meteorological Service reported that the island had extensive flooding and damage to banana trees. The other damage reported was from the wind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-26"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-32">[32]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_areas">Other areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> reported sustained winds of 45\xc2\xa0mph and a highest gust of 60\xc2\xa0mph (97\xc2\xa0km/h). <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a> reported sustained winds of just 35\xc2\xa0mph (56\xc2\xa0km/h), tropical depression-strength. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Maarten" title="St. Maarten">St. Maarten</a>, sustained winds were 42.5\xc2\xa0mph (68.4\xc2\xa0km/h) and the peak gust was 61\xc2\xa0mph (98\xc2\xa0km/h). Rainfalls in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Maarten" title="St. Maarten">St. Maarten</a> peaked at 3.35\xc2\xa0in (85\xc2\xa0mm) in an unknown location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-27"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> reported that the <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a> had little to no damage from Marilyn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-28"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MWR_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-MWR-32">[32]</a></sup> The highest gust in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint-Barthelemy" title="Saint-Barthelemy">Saint-Barthelemy</a> was 59\xc2\xa0mph (95\xc2\xa0km/h) and the highest sustained winds of 45\xc2\xa0mph (72\xc2\xa0km/h) was reported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TCR_3-29"><a href="#cite_note-TCR-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico_2">Puerto Rico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Puerto Rico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Relief supplies in the wake of Hurricane Marilyn were sent to <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a>. Officials of FEMA set up camps on the islands to give out food, water and shelter supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aftermath1_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aftermath1-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Virgin_Islands_2">U.S. Virgin Islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: U.S. Virgin Islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Marilyn-13650.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2940" data-file-width="1952" height="301" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Hurricane_Marilyn-13650.jpg/200px-Hurricane_Marilyn-13650.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Hurricane_Marilyn-13650.jpg/300px-Hurricane_Marilyn-13650.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Hurricane_Marilyn-13650.jpg/400px-Hurricane_Marilyn-13650.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Marilyn-13650.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nFEMA distributed food, water, clothing in the U.S. Virgin Islands.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> declared the U.S. Virgin Islands a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_disaster_area" title="Federal disaster area">federal disaster area</a>, making it available to federal aid. However, the National Guard did not appear on St. Thomas until two days following the hurricane, and FEMA did not appear until three days following. In the meantime, significant looting, particularly of grocery stores, prevented an orderly distribution of food and water. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-News1_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-News1-30">[30]</a></sup> Five supply distribution sites were being operated by the Virgin Islands Territorial Emergency Management Agency (VITEMA). More than 2,100\xc2\xa0federal agency personnel had been deployed to help out with the cleanup from Hurricane Marilyn. Four contracts were quickly accepted for immediate building repairs. <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Diving_teams&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Diving teams (page does not exist)">Diving teams</a> were sent to check for harbor damage and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> Seabees started working on repairing public buildings. Military and security forces included about 500\xc2\xa0Army, Air Force, and Navy personnel, 500\xc2\xa0National Guardsmen, and 500 federal law enforcement personnel. The airport in St. Thomas was open for flight using a mobile control tower. The St. Thomas hospital was open, but was running on generators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aftermath2_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aftermath2-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The first FEMA Disaster Recovery Centers opened on September\xc2\xa023 on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Thomas,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="St. Thomas, U.S. Virgin Islands">St. Thomas</a>. A reported indicated that by October\xc2\xa011, 1500\xc2\xa0loans were granted for home repair, mostly for under $2500. More than 19,000\xc2\xa0disaster housing applications were filed, with over 2800&amp;nbsolbeing accepted. About 5000&amp;nbsolof 15,000&amp;nbsolassigned inspections were completed. Over 7800\xc2\xa0applications for Individual and Family Grants had been received and, of the 4,000\xc2\xa0SBA loan applications filed, 43\xc2\xa0had been approved for a total of $744,100 \xe2\x80\x93 an average loan of $17,300.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aftermath2_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aftermath2-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Record_and_retirement">Record and retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Marilyn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Record and retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>\n<p>Marilyn was the last of three <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> to affect the Leeward Islands especially <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> in a short period of time of the 1995 season, the first being <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Iris_(1995)" title="Hurricane Iris (1995)">Hurricane Iris</a>, late August and the second being <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Luis" title="Hurricane Luis">Hurricane Luis</a>, ten days before.</p>\n<p>Due to intense destruction in the US Virgin Islands, the name Marilyn was retired in the spring of 1996, and will never again be used for an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a>, becoming the first storm name beginning by "M" to be retired. It was replaced with the name <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Michelle" title="Hurricane Michelle">Michelle</a> for the <a href="/wiki/2001_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2001 Atlantic hurricane season">2001 season</a>, which was later itself retired.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
