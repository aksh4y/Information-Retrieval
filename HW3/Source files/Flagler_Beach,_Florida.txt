The population was 4,484 at the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 census</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Since its incorporation in April 1925, Flagler Beach has developed as a unique seaside community. It is home to six miles of uncrowded beaches, many great fishing spots including the Flagler Beach Municipal Pier, restaurants with oceanfront dining, gift shops and a whole host of recreational facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Flagler Beach is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deltona%E2%80%93Daytona_Beach%E2%80%93Ormond_Beach,_FL_metropolitan_statistical_area" title="Deltona\xe2\x80\x93Daytona Beach\xe2\x80\x93Ormond Beach, FL metropolitan statistical area">Deltona\xe2\x80\x93Daytona Beach\xe2\x80\x93Ormond Beach, FL metropolitan statistical area</a>. It is named for oil tycoon and Florida railroad developer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Flagler" title="Henry Flagler">Henry Flagler</a>, who was a key figure in the development of East Florida as resort and vacation destinations.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Geography"><span The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 519.8/km\xc2\xb2 (1,345.1/mi\xc2\xb2). There were 3,224 housing units at an average density of 338.3/km\xc2\xb2 (875.4/mi\xc2\xb2). The racial makeup of the city was 97.86% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 0.52% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_(U.S._Census)" title="Black (U.S. Census)">Black</a>, 0.22% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Indian_(U.S._Census)" title="American Indian (U.S. Census)">American Indian</a>, 0.57% <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oriental_(U.S._Census)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oriental (U.S. Census) (page does not exist)">Oriental</a>, 0.02% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.14% from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 0.67% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 1.84% of the population.</p>\n<p>There were 2,535 households out of which 12.9% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 50.6% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 6.5% had a female householder with no husband present, and 41.1% were non-families. 33.8% of all households were made up of individuals and 16.9% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 1.95 and the average family size was 2.43.</p>\n<p>In the city, the population was spread out with 11.5% under the age of 18, 4.4% from 18 to 24, 20.0% from 25 to 44, 32.5% from 45 to 64, and 31.5% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 53 years. For every 100 females there were 92.8 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 91.2 males.</p>\n<p>The median income for a household in the city was $37,917, and the median income for a family was $47,073. Males had a median income of $31,848 versus $30,132 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $24,600. About 9.0% of families and 10.9% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 18.4% of those under age 18 and 10.4% of those age 65 or over.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attractions">Attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagler_Beach,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span id="America\'s_Coolest_Small_Town_finalist"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="America.27s_Coolest_Small_Town_finalist">America\'s Coolest Small Town finalist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagler_Beach,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: America\'s Coolest Small Town finalist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Flagler Beach was a finalist in the 2013 <i>Budget Travel Magazine</i> contest for "Coolest Small Town". The magazine described it by the following:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>"Twenty miles north of <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach,_Florida" title="Daytona Beach, Florida">Daytona Beach</a> on A1A, Flagler Beach couldn\'t be more different from its party-hardy neighbor to the south. In fact, the area seems to attract more sea turtles and right whales than spring breakers. And it\'s not hard to see why: This thin strip of a beach town, between the Atlantic Ocean and the <a href="/wiki/Intracoastal_Waterway" title="Intracoastal Waterway">Intracoastal Waterway</a>, has remained significantly less developed than its neighbors. The six miles of pristine sand\xe2\x80\x94which boast an orange hue thanks to crushed coquina shells\xe2\x80\x94are only interrupted by one fishing pier. In town, the vibe is laid back and retro, thanks to spots like Grampa\'s Uke Joint, which sells ukuleles, and High Tides at Snack Jack, a 1950s fish shack that attracts surfers with funky dishes like tuna reubens, ahi club sandwiches, and <a href="/wiki/Sake" title="Sake">sake</a> Bloody Marys."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n</blockquote>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_location">Retirement location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagler_Beach,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Retirement location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Flagler Beach was ranked by <i>Where to Retire</i> magazine as one of the top retirement destinations in Florida. It was featured in the November\xe2\x80\x93December 2012 issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flagler_Beach_Historical_Museum">Flagler Beach Historical Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagler_Beach,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Flagler Beach Historical Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Flagler_Beach_Historical_Museum" title="Flagler Beach Historical Museum">Flagler Beach Historical Museum</a>\'s permanent collection is entirely dedicated to Florida history, featuring Flagler Beach and Flagler County. The collection ranges from prehistoric bones and other remains of the Stone Age to a "Space Age" side. The latter has an exhibit with items provided by NASA astronauts, including space food and the Flagler Beach city flag, which was sent to orbit the earth aboard the <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Endeavour" title="Space Shuttle Endeavour">Space Shuttle Endeavour</a>.</i></p>\n<p>Orange Period pottery pieces from indigenous peoples of the region date from 2000 B.C. to 500 A.D. Historic Native American Indian life is represented by an exhibit of arrowheads and other artifacts, all discovered in Flagler County.</p>\n<p>The Florida territory had changing European rulers in the war years between the 1500s and the early 1800s: Spanish, English and American. Mill and plantation artifacts make up the display about the Plantation Period. Personal use items, such as buttons and bottles obtained from area missions, represent the history of individuals in the area. From the Mala Compra Plantation, burned down during the Second Seminole War, the Museum has items from the early 19th-century home of Joseph Hernandez, who was elected as the first Hispanic congressman in the U.S.</p>\n<p>The period of the late 1800s and early 1900s are represented by books and exhibits about the area\'s economy: county farming of cabbage and potatoes, timber industry, railroad artifacts, and turpentine camp items. Exhibits also include documents and memorabilia associated with the 20th-century development boom that began in the western portion of Flagler County and was continued oceanside. Displays feature four Flagler Beach "first families" who purchased land and built what was once considered a seaside resort.</p>\n<p>Other display items from the early 1900s include memorabilia from early city government, the county\'s Old Brick Road, the Flagler Beach Hotel, World War II items, the A1A highway, Marineland, and early advertising brochures. Representations of local organizations from 1925 forward are represented in displays featuring the Flagler Beach Fire Department, Boy Scouts of Flagler, and Flagler Beach schools.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_department">Fire department</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagler_Beach,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fire department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Flagler Beach Fire Department was established in 1926 by the Town of Flagler Beach\'s Commission. Currently, Flagler Beach has a population of roughly 5,500 year-round residents and its fire department responds to approximately 1,000 calls a year. The City of Flagler Beach is served by a full-time professional staff on duty 24 hours a day, 7 days a week to provide immediate response 365 days a year. In the 21st century, the city has seven full-time employees in the <a href="/wiki/Fire_department" title="Fire department">fire department</a>, 1-Fire Chief, 1-Asst. Fire Chief, and 5-<a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">Firefighter</a>/<a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_technician" title="Emergency medical technician">EMTs</a>. To complement the career staff, about twenty <a href="/wiki/Volunteering" title="Volunteering">volunteer</a> firefighters participate in actions.</p>\n<p>For firefighting they have two <a href="/wiki/Fire_engine" title="Fire engine">engines</a> (1996 Pierce &amp; 2005 E-One) and an aerial apparatus (1984 Grumman). For <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildland" title="Wildland">wildland</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brush_fire" title="Brush fire">brush fires</a>, they have a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Woods_truck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Woods truck (page does not exist)">woods truck</a> (1967 Military 6x6) and a small <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brush_truck" title="Brush truck">brush truck</a> (2003 Chevrolet). As this is a coastal community with water on two sides, the Atlantic Ocean to the east and <a href="/wiki/Intracoastal_Waterway" title="Intracoastal Waterway">Intracoastal Waterway</a> to the west, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Water_rescue&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Water rescue (page does not exist)">water rescue</a> equipment is essential. The department has two boats (1994 AVON Inflatable &amp; 2006 Patten Inflatable), along with a <a href="/wiki/WaveRunner" title="WaveRunner">WaveRunner</a> <a href="/wiki/Personal_water_craft" title="Personal water craft">personal water craft</a> (2005 Yamaha). They have a 4-wheeler ATV (2005 Yamaha) for hauling this equipment. The Fire Chief uses a Command Vehicle for daily operations; a Fire Police unit carries <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highway_safety" title="Highway safety">highway safety</a> equipment used to protect firefighters and victims at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traffic_accident" title="Traffic accident">traffic accidents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flagler_Beach,_Florida&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-CenPopGazetteer2016-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-CenPopGazetteer2016_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a class="external text" href="https://www2.census.gov/geo/docs/maps-data/data/gazetteer/2016_Gazetteer/2016_gaz_place_12.txt" rel="nofollow">"2016 U.S. Gazetteer Files"</a>.
