The red line is temperature, the green line is the dew point, and the yellow line is the air parcel lifted.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <b>convective available potential energy</b> (CAPE),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the amount of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">parcel</a> of air would have if lifted a certain distance vertically through the atmosphere. CAPE is effectively the positive <a href="/wiki/Buoyancy" title="Buoyancy">buoyancy</a> of an air parcel and is an indicator of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">atmospheric instability</a>, which makes it very valuable in predicting <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">severe weather</a>. It is a form of fluid instability found in thermally stratified atmospheres in which a colder fluid overlies a warmer one. As explained below, when an air mass is unstable, the element of the air mass that is displaced upwards is accelerated by the pressure differential between the displaced air and the ambient air at the (higher) altitude to which it was displaced. This usually creates vertically developed clouds from <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convection</a>, due to the rising motion, which can eventually lead to thunderstorms. It could also be created by other phenomena, such as a cold front. Even if the air is cooler on the surface, there is still warmer air in the mid-levels, that can rise into the upper-levels. However, if there is not enough water vapor present, there is no ability for condensation, thus storms, clouds, and rain will not form.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">\n<semantics>\n<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">\n<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">\n<mi>g</mi>\n</mstyle>\n</mrow>\n<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\\displaystyle g}</annotation>\n</semantics>\n</math></span><img alt="g" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.126ex; height:2.009ex;"/></span> is the <a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity">acceleration due to gravity</a>. CAPE for a given region is most often calculated from a <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_diagrams" title="Thermodynamic diagrams">thermodynamic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sounding" title="Atmospheric sounding">sounding</a> diagram (e.g., a <a href="/wiki/Skew-T_log-P_diagram" title="Skew-T log-P diagram">Skew-T log-P diagram</a>) using air <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> data usually measured by a <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">weather balloon</a>.</p>\n<p>CAPE is effectively positive buoyancy, expressed <i>B+</i> or simply <i>B</i>; the opposite of <a href="/wiki/Convective_inhibition" title="Convective inhibition">convective inhibition (CIN)</a>, which is expressed as <i>B-</i>, and can be thought of as "negative CAPE". As with CIN, CAPE is usually expressed in J/kg but may also be expressed as m<sup>2</sup>/s<sup>2</sup>, as the values are equivalent. In fact, CAPE is sometimes referred to as <i>positive buoyant energy</i> (<i>PBE</i>). This type of CAPE is the maximum energy available to an ascending parcel and to moist convection. When a layer of CIN is present, the layer must be eroded by surface heating or mechanical lifting, so that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Convective_boundary_layer" title="Convective boundary layer">convective boundary layer</a> parcels may reach their <a href="/wiki/Level_of_free_convection" title="Level of free convection">level of free convection</a> (LFC).</p>\n<p>On a sounding diagram, CAPE is the <i>positive area</i> above the LFC, the area between the parcel\'s virtual temperature line and the environmental virtual temperature line where the ascending parcel is warmer than the environment. Neglecting the virtual temperature correction may result in substantial relative errors in the calculated value of CAPE for small CAPE values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> CAPE may also exist below the LFC, but if a layer of CIN (<a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>) is present, it is unavailable to deep, moist convection until CIN is exhausted. When there is mechanical lift to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturated_fluid" title="Saturated fluid">saturation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cloud_base" title="Cloud base">cloud base</a> begins at the <a href="/wiki/Lifted_condensation_level" title="Lifted condensation level">lifted condensation level</a> (LCL); absent forcing, <a href="/wiki/Cloud_base" title="Cloud base">cloud base</a> begins at the <a href="/wiki/Convective_condensation_level" title="Convective condensation level">convective condensation level</a> (CCL) where heating from below causes spontaneous buoyant lifting to the point of <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Convective_temperature" title="Convective temperature">convective temperature</a> is reached. When CIN is absent or is overcome, saturated parcels at the LCL or CCL, which had been small <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus clouds</a>, will rise to the LFC, and then spontaneously rise until hitting the stable layer of the equilibrium level. The result is deep, moist convection (DMC), or simply, a thunderstorm.</p>\n<p>When a parcel is unstable, it will continue to move vertically, in either direction, dependent on whether it receives upward or downward forcing, until it reaches a stable layer (though momentum, gravity, and other forcing may cause the parcel to continue). There are multiple types of CAPE, <i>downdraft CAPE</i> (<i>DCAPE</i>), estimates the potential strength of rain and evaporatively cooled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Downdraft" title="Downdraft">downdrafts</a>. Other types of CAPE may depend on the depth being considered. Other examples are <i>surface based CAPE</i> (<i>SBCAPE</i>), <i>mixed layer</i> or <i>mean layer CAPE</i> (<i>MLCAPE</i>), <i>most unstable</i> or <i>maximum usable CAPE</i> (<i>MUCAPE</i>), and <i>normalized CAPE</i> (<i>NCAPE</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPC_parameters_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPC_parameters-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Fluid elements displaced upwards or downwards in such an atmosphere expand or compress <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatically" title="Adiabatically">adiabatically</a> in order to remain in pressure equilibrium with their surroundings, and in this manner become less or more dense.</p>\n<p>If the adiabatic decrease or increase in density is <i>less</i> than the decrease or increase in the density of the ambient (not moved) medium, then the displaced fluid element will be subject to downwards or upwards pressure, which will function to restore it to its original position. Hence, there will be a counteracting force to the initial displacement. Such a condition is referred to as <i>convective stability</i>.</p>\n<p>On the other hand, if adiabatic decrease or increase in density is <i>greater</i> than in the ambient fluid, the upwards or downwards displacement will be met with an additional force in the same direction exerted by the ambient fluid. In these circumstances, small deviations from the initial state will become amplified. This condition is referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/Convective_instability" title="Convective instability">convective instability</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Convective instability is also termed <i>static instability</i>, because the instability does not depend on the existing motion of the air; this contrasts with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynamic_instability_(fluid_mechanics)" title="Dynamic instability (fluid mechanics)">dynamic instability</a> where instability is dependent on the motion of air and its associated effects such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamic_lifting&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dynamic lifting (page does not exist)">dynamic lifting</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance_to_thunderstorms">Significance to thunderstorms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convective_available_potential_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Significance to thunderstorms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">Thunderstorms</a> form when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_parcel" title="Air parcel">air parcels</a> are lifted vertically. Deep, moist convection requires a parcel to be lifted to the LFC where it then rises spontaneously until reaching a layer of non-positive buoyancy. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> is warm at the surface and lower levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> where there is <a href="/wiki/Mixed_layer" title="Mixed layer">mixing</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">planetary boundary layer (PBL)</a>), but becomes substantially cooler with height. The temperature profile of the atmosphere, the change in temperature, the degree that it cools with height, is the <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a>. When the rising air parcel cools more slowly than the surrounding atmosphere, it remains warmer and less <a href="/wiki/Density_of_air" title="Density of air">dense</a>. The parcel continues to rise freely (<a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convectively</a>; without mechanical lift) through the atmosphere until it reaches an area of air less dense (warmer) than itself.</p>\n<p>The amount, and shape, of the positive-buoyancy area modulates the speed of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updrafts</a>, thus extreme CAPE can result in explosive thunderstorm development; such rapid development usually occurs when CAPE stored by a <a href="/wiki/Capping_inversion" title="Capping inversion">capping inversion</a> is released when the "lid" is broken by heating or mechanical lift. The amount of CAPE also modulates how low-level <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> is entrained and then stretched in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Updraft" title="Updraft">updraft</a>, with importance to <a href="/wiki/Tornadogenesis" title="Tornadogenesis">tornadogenesis</a>. The most important CAPE for <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> is within the lowest 1 to 3\xc2\xa0km (0.6 to 1.9\xc2\xa0mi) of the atmosphere, whilst deep layer CAPE and the width of CAPE at mid-levels is important for <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercells</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tornado_outbreak" title="Tornado outbreak">Tornado outbreaks</a> tend to occur within high CAPE environments. Large CAPE is required for the production of very large hail, owing to updraft strength, although a rotating updraft may be stronger with less CAPE. Large CAPE also promotes lightning activity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climatology_parameters_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-climatology_parameters-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Two notable days for severe weather exhibited CAPE values over 5 kJ/kg. Two hours before the <a href="/wiki/1999_Oklahoma_tornado_outbreak" title="1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak">1999 Oklahoma tornado outbreak</a> occurred on May 3, 1999, the CAPE value sounding at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City,_Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma City, Oklahoma">Oklahoma City</a> was at 5.89 kJ/kg. A few hours later, an <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">F5</a> tornado ripped through the southern suburbs of the city. Also on May 4, 2007 CAPE values of 5.5 kJ/kg were reached and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_Scale" title="Enhanced Fujita Scale">EF5</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_2007_Tornado_Outbreak" title="May 2007 Tornado Outbreak">tornado</a> tore through <a href="/wiki/Greensburg,_Kansas" title="Greensburg, Kansas">Greensburg, Kansas</a>. On these days, it was apparent that conditions were ripe for tornadoes and CAPE wasn\'t a crucial factor. However, extreme CAPE, by modulating the updraft (and downdraft), can allow for exceptional events, such as the deadly F5 tornadoes that hit <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plainfield_Tornado" title="Plainfield Tornado">Plainfield, Illinois</a> on August 28, 1990 and <a href="/wiki/Jarrell,_Texas" title="Jarrell, Texas">Jarrell, Texas</a> on May 27, 1997 on days which weren\'t readily apparent as conducive to large tornadoes. CAPE was estimated to exceed 8 kJ/kg in the environment of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plainfield_Tornado" title="Plainfield Tornado">Plainfield storm</a> and was around 7 kJ/kg for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Texas_tornado_outbreak" title="Central Texas tornado outbreak">Jarrell storm</a>.</p>\n<p>Severe weather and tornadoes can develop in an area of low CAPE values. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/April_2004_Utica_tornado_outbreak" title="April 2004 Utica tornado outbreak">surprise severe weather event</a> that occurred in <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a> on April 20, 2004 is a good example. Importantly in that case, was that although overall CAPE was weak, there was strong CAPE in the lowest levels of the troposphere which enabled an outbreak of minisupercells producing large, long-track, intense tornadoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Utica_outbreak_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Utica_outbreak-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example_from_meteorology">Example from meteorology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convective_available_potential_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Example from meteorology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A good example of convective instability can be found in our own atmosphere. If dry mid-level air is drawn over very warm, moist air in the lower <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrolapse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hydrolapse (page does not exist)">hydrolapse</a> (an area of rapidly decreasing dew point temperatures with height) results in the region where the moist <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a> and mid-level air meet. As daytime heating increases mixing within the moist boundary layer, some of the moist air will begin to interact with the dry mid-level air above it. Owing to thermodynamic processes, as the dry mid-level air is slowly saturated its temperature begins to drop, increasing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Adiabatic lapse rate">adiabatic lapse rate</a>. Under certain conditions, the lapse rate can increase significantly in a short amount of time, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>. High convective instability can lead to severe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tornadoes" title="Tornadoes">tornadoes</a> as moist air which is trapped in the boundary layer eventually becomes highly negatively buoyant relative to the adiabatic lapse rate and escapes as a rapidly rising bubble of humid air triggering the development of a <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" title="Cumulonimbus">cumulonimbus</a> cloud.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convective_available_potential_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_thermodynamics" title="Atmospheric thermodynamics">Atmospheric thermodynamics</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Lifted_index" title="Lifted index">Lifted index</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Convective_available_potential_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 35em; -webkit-column-width: 35em; column-width: 35em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation journal">M.
