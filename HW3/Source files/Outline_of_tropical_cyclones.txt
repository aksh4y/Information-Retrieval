lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The following <a href="/wiki/Outline_(list)" title="Outline (list)">outline</a> is provided as an overview of and topical guide to tropical cyclones:</p>\n<p><b><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></b> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm system</a> characterized by a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_pressure_system" title="Low pressure system">low-pressure</a> center and numerous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a> that produce strong winds and heavy rain. Tropical <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a> strengthen when water evaporated from the ocean is released as the saturated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air" title="Air">air</a> rises, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> contained in the moist air. They are fueled by a different heat mechanism than other cyclonic windstorms such as <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor\'easter">nor\'easters</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_windstorm" title="European windstorm">European windstorms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a>. The characteristic that separates tropical cyclones from other cyclonic systems is that at any height in the atmosphere, the center of a tropical cyclone will be warmer than its surroundings; a phenomenon called "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warm_core" title="Warm core">warm core</a>" storm systems.</p>\n<div class="toclimit-2">\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div
