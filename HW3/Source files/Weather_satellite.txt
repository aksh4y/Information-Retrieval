href="/wiki/File:GOES_8_Spac0255.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOES" title="GOES">GOES-8</a>, a United States weather satellite of the <a href="/wiki/Meteorological-satellite_service" title="Meteorological-satellite service">meteorological-satellite service</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <b>weather satellite</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> that is primarily used to monitor the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>. Satellites can be <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">polar orbiting</a>, covering the entire Earth asynchronously, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geostationary" title="Geostationary">geostationary</a>, hovering over the same spot on the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Meteorological <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> see more than clouds and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_system_(meteorological)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cloud system (meteorological) (page does not exist)">cloud systems</a>. City lights, fires, effects of pollution, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auroral_light" title="Auroral light">auroras</a>, sand and <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust storms</a>, snow cover, ice mapping, boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a>, energy flows, etc. Other types of environmental information are collected using weather satellites. Weather satellite images helped in monitoring the volcanic ash cloud from <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">Mount St. Helens</a> and activity from other volcanoes such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Etna" title="Mount Etna">Mount Etna</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Smoke from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_fires" title="Forest fires">fires</a> in the western United States such as <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> and <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a> have also been monitored.</p>\n<p>Other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_satellite" title="Environmental satellite">environmental satellites</a> can detect changes in the Earth\'s vegetation, <a href="/wiki/Sea_state" title="Sea state">sea state</a>, ocean color, and ice fields. For example, the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Prestige_oil_spill" title="Prestige oil spill">Prestige oil spill</a> off the northwest coast of Spain was watched carefully by the European <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ENVISAT" title="ENVISAT">ENVISAT</a>, which, though not a weather satellite, flies an instrument (ASAR) which can see changes in the sea surface.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</a> and its effects on weather are monitored daily from satellite images. The Antarctic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ozone_hole" title="Ozone hole">ozone hole</a> is mapped from weather satellite data. Collectively, weather satellites flown by the U.S., Europe, India, China, Russia, and Japan provide nearly continuous observations for a global weather watch.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TIROS-1-Earth.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="582" data-file-width="527" height="243" being developed. This was due to sparse data observation coverage and the expense of using cloud cameras on rockets. By 1958, the early prototypes for TIROS and Vanguard (developed by the Army Signal Corps) were created.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-first-3">[3]</a></sup> The first weather satellite, <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_2" title="Vanguard 2">Vanguard 2</a>, was launched on February\xc2\xa017, 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was designed to measure cloud cover and resistance, but a poor axis of rotation and its elliptical orbit kept it from collecting a notable amount of useful data. The Explorer VI and VII satellites also contained weather-related experiments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-first-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The first weather satellite to be considered a success was <a href="/wiki/TIROS-1" title="TIROS-1">TIROS-1</a>, launched by NASA on April\xc2\xa01, 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apupi_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-apupi-5">[5]</a></sup> TIROS operated for 78\xc2\xa0days and proved to be much more successful than Vanguard\xc2\xa02. TIROS paved the way for the <a href="/wiki/Nimbus_program" title="Nimbus program">Nimbus program</a>, whose technology and findings are the heritage of most of the Earth-observing satellites NASA and NOAA have launched since then. Beginning with the Nimbus\xc2\xa03 satellite in 1969, temperature information through the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">tropospheric</a> column began to be retrieved by satellites from the eastern Atlantic and most of the Pacific Ocean, which led to significant improvements to <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The ESSA and NOAA polar orbiting satellites followed suit from the late 1960s onward. Geostationary satellites followed, beginning with the ATS and SMS series in the late\xc2\xa01960s and early\xc2\xa01970s, then continuing with the GOES series from the 1970s onward. Polar orbiting satellites such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/QuikScat" title="QuikScat">QuikScat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Rainfall_Measuring_Mission" title="Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission">TRMM</a> began to relay wind information near the ocean\'s surface starting in the late\xc2\xa01970s, with microwave imagery which resembled radar displays, which significantly improved the diagnoses of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> strength, intensification, and location during the 2000s and 2010s.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observation">Observation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Observation is typically made via different \'channels\' of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a>, in particular, the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible</a> and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> portions.</p>\n<p>Some of these channels include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUMETSAT_-_MSG_Spectrum_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EUMETSAT_-_MSG_Spectrum-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EUMETSAT_-_MFG_PL_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EUMETSAT_-_MFG_PL-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<ul>\n<li><i>Visible and Near Infrared:</i> 0.6\xe2\x80\x931.6\xc2\xa0\xce\xbcm\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x93 for recording cloud cover during the day</li>\n<li><i>Infrared:</i> 3.9\xe2\x80\x937.3\xc2\xa0\xce\xbcm (water vapor), 8.7\xe2\x80\x9313.4\xc2\xa0\xce\xbcm (thermal imaging)</li>\n</ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visible_spectrum">Visible spectrum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Visible spectrum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Visible-light images from weather satellites during local daylight hours are easy to interpret even by the average person; clouds, cloud systems such as fronts and tropical storms, lakes, forests, mountains, snow ice, fires, and pollution such as smoke, smog, dust and haze are readily apparent. Even wind can be determined by cloud patterns, alignments and movement from successive photos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_spectrum">Infrared spectrum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Infrared spectrum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thermography" title="Thermography">thermal</a> or infrared images recorded by sensors called scanning <a href="/wiki/Radiometer" title="Radiometer">radiometers</a> enable a trained analyst to determine cloud heights and types, to calculate land and surface water temperatures, and to locate ocean surface features. <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">Infrared</a> satellite imagery can be used effectively for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> with a visible <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> pattern, using the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a>, where the difference between the temperature of the warm eye and the surrounding cold cloud tops can be used to determine its intensity (colder cloud tops generally indicate a more intense storm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Infrared pictures depict ocean eddies or vortices and map currents such as the Gulf Stream which are valuable to the shipping industry. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishermen" title="Fishermen">Fishermen</a> and farmers are interested in knowing land and water temperatures to protect their crops against frost or increase their catch from the sea. Even El Ni\xc3\xb1o phenomena can be spotted. Using color-digitized techniques, the gray shaded <a href="/wiki/Thermographic_camera" title="Thermographic camera">thermal images</a> can be converted to color for easier identification of desired information.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a id="Geostationary">Geostationary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geostationary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"geostationary meteorological satellite" redirects here. For the Japanese satellites called "Geostationary Meteorological Satellite", see <a href="/wiki/Himawari_(satellite)" title="Himawari (satellite)">Himawari (satellite)</a>.</div>\n<p>Geostationary weather satellites orbit the Earth above the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> at altitudes of 35,880\xc2\xa0km (22,300 miles). Because of this <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>, they remain stationary with respect to the rotating Earth and thus can record or transmit images of the entire hemisphere below continuously with their visible-light and infrared sensors. The news media use the geostationary photos in their daily weather presentation as single images or made into movie loops. These are also available on the city forecast pages of www.noaa.gov (example Dallas, TX).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Several geostationary meteorological spacecraft are in operation. The United States has three in operation; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOES" title="GOES">GOES</a>-12, GOES-13, and GOES-15. GOES-12, previously designated GOES-East and now used for South America, is located at 60 degrees west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> GOES-13 took over the role of GOES-East on April 14, 2010 and is located at 75 degrees west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> GOES-11 was GOES-West over the eastern Pacific Ocean until it was decommissioned December 2011 and replaced by GOES-15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>\'s new-generation weather satellite <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elektro-L" title="Elektro-L">Elektro-L</a> <a href="/wiki/Elektro-L_No.1" title="Elektro-L No.1">No.1</a> operates at 76\xc2\xb0E over the Indian Ocean. The Japanese have the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTSAT" title="MTSAT">MTSAT</a>-2 located over the mid Pacific at 145\xc2\xb0E and the <a href="/wiki/Himawari_8" title="Himawari 8">Himawari 8</a> at 140\xc2\xb0E. The Europeans have four in operation, <a href="/wiki/Meteosat" title="Meteosat">Meteosat</a>-8 (3.5\xc2\xb0W) and Meteosat-9 (0\xc2\xb0) over the Atlantic Ocean and have Meteosat-6 (63\xc2\xb0E) and Meteosat-7 (57.5\xc2\xb0E) over the Indian Ocean. China currently has three <a href="/wiki/Fengyun" title="Fengyun">Fengyun</a> (\xe9\xa3\x8e\xe4\xba\x91) geostationary satellites (FY-2E at 86.5\xc2\xb0E, FY-2F at 123.5\xc2\xb0E, and FY-2G at 105\xc2\xb0E) operated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> also operates geostationary satellites called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INSAT" title="INSAT">INSAT</a> which carry instruments for meteorological purposes.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_orbiting">Polar orbiting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Polar orbiting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Parabolic-antenna-LEO.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1168" data-file-width="725" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Parabolic-antenna-LEO.jpg/150px-Parabolic-antenna-LEO.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Parabolic-antenna-LEO.jpg/225px-Parabolic-antenna-LEO.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Parabolic-antenna-LEO.jpg/300px-Parabolic-antenna-LEO.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Parabolic-antenna-LEO.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nComputer-controlled motorized parabolic dish antenna for tracking <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">LEO</a> weather satellites.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Polar orbiting weather satellites circle the Earth at a typical altitude of 850\xc2\xa0km (530 miles) in a north to south (or vice versa) path, passing over the poles in their continuous flight. Polar orbiting weather satellites are in <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">sun-synchronous orbits</a>, which means they are able to observe any place on Earth and will view every location twice each day with the same general lighting conditions due to the near-constant local <a href="/wiki/Solar_time" title="Solar time">solar time</a>. Polar orbiting weather satellites offer a much better resolution than their geostationary counterparts due their closeness to the Earth.</p>\n<p>The United States has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> series of polar orbiting meteorological satellites, presently NOAA 17 and NOAA 18 as primary spacecraft, NOAA 15 and NOAA 16 as secondary spacecraft, NOAA 14 in standby, and NOAA 12. Europe has the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metop" title="Metop">Metop</a>-A and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metop" title="Metop">Metop</a>-B satellites operated by <a href="/wiki/EUMETSAT" title="EUMETSAT">EUMETSAT</a>. Russia has the <a href="/wiki/Meteor_(satellite)" title="Meteor (satellite)">Meteor</a> and RESURS series of satellites. China has <a href="/wiki/Fengyun" title="Fengyun">FY</a>-3A, 3B and 3C. India has polar orbiting satellites as well.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="DMSP">DMSP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: DMSP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SatelliteAntenna-137MHz.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="921" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/SatelliteAntenna-137MHz.jpg/220px-SatelliteAntenna-137MHz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/SatelliteAntenna-137MHz.jpg/330px-SatelliteAntenna-137MHz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/SatelliteAntenna-137MHz.jpg/440px-SatelliteAntenna-137MHz.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SatelliteAntenna-137MHz.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Turnstile_antenna" title="Turnstile antenna">Turnstile antenna</a> for reception of 137 MHz <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_Earth_Orbit" title="Low Earth Orbit">LEO</a> weather satellite transmissions</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a>\'s Meteorological Satellite (<a href="/wiki/Defense_Meteorological_Satellite_Program" title="Defense Meteorological Satellite Program">DMSP</a>) can "see" the best of all weather vehicles with its ability to detect objects almost as \'small\' as a huge <a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">oil tanker</a>. In addition, of all the weather satellites in orbit, only DMSP can "see" at night in the visual. Some of the most spectacular photos have been recorded by the night visual sensor; city lights, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, fires, lightning, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">meteors</a>, oil field burn-offs, as well as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_Borealis" title="Aurora Borealis">Aurora Borealis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_Australis" title="Aurora Australis">Aurora Australis</a> have been captured by this 450-mile-high space vehicle\'s low moonlight sensor.</p>\n<p>At the same time, energy use and city growth can be monitored since both major and even minor cities, as well as highway lights, are conspicuous. This informs <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">light pollution</a>. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_City_Blackout_of_1977" title="New York City Blackout of 1977">New York City Blackout of 1977</a> was captured by one of the night orbiter DMSP space vehicles.</p>\n<p>In addition to monitoring city lights, these photos are a life saving asset in the detection and monitoring of fires. Not only do the satellites see the fires visually day and night, but the thermal and <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> scanners on board these weather satellites detect potential fire sources below the surface of the Earth where smoldering occurs. Once the fire is detected, the same weather satellites provide vital information about wind that could fan or spread the fires. These same cloud photos from space tell the <a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">firefighter</a> when it will rain.</p>\n<p>Some of the most dramatic photos showed the 600 <a href="/wiki/Kuwaiti_oil_fires" title="Kuwaiti oil fires">Kuwaiti oil fires</a> that the fleeing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Army_of_Iraq" title="Army of Iraq">Army of Iraq</a> started on February 23, 1991. The night photos showed huge flashes, far outstripping the glow of large populated areas. The fires consumed millions of gallons of oil; the last was doused on November 6, 1991.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Snowfield monitoring, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a>, can be helpful to the hydrologist keeping track of available <a href="/wiki/Snowpack" title="Snowpack">snowpack</a> for runoff vital to the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watersheds</a> of the western United States. This information is gleaned from existing satellites of all agencies of the U.S. government (in addition to local, on-the-ground measurements). Ice floes, packs and bergs can also be located and tracked from weather space craft.</p>\n<p>Even pollution whether it is nature-made or man-made can be pinpointed. The visual and infrared photos show effects of pollution from their respective areas over the entire earth. Aircraft and <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> pollution, as well as <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">condensation trails</a>, can also be spotted. The ocean current and low level wind information gleaned from the space photos can help predict oceanic oil spill coverage and movement. Almost every summer, sand and dust from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a> in Africa drifts across the equatorial regions of the Atlantic Ocean. GOES-EAST photos enable meteorologists to observe, track and forecast this sand cloud. In addition to reducing visibilities and causing respiratory problems, sand clouds suppress <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> formation by modifying the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> balance of the tropics. Other <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust storms</a> in Asia and <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a> are common and easy to spot and monitor, with recent examples of dust moving across the Pacific Ocean and reaching North America.</p>\n<p>In remote areas of the world with few local observers, fires could rage out of control for days or even weeks and consume millions of acres before authorities are alerted. Weather satellites can be a tremendous asset in such situations. Nighttime photos also show the burn-off in gas and oil fields. Atmospheric temperature and moisture profiles have been taken by weather satellites since 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span
