class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>Long March rocket</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">\xe9\x95\xbf\xe5\xbe\x81\xe7\xb3\xbb\xe5\x88\x97\xe8\xbf\x90\xe8\xbd\xbd\xe7\x81\xab\xe7\xae\xad</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant">\xe9\x95\xb7\xe5\xbe\x81\xe7\xb3\xbb\xe5\x88\x97\xe9\x81\x8b\xe8\xbc\x89\xe7\x81\xab\xe7\xae\xad</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span In English, the rockets are abbreviated as LM- for export and CZ- within China, as "Chang Zheng" means "Long March" in Chinese <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>. The rockets are named after the <a href="/wiki/Long_March" title="Long March">Long March</a> of <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="History of the People\'s Republic of China">Chinese communist history</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#History"><span tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:144px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chang_zheng_3b.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="194" data-file-width="142" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/Chang_zheng_3b.png" width="142"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chang_zheng_3b.png" <a href="/wiki/List_of_Long_March_launches" title="List of Long March launches">List of Long March launches</a></div>\n<p>China launched its first <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a>, known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dong_Fang_Hong_1" title="Dong Fang Hong 1">Dong Fang Hong 1</a> (lit. "The East is Red 1"), into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_orbit" title="Earth orbit">Earth orbit</a> on its Long March space rocket on April 24, 1970, becoming the fifth nation to achieve independent launch capability. Early launches had an inconsistent record, focusing on launching of Chinese satellites.</p>\n<p>Starting in 1990, the Long March rocket entered the international market. However, several setbacks occurred during the early 1990s. On January 26, 1995, a Long March 2E rocket veered off course two seconds after take-off from Xichang space center and exploded, killing at least six on the ground.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On February 14, 1996, a similar failure occurred during the launch of <a href="/wiki/Intelsat_708" title="Intelsat 708">Intelsat 708</a>: The rocket veered severely off course immediately after clearing the launch tower and crashed into a village.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Following the disaster, foreign media were sequestered in a bunker for five hours while, some have alleged, the Chinese military attempted to \'clean up\' the damage<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Officials later blamed the failure on an "unexpected gust of wind"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Xinhua_News_Agency" title="Xinhua News Agency">Xinhua News Agency</a> initially reported 6 deaths and 57 injuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the aftermath of the explosion, U.S. satellite makers shared information that allowed the Chinese to determine that the problem was in the welds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This sharing of information was later deemed illegal by the United States, and U.S. satellite maker <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loral_Space_and_Communications" title="Loral Space and Communications">Loral Space and Communications</a> was fined $14 million by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._government" title="U.S. government">U.S. government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loralfine_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-loralfine-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>For thirteen years starting in August 1996, 75 consecutive successful launches were conducted, including multiple milestones:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>On October 15, 2003, the Long March 2F rocket successfully launched the <i><a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_5" title="Shenzhou 5">Shenzhou 5</a></i> spacecraft/orbiter carrying China\'s first astronaut into space; China thus became the third nation to send a person in space independently, after the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>/Russia and the United States.</li>\n<li>On October 12, 2005, Long March 2F launched the <i><a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_6" title="Shenzhou 6">Shenzhou 6</a></i> with two astronauts.</li>\n<li>On June 1, 2007, Long March rockets completed their 100th launch overall.</li>\n<li>On October 24, 2007, the Long March 3A successfully launched (18:05 GMT+8) the "<a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_1" title="Chang\'e 1">Chang\'e 1</a>" <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">lunar</a> orbiting <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Xichang_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Xichang Satellite Launch Center">Xichang Satellite Launch Center</a>.</li>\n<li>On September 25, 2008, a Long March 2F launched <i><a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_7" title="Shenzhou 7">Shenzhou 7</a></i>, China\'s first three-man mission and first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">EVA</a> mission.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>The string of successful launches ended on August 31, 2009, when the launch of <a href="/wiki/Palapa-D" title="Palapa-D">Palapa-D</a> partially failed due to a third stage malfunction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Palapa_failure_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Palapa_failure-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On the June 11, 2013, a <a href="/wiki/Long_March_2F" title="Long March 2F">Long March 2F</a>, launched from the <a href="/wiki/Jiuquan_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center">Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center</a>, putting three astronauts into orbit for the <a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_10" title="Shenzhou 10">Shenzhou 10</a> mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As of March 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there have been 225 Long March missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Payloads">Payloads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Payloads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Long March is China\'s primary expendable launch system family. The <a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_(spacecraft)" title="Shenzhou (spacecraft)">Shenzhou</a> spacecraft and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Lunar_Exploration_Program" title="Chinese Lunar Exploration Program">Chang\'e</a> lunar orbiters are also launched on the Long March rocket. The maximum payload for <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">LEO</a> is 12,000 kilograms (CZ-3B), the maximum payload for <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_transfer_orbit" title="Geostationary transfer orbit">GTO</a> is 5,500\xc2\xa0kg (CZ-3B/E). The next generation rocket\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x93 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_March_5_rocket_family" title="Long March 5 rocket family">Long March 5</a> variants will offer more payload in the future.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Propellants">Propellants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Propellants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Long_March_1" title="Long March 1">Long March 1</a>\'s 1st and 2nd stage uses <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UDMH" title="UDMH">UDMH</a> propellants, and its upper stage uses a spin-stabilized solid rocket engine.</p>\n<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_March_2" title="Long March 2">Long March 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_March_3" title="Long March 3">Long March 3</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_March_4" title="Long March 4">Long March 4</a>, the main stages and associated <a href="/wiki/Liquid_rocket_booster" title="Liquid rocket booster">liquid rocket boosters</a> use <a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_tetroxide" title="Dinitrogen tetroxide">dinitrogen tetroxide</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UDMH" title="UDMH">UDMH</a> as the fuel. The upper stages (third stage) of Long March 3 rockets use <a href="/wiki/YF-73" title="YF-73">YF-73</a> and <a href="/wiki/YF-75" title="YF-75">YF-75</a> engines, using <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">Liquid hydrogen</a> (LH<sub>2</sub>) as the fuel and <a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">Liquid oxygen</a> (LOX) as the oxidizer.</p>\n<p>The new generation of Long March rocket family, <a href="/wiki/Long_March_5" title="Long March 5">Long March 5</a>, and its derivations <a href="/wiki/Long_March_6" title="Long March 6">Long March 6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_March_7" title="Long March 7">Long March 7</a> will use <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LOX" title="LOX">LOX</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerosene" title="Kerosene">kerosene</a> as core stage and liquid booster propellant, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LOX" title="LOX">LOX</a> and LH<sub>2</sub> in upper stages.</p>\n<h2><span href="/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Long March 8">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A new series of launch vehicles in study, which is geared towards <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">SSO</a> launches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_March_9">Long March 9</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Long March 9">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <i>Long March 9</i> (LM-9, CZ-9, or Changzheng 9, Chinese: \xe9\x95\xbf\xe5\xbe\x81\xe4\xb9\x9d\xe5\x8f\xb7) is a Chinese super-heavy carrier rocket that is currently in study. It is planned for a maximum payload capacity of at least 140,000\xc2\xa0kg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> to <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">LEO</a> or at least 50,000\xc2\xa0kg to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Transfer_Orbit" title="Lunar Transfer Orbit">Lunar Transfer Orbit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Its first flight is expected in 2025 in preparation for a lunar landing sometime in the 2030s. It has been stated that around 70% of the hardware and components needed for a test flight are currently undergoing testing, with the first engine test to occur by the end of 2018. The proposed design would be a three-staged rocket, with the initial core having a diameter of 10 meters and use a cluster of four engines. Multiple variants on the rocket have been proposed, CZ-9 being the largest with four liquid-fuel boosters with the aforementioned LEO payload capacity of 140,000\xc2\xa0kg, CZ-9A having just two boosters and a LEO payload capacity of 100,000\xc2\xa0kg, and finally CZ-9B having just the core stage and a LEO payload capacity of 50,000\xc2\xa0kg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-16">[16]</a></sup> If produced, it would be classified as a <a href="/wiki/Super_heavy-lift_launch_vehicle" title="Super heavy-lift launch vehicle">super heavy-lift launch vehicle</a> along with the American <a href="/wiki/Saturn_V" title="Saturn V">Saturn V</a> and unsuccessful Soviet <a href="/wiki/N1_(rocket)" title="N1 (rocket)">N1</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">Space Launch System</a> and <a href="/wiki/Falcon_Heavy" title="Falcon Heavy">Falcon Heavy</a> currently under development in the United States.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_March_11">Long March 11</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Long March 11">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <i>Long March 11</i> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">solid-fuel rocket</a> that applies China\'s largest solid-fuel rocket engine and was designed to meet the need to rapidly launch satellites in case of emergencies or disasters. Its first launch on 25 September 2015, was reported to be successful and launched 4 micro satellites into <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">sun-synchronous orbits</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Long_March_1" title="Long March 1">Long March 1</a> rocket is derived from earlier Chinese 2-stage <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IRBM" title="IRBM">IRBM</a> <a href="/wiki/DF-4" title="DF-4">DF-4</a>, or <b>Dong Feng 4 missile</b>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_March_2" title="Long March 2">Long March 2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_March_3" title="Long March 3">Long March 3</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_March_4" title="Long March 4">Long March 4</a> rocket families are derivatives of the Chinese 2-stage <a href="/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile" title="Intercontinental ballistic missile">ICBMs</a> <a href="/wiki/DF-5" title="DF-5">DF-5</a>, or <b>Dong Feng 5 missile</b>. However, like its counterparts in both the United States and in Russia, the differing needs of space rockets and strategic missiles have caused the development of space rockets and missiles to diverge. The main goal of a space rocket is to maximize payload, while for strategic missiles increased throw weight is much less important than the ability to launch quickly and to survive a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_strike" title="First strike">first strike</a>. This divergence has become clear in the next generation of Long March rockets, which use <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic_fuel" title="Cryogenic fuel">cryogenic propellants</a> in sharp contrast to the next generation of strategic missiles, which are mobile and <a href="/wiki/Solid_fuel" title="Solid fuel">solid fuelled</a>.</p>\n<p>The next generation of Long March rocket, <a href="/wiki/Long_March_5" title="Long March 5">Long March 5</a> rocket family will be a brand new design, while <a href="/wiki/Long_March_6" title="Long March 6">Long March 6</a> and <a href="/wiki/Long_March_7" title="Long March 7">Long March 7</a> can be seen as derivations because they use the <a href="/wiki/Liquid_rocket_booster" title="Liquid rocket booster">liquid rocket booster</a> design of <a href="/wiki/Long_March_5" title="Long March 5">Long March 5</a> to build small-to-mid capacity launch vehicles.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch_sites">Launch sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Launch sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are four launch centers in China. They are:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Jiuquan_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center">Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Taiyuan_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Taiyuan Satellite Launch Center">Taiyuan Satellite Launch Center</a></li>\n<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wenchang_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Wenchang Satellite Launch Center">Wenchang Satellite Launch Center</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Xichang_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Xichang Satellite Launch Center">Xichang Satellite Launch Center</a></li>\n</ul>\n<p>Most of the commercial satellite launches of Long March vehicles have been from <a href="/wiki/Xichang_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Xichang Satellite Launch Center">Xichang Satellite Launch Center</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Xichang" title="Xichang">Xichang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a> province. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wenchang_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Wenchang Satellite Launch Center">Wenchang Satellite Launch Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a> province is under expansion and will be the main launch center for future commercial satellite launches. Long March launches also take place from the more military oriented <a href="/wiki/Jiuquan_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center">Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gansu" title="Gansu">Gansu</a> province from which the manned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shenzhou_spacecraft" title="Shenzhou spacecraft">Shenzhou spacecraft</a> also launches. <a href="/wiki/Taiyuan_Satellite_Launch_Center" title="Taiyuan Satellite Launch Center">Taiyuan Satellite Launch Center</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/Shanxi" title="Shanxi">Shanxi</a> province and focuses on the launches of <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">Sun-synchronous orbit</a> satellites.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_launch_services">Commercial launch services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Long_March_(rocket_family)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Commercial launch services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>China markets launch services under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/China_Great_Wall_Industry_Corporation" title="China Great Wall Industry Corporation">China Great Wall Industry Corporation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Its efforts to launch communications satellites were dealt a blow in the mid-1990s after the United States stopped issuing export licenses to companies to allow them to launch on Chinese launch vehicles out of fear that this would help <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army" title="People\'s Liberation Army">China\'s military</a>. In the face of this, <a href="/wiki/Thales_Alenia_Space" title="Thales Alenia Space">Thales Alenia Space</a> built the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinasat-6B" title="Chinasat-6B">Chinasat-6B</a> satellite with no components from the United States whatsoever. This allowed it to be launched on a Chinese launch vehicle without violating U.S. <a href="/wiki/International_Traffic_in_Arms_Regulations" title="International Traffic in Arms Regulations">ITAR</a> restrictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The launch, on a Long March 3B rocket, was successfully conducted on July 5, 2007. A Chinese Long March 2D launched Venezuela\'s <a href="/wiki/VRSS-1" title="VRSS-1">VRSS-1 (Venezuelan Remote Sensing Satellite 1)</a> "Francisco de Miranda" on September 29, 2012.</p>\n<h2><span
