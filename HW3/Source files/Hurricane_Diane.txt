colspan="2"></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Hurricane Diane</b> was the costliest <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a> of its time. One of three hurricanes to hit <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> during the <a href="/wiki/1955_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1955 Atlantic hurricane season">1955 Atlantic hurricane season</a>, it <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">formed</a> on August\xc2\xa07 from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>. Diane initially moved west-northwestward with little change in its intensity, but began to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">strengthen rapidly</a> after turning to the north-northeast. On August\xc2\xa012, the hurricane reached peak <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_winds" title="Maximum sustained winds">sustained winds</a> of 105\xc2\xa0mph (165\xc2\xa0km/h), making it a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">Category 3 hurricane</a>. Gradually weakening after veering back west, Diane made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>, as a strong tropical storm on August\xc2\xa017, just five days after <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Connie" title="Hurricane Connie">Hurricane Connie</a> struck near the same area. Diane weakened further after moving inland, at which point the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">United States Weather Bureau</a> noted a decreased threat of further destruction. The storm turned to the northeast, and warm waters from the Atlantic Ocean helped produce record rainfall across the northeastern United States. On August\xc2\xa019, Diane emerged into the Atlantic Ocean southeast of New York City, becoming <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> two days later and completely dissipating by August\xc2\xa023.</p>\n<p>The first area affected by Diane was North Carolina, which suffered coastal flooding but little wind and rain damage. After the storm weakened in Virginia, it maintained an area of moisture that resulted in heavy rainfall after interacting with the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ridge_Mountains" title="Blue Ridge Mountains">Blue Ridge Mountains</a>, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic lift</a>. Flooding affected roads and low-lying areas along the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a>. The northernmost portion of Delaware also saw freshwater flooding, although to a much lesser extent than adjacent states. Diane produced heavy rainfall in eastern Pennsylvania, causing the worst floods on record there, largely in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poconos" title="Poconos">Poconos</a> and along the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a>. Rushing waters demolished about 150\xc2\xa0road and rail bridges and breached or destroyed 30\xc2\xa0dams. The swollen <a href="/wiki/Brodhead_Creek" title="Brodhead Creek">Brodhead Creek</a> virtually submerged a summer camp, killing 37\xc2\xa0people. Throughout Pennsylvania, the disaster killed 101\xc2\xa0people and caused an estimated $70\xc2\xa0million in damage (1955\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[nb 1]</a></sup> Additional flooding spread through the northwest portion of neighboring New Jersey, forcing hundreds of people to evacuate and destroying several bridges, including one built in 1831. Storm damage was evident but less significant in southeastern New York.</p>\n<p>Damage from Diane was heaviest in Connecticut, where rainfall peaked at 16.86\xc2\xa0in (428\xc2\xa0mm) near <a href="/wiki/Torrington,_Connecticut" title="Torrington, Connecticut">Torrington</a>. The storm produced the state\'s largest flood on record, which effectively split the state into two by destroying bridges and cutting communications. All major streams and valleys were flooded, and 30\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/Stream_gauge" title="Stream gauge">stream gauges</a> reported their highest levels on record. The <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_River" title="Connecticut River">Connecticut River</a> at <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford</a> reached a water level of 30.6\xc2\xa0ft (9.3\xc2\xa0m), the third highest on record there. The flooding destroyed a large section of downtown <a href="/wiki/Winsted,_Connecticut" title="Winsted, Connecticut">Winsted</a>, much of which was never rebuilt. Record-high tides and flooded rivers heavily damaged <a href="/wiki/Woonsocket,_Rhode_Island" title="Woonsocket, Rhode Island">Woonsocket, Rhode Island</a>. In Massachusetts, flood water levels surpassed those during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1938_Long_Island_hurricane" title="1938 Long Island hurricane">1938 Long Island hurricane</a>, breaching multiple dams and inundating adjacent towns and roads. Throughout New England, 206\xc2\xa0dams were damaged or destroyed, and about 7,000\xc2\xa0people were injured. Nationwide, Diane killed at least 184\xc2\xa0people and destroyed 813\xc2\xa0houses, with another 14,000\xc2\xa0homes heavily damaged. Monetary losses totaled $754.7\xc2\xa0million, although the inclusion of loss of business and personal revenue increased the total to over $1\xc2\xa0billion. In the hurricane\'s wake, eight states were declared federal disaster areas, and the name Diane was <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Meteorological_history"><span history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Hurricane Diane originated in a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> first observed as a tropical depression on August\xc2\xa07 between the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> The system moved generally to the west-northwest, intensifying into a tropical storm on August\xc2\xa09.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bt_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bt-3">[2]</a></sup> By the time the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">Weather Bureau</a> first classified the storm on August\xc2\xa010, Diane was south of the <a href="/wiki/Azores_High" title="Azores High">Bermuda high</a>, a semi-permanent <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge in the jet stream</a> just east of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. Ships in the region of the storm reported winds of 45\xc2\xa0mph (72\xc2\xa0km/h). During the next day, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> reported no increase in strength, and Diane initially remained disorganized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> The storm interacted with <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Connie" title="Hurricane Connie">Hurricane Connie</a> to its northwest in a process known as the <a href="/wiki/Fujiwhara_effect" title="Fujiwhara effect">Fujiwhara effect</a>, in which Diane turned toward the north. Quick intensification ensued, potentially due to interaction with a <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core low</a> that increased <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">atmospheric instability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> On August\xc2\xa012, the storm <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensified</a> into a hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bt_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bt-3">[2]</a></sup> The intensification was so quick that a ship southeast of the center believed Diane was undergoing a loop due to a steady drop in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a>, despite moving away from the hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At its peak, Diane developed a well-defined <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> about 30\xc2\xa0mi (48\xc2\xa0km) in diameter, described by <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">reconnaissance aircraft</a> as taking the shape of an "inverted teacup". The strongest winds were located in the northeast quadrant, where there was a secondary pressure minimum located 62\xc2\xa0mi (100\xc2\xa0km) northeast of the eye.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> After moving to the north for about a day, Diane resumed its westward motion on August\xc2\xa013, after Hurricane Connie to the northwest had weakened. That day, Diane reached its lowest pressure of 969\xc2\xa0mbar (28.6\xc2\xa0inHg),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> and peak winds of 105\xc2\xa0mph (170\xc2\xa0km/h); originally the hurricane was analyzed to reach peak winds of 120\xc2\xa0mph (195\xc2\xa0km/h), although the large size and slow forward speed suggested the lower winds. It maintained its peak winds for about 12\xc2\xa0hours,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup> after which it weakened due to cooler air in the region. By August\xc2\xa015, the eye had become poorly defined, and winds steadily weakened. As it approached land, its center deteriorated, with minimal precipitation near the center; the eye was observed on a radar installed in July 1955. On August\xc2\xa017, Diane made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on the coast of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> near <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> Pressure at landfall was estimated at 986 millibars (29.1\xc2\xa0inHg), accompanied by winds just under hurricane intensity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hrd_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-hrd-5">[4]</a></sup> Diane struck the state only five days after Hurricane Connie struck the same general area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bt_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bt-3">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Diane quickly weakened as a tropical storm over the mountainous terrain of central North Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup> The associated area of precipitation expanded and spread away from the center to the north and northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grossman_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-grossman-6">[5]</a></sup> The weakening system turned to the north and recurved toward the northeast through Virginia after a ridge built in from the west.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-paths_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-paths-7">[6]</a></sup> It did not interact much with the non-tropical <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a>, and as a result it remained a distinct tropical cyclone over land. Convection redeveloped as the storm approached the Atlantic coast once again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grossman_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-grossman-6">[5]</a></sup> Diane passed through the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic states</a>, exiting New Jersey on August\xc2\xa019 into the Atlantic Ocean southeast of New York City. Paralleling the southern coast of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>, the storm later accelerated east-northeastward, becoming <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> on August\xc2\xa021. Passing south and east of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, the remnants of Diane accelerated and restrengthened slightly while moving to the northeast. Late on August\xc2\xa023, the storm dissipated between Greenland and Iceland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations_and_background">Preparations and background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diane_1955_rainfall.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="871" data-file-width="787" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Diane_1955_rainfall.gif/220px-Diane_1955_rainfall.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Diane_1955_rainfall.gif/330px-Diane_1955_rainfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Diane_1955_rainfall.gif/440px-Diane_1955_rainfall.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diane_1955_rainfall.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nRainfall totals from Hurricane Diane</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Late on August\xc2\xa014, more than two days before Diane made landfall, the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">United States Weather Bureau</a> issued a hurricane alert from Georgia through North Carolina. On August\xc2\xa015, the agency issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warning</a> from <a href="/wiki/Brunswick,_Georgia" title="Brunswick, Georgia">Brunswick, Georgia</a> to Wilmington, North Carolina, although the warning was later extended to the south and north to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fernandina,_Florida" title="Fernandina, Florida">Fernandina, Florida</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Cape Hatteras, North Carolina">Cape Hatteras, North Carolina</a>, respectively. The agency also issued storm warnings southward to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Augustine,_Florida" title="Saint Augustine, Florida">Saint Augustine, Florida</a> and northward to <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_City,_New_Jersey" title="Atlantic City, New Jersey">Atlantic City, New Jersey</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware</a> bays. Throughout the warned region, small ships were advised to remain at port.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> Before Diane made landfall, the North Carolina National Guard assisted in evacuating people near the <a href="/wiki/Pamlico_River" title="Pamlico River">Pamlico River</a>, and 700\xc2\xa0residents left their homes near <a href="/wiki/New_Bern,_North_Carolina" title="New Bern, North Carolina">New Bern</a>; thousands of tourists also evacuated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up817_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-up817-9">[8]</a></sup> The threat of the hurricane forced the planned retirement ceremony for <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Carney" title="Robert Carney">Robert Carney</a> to be transferred from an aircraft carrier in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a> to an academy dormitory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> All aircraft at <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Air_Station_Cherry_Point" title="Marine Corps Air Station Cherry Point">Marine Corps Air Station Cherry Point</a> were flown to safer locations further inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>All hurricane warnings were dropped after Diane moved inland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> Forecasters downplayed the threat of Diane after it weakened over Virginia;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> the Weather Bureau agreed they did not foresee the extent of the rain that would occur, instead calling for just "some local flooding".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> The agency later admitted they "goofed" in downplaying the storm\'s destructive potential after weakening,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91mj_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-91mj-14">[13]</a></sup> noting their lack of experience with extreme rainfall events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup> Once the storm moved ashore, the Weather Bureau transferred official forecasting duties to regional offices, and local newspapers also issued their own forecasts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91mj_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-91mj-14">[13]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/The_Republican_(Springfield,_Massachusetts)" title="The Republican (Springfield, Massachusetts)">Springfield Daily News</a> in Massachusetts noted that "moderate rains [were] possible" in its daily weather forecast ahead of the storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masslive_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-masslive-16">[15]</a></sup> Still, flood warnings were issued, with stream flooding forecasts of over 12\xc2\xa0hours in advance. Along smaller rivers, including the <a href="/wiki/Lehigh_River" title="Lehigh River">Lehigh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schuylkill_River" title="Schuylkill River">Schuylkill</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Farmington_River" title="Farmington River">Farmington</a>, forecasts were issued every few hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the summer of 1955, the eastern United States experienced generally hot and dry weather, leading to drought conditions and decreased water levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> When Hurricane Connie struck, its rainfall moistened the soil and heightened creeks throughout the Mid-Atlantic and New England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> Hurricane Diane struck North Carolina just five days later and affected the same general area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> After floods in 1936, the United States federal government enacted plans to prevent future devastating floods, although they made no progress by the time Connie and Diane struck in 1955.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hc_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-hc-18">[17]</a></sup> Along the Delaware River in the 1930s, state legislatures in New Jersey and Pennsylvania had established a commission that worked to clean up polluted water, but the legislators and commission blocked federal help, comparing it to European <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>; this was in contrast to the federally funded <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Valley_Authority" title="Tennessee Valley Authority">Tennessee Valley Authority</a>, which mitigated flooding along the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_River" title="Tennessee River">Tennessee River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Hurricane Diane\'s path over the eastern United States brought heavy rainfall, fueled by unusually moist air resulting from abnormally high <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aug_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-aug-20">[19]</a></sup> The worst flooding was in eastern Pennsylvania, northern New Jersey, southeastern New York, and southern New England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> Of the 287\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/Stream_gauge" title="Stream gauge">stream gauges</a> in the region, 129\xc2\xa0reported record levels during the course of the event. Many streams reported <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(hydrology)" title="Discharge (hydrology)">discharge</a> rates of more than double the previous records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Most of the flooding occurred along small rivers that rose to flood stage within hours, largely impacting populated areas;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> there were around 30\xc2\xa0million people in the region affected by the floods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Overall, 813\xc2\xa0houses were destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> with 14,000 heavily damaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> The floods severed infrastructure and affected several summer camps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-6"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> Damage to public utilities was estimated at $79\xc2\xa0million. Flooding in rural areas resulted in landslides in the mountains, while destroyed crops cost an estimated $7\xc2\xa0million. Hundreds of miles of roads and bridges were also destroyed, accounting for $82\xc2\xa0million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> Damage from Diane\'s winds were generally minor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> The hurricane caused $754,706,000 in damage (1955\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>), of which $600\xc2\xa0million was in New England,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> making it the costliest hurricane in American history at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> Taking into account indirect losses, such as loss of wages and business earnings, Diane was described as "the first billion dollar hurricane."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> This contributed to 1955 being the costliest <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> on record at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> Overall, there were at least 184\xc2\xa0deaths, potentially as many as 200.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carolinas">Carolinas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Carolinas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The strongest <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">sustained winds</a> associated with Diane\'s landfall in North Carolina reached 50\xc2\xa0mph (80\xc2\xa0km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Hatteras, North Carolina">Hatteras</a>, with gusts to 74\xc2\xa0mph (119\xc2\xa0km/h) in Wilmington. Any hurricane-force gusts were likely very sporadic and isolated in nature. Tides ran 6 to 8\xc2\xa0ft (1.8 to 2.4\xc2\xa0m) above normal near Wilmington, and waves 12\xc2\xa0ft (3.7\xc2\xa0m) in height struck the coast. The resultant <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> damaged beach houses, flooded coastal roads, and destroyed seawalls damaged by Hurricane Connie a few days prior.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up817_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-up817-9">[8]</a></sup> The center of the hurricane passed over Wilmington without much of a decrease in winds, suggesting the eye was disorganized or even nonexistent. Little precipitation fell in and around the city,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> though precipitation was more substantial elsewhere in the state, peaking at 7.04\xc2\xa0in (179\xc2\xa0mm) in New Bern. At <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oakway,_South_Carolina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oakway, South Carolina (page does not exist)">Oakway</a> in neighboring South Carolina, rainfall amounted to 2.39\xc2\xa0in (61\xc2\xa0mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-Atlantic">Mid-Atlantic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mid-Atlantic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Diane_1955_floods.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="529" data-file-width="793" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Diane_1955_floods.png/220px-Diane_1955_floods.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Diane_1955_floods.png/330px-Diane_1955_floods.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Diane_1955_floods.png/440px-Diane_1955_floods.png 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Diane_1955_floods.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nExtent of flooding in the northeastern United States, caused by Diane</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>After Diane crossed into Virginia, it dropped heavy rainfall of over 10\xc2\xa0in (250\xc2\xa0mm) in 24\xc2\xa0hours in the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Ridge_Mountains" title="Blue Ridge Mountains">Blue Ridge Mountains</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-7"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> peaking at 11.72\xc2\xa0in (298\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Big_Meadows" title="Big Meadows">Big Meadows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midrain_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-midrain-25">[24]</a></sup> There, the rains were enhanced by moist air rising over the mountain peaks and condensing, a process known as <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic lift</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-8"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> Rainfall of over 3\xc2\xa0in (76\xc2\xa0mm) occurred throughout Virginia, as well as into the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Panhandle_of_West_Virginia" title="Eastern Panhandle of West Virginia">Eastern Panhandle of West Virginia</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-rain-26">[25]</a></sup> where 5.71\xc2\xa0in (145\xc2\xa0mm) was reported at <a href="/wiki/Stony_River_Reservoir" title="Stony River Reservoir">Stony River Reservoir</a>. Similar precipitation amounts fell through Delaware, including 3.27\xc2\xa0in (83\xc2\xa0mm) at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Arboretum" title="United States National Arboretum">National Arboretum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midrain_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-midrain-25">[24]</a></sup> Rivers across the region rose above flood stage, including the <a href="/wiki/James_River" title="James River">James River</a> which crested at 30.4\xc2\xa0ft (9.3\xc2\xa0m) in <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Virginia" title="Columbia, Virginia">Columbia, Virginia</a>, which was 14.6\xc2\xa0ft (4.5\xc2\xa0m) above flood stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-9"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> High amounts of rainfall accrued in eastern Pennsylvania, peaking at 11.11\xc2\xa0in (282\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Big_Bushkill_Creek" title="Big Bushkill Creek">Pecks Pond</a> in the northeast portion of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midrain_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-midrain-25">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-rain-26">[25]</a></sup> As with Virginia, the heaviest rainfall occurred due to orographic lift near a mountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-10"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> In neighboring New Jersey, the highest precipitation was 8.10\xc2\xa0in (206\xc2\xa0mm) near <a href="/wiki/Sussex,_New_Jersey" title="Sussex, New Jersey">Sussex</a>. Rainfall in New York peaked at 9.05\xc2\xa0in (230\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mohonk" title="Lake Mohonk">Lake Mohonk</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midrain_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-midrain-25">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In Virginia, severe flooding occurred near <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a> and along the Blue Ridge Mountains. Near the coast, Diane damaged large areas of farmlands due to slow-moving floods. In the state, 21\xc2\xa0gauges reported their highest levels on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-5"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> High levels along the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a> flooded low-lying portions of Virginia and Washington, D.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> Wind gusts reached 62\xc2\xa0mph (100\xc2\xa0km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Roanoke,_Virginia" title="Roanoke, Virginia">Roanoke</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> In the state, flooding covered several roads, prompting closures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap822_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap822-28">[27]</a></sup> Due to the flat terrain, flooding in Delaware was described by the United States Geological Survey as "comparably mild". Flooding along the <a href="/wiki/Brandywine_Creek_(Christina_River)" title="Brandywine Creek (Christina River)">Brandywine Creek</a> was at least the fifth highest in 45\xc2\xa0years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-6"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Flooding was worst in the northernmost portion of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sum_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-sum-29">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Flooding began in many streams in eastern Pennsylvania on August\xc2\xa018. The <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware River</a> crested at over 40\xc2\xa0ft (12\xc2\xa0m) in <a href="/wiki/Easton,_Pennsylvania" title="Easton, Pennsylvania">Easton</a>, which was 4\xc2\xa0ft (1.2\xc2\xa0m) above the previous record set in 1903. In <a href="/wiki/Allentown,_Pennsylvania" title="Allentown, Pennsylvania">Allentown</a>, the Lehigh River crested at 23.4\xc2\xa0ft (7.1\xc2\xa0m), surpassing the previous record of 21.7\xc2\xa0ft (6.6\xc2\xa0m) set in 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-11"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> The floods were the worst in record across eastern portions of the state, notably in the Poconos and along all tributaries of the Delaware River from <a href="/wiki/Honesdale,_Pennsylvania" title="Honesdale, Pennsylvania">Honesdale</a> to Philadelphia. <a href="/wiki/Lake_Wallenpaupack" title="Lake Wallenpaupack">Lake Wallenpaupack</a> and other reservoirs mitigated flooding. Floods destroyed 17\xc2\xa0bridges and 55\xc2\xa0mi (89\xc2\xa0km) of track along the <a href="/wiki/Delaware,_Lackawanna_and_Western_Railroad" title="Delaware, Lackawanna and Western Railroad">Delaware, Lackawanna and Western Railroad</a>, which is the primary rail line in northeastern Pennsylvania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-7"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Damage to the line totaled several million dollars, and overall railroad damage in the state totaled $16\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> Hundreds of cars were damaged in the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-8"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Damage extended into Philadelphia due to flooding along the Schuylkill River, but the damage was minor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> In the small village of <a href="/wiki/Upper_Black_Eddy,_Pennsylvania" title="Upper Black Eddy, Pennsylvania">Upper Black Eddy</a>, hundreds of people became homeless, and the post office was washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-811nj_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-811nj-30">[29]</a></sup> Statewide, the floods destroyed or breached 30\xc2\xa0dams, and destroyed about 150\xc2\xa0road of rail bridges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sum_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-sum-29">[28]</a></sup> Flooding left home and factory damage in the Allentown area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poconos" title="Poconos">Poconos</a> in Pennsylvania, the <a href="/wiki/Brodhead_Creek" title="Brodhead Creek">Brodhead Creek</a> nearly destroyed a camp,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-12"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> killing 37\xc2\xa0people, mostly children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-9"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Many people at the camp fled to a lodge that was ultimately destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> The Brodhead Creek also washed out a bridge along <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_209" title="U.S. Route 209">U.S. Route 209</a> between <a href="/wiki/Stroudsburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Stroudsburg, Pennsylvania">Stroudsburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Stroudsburg,_Pennsylvania" title="East Stroudsburg, Pennsylvania">East Stroudsburg</a>, flooding both cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-10"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> There were about 75\xc2\xa0deaths in the area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-13"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> and another 10\xc2\xa0deaths occurred in <a href="/wiki/Greentown,_Pennsylvania" title="Greentown, Pennsylvania">Greentown</a> due to flooding along the <a href="/wiki/Lackawaxen_River" title="Lackawaxen River">Lackawaxen River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> Overall, there were 101\xc2\xa0deaths in the state,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-11"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> and damage totaled at least $70\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In New Jersey, flooding largely occurred north of <a href="/wiki/Trenton,_New_Jersey" title="Trenton, New Jersey">Trenton</a> and west of <a href="/wiki/Perth_Amboy,_New_Jersey" title="Perth Amboy, New Jersey">Perth Amboy</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-12"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> rainfall in the southern two\xe2\x80\x93thirds of the state was less than 3\xc2\xa0in (76\xc2\xa0mm).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> The three major rivers in the area - the Delaware, <a href="/wiki/Passaic_River" title="Passaic River">Passaic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Raritan_River" title="Raritan River">Raritan</a> - had severe flooding, and damage was widespread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-13"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> When the <a href="/wiki/Millstone_River" title="Millstone River">Millstone River</a> flooded, two teenagers drowned while canoeing, and a police officer drowned while attempting to rescue them. About 200\xc2\xa0families were evacuated in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_New_Jersey" title="Oakland, New Jersey">Oakland</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Ramapo_River" title="Ramapo River">Ramapo River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> Damage in the state was heaviest along the Delaware from <a href="/wiki/Port_Jervis,_New_York" title="Port Jervis, New York">Port Jervis, New York</a> to Trenton, where flooding inundated adjacent towns. Between the two towns, all but two bridges were damaged, including four that were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-14"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> About 500\xc2\xa0children had to be rescued from camps on three islands in the Delaware River; they were airlifted to a high school in <a href="/wiki/Frenchtown,_New_Jersey" title="Frenchtown, New Jersey">Frenchtown</a>. In that city, about 200\xc2\xa0people were forced to evacuate their houses along the water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-811nj_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-811nj-30">[29]</a></sup> In Trenton, workers used sandbags to prevent flooding from affecting government buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> Flooding destroyed the <a href="/wiki/Portland%E2%80%93Columbia_Pedestrian_Bridge" title="Portland\xe2\x80\x93Columbia Pedestrian Bridge">Portland\xe2\x80\x93Columbia Pedestrian Bridge</a>, first constructed in 1831, after most of it was submerged. The center of the <a href="/wiki/Northampton_Street_Bridge" title="Northampton Street Bridge">Northampton Street Bridge</a> between Easton, Pennsylvania and <a href="/wiki/Phillipsburg,_New_Jersey" title="Phillipsburg, New Jersey">Phillipsburg, New Jersey</a> collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-15"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> A dam near <a href="/wiki/Branchville,_New_Jersey" title="Branchville, New Jersey">Branchville</a> collapsed, flooding the town and causing heavy damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-7"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> About 200\xc2\xa0homes were damaged or destroyed in <a href="/wiki/Lambertville,_New_Jersey" title="Lambertville, New Jersey">Lambertville</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> Statewide, 93\xc2\xa0homes were destroyed. Damage was estimated at $27.5\xc2\xa0million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-8"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Flash floods occurred in mountainous regions of southeastern New York, including Port Jervis along the Delaware River. <a href="/wiki/Wappinger_Creek" title="Wappinger Creek">Wappinger Creek</a> flooded to cause heavy damage. Most streams in the <a href="/wiki/Rondout_Creek" title="Rondout Creek">Rondout Creek</a> basin left damage due to fast-moving waters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-16"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> including heavy damage near <a href="/wiki/Ellenville,_New_York" title="Ellenville, New York">Ellenville</a>. Damage in New York was largely limited to an area between Port Jervis and <a href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie,_New_York" title="Poughkeepsie, New York">Poughkeepsie</a>. Several bridges were destroyed along the <a href="/wiki/Bash_Bish_Falls" title="Bash Bish Falls">Bash Bish Brook</a>, and portions of U.S. Route 209 were flooded. Damage totaled $16.2\xc2\xa0million, and there was one death in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-9"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_England">New England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Diane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: New England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" Diane</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flood_of_1955_(Connecticut)" title="Flood of 1955 (Connecticut)">Flood of 1955 (Connecticut)</a></div>\n<p>Diane produced heavy rainfall after recurving inland, setting rainfall records in several areas. <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Locks,_Connecticut" title="Windsor Locks, Connecticut">Windsor Locks, Connecticut</a> reported 12.05\xc2\xa0in (306\xc2\xa0mm) in a 23\xe2\x80\x91hour period;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-2">[1]</a></sup> the station\'s total, located near <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford</a>, was 5.32\xc2\xa0in (135\xc2\xa0mm) higher than the 24\xe2\x80\x91hour rainfall record in Hartford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-14"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> Some locations along the <a href="/wiki/Housatonic_River" title="Housatonic River">Housatonic River</a> experienced 0.75\xc2\xa0in (19\xc2\xa0mm) per hour over 24\xc2\xa0hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sum_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-sum-29">[28]</a></sup> The highest total in the state was 16.86\xc2\xa0in (428\xc2\xa0mm) at a station near <a href="/wiki/Torrington,_Connecticut" title="Torrington, Connecticut">Torrington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nerain_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-nerain-31">[30]</a></sup> This is the highest rainfall on record in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statemaxima_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-statemaxima-32">[31]</a></sup> The highest rainfall in the United States related to the storm was 19.75\xc2\xa0in (502\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_Massachusetts" title="Westfield, Massachusetts">Westfield, Massachusetts</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rain_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-rain-26">[25]</a></sup> which was also the wettest known storm in the state\'s history as well as throughout New England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statemaxima_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-statemaxima-32">[31]</a></sup> Other statewide rainfall maxima in New England included 8.45\xc2\xa0in (215\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_Rhode_Island" title="Greenville, Rhode Island">Greenville, Rhode Island</a>, 4.34\xc2\xa0in (110\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Essex_Junction,_Vermont" title="Essex Junction, Vermont">Essex Junction, Vermont</a>, 3.31\xc2\xa0in (84\xc2\xa0mm) in <a href="/wiki/Fitzwilliam,_New_Hampshire" title="Fitzwilliam, New Hampshire">Fitzwilliam, New Hampshire</a>, and 0.62\xc2\xa0in (16\xc2\xa0mm) at <a href="/wiki/Flagstaff_Lake_(Maine)" title="Flagstaff Lake (Maine)">Long Falls Dam</a> in Maine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nerain_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-nerain-31">[30]</a></sup> Throughout New England, 206\xc2\xa0dams were damaged or destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-17"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> mostly in the region south of <a href="/wiki/Worcester,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester, Massachusetts">Worcester, Massachusetts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erh_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-erh-33">[32]</a></sup> About 7,000\xc2\xa0people were injured throughout New England, most of whom in Connecticut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-10"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Damage was greatest in Connecticut, where floods affected about two-thirds of the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-18"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> It was the largest flood on record in the state\'s history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sum_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-sum-29">[28]</a></sup> All major streams and valleys were flooded during the storm, including hundreds of tributaries, and 30\xc2\xa0gauges in the state reported the highest level on record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-19"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_River" title="Connecticut River">Connecticut River</a> at Hartford reached the third-highest level on record at the time, cresting at 30.6\xc2\xa0ft (9.3\xc2\xa0m), or 14.6\xc2\xa0ft (4.5\xc2\xa0m) above flood stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prelim_8-15"><a href="#cite_note-prelim-8">[7]</a></sup> Although there was rural damage, the city of Hartford was spared from flooding due to previously constructed dykes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-11"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Naugatuck_River" title="Naugatuck River">Naugatuck River</a> had significant flooding that damaged or destroyed every bridge across it and did extensive damage in Ansonia. In <a href="/wiki/Waterbury,_Connecticut" title="Waterbury, Connecticut">Waterbury</a>, the river washed buildings and railroad girders into a bridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-20"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> In the city, 30\xc2\xa0people were killed, including 26 in 13\xc2\xa0houses that were washed away in one block.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt814_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt814-34">[33]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Quinebaug_River" title="Quinebaug River">Quinebaug River</a> flooded the city of <a href="/wiki/Putnam,_Connecticut" title="Putnam, Connecticut">Putnam</a> at the same time that a major fire originated at a <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-21"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap823_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap823-35">[34]</a></sup> Much of the commercial district of <a href="/wiki/Winsted,_Connecticut" title="Winsted, Connecticut">Winsted</a> was destroyed by the Mad River, which reached 10\xc2\xa0ft (3.0\xc2\xa0m) deep; the floods destroyed most buildings on the south side of the town\'s Main Street, and carried away several cars from a car dealership. The local newspaper reported that 95% of businesses were destroyed or severely damaged in Winsted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt814_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt814-34">[33]</a></sup> High rivers destroyed historical sites and buildings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-22"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> and statewide Diane destroyed 563\xc2\xa0houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-12"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> There were 77\xc2\xa0deaths in the state and $350\xc2\xa0million in damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-erh_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-erh-33">[32]</a></sup> Most of the damage in the state was industrial or commercial damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-13"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In Rhode Island, flooding was worst in the northern portion of the state, mostly along the <a href="/wiki/Blackstone_River" title="Blackstone River">Blackstone River</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-23"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> which expanded to a width of about 1\xc2\xa0mi (1.6\xc2\xa0km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> The Horseshoe Dam was washed out, causing heavy damage in <a href="/wiki/Woonsocket,_Rhode_Island" title="Woonsocket, Rhode Island">Woonsocket</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-24"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> There, about 6,000 of its 50,000\xc2\xa0residents were left unemployed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap823_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap823-35">[34]</a></sup> Record high tides were also reported. In Rhode Island, damage was estimated at $21\xc2\xa0million, mostly in Woonsocket, and there were three deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sum_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-sum-29">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Much of southern Massachusetts, from its border with New York toward Worcester and to the ocean, experienced flooding. Most streams in western Massachusetts overflowed their banks, and in southeastern Massachusetts, which is largely flat terrain, streams flooded large areas along their channels; these streams moved slowly, while other areas in New England sustained damage due to the fast-moving nature of the floods. Record flooding was reported along 24\xc2\xa0stream gauges in the state, including ones that surpassed the peak set by the <a href="/wiki/1938_New_England_hurricane" title="1938 New England hurricane">1938 New England hurricane</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-25"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Both the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neponset_River" title="Neponset River">Neponset</a> rivers were among those that flooded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walpole_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-walpole-36">[35]</a></sup> About 40% of the city of Worcester was flooded during Diane,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Russell,_Massachusetts" title="Russell, Massachusetts">Russell</a>, the state police forced many residents to evacuate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masslive_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-masslive-16">[15]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Weymouth,_Massachusetts" title="Weymouth, Massachusetts">Weymouth</a>, the floods were considered at least a <a href="/wiki/Return_period" title="Return period">1 in 50 year</a> event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walpole_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-walpole-36">[35]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Little_River_(Merrimack_River)" title="Little River (Merrimack River)">Little River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Buffumville_Lake" title="Buffumville Lake">Buffumville, Massachusetts</a> had a peak discharge of 8,340\xc2\xa0ft\xc2\xb3/s (236\xc2\xa0m\xc2\xb3/s), which was 6.2\xc2\xa0times greater than the previous peak and 28.5\xc2\xa0times the average annual flooding. Flooded rivers breached <a href="/wiki/Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity" title="Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity">run-of-the-river dams</a> and covered nearby roadways, although dams with reservoirs resulted in less flooding. Nearly all dams along the <a href="/wiki/French_River_(Massachusetts)" title="French River (Massachusetts)">French River</a> were severely damaged or destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-26"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> One failed dam in <a href="/wiki/Auburn,_Massachusetts" title="Auburn, Massachusetts">West Auburn</a> washed out a portion of <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_20_in_Massachusetts" title="U.S. Route 20 in Massachusetts">U.S. Route 20</a>, and the same route was washed out near <a href="/wiki/Charlton,_Massachusetts" title="Charlton, Massachusetts">Charlton</a>. An overflown brook also damaged the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Turnpike" title="Massachusetts Turnpike">Massachusetts Turnpike</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-14"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> A train on the <a href="/wiki/Boston_and_Albany_Railroad" title="Boston and Albany Railroad">Boston and Albany Railroad</a> line plunged into a washed out portion along the <a href="/wiki/Westfield_River" title="Westfield River">Westfield River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-27"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Along the same river, floods destroyed roads and tobacco farms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-15"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> In the state, 97\xc2\xa0houses were destroyed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-16"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> Damage in Massachusetts was second worst of the affected states,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-28"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> totaling $110\xc2\xa0million;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-17"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> the damage was largely due to flooded basements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walpole_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-walpole-36">[35]</a></sup> There were 12\xc2\xa0deaths in the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-18"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:left;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Diane</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In Diane\'s immediate aftermath, one of the first priorities in response was to distribute adequate inoculations for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Typhoid" title="Typhoid">typhoid</a> amongst the widespread areas left without clean drinking water. The United States Army assisted in search and rescue operations using helicopters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-milw_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-milw-37">[36]</a></sup> After the floods of Hurricane Diane, more than 100,000\xc2\xa0people fled to shelter or away from their houses. The <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a> quickly provided aid to the affected residents,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-11"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> using churches and public buildings to house homeless people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctf_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ctf-38">[37]</a></sup> In the two weeks after the storm, Americans donated about $10\xc2\xa0million to the Red Cross.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> The countries of Great Britain, Netherlands, Australia, Canada, France, Austria, and Venezuela offered aid to help the flood victims, sending emergency supplies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup> Additional flooding affected New England in September and October 1955, although neither was as major as those caused by Hurricane Diane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-29"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> Following Diane, hundreds of companies affected by the flooding installed waterproof doors and windows to preempt similar disasters in the future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up828_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-up828-41">[40]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dwight_Eisenhower" title="Dwight Eisenhower">Dwight Eisenhower</a> declared eight states as disaster areas, making them eligible for federal aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Small_Business_Administration" title="Small Business Administration">Small Business Administration</a> opened 18\xc2\xa0temporary offices in the eastern United States for people to take out disaster loan applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> In the months after the storm, both the United States federal government and the American Red Cross had difficulty raising enough funds for the storm victims; collectively, the Red Cross, the Small Business Administration, and <a href="/wiki/Farmers_Home_Administration" title="Farmers Home Administration">Farmers Home Administration</a> raised $37\xc2\xa0million, which was less than 8% of Diane\'s damage total. Throughout 1955, the Red Cross assisted about 10,000\xc2\xa0families in New England and the Mid-Atlantic states; some of the families received aid to move to a new house not in a flood zone. The Small Business Administration provided about 1,600\xc2\xa0loans, totaling $25\xc2\xa0million, for small businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newburgh_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-newburgh-44">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbert_H._Lehman" title="Herbert H. Lehman">Herbert H. Lehman</a> proposed a $12\xc2\xa0billion <a href="/wiki/National_Flood_Insurance_Program" title="National Flood Insurance Program">federal flood insurance program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> In 1956, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> passed the Federal Flood Insurance Act, but the program was not enacted due to lack of funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> A nationwide flood program was not enacted until the passage of the <a href="/wiki/National_Flood_Insurance_Act_of_1968" title="National Flood Insurance Act of 1968">National Flood Insurance Act of 1968</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> After the floods from Diane, the American federal government provided funding for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">Army Corps of Engineers</a> to construct dams and reservoirs throughout New England to mitigate future flooding. In about 14\xc2\xa0years, the Corps built 29\xc2\xa0dams in Connecticut alone at the cost of $70\xc2\xa0million, including three along the Connecticut River.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctf_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-ctf-38">[37]</a></sup> The federal government restored plans from the 1930s to build dams along the Delaware River, one of which along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tocks_Island" title="Tocks Island">Tocks Island</a>. A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tocks_Island_Dam_Controversy" title="Tocks Island Dam Controversy">controversy</a> arose there due to the 40\xc2\xa0mi (64\xc2\xa0km) long reservoir the dam would have created, causing 600\xc2\xa0families to be displaced. The project was canceled in 1975, and the acquired lands became the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Water_Gap_National_Recreation_Area" title="Delaware Water Gap National Recreation Area">Delaware Water Gap National Recreation Area</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In Pennsylvania, washed-out rail lines prevented operation along the Delaware, Lackawanna and Western Railroad for several weeks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-30"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> and lines reopened after about two months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrd_21-19"><a href="#cite_note-wrd-21">[20]</a></sup> The expense of reopening, and the loss of being closed, led to the railroad merging with the <a href="/wiki/Erie_Railroad" title="Erie Railroad">Erie Railroad</a> to become the <a href="/wiki/Erie_Lackawanna_Railway" title="Erie Lackawanna Railway">Erie Lackawanna Railway</a> in 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> One stranded train along the line prompted a helicopter to rescue 235\xc2\xa0people. Flooding along the Lehigh River destroyed 15\xc2\xa0industrial plants, which left more than 15,000\xc2\xa0people near Allentown, Pennsylvania without work temporarily. The mayor of <a href="/wiki/Scranton,_Pennsylvania" title="Scranton, Pennsylvania">Scranton</a> declared a state of emergency due to the floods, ordering all businesses to close. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> soldiers provided water to residents after the town lost its water supply. Elsewhere, the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_National_Guard" title="Pennsylvania National Guard">Pennsylvania National Guard</a> was on duty on streets in damaged towns,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-12"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup> including 50 to prevent looting in Upper Black Eddy, which was one of the hardest hit towns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-811nj_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-811nj-30">[29]</a></sup> Helicopters assisted in discovering bodies at Camp Davis, where many deaths occurred during the storm. Statewide, thousands of people were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap822_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-ap822-28">[27]</a></sup> In Stroudsburg, there was a food shortage, and officials enacted a curfew, after reports of looting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> In the same city, water was shipped in milk cartons to the flood victims, which later inspired a <a href="/wiki/Federal_Civil_Defense_Administration" title="Federal Civil Defense Administration">Federal Civil Defense Administration</a> proposal to use water packaged in milk containers in the event of a nuclear attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> The state government implemented a tax on cigarettes to help pay for storm damage, which lasted for about two years;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> this was partially due to a lack of significant funding from the federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapp_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-shapp-53">[52]</a></sup> Pennsylvania also enacted an increase in the gasoline tax that was later made permanent to pay for the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway_System" title="Interstate Highway System">Interstate Highway System</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup> The two taxes, each an increase of 1\xc2\xa0<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._penny" title="U.S. penny">penny</a>, totaled $71\xc2\xa0million, a part of which was set aside for future disasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup> The experience of the storm\'s aftermath provided the basis for the aftermath for <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Agnes" title="Hurricane Agnes">Hurricane Agnes</a> in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapp_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-shapp-53">[52]</a></sup> In New Jersey, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_New_Jersey" title="List of Governors of New Jersey">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_B._Meyner" title="Robert B. Meyner">Robert B. Meyner</a> declared the floods as at the time the state\'s worst natural disaster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_12-13"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-12">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After the Naugatuck River flood in Connecticut cut off communications and bridges, the state was effectively cut in two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-geo_17-31"><a href="#cite_note-geo-17">[16]</a></sup> The state\'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Connecticut_National_Guard" title="Connecticut National Guard">National Guard</a> used helicopters to rescue people. Governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abraham_A._Ribicoff" title="Abraham A. Ribicoff">Abraham A. Ribicoff</a> visited areas affected by the flooding, due to the damage, Connecticut was declared a federal disaster area on August\xc2\xa020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctf_38-2"><a href="#cite_note-ctf-38">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> The declaration allocated $25\xc2\xa0million in assistance to the state. Governor Ribicoff requested $34\xc2\xa0million in funds to rebuild and produce future flood mitigation projects; the state\'s funding was paid by a combination of <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a> and tax increases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt814_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyt814-34">[33]</a></sup> Including subsequent storms, the 1955 floods cumulatively killed 91\xc2\xa0people and left 1,100\xc2\xa0families homeless. Flooding occurred in 67\xc2\xa0towns, resulting in damage to 20,000\xc2\xa0families. About 86,000\xc2\xa0people were left unemployed after the floods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ctf_38-3"><a href="#cite_note-ctf-38">[37]</a></sup> In Winsted, the buildings that were washed away along the south side of Main Street were never rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt814_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-nyt814-34">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Massachusetts Governor <a href="/wiki/Christian_Herter" title="Christian Herter">Christian Herter</a> also issued a state of emergency, due to the widespread flooding damage. As a result, the state\'s National Guard and the Army Corps assisted in cleanup, and most roads took three weeks to clear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-masslive_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-masslive-16">[15]</a></sup> Residents in areas affected by Diane\'s flooding were advised to boil water and not to use gas cooking equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap822_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-ap822-28">[27]</a></sup> Diane\'s historic rainfall resulted in the wettest month on record in Boston with a total of 17\xc2\xa0in (430\xc2\xa0mm), a record that stands as of 2010;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup> Boston\'s 24\xe2\x80\x91hour total of 8.4\xc2\xa0in (210\xc2\xa0mm) remained the highest daily total as of 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> Following Diane\'s floods, cities in Massachusetts enlarged <a href="/wiki/Culvert" title="Culvert">culverts</a> and improved draining systems, as well as constructing <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weirs</a>; these systems helped mitigate against future flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-walpole_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-walpole-36">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The name Diane was <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">retired</a> from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a> naming list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup> Due to the damage from hurricanes in 1954 and 1955, including Diane, public outcry over storm damage led to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup> Using a monetary <a href="/wiki/Deflator" title="Deflator">deflator</a> in 2010 United States dollars, the damage from Diane would be about $7.4\xc2\xa0billion, which would have been the 17th highest in the United States. Accounting for inflation, changes in personal wealth, and population changes, it is estimated Diane would have caused $18\xc2\xa0billion in damage in 2010, or the 15th highest for a United States hurricane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
