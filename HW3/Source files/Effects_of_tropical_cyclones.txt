href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Deaths.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nPie graph of American tropical cyclone casualties by cause from 1970\xe2\x80\x931999</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The main effects of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> include heavy <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, strong <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surges</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. The destruction from a tropical cyclone depends mainly on its <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">intensity</a>, its size, and its location. Tropical cyclones act to remove forest canopy as well as change the landscape near coastal areas, by moving and reshaping sand dunes and causing extensive erosion along the coast. Even well inland, heavy rainfall can lead to mudslides and landslides in mountainous areas. Their effects can be sensed over time by studying the concentration of the Oxygen-18 isotope within caves within the vicinity of cyclones\' paths being very hazardous to people\'s life.</p>\n<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> has passed, devastation often continues. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standing_water" title="Standing water">Standing water</a> can cause the spread of disease, and transportation or communication infrastructure may have been destroyed, hampering clean-up and rescue efforts. Nearly two million people have died globally due to tropical cyclones. Despite their devastating effects, tropical cyclones are also beneficial, by potentially bringing rain to dry areas and moving heat from the tropics poleward. Out at sea, ships take advantage of their known characteristics by navigating through their weaker, western half.</p>\n<p>When a cyclone hits it causes PST hazards. PST is an acronym standing for Primary, Secondary and Tertiary. A primary hazard involves destructive winds, debris and storm surge. Secondary hazard is flooding, fires and of course fresh water flooding. Finally Tertiary hazards involves food prices that go majorly up and other long term hazards like water poisoning.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="At_sea">At sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: At sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A mature tropical cyclone can release heat at a rate upwards of 6x10<sup>14</sup> <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NOAA_Question_of_the_Month_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Question_of_the_Month-1">[1]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones on the open sea cause large waves, heavy rain, and high winds, disrupting international shipping and, at times, causing <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">shipwrecks</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18cva_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-18cva-2">[2]</a></sup> Generally, after its passage, a tropical cyclone stirs up ocean water, lowering <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> behind it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Cooling_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_Cooling-3">[3]</a></sup> This cool wake can cause the region to be less favorable for a subsequent tropical cyclone. On rare occasions, tropical cyclones may actually do the opposite. <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dennis" title="Hurricane Dennis">Hurricane Dennis</a> blew warm water behind it, contributing to the unprecedented intensity of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emily_(2005)" title="Hurricane Emily (2005)">Hurricane Emily</a>, which followed it closely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHC_Emily_disc_8_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Emily_disc_8-4">[4]</a></sup> Hurricanes help to maintain the global heat balance by moving warm, moist tropical air to the mid-latitudes and polar regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zurich_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zurich-5">[5]</a></sup> Were it not for the movement of heat poleward (through other means as well as hurricanes), the tropical regions would be unbearably hot.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_colonization">North American colonization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: North American colonization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Shipwrecks are common with the passage of strong tropical cyclones. Such shipwrecks can change the course of history,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as well as influence art and literature. A hurricane led to a victory of the Spanish over the French for control of Fort Caroline, and ultimately the Atlantic coast of North America, in 1565.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Sea_Venture" title="Sea Venture">Sea Venture</a></i> was wrecked near Bermuda in 1609 which led to the colonization of Bermuda<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and provided the inspiration for <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare\'s</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(play)" title="The Tempest (play)">The Tempest</a></i>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shipping">Shipping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shipping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Typhoons_and_Wind_Speeds.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="309" data-file-width="367" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Typhoons_and_Wind_Speeds.gif/220px-Typhoons_and_Wind_Speeds.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Typhoons_and_Wind_Speeds.gif/330px-Typhoons_and_Wind_Speeds.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/Typhoons_and_Wind_Speeds.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Typhoons_and_Wind_Speeds.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe <b>dangerous semicircle</b> is the upper-right corner, with the arrow marking the direction of motion of a Northern Hemisphere storm. Note that typhoons, etc. are asymmetrical, and <i>semicircle</i> is a convenient misnomer.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Mariners have a way to safely navigate around tropical cyclones. They split tropical cyclones in two, based on their direction of motion, and maneuver to avoid the right segment of the cyclone in the Northern Hemisphere (the left in the Southern Hemisphere). Sailors term the right side the <b>dangerous semicircle</b> since the heaviest rain and strongest winds and seas were located in this half of the storm, as the cyclone\'s translation speed and its rotational wind are additive. The other half of the tropical cyclone is called the <b>navigable semicircle</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> since weather conditions are lessened (subtractive) in this portion of the storm (but are still potentially quite hazardous). The rules of thumb for ship travel when a tropical cyclone is in their vicinity are to avoid them if at all possible and do not cross their forecast path (crossing the T). Those traveling through the dangerous semicircle are advised to keep to the true wind on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Starboard" title="Starboard">starboard</a> bow and make as much headway as possible. Ships moving through the navigable semicircle are advised to keep the true wind on the starboard quarter while making as much headway as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Upon_landfall">Upon landfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Upon landfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The most significant effects of a tropical cyclone occur when they cross coastlines, making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> then it destroys ships and lives.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strong_winds">Strong winds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Strong winds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_windspeed_climatology" title="Tropical cyclone windspeed climatology">Tropical cyclone windspeed climatology</a></div>\n<p>Strong <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> can damage or destroy vehicles, buildings, bridges, personal property and other outside objects, turning loose debris into deadly flying projectiles. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricanes</a> comprise just 21% of all land falling tropical cyclones, but account for 83% of all damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faqd5_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-faqd5-12">[12]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones often knock out power to tens or hundreds of thousands of people, preventing vital communication and hampering rescue efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Power_failures_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Power_failures-13">[13]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones often destroy key bridges, overpasses, and roads, complicating efforts to transport food, clean water, and medicine to the areas that need it. Furthermore, the damage caused by tropical cyclones to buildings and dwellings can result in economic damage to a region, and to a <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a> of the population of the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfo_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-oxfo-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_surge">Storm surge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Storm surge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg/180px-Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg/270px-Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg/360px-Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_katrina_damage_gulfport_mississippi.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe aftermath of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> in <a href="/wiki/Gulfport,_Mississippi" title="Gulfport, Mississippi">Gulfport, Mississippi</a>. Katrina was the costliest tropical cyclone in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> history.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">Storm surge</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, or the increase in sea level due to the cyclone, is typically the worst effect from landfalling tropical cyclones, historically resulting in 90% of tropical cyclone deaths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfo_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-oxfo-14">[14]</a></sup> The relatively quick surge in sea level can move miles/kilometers inland, flooding homes and cutting off escape routes. The storm surges and winds of hurricanes may be destructive to human-made structures, but they also stir up the waters of coastal estuaries, which are typically important fish breeding locales.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_rainfall">Heavy rainfall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Heavy rainfall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology" title="Tropical cyclone rainfall climatology">Tropical cyclone rainfall climatology</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> activity in a tropical cyclone produces intense <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a>, potentially resulting in flooding, mudslides, and landslides. Inland areas are particularly vulnerable to freshwater <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flooding" title="Flooding">flooding</a>, due to residents not preparing adequately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NHCFlooding_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-NHCFlooding-15">[15]</a></sup> Heavy inland rainfall eventually flows into coastal estuaries, damaging marine life in coastal estuaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The wet environment in the aftermath of a tropical cyclone, combined with the destruction of sanitation facilities and a warm tropical climate, can induce epidemics of disease which claim lives long after the storm passes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfo_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-oxfo-14">[14]</a></sup> Infections of cuts and bruises can be greatly amplified by wading in sewage-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polluted" title="Polluted">polluted</a> water. Large areas of standing water caused by flooding also contribute to mosquito-borne illnesses. Furthermore, crowded evacuees in <a href="/wiki/Emergency_shelter" title="Emergency shelter">shelters</a> increase the risk of disease propagation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxfo_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-oxfo-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FEMA_-_37841_-_Flooding_in_Seminole_County.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2848" data-file-width="4288" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/FEMA_-_37841_-_Flooding_in_Seminole_County.jpg/350px-FEMA_-_37841_-_Flooding_in_Seminole_County.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/FEMA_-_37841_-_Flooding_in_Seminole_County.jpg/525px-FEMA_-_37841_-_Flooding_in_Seminole_County.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/FEMA_-_37841_-_Flooding_in_Seminole_County.jpg/700px-FEMA_-_37841_-_Flooding_in_Seminole_County.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FEMA_-_37841_-_Flooding_in_Seminole_County.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nFlooding in <a href="/wiki/Seminole_County,_Florida" title="Seminole County, Florida">Seminole County, Florida</a> from <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Fay_(2008)" title="Tropical Storm Fay (2008)">Tropical Storm Fay (2008)</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Although cyclones take an enormous toll in lives and personal property, they may be important factors in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> regimes of places they affect and bring much-needed precipitation to otherwise dry regions. Hurricanes in the eastern north Pacific often supply moisture to the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwestern United States</a> and parts of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005_EPac_outlook_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-2005_EPac_outlook-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> receives over half of its rainfall from typhoons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Whipple_54_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Whipple_54-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">Hurricane Camille</a> averted drought conditions and ended water deficits along much of its path,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christopherson_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christopherson-19">[19]</a></sup> though it also killed 259\xc2\xa0people and caused $9.14\xc2\xa0billion (2005 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USD" title="USD">USD</a>) in damage.</p>\n<p>On the other hand, the occurrence of tropical cyclones can cause tremendous variability in rainfall over the areas they affect: indeed cyclones are the primary cause of the most extreme rainfall variability in the world, observed in places such as <a href="/wiki/Onslow,_Western_Australia" title="Onslow, Western Australia">Onslow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Port_Hedland,_Western_Australia" title="Port Hedland, Western Australia">Port Hedland</a> in subtropical <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> where the annual rainfall can range from practically nothing with no cyclones to over 1,000 millimetres (39\xc2\xa0in) if cyclones are abundant.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornadoes">Tornadoes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tornadoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_tropical_cyclone-spawned_tornadoes" title="History of tropical cyclone-spawned tornadoes">History of tropical cyclone-spawned tornadoes</a></div>\n<p>The broad rotation of a land falling tropical cyclone often spawns <a href="/wiki/History_of_tropical_cyclone-spawned_tornadoes" title="History of tropical cyclone-spawned tornadoes">tornadoes</a>, particularly in their right front quadrant. While these tornadoes are normally not as strong as their non-tropical counterparts, heavy damage or loss of life can still occur. Tornadoes can also be spawned as a result of <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)#eyewall." title="Eye (cyclone)">eyewall mesovortices</a>, which persist until landfall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_L6_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_L6-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deaths">Deaths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Deaths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table align="right" border="1" cellpadding="2" cellspacing="0" class="wikitable">\n<tr>\n<th colspan="2">Deaths per year from tropical cyclones</th>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Australia</td>\n<td>5<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>United States</td>\n<td>25<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADLER_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-ADLER-22">[22]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>East Asia</td>\n<td>740<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Globally</td>\n<td>10000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADLER_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-ADLER-22">[22]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>During the last two centuries, tropical cyclones have been responsible for the deaths of about 1.9\xc2\xa0million people worldwide. It is estimated that 10,000\xc2\xa0people per year perish due to tropical cyclones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ADLER_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-ADLER-22">[22]</a></sup> The deadliest tropical cyclone was the <a href="/wiki/1970_Bhola_cyclone" title="1970 Bhola cyclone">1970 Bhola cyclone</a>, which had a death toll of anywhere from 300,000 to 500,000 lives.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Before <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, the average death rate for tropical cyclones in the United States was decreasing. The main cause of storm-related fatalities was shifting away from storm surge and towards freshwater flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Median" title="Median">median</a> death rate per storm had increased through 1979, with a lull during the 1980\xe2\x80\x931995 period. This was due to greater numbers of people moving to the coastal margins and into harm\'s way. Despite advances in warning strategies and reduction in track forecast error, this increase in fatalities is expected to continue for as long as people migrate towards the shore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction_and_repopulation">Reconstruction and repopulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reconstruction and repopulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3744" data-file-width="5616" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg/350px-Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg/525px-Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg/700px-Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aerial_view_of_Tacloban_after_Typhoon_Haiyan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAerial image of destroyed houses in <a href="/wiki/Tacloban" title="Tacloban">Tacloban</a>, following <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>While tropical cyclones may well seriously damage settlement, total destruction encourages rebuilding. For example, the destruction wrought by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">Hurricane Camille</a> on the Gulf coast spurred redevelopment, greatly increasing local property values.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christopherson_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christopherson-19">[19]</a></sup> Research indicates that the typical hurricane strike raises real house prices for a number of years, with a maximum effect of between 3 percent to 4 percent three years after occurrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> However, disaster response officials point out that redevelopment encourages more people to live in clearly dangerous areas subject to future deadly storms. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> is the most obvious example, as it devastated the region that had been revitalized after Hurricane Camille. Many former residents and businesses do relocate to inland areas away from the threat of future hurricanes as well.</p>\n<p>In isolated areas with small populations, tropical cyclones may cause enough casualties to contribute to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Founder%27s_effect" title="Founder\'s effect">founder\'s effect</a> as survivors repopulate their place. For example, around 1775, a typhoon hit <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pingelap_Atoll&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pingelap Atoll (page does not exist)">Pingelap Atoll</a>, and in combination with a subsequent famine, reduced the island\'s population to a low level. Several generations after the disaster, as many as 10% of Pingelapese have a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">genetic</a> form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Color-blindness" title="Color-blindness">color-blindness</a> called <a href="/wiki/Achromatopsia" title="Achromatopsia">achromatopsia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pingelap_in_Nature_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pingelap_in_Nature-27">[27]</a></sup> This is due to one of the survivors of the depopulation brought on by the typhoon having a mutated gene, which the <a href="/wiki/Population_bottleneck" title="Population bottleneck">population bottleneck</a> caused to be at a higher-than-usual level in succeeding generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pingelap_in_Nature_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pingelap_in_Nature-27">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FEMA_-_8414_-_Photograph_by_Mark_Wolfe_taken_on_09-20-2003_in_North_Carolina.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> (2003)\'s effect on the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_natural_resources">Effects on natural resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Effects on natural resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomorphology">Geomorphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Geomorphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Tropical cyclones reshape the <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> near the coast by eroding sand from the <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beach</a> as well as offshore, rearranging coral, and changing dune configuration onshore. Their rain water gets absorbed into <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a> within caves, creating a record of past tropical cyclone impacts.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_ridges">Coastal ridges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Coastal ridges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Waves and storm surges accompanying tropical cyclones erode undersea sands, erode shell deposits, break off corals from near shore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reefs" title="Reefs">reefs</a> in their paths, and carry all this detritus landwards in a rolling wave of material that is deposited onshore, above highest astronomical <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tide</a> as a ridge of sand, shell and coral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nott01_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nott01-28">[28]</a></sup> For example, each severe tropical cyclone (i.e. Category\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935 on the Saffir-Simpson scale) crossing northeast <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>\'s tropical coastline since the last significant change in sea levels (about 5000\xc2\xa0years ago) has \'emplaced\' such ridges within the coastal landscape forming, in some places, series of ridges and a geomorphological record of highest magnitude cyclones hitting the coast over 3000 \xe2\x80\x93 5000\xc2\xa0years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nott_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nott-29">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Eyewitness accounts verify ridges of this kind are formed by severe tropical cyclones and two clear examples cited are the 18 kilometres (11\xc2\xa0mi) long, 35 metres (115\xc2\xa0ft) wide, 3.5 metres (11\xc2\xa0ft) high coral shingle ridge deposited on Funafuti Atoll (Central South Pacific) by <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Bebe" title="Cyclone Bebe">Cyclone Bebe</a> in October 1972, and the large coral shingle ridge deposited on Jaluit Atoll (<a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a>) by <a href="/wiki/1958_Pacific_typhoon_season#Super_Typhoon_Ophelia" title="1958 Pacific typhoon season">Typhoon Ophelia</a> in January 1958. In tropical northeast Australia, an intense tropical cyclone hit in March 1918 (crossing over the town of <a href="/wiki/Innisfail,_Queensland" title="Innisfail, Queensland">Innisfail</a>), at which time there were eyewitness accounts of a 4.5 metres (15\xc2\xa0ft) to 5.1 metres (17\xc2\xa0ft) high ridge of pumice being deposited by that cyclone\'s surge as it crossed the coast.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nott01_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nott01-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Limestone_cave_stalagmites">Limestone cave stalagmites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Limestone cave stalagmites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>When tropical cyclones cross land, thin layers of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> of \'light\' composition (i.e. unusual <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopic</a> ratio of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxygen_18" title="Oxygen 18">Oxygen-18</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">Oxygen-16</a>) are deposited onto <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a> in <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> caves up to 300 kilometres (190\xc2\xa0mi) from the cyclone\'s path.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nott_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nott-29">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As the cloud tops of tropical cyclones are high and cold, and their air is humid \xe2\x80\x93 their rainwater is \'lighter\'. In other words, the rainfall contains significantly higher quantities of unevaporated Oxygen-18 than other tropical rainfall. The isotopically lighter rainwater soaks into the ground, percolates down into caves, and, within a couple of weeks, Oxygen-18 transfers from the water into calcium carbonate, before being deposited in thin layers or \'rings\' within stalagmites. A succession of such events created within stalagmites maintain a record of cyclones tracking within a 300 kilometres (190\xc2\xa0mi) radius of caves going back centuries, millennia, or even millions of years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frappier_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frappier-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At Actun Tunichil Muknal cave in central <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, researchers drilling stalagmites with a computer- controlled dental drill accurately identified and verified evidence of isotopically light rainfall for 11 tropical cyclones occurring over a 23-year period (1978\xe2\x80\x932001).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frappier_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frappier-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chillagoe" title="Chillagoe">Chillagoe</a> limestone caves in northeast Australia (130 kilometres (81\xc2\xa0mi) inland from <a href="/wiki/Cairns" title="Cairns">Cairns</a>) researchers identified and matched evidence of isotopically light rainfall with 100\xc2\xa0years of cyclone records, and from this have created a record of tropical cyclones from 2004 back to 1200 A.D. (an 800-year record).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nott_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nott-29">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Landscapes">Landscapes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_tropical_cyclones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Landscapes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Severe tropical cyclones defoliate tropical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forest_canopy" title="Forest canopy">forest canopy</a> trees, remove <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epiphytes" title="Epiphytes">epiphytes</a> from the trees, break tree crown stems, and cause tree falls. The degree of damage they do along their paths, at a landscape level (i.e. &gt; 10 kilometres (6.2\xc2\xa0mi)), can be catastrophic yet variable and patchy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Trees break at 42\xc2\xa0m/s, regardless of size and type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Stripping trees and scattering forest debris also provides fuel for <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a>, such as a blaze that lasted three months in 1989 and burned 460 square miles (1,200\xc2\xa0km<sup>2</sup>) of forest that had been stripped by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilbert" title="Hurricane Gilbert">Hurricane Gilbert</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Natural_Hazards_Map_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Natural_Hazards_Map-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<ul>\n<li>Wind velocity gradients or horizontal <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> (size of cyclone, the intensity of cyclone, proximity to the cyclone, and local scale cyclonic convection effects).</li>\n<li>Degree of exposure (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Windward" title="Windward">windward</a> exposure, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leeward" title="Leeward">leeward</a> acceleration, or local topographic sheltering/shading); and</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">Ecosystem</a> species composition and forest structure</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Assessments of cyclone damage done to tropical rainforest landscapes in northeast <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, have produced the following <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/typology" title="wiktionary:typology">typology</a> for describing and \'mapping\' the variable impacts they have along their paths, as follows:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<ol>\n<li><b>Severe and extensive</b> closest to the centre of cyclone: impact appears to be multidirectional and is evidenced by crowns of most trees having been broken, smashed or windthrown</li>\n<li><b>Severe and localised</b> closer to the cyclone centre than its edge: direction of the destructive winds is clearly identifiable, and severe canopy disruption is limited to the windward aspect of these forested areas</li>\n<li><b>Moderate canopy disturbance</b> closer to cyclone edge than its centre: most of the tree stems are still standing, with only some treefalls, and most of the damage is the defoliation of the canopy and branch breakage;</li>\n<li><b>Slight canopy disturbance</b> closest to cyclone edge: occasional stem fall or branch breakage, with most of the damage consisting of loss of foliage on the forest edges only, subsequently followed by leaf damage and heavy leaf litter falls.</li>\n</ol>\n<h2><span
