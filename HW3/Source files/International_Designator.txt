message</a>)</i></small></div>\n</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The <b>International Designator</b>, also known as <b>COSPAR designation</b>, and in the United States as <b>NSSDC ID</b>, is an international naming convention for <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a>. It consists of the launch year, a 3-digit incrementing launch number of that year and up to a 3-letter code representing the sequential identifier of a piece in a launch.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>For example, <a class="external text" href="http://nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov/nmc/spacecraftDisplay.do?id=1990-037A" rel="nofollow">1990-037A</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery" title="Space Shuttle Discovery">Space Shuttle Discovery</a> on mission <a href="/wiki/STS-31" title="STS-31">STS-31</a>, which carried the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">Hubble Space Telescope</a> (<a class="external text" href="http://nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov/nmc/spacecraftDisplay.do?id=1990-037B" rel="nofollow">1990-037B</a>) into space. This launch was the 37th known successful launch worldwide in 1990. The number reveals that it was launched in 1990 and that it was the 37th launch made that year. Spacecraft which do not complete an orbit of the Earth, for example launches which fail to achieve orbit, are not usually assigned IDs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>The designation system has been generally known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COSPAR" title="COSPAR">COSPAR</a> system, named for the Committee on Space Research (COSPAR) of the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_for_Science" title="International Council for Science">International Council for Science</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> COSPAR subsumed the first designation system, devised at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> in 1958. That system used letters of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> to designate artificial satellites. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a> was designated 1957 Alpha 2. The Harvard designation system continued to be used for satellites launched up to the end of 1962, when it was replaced with the modern system. The first satellite to receive a new-format designator was <a href="/wiki/Luna_E-6_No.2" title="Luna E-6 No.2">Luna E-6 No.2</a>, 1963-001B, although some sources, including the NSSDC website, anachronistically apply the new-format designators to older satellites, even those no longer in orbit at the time of its introduction.</p>\n<p>The catalog is administered in the United States by the <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Science_Data_Center" title="National Space Science Data Center">National Space Science Data Center</a> (NSSDC), part of <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>.<sup
