template">e</abbr></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Drizzle</b> is a light liquid <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> consisting of liquid <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> drops smaller than those of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> \xe2\x80\x93 generally smaller than 0.5\xc2\xa0mm (0.02\xc2\xa0in) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Drizzle is normally produced by low <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratiform</a> <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus_cloud" title="Stratocumulus cloud">stratocumulus clouds</a>. Precipitation rates from drizzle are on the order of a millimetre per day or less at the ground. Owing to the small size of drizzle drops, under many circumstances drizzle largely evaporates before reaching the surface and so may be undetected by observers on the ground. The <a href="/wiki/METAR" title="METAR">METAR</a> code for drizzle is <b>DZ</b> and for freezing drizzle is <b>FZDZ</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spence2006_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spence2006-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Effects"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Effects</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Occurrence"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Occurrence</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Influence_of_aerosols"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Influence of aerosols</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drizzle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While most drizzle has only a minor immediate impact upon humans, <a href="/wiki/Freezing_drizzle" title="Freezing drizzle">freezing drizzle</a> can lead to treacherous conditions. Freezing drizzle occurs when supercooled drizzle drops land on a surface whose temperature is below freezing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-https:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3Dz1jDJtLvJEAC.26pg.3DPA63.26dq.3D.2522freezing.2Bdrizzle.2522.26hl.3Den.26sa.3DX.26ved.3D0ahUKEwit9-vrqezWAhVR-lQKHTpEAxkQ6AEILDAB.23v.3Donepage.26q.3D.2522freezing.2520drizzle.2522.26f.3Dfalse2007_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-https:.2F.2Fbooks.google.com.2Fbooks.3Fid.3Dz1jDJtLvJEAC.26pg.3DPA63.26dq.3D.2522freezing.2Bdrizzle.2522.26hl.3Den.26sa.3DX.26ved.3D0ahUKEwit9-vrqezWAhVR-lQKHTpEAxkQ6AEILDAB.23v.3Donepage.26q.3D.2522freezing.2520drizzle.2522.26f.3Dfalse2007-3">[3]</a></sup> These drops immediately freeze upon impact, leading to the buildup of sheet ice (sometimes called <a href="/wiki/Black_ice" title="Black ice">black ice</a>) on the surface of roads.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drizzle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Drizzle tends to be the most frequent form of precipitation over large areas of the world\'s oceans, particularly in the colder regions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">subtropics</a>. These regions are dominated by shallow marine <a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus_cloud" title="Stratocumulus cloud">stratocumulus</a> and trade wind <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus</a> clouds, which exist entirely within the marine <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a>. Despite the low rates of surface accumulation, it has become apparent that drizzle actually exerts a major influence over the cloud structure, coverage, and radiative properties in these regions. This has motivated scientists to design more sophisticated, sensitive instruments such as high frequency <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radars</a> that can detect drizzle. These studies have shown that the quantity of drizzle is strongly linked to cloud morphology and tends to be associated with updrafts within the marine <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a>. Increased amounts of drizzle tend to be found in marine clouds that form in clean airmasses that have low concentrations of cloud droplets. This interconnection between clouds and drizzle can be explored using high resolution numerical modelling such as <a href="/wiki/Large_eddy_simulation" title="Large eddy simulation">large eddy simulation</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_aerosols">Influence of aerosols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drizzle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influence of aerosols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It has been hypothesized that increasing the amounts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Particulate" title="Particulate">particulates</a> in the atmosphere through human activities may lead to a suppression of drizzle. According to this hypothesis, because drizzle can be an effective means of removing moisture from a cloud, its suppression could help to increase the thickness, coverage, and longevity of marine <a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus_cloud" title="Stratocumulus cloud">stratocumulus</a> clouds. This would lead to increased cloud <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> on the regional to global scale, and a cooling effect on the atmosphere. Estimates using complex global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_models" title="Climate models">climate models</a> suggest that this effect may be partially masking the effects of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse gas</a> increases on the global surface temperature. However, it is not clear that the representation of the chemical and physical processes needed to accurately simulate the interaction between aerosols, clouds, and drizzle in our current climate models is sufficient to fully understand the global impacts of changes in particulates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drizzle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">Fog</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mist" title="Mist">Mist</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">Rain</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drizzle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">National Weather Service Observing Handbook No. 8, Aviation Weather Observations for Supplementary Aviation Weather Reporting Stations (SAWRS), Manual Observations, October 1996</span></li>\n<li id="cite_note-Spence2006-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-Spence2006_2-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Spence, Charles F.
