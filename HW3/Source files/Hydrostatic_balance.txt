This occurs when external forces such as <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">gravity</a> are balanced by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_force" title="Pressure gradient force">pressure gradient force</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> For instance, the pressure-gradient force prevents gravity from collapsing <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> into a thin, dense shell, whereas gravity prevents the pressure gradient force from diffusing the atmosphere into space.</p>\n<p>Hydrostatic equilibrium is the current distinguishing criterion between <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Small_Solar_System_body" title="Small Solar System body">small Solar System bodies</a>, and has other roles in <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">astrophysics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Planetary_geology" title="Planetary geology">planetary geology</a>. This qualification typically means that the object is symmetrically rounded into a <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">spheroid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">ellipsoid</a> shape, where any irregular surface features are due to a relatively thin solid <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>. There are <a href="/wiki/List_of_gravitationally_rounded_objects_of_the_Solar_System" title="List of gravitationally rounded objects of the Solar System">31 observationally confirmed such objects</a> (apart from the Sun), sometimes called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planemo" title="Planemo">planemos</a>,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, seven more<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-brown-3">[3]</a></sup> that are virtually certain, and <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets" title="List of possible dwarf planets">a hundred or so more</a> that are likely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown_3-1"><a class="mw-headline" id="Fluids">Fluids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a equilibrium pertains to <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatics" title="Hydrostatics">hydrostatics</a> and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Principles_of_equilibrium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Principles of equilibrium (page does not exist)">principles of equilibrium</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">fluids</a>. A hydrostatic balance is a particular balance for weighing substances in water. Hydrostatic balance allows the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_(observation)" title="Discovery (observation)">discovery</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravities</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrophysics">Astrophysics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrostatic_equilibrium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Astrophysics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In any given layer of a <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">star</a>, there is a hydrostatic equilibrium between the outward thermal pressure from below and the weight of the material above pressing inward. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">isotropic</a> gravitational field compresses the star into the most compact shape possible. A rotating star in hydrostatic equilibrium is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">oblate spheroid</a> up to a certain (critical) angular velocity. An extreme example of this phenomenon is the star <a href="/wiki/Vega" title="Vega">Vega</a>, which has a rotation period of 12.5 hours. Consequently, Vega is about 20% larger at the equator than at the poles. A star with an angular velocity above the critical angular velocity becomes a Jacobi (<a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">scalene</a>) ellipsoid, and at still faster rotation it is no longer ellipsoidal but <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pyriform" title="wikt:pyriform">piriform</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviform" title="Oviform">oviform</a>, with yet other shapes beyond that, though shapes beyond scalene are not stable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>If the star has a massive nearby companion object then <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">tidal forces</a> come into play as well, distorting the star into a scalene shape when rotation alone would make it a spheroid. An example of this is <a href="/wiki/Beta_Lyrae" title="Beta Lyrae">Beta Lyrae</a>.</p>\n<p>Hydrostatic equilibrium is also important for the <a href="/wiki/Intracluster_medium" title="Intracluster medium">intracluster medium</a>, where it restricts the amount of fluid that can be present in the core of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_of_galaxies" title="Cluster of galaxies">cluster of galaxies</a>.</p>\n<p>We can also use the principle of hydrostatic equilibrium to estimate the <a href="/wiki/Velocity_dispersion" title="Velocity dispersion">velocity dispersion</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">dark matter</a> in clusters of galaxies. Values for the ratio range from .11 to .14 for various surveys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_geology">Planetary geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrostatic_equilibrium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Planetary geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The concept of hydrostatic equilibrium has also become important in determining whether an astronomical object is a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">dwarf planet</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Small_Solar_System_body" title="Small Solar System body">small Solar System body</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Definition_of_planet" title="Definition of planet">definition of planet</a> adopted by the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a> in 2006, one defining characteristic of planets and dwarf planets is that they are objects that have sufficient gravity to overcome their own rigidity and assume hydrostatic equilibrium. Such a body will normally have the differentiated interior and geology of a world (a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planemo" title="Planemo">planemo</a>), though near-hydrostatic bodies such as the proto-planet <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">4 Vesta</a> may also be differentiated. Sometimes the equilibrium shape is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">oblate spheroid</a>, as is the case with Earth. However, in the cases of moons in synchronous orbit, nearly unidirectional tidal forces create a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scalene_ellipsoid" title="Scalene ellipsoid">scalene ellipsoid</a>. Also, the dwarf planet <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">Haumea</a> is scalene due to its rapid rotation.</p>\n<p>It had been thought that icy objects with a diameter larger than roughly 400\xc2\xa0km are usually in hydrostatic equilibrium, whereas those smaller than that are not. Icy objects need less mass for hydrostatic equilibrium than rocky objects. The smallest object that is known to have an equilibrium shape is the icy moon <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">Mimas</a> at 397\xc2\xa0km, whereas the largest object known to have an obviously non-equilibrium shape is the rocky asteroid <a href="/wiki/2_Pallas" title="2 Pallas">Pallas</a> at 532\xc2\xa0km (582\xc2\xa0\xc3\x97\xc2\xa0556\xc2\xa0\xc3\x97\xc2\xa0500\xc2\xa0\xc2\xb1\xc2\xa018\xc2\xa0km). However, Mimas is not actually in hydrostatic equilibrium for its current rotation. The smallest body confirmed to be in hydrostatic equilibrium is the icy moon <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(moon)" title="Rhea (moon)">Rhea</a>, at 1,528\xc2\xa0km, whereas the largest body known to not be in hydrostatic equilibrium is the icy moon <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">Iapetus</a>, at 1,470\xc2\xa0km.</p>\n<p>Because the <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">terrestrial planets</a> and dwarf planets (and likewise the larger <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">satellites</a>, like the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>) have irregular surfaces, this definition evidently has some flexibility, but a specific means of quantifying an object\'s shape by this standard has not yet been announced. Local irregularities may be consistent with global equilibrium. For example, the massive base of the tallest mountain on Earth, <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea" title="Mauna Kea">Mauna Kea</a>, has deformed and depressed the level of the surrounding crust, so that the overall distribution of mass approaches equilibrium. The amount of leeway afforded the definition could affect the classification of the asteroid <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">Vesta</a>, which may have solidified while in hydrostatic equilibrium but was subsequently significantly deformed by large impacts (now 572.6\xc2\xa0\xc3\x97\xc2\xa0557.2\xc2\xa0\xc3\x97\xc2\xa0446.4\xc2\xa0km).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hubble_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hubble-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospherics">Atmospherics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrostatic_equilibrium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Atmospherics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the atmosphere, the pressure of the air decreases with increasing altitude. This pressure difference causes an upward force called the <a href="/wiki/Pressure-gradient_force" title="Pressure-gradient force">pressure-gradient force</a>. The force of gravity balances this out, keeping the atmosphere bound to Earth and maintaining pressure differences with altitude.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
