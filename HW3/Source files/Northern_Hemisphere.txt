For other planets in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, north is defined as being in the same celestial hemisphere relative to the <a href="/wiki/Invariable_plane" title="Invariable plane">invariable plane</a> of the solar system as Earth\'s North pole.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-report_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-report-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Owing to the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a> in the Northern Hemisphere lasts from the <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">December solstice</a> (typically December\xc2\xa021 UTC) to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/March_Equinox" title="March Equinox">March Equinox</a> (typically March\xc2\xa020 UTC), while <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a> lasts from the <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">June solstice</a> (typically June\xc2\xa021 UTC) through to the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">September equinox</a> (typically September\xc2\xa023 UTC). The dates vary each year due to the difference between the <a href="/wiki/Calendar_year" title="Calendar year">calendar year</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Year#Astronomical_years" title="Year">astronomical year</a>.</p>\n<p>Its surface is 60.7% water, compared with 80.9% water in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>, and it contains 67.3% of Earth\'s land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Geography_and_climate"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Geography and climate</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Demographics"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Demographics</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#List_of_continents"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">List of continents</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Northern_Hemisphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Arctic is the region north of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a>. Its climate is characterized by cold winters and cool summers. Precipitation mostly comes in the form of snow. The Arctic experiences some days in summer when the Sun never sets, and some days during the winter when it never rises. The duration of these phases varies from one day for locations right on the Arctic Circle to several months near the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a>, which is the middle of the Northern Hemisphere.</p>\n<p>Between the Arctic Circle and the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> lies the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_temperate_zone" title="Northern temperate zone">Northern temperate zone</a>. The changes in these regions between summer and winter are generally mild, rather than extreme hot or cold. However, a temperate climate can have very unpredictable weather.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">Tropical</a> regions (between the Tropic of Cancer and the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>) are generally hot all year round and tend to experience a <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">rainy season</a> during the summer months, and a <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a> during the winter months.</p>\n<p>In the Northern Hemisphere, objects moving across or above the surface of the Earth tend to turn to the right because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">coriolis effect</a>. As a result, large-scale horizontal flows of air or water tend to form <a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">clockwise</a>-turning <a href="/wiki/Ocean_gyre" title="Ocean gyre">gyres</a>. These are best seen in ocean circulation patterns in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">North Pacific</a> oceans.</p>\n<p>For the same reason, flows of air down toward the northern surface of the Earth tend to spread across the surface in a clockwise pattern. Thus, clockwise air circulation is characteristic of <a href="/wiki/High_pressure" title="High pressure">high pressure</a> weather cells in the Northern Hemisphere. Conversely, air rising from the northern surface of the Earth (creating a region of low pressure) tends to draw air toward it in a counterclockwise pattern. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Hurricanes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical storms</a> (massive low-pressure systems) spin <a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">counter-clockwise</a> in the Northern Hemisphere.</p>\n<p>The shadow of a sundial moves clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere (opposite of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>). During the day, the Sun tends to rise to its maximum at a southerly position except between the Tropic of Cancer and the Equator, where the sun can be seen to the north, directly overhead, or to the south at noon dependent on the time of year.</p>\n<p>When viewed from the Northern Hemisphere, the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> appears inverted compared to a view from the Southern Hemisphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The North Pole faces away from the galactic center of the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">Milky Way</a>. This results in the Milky Way being sparser and dimmer in the Northern Hemisphere compared to the Southern Hemisphere, making the Northern Hemisphere more suitable for deep-space observation, as it is not "blinded" by the Milky Way.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
