style="text-align:center;padding:0px 2px"></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center; border-top:#aaa 1px solid;">Part of the <b><a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933 Atlantic hurricane season</a></b></td>\n</tr>\n<tr style="display:none">\n<td colspan="2"></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The <b>1933 Tampico hurricane</b> was one of two storms in the <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933 Atlantic hurricane season</a> to reach <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> intensity on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[nb 1]</a></sup> It developed on September\xc2\xa016 near the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, and slowly intensified while moving across the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. Becoming a hurricane on September\xc2\xa019, its strengthening rate increased while passing south of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>. Two days later, the hurricane reached peak winds, estimated at 160\xc2\xa0mph (260\xc2\xa0km/h). After weakening, it made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucat\xc3\xa1n Peninsula">Yucat\xc3\xa1n Peninsula</a>, destroying several houses. One person was killed offshore <a href="/wiki/Progreso,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Progreso, Yucat\xc3\xa1n">Progreso, Yucat\xc3\xa1n</a> during the storm.</p>\n<p>Over land, the hurricane weakened to a tropical storm, although it re-intensified slightly in the Gulf of Mexico. On September\xc2\xa025, it made a second landfall just south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a> with winds at around 110\xc2\xa0mph (180\xc2\xa0km/h), and it quickly dissipated over land. Damage was heaviest there, estimated at $5\xc2\xa0million (1933\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>) and there were hundreds of deaths. About 75% of the houses in Tampico were damaged, including about 50% of houses that had severe to total destruction to their roofs. The destruction prompted the declaration of <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a>, and there was a curfew instated.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div class="toc" id="toc">\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1933_Tampico_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="633" data-file-width="1024" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png/220px-1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png/330px-1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png/440px-1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png 2x" width="220"/></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1933_Atlantic_hurricane_15_track.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMap plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A tropical disturbance moved westward across the tropical Atlantic Ocean in mid-September. On September\xc2\xa016, it was estimated that a tropical depression <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">developed</a> about 185\xc2\xa0mi (300\xc2\xa0km) east-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Tobago" title="Tobago">Tobago</a>. It moved west-northwestward through the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, passing about 14\xc2\xa0mi (22\xc2\xa0km) south of <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>; however, the system was very weak, and the island reported east winds of only 12\xc2\xa0mph (19\xc2\xa0km/h). After entering the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, the depression intensified into a tropical storm on September\xc2\xa018, and a day later it became a hurricane. On September\xc2\xa020 it passed to the south of Jamaica while quickly strengthening. At 0000\xc2\xa0<a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on September\xc2\xa021, a ship in the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> reported a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 929\xc2\xa0mbar (27.4\xc2\xa0inHg). Ordinarily, the pressure reading would suggest winds of 153\xc2\xa0mph (246\xc2\xa0km/h); however, observations from the ship indicated the hurricane was smaller than normal, with a <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">radius of maximum winds</a> of 8\xc2\xa0mi (13\xc2\xa0km). As a result, the peak winds were estimated at 160\xc2\xa0mph (260\xc2\xa0km/h), or a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After peaking in intensity, the hurricane continued to the northwest. Early on September\xc2\xa022, it passed about 50\xc2\xa0mi (80\xc2\xa0km)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[nb 2]</a></sup> south of <a href="/wiki/Cozumel" title="Cozumel">Cozumel</a> before making <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_peninsula" title="Yucat\xc3\xa1n peninsula">Yucat\xc3\xa1n peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> Its landfall intensity was unknown; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis">Atlantic hurricane reanalysis</a> in 2012 suggested that the storm could have retained Category\xc2\xa05 status until landfall, although local observations did not validate the theory. In the original analysis of the season, the landfall intensity was estimated at 105\xc2\xa0mph (165\xc2\xa0km/h). As a compromise, the 2012 reanalysis estimated the hurricane struck land with winds of 140\xc2\xa0mph (230\xc2\xa0km/h). The cyclone rapidly weakened while crossing the Yucat\xc3\xa1n peninsula, and on September\xc2\xa023 it emerged into the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> with winds of 65\xc2\xa0mph (105\xc2\xa0km/h). It quickly reintensified into a hurricane while moving toward the northeast Mexican coastline. At around 0000\xc2\xa0UTC on September\xc2\xa025, the hurricane made its final landfall just south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tampico,_Tamaulipas" title="Tampico, Tamaulipas">Tampico, Tamaulipas</a> with winds of 110\xc2\xa0mph (180\xc2\xa0km/h); this was based on a ship in the eye reporting a pressure of 960\xc2\xa0mbar (28\xc2\xa0inHg). Within 12\xc2\xa0hours, the storm dissipated over land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1933_Tampico_hurricane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>While passing south of Jamaica, the hurricane produced high surf in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Jamaica" title="Kingston, Jamaica">Kingston</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> Heavy rains from the storm affected much of the island, which disrupted travel and caused one mudslide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[5]</a></sup> In Cozumel offshore the Yucat\xc3\xa1n Peninsula, winds reached 76\xc2\xa0mph (122\xc2\xa0km/h) from the southeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup> There, the hurricane destroyed a fishing pier and several houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eve_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-eve-8">[6]</a></sup> While the storm crossed the peninsula, <a href="/wiki/Progreso,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Progreso, Yucat\xc3\xa1n">Progreso, Yucat\xc3\xa1n</a> reported east winds of 55\xc2\xa0mph (89\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup> Offshore Progreso, the storm capsized a boat, killing one person.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eve_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-eve-8">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Before the hurricane made its final landfall, the threat of heavy rainfall prompted evacuations around Tampico,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eve_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-eve-8">[6]</a></sup> just 10\xc2\xa0days after another hurricane hit the same region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mwr_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-5">[4]</a></sup> Near Tampico, a ship reported winds of 81\xc2\xa0mph (130\xc2\xa0km/h), and a station in Tampico recorded winds of 53\xc2\xa0mph (85\xc2\xa0km/h).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meta_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-meta-4">[3]</a></sup> High winds cut power lines and blew off or heavily damaged the roofs of half of the city\'s houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> A high accompanying <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> washed away many people from their homes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nature-11">[9]</a></sup> and several barges were washed away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> The harbor was filled with debris after the storm, rendering it unusable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> Portions of the city were flooded up to 15\xc2\xa0ft (4.6\xc2\xa0m) deep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up925_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-up925-13">[11]</a></sup> According to news reports, the hurricane damaged about 75% of Tampico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup> Majority of the damage was to poorly constructed houses,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> although a large hospital in Tampico was also destroyed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pain_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-pain-14">[12]</a></sup> killing 87\xc2\xa0people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calg_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-calg-15">[13]</a></sup> Roads were blocked throughout the city, and the river was closed to boating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> The rail line was impacted, which disrupted relief supplies from reaching the region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> Outside of the city, the hurricane increased levels along the <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1nuco_River" title="P\xc3\xa1nuco River">P\xc3\xa1nuco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tames%C3%AD_River" title="Tames\xc3\xad River">Tames\xc3\xad</a> rivers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-up925_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-up925-13">[11]</a></sup> which remained above flood stage for several days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-port_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-port-16">[14]</a></sup> West of Tampico, the hurricane flooded the entire city of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1rdenas,_San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="C\xc3\xa1rdenas, San Luis Potos\xc3\xad">C\xc3\xa1rdenas, San Luis Potos\xc3\xad</a>, killing 20 and injuring 200\xc2\xa0people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A1nuco,_Veracruz" title="P\xc3\xa1nuco, Veracruz">P\xc3\xa1nuco, Veracruz</a>, about 5,000\xc2\xa0people were left homeless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="San Luis Potos\xc3\xad">San Luis Potos\xc3\xad</a> state, 30\xc2\xa0people were killed when a dam burst. Heavy rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Monterrey" title="Monterrey">Monterrey</a> caused rivers to flood. Damage spread as far as the west coast of Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pain_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-pain-14">[12]</a></sup> Throughout the country, air travel was disrupted, and several train lines were washed out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> leaving three trains missing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pain_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-pain-14">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Initial news reports suggested a death toll of up to 5,000\xc2\xa0people, and the storm was considered "the greatest disaster in recent Mexican history".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup> Two days after the storm dissipated, the death toll was set at 54, with 850\xc2\xa0people injured and potentially thousands that were buried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> Search and rescue teams dug through the debris of the Tampico to find survivors and victims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[15]</a></sup> In 1997, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> listed the death toll between 184\xe2\x80\x93200,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[16]</a></sup> and damage was estimated at $5\xc2\xa0million (1933\xc2\xa0USD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nature_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-nature-11">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the aftermath of the storm, food and medical supplies rapidly diminished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ellen_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-ellen-12">[10]</a></sup> After the storm dissipated, doctors and nurses traveled to the city to assist in the aftermath, while trains carried food and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-apa927_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-apa927-9">[7]</a></sup> A train from Monterrey to Tampico carried food, medicine, and soldiers to assist in rebuilding, but was delayed by ongoing flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-port_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-port-16">[14]</a></sup> Military planes were utilized to transport aid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-calg_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-calg-15">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">Martial law</a> was declared in Tampico following the storm, and officials imposed a 7 p.m. curfew. Uninjured citizens helped clear the roads. President <a href="/wiki/Abelardo_L._Rodr%C3%ADguez" title="Abelardo L. Rodr\xc3\xadguez">Abelardo L. Rodr\xc3\xadguez</a> asked the governors of the Mexican states to send aid and for residents to send money to the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Mexico" title="Bank of Mexico">Bank of Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mont_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-mont-10">[8]</a></sup> Following the storm, residents overcrowded the buildings that remained standing in Tampico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
